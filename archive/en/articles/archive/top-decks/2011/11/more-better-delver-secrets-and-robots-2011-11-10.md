==========================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on November 10, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic."

Corbett Gray made Top 8 of the Star City Games Open Series event in Las Vegas with this very different look at Wolf Run Ramp: Wolf Run Robots.


Many of us first saw the "Robots" look at the Wolf Run Ramp strategy as a pre-States version innovated by [Living End](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End) progenitor Travis Woo, though Travis credits ascending R&D member Gavin Verhey with many of the deck's innovations.


Corbett Gray ran with the Robots and earned himself a semifinals appearance in the Open.


*How Does This Deck Work?*


Hmmm... Most of you probably know how the Wolf Run Ramp family of decks work already. You set up [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) and [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) with [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) and then kill the opponent with massive infect (which may or may not be Plan B; I mean an active [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) is quite the threat already, no?).

Since Player of the Year frontrunner Owen Turtenwald's win at the Wisconsin 2011s, the [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) version of Wolf Run Ramp seems to have been the dominant build. [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) is a fast potential turn two play, and a difficult threat to deal with for especially control decks.


... So all of that together makes decks like Gray's look positively *different*. What *is* different about this deck?

**[Sphere of the Suns](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+the+Suns) and [Copper Myr](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copper+Myr).** These probably seem like strange additions in a green-based Ramp deck, especially given the advocacy for [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) and [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) by some of the game's top players. Isn't [Copper Myr](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copper+Myr) just a terrible [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves)? Isn't [Sphere of the Suns](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+the+Suns) generally just worse in a green deck? Yes, and yes... except for this deck's mana base.



![Glimmerpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Glimmerpost)

 

**[Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost). Four [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost)s.** While playing [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) is generally awesome, sometimes you are so far behind beatdown that you can play the Titan... and then just die. Playing four copies of [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost) makes [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) very helpful against Red Decks and their aggressive cousins. [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) for two [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost)s, gaining 4. Attack, get the other two [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost)s, gain 8. Card advantage plus life gain (plus having a gigantic 6/6 attacking them) can really help turn around games where your back is to the wall.



![Kessig Wolf Run](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Kessig+Wolf+Run)

 

**[Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run). Four [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run)s.** In addition to four [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost)s, the Wolf Run Robots version runs not two but all four copies of [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run). One of the issues with these kinds of decks is that—especially given the popularity of Blue-Black Control, a deck with not just counterspells but [Doom Blade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade)s—they can be threat-locked. When you play all the [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run)s, you are more likely to naturally draw one even without [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)... and then every creature you draw—every mana dork from the lowly [Copper Myr](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copper+Myr) to the even more inoffensive [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)—becomes a potential threat.


With four [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost)s, four [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run)s, four [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus)es, plus even more colorless-producing lands, we have a deck with fewer [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s, therefore less likelihood of hitting green on the first turn... which explains the odd choice of mana accelerators (and you can forget about building around [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)).



![Blasphemous Act](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Blasphemous+Act)

 

The other major super cool thing going on in this deck is unlucky out of the sideboard. *Thirteen* unlucky! [Blasphemous Act](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blasphemous+Act), upon first glance, seems like it should be prohibitively expensive... Until you realize this is a card that is designed to mess up lots of creatures. An opponent playing White-Blue Humans or Green-White Tokens will often have tons of little guys on the battlefield (plus, your creatures help set up the discount). Generally, [Blasphemous Act](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blasphemous+Act) will often be comparable to [Slagstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slagstorm) or [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) in terms of cost; and, long story short, ka-pow. Thirteen damage is enough to take down almost every reasonable creature in the format.

I think that we will see Blue-Red Tempo rise into one of the most popular decks in the format... The cards out of *Innistrad* are just so conducive to its success.


[Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) (and, in Cai's build, [Stromkirk Noble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromkirk+Noble)) let the deck come out of the gates on turn one, racking up fast damage like a Red Deck. But like a blue aggro-control deck, Blue-Red Tempo can lean on [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) or [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) to defend even a small lead, if for a short time, with the main goal being more turns dealing more damage.

But what this deck really wants to do is just burn the opponent out.


One of the most powerful synergies in the Standard format is [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) + [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley). It's not just that [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) can re-buy [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley), but the best card in Standard is *also* a superb set-up for [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley)'s morbid ability.

Consider...


Opponent attacks with whatever.


You cast [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), giving a [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley) in your graveyard flashback. The [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) blocks, and probably dies.


Before the end of the turn, you can cast [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley) and deal your opponent 5 damage rather than 3. Really the question is "How many combinations of [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley) and [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) do you need to deal 20 damage?"


Or, in this context, how much damage do you need [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) and [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley) to do when you have set up your opponent with a first-turn [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) and/or [Stromkirk Noble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromkirk+Noble)?

One of the best cards in the Blue-Red Tempo deck is [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder). Now provided you draw an [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island), [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) helps this land-light deck play like a 24-land deck, but in this deck, [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder)*also* sets up the transformation from [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) into its 3/2 flying form.

Essentially we see the Illusions shell ([Lord of the Unreal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lord+of+the+Unreal) locking arms with [Phantasmal Bear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Bear) and its buddies), but with [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) again helping out the offense from turn one. On the one hand the deck loses [Phantasmal Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Dragon), but on the other hand it gets a massive discount in mana (three cheaper) for probably a more reliable source of damage (potentially 3/2 attacking on turn two, no "look at me and I die" Illusion drawback). Again, we see [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) helping to set up [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) (on top of doing all the other stuff that is awesome that [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) can accomplish in terms of hand-sculpting and answer-finding).


Many of these [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) decks play the previously unplayed [Vapor Snag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vapor+Snag). This card is both a great answer to expensive creatures (if short term), with its 1 point of life loss combining conveniently with "being the beatdown" and a route to "building your own [Æther Adept](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Adept)" when combined with [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) (though it can be an entirely instant [Æther Adept](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Adept), as a bonus); you can also use [Vapor Snag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vapor+Snag) (if a little bit painfully) to set your [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) back up for an additional re-buy.

The Delver + Illusions deck is an almost pure aggro-control deck... Awesome offense backed up by [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak). Its goal is a tempo-based attack further facilitated by the speed of Phyrexian mana ([Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe) is not just the fastest cantrip of all time, but helps balance the relatively low land count of this deck). To that swift and tempo-tacular end, I think the coolest thing about Adam's deck is the presence of [Frost Breath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Breath)... not a card I would have originally pegged as being Constructed playable (but probably quite synergistic with [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) in the right matchups).

So what's going on with this deck?


Superficially it is a blue-black deck that relies more on creatures than instants; but more than that, there is method to the shift. [Heartless Summoning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartless+Summoning) significantly discounts Mukhar's creatures... kind of like a persistent [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual).



![Heartless Summoning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Heartless+Summoning)

 

[Sphinx of Uthuun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx+of+Uthuun) is a 4/5 flying [Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction) for just one more mana than the original with [Heartless Summoning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartless+Summoning) on the battlefield. I'd say that is downright reasonable... except that would be a complete understatement.

With two [Heartless Summoning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartless+Summoning)s on the battlefield, you get some strange synergies. For example, a completely free [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum) that dies immediately... ergo kind of like a zero-mana [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) + cantrip.


Week after week after week I find myself liking *Innistrad* more and more and more. This is a set that combines well with existing awesome cards ([Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) setting up not just [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) but now [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost); [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) recycling [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)), but it allows players to play all kinds of goofball different stuff... and it is all good! We have everything from the return of [Dismember](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember) to a discounted seven-drop finisher to the surprising playability of an M12 "Limited" card to hold a lead.


We've got weenies, several flavors of control (from battlefield to counterspells), and a shifting palette of threats (to [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) to not to [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder), or where to stick your [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets)), and it isn't clear what is going to be best (or what is going to show up even) from one week to the next. Heck, every couple of minutes it seems like the best thing you can do is attack someone with a [Plague Stinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plague+Stinger).


Now really does seem like a pretty great time to be playing Standard.
