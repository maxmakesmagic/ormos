Gods of the Game—Pro Tour Born of the Gods Modern Preview
=========================================================



 Posted in **Perilous Research**
 on February 13, 2014 










Welcome back to Perilous Research, DailyMTG.com's exclusive [Magic Online](https://accounts.onlinegaming.wizards.com/) column. Pro Tour *Born of the Gods* is just nine days away. We'll all be tuning in to [the live coverage here](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/tcg/events.aspx?x=mtg/event/protour/bornofthegods14#viewer), but we can get a lot more out of our viewing experience if we have a strong understanding of the format. [Last week](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/pr/285), we discussed the changes to the Modern banned list and their implications. Today, we'll be taking a look at the Modern format in its final iteration before the new Pro Tour format.


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/pr/2014/pr_wk06_286_cardart_cruelultimatum_c13.jpg)
[Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) | Art by [Todd Lockwood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/pages/search/default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22todd%20lockwood%22%5D)


Modern is an exciting format that affords players the opportunity to stick with a deck they've come to know and love for many years. Since its inception, Modern has allowed players to get creative while remaining successful. The massive volume of cards in the Modern format makes for a lot of interesting game play and the variance of the fastest or most disruptive decks is balanced by the incredibly difficult degree of play skill needed to play well. As we'll see, Modern is a place where innovation and creativity are rewarded. Let's take a look at some decks we can expect to see at Pro Tour *Born of the Gods* next weekend:

This may look like a pile of random cards, but it's actually one of the most innovative Modern decks we've seen in some time. [Amulet of Vigor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Amulet+of+Vigor) provides this deck with a very powerful effect. Using the artifact alongside Ravnica karoos ([Simic Growth Chamber](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Simic+Growth+Chamber), [Selesnya Sanctuary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Selesnya+Sanctuary), [Golgari Rot Farm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Rot+Farm)) allows the deck to operate a turn ahead of its opponent. We can tap our land for mana, play our karoo, then tap it for two mana. Things start getting crazy when we're able to play multiple lands in a turn or we have multiple [Amulet of Vigor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Amulet+of+Vigor) in play. We get two triggers when we have two Amulets, so our karoos begin making four mana the turn we play them. We can make absurd amounts of mana with an [Amulet of Vigor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Amulet+of+Vigor) when we're afforded the opportunity to play multiple lands in a single turn via [Summer Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Summer+Bloom) or [Azusa, Lost but Seeking](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azusa%2C+Lost+but+Seeking). We can even play a karoo, tap it for mana, then return it to our hand from its own bounce trigger and replay it for two more mana if we don't have more lands or we need the ramp to cast [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan).

The first [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) is scary, but the second one is usually attacking for 16 points as an 8/6 double strike, haste, trampler the turn it enters the battlefield. This deck is complex, fun, and super outside of the box. Let's hope this one gains some traction!

In the absence of [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl), Robots has been the aggro deck of choice in Modern. Robots aims to use a large number of inexpensive or free artifact creatures to overrun its opponent and control the pace of the game with interactive lands and [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager). The deck has weaknesses to a lot of popular sideboard cards like [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge), but those cards are becoming significantly harder to fit into a sideboard that needs to combat dedicated control, blisteringly fast combo, and Zoo decks. This deck suffers from the same splash damage that Merfolk does, but the deck's ability to dump its resources onto a single threat means that it has more resilience, especially when we're able to leave open mana to activate one of our lands. The deck gets a lot of free wins from [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating), but the interactions available here are incredibly complex. Playing Robots perfectly is close to impossible, and the few who have mastered the ins and outs of the strategy have been rewarded handsomely for their efforts with a number of high-level finishes. We can expect Robots and Zoo to be the biggest aggressive strategies in Valencia, but we can expect Robots to be in a very strong position if players' sideboards aren't prepared.

Red-White-Blue Control and Jund were the dominant decks of last year's [World Championship](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/wc13/welcome)'s Modern portion. Red-White-Blue Control aims to control the game with removal and countermagic until it's able to pull far enough into the lead with cantrips, [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), Planeswalkers, and [Sphinx's Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation). The deck functions similarly to many of the Esper decks in the current Standard format, but the sheer power level of the cards therein makes it a nightmare for the format's most aggressive decks. [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl)'s reintroduction to the format is sure to make this a popular choice in Valencia.

We all love a good [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) deck. This deck aims to win by copying its spells with [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension), thus drawing a lot of cards and producing a lot of mana, which can be traded in for more cards. Eventually, the deck creates a high enough storm count to fire off a [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot) or two for lethal damage. Unfortunately, the deck has been under the radar for a while because of [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman)'s popularity. It was frustrating when players lost Game One to graveyard hate, but the boogieman is gone and the window has been opened up for players to ascend all over again. We can expect this and other versions of [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) to be very strong at Pro Tour *Born of the Gods*.
