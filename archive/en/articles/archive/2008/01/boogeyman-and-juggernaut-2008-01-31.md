Let us begin with a few words on our Top 8 chart for the week. Sixteen decks... *Three* blue envelopes? Is that a misprint? Sadly... not really. Somehow we here at Swimming with Sharks only got Top 8 decks from two PTQs, and one of them was mysteriously down a list (15/16). The supplemental sixteenth little box, an out of place third blue envelope, belongs to onetime Grand Prix–Charlotte Top 8 competitor and Star City Games columnist Tom LaPille, who was playing a hybrid Next Level Blue deck initially designed by spirit guide and Worlds Finalist Patrick Chapin, developed in collaboration with Gerry Thomson.

This update to Next Level Blue shows significant development over the Chapin and Turtenwald lists we discussed last week. Tom was back to [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare)s rather than [Force Spike](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+Spike)s, and returned the [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) package from the original style of [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) (more reminiscent of Fortier's deck from the Pro Tour) instead of Chapin's [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish)es. In this deck, [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) could search up [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) for acceleration, [Tree of Tales](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tree+of+Tales) to hit a land drop (or set up a [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) when wanting green mana), or find bullets like [Engineered Explosives](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Explosives) against Gaea's Might Get There, [Pithing Needle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle) against [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed), [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) 'cause mise (that is, to complement [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance)), or [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt) against Dredge.

The defining and more unique element of this deck, though, was *tons and tons of Control Magics!* Tom ran both [Vedalken Shackles](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vedalken+Shackles)*and* [Threads of Disloyalty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Threads+of+Disloyalty) main, and sided in [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation) for something like *nine* ways to steal the opponent's creatures. [Global Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Global+Ruin) was yet another innovation (probably something that came out of nowhere for most of Tom's opponents), an interesting weapon to say the least given today's Extended mana bases. Lastly, a singleton [Steam Vents](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steam+Vents) (powered out by the eight-pack of [Flooded Strand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flooded+Strand) and [Polluted Delta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta), [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)-finders all) allowed for three copies of the onetime best card in Extended, [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge), to compliment Tom's essentially straight blue strategy out of the sideboard.

![Gaddock Teeg](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Gaddock+Teeg)

Stratton had to get past Dredge in the Kentucky finals; no doubt he was helped by the combination of main deck and sideboard [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg)s, [Yixlid Jailer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yixlid+Jailer), and [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic). [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) might look like a vanilla two-drop in a matchup where one player basically never plays a spell, but don't forget that he shuts off four-mana spells... even when they cost nothing to play. Therefore the Kithkin Advisor can give thumbs down to [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) played from the graveyard; no [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba)s get sacrificed, no tokens are produced by [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below), and no [Flame-Kin Zealot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame-Kin+Zealot) or [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%2C+Angel+of+Wrath) comes hasty from the nether. [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) is not a perfect solution to Dredge because that deck can answer a 2/2 with [Chain of Vapor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Vapor) or [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast), but he certainly buys a fair amount of time. Gaea's Might Get There is a deck that can leverage even one or two turns into an easy win thanks to five-point Gaea's Mights and [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames). [Yixlid Jailer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yixlid+Jailer) is pretty self-explanatory: With this Zombie Wizard in play, Dredge cannot, you know, *dredge*, so its engine goes offline and no mischief gets managed; ditto on the back end of [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return). Until that 2/1 is removed from play, it's all mediocre 1/x flyers and crossed fingers from the Dredge side. [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) doesn't automatically win the matchup or anything, but don't forget that any quick way to remove a creature on your own side of the board can catch [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below), even when there is something saucy on the stack. [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) and [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) are the biggest standouts in the Bridge-hosing category because they are always outstanding *and* double as self-contained Bridge destruction crews, but flexible thinking can be your friend here with a variety of decks and tools. If you are playing Doran, don't forget that a well-placed [Smother](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother)*on your own [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)* might make the difference between stretching a game out another turn—possibly leveraging it into a bona fide win—and just packing it in on the fourth turn.

So what *about* Dredge? I have to admit that I was completely wrong with my prediction going into this season. Josh Ravitz may be right on this one. The boogeyman might just get ten wins on the season (I predicted, and horribly wrongly, that Dredge wouldn't muster a one)! Jeremy Manthei's victory in Houston represents the third (recorded) Dredge victory in North America this season, which leads all archetypes.

Jeremy ran an extremely straightforward ten-land version of Dredge. All his lands tapped for all the colors, allowing him to play things like [Tireless Tribe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tireless+Tribe) on the first turn to help out [Putrid Imp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Imp)... More quick outlets for [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll), [Stinkweed Imp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stinkweed+Imp), and company. Note that this version could actually win on the first turn with the right draw...


[City of Brass](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=City+of+Brass), tap it for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif): [Careful Study](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Careful+Study) discarding two [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll)s.


 



![Street Wraith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=STREET+WRAITH)

Cycle [Street Wraith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Street+Wraith), electing to Dredge [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll), miraculously flipping over three [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba)s. Cycle the *other* [Street Wraith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Street+Wraith), following up on the other [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll). Of course the remaining nine cards flipped by the Grave-Trolls are all butter: Bridges and [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return), for the sake of the story a [Cephalid Sage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cephalid+Sage), and of course, additional cards with the word "dredge" on them.

Play [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) from the graveyard (one of nine down), sacrificing the [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba)s, picking up [Cephalid Sage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cephalid+Sage). Six 2/2 tokens come from down Below, and the Sage, upon reaching play, begins the dredge cycle all over again, producing both the [Flame-Kin Zealot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame-Kin+Zealot) and another [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return). Sacrifice the Sage along with two tokens and you have yourself eight 2/2 creatures, boosted to 3 power thanks to the Zealot... Attacking for 27 on the first turn might not be high percentage, but the fact that the deck *can* do it—a deck that *will* produce a fourth turn combo kill every game if left unfettered—seems awfully scary.


Not to diminish Jeremy's victory in any way, but his opposition in the Top 8 seemed a bit under-prepared for Dredge. There were a total of nine [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt)s, four [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate)s, four Leylines, and one [Yixlid Jailer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yixlid+Jailer) (18 cards total) between the other six (known) decks in the Top 8... Only three "hate" cards apiece disregarding [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage)s. Dredge is obviously the most powerful and fastest deck in the format, winning an estimated 80% of Game 1s... The theory against it has always been that while its Chains should allow it to beat one [Leyline of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+the+Void) or [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt) in a particular game, the same could not be said for, say, fourteen. It looks like Dredge is going to be a staple of the format, popular in Top 8s, and an actual blue envelope winner on a regular basis... If that is the case, keep in mind that three or four anti-Dredge cards will probably not give the average deck enough resistance. I don't think that you can go the "If I win in the first two rounds, I don't have to worry about Dredge" route that was advocated by some early on; Dredge is obviously capable of taking down tournaments. Respect it, devote real sideboard slots to it, or you will probably regret that you didn't, late in what seems like a winnable event.

This deck is a hybrid of... *exactly what it sounds like.* Reminiscent of Jeff Novakoff's deck from PT–LA two years ago, Beasts Rock is a slow, mid-range Rock deck (not unlike Barra Rock), but it plays a Red splash, minimizing the more common white splash to one [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch), one [Kataki, War's Wage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kataki%252C%2BWar%2527s%2BWage), and one [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg), all in the sideboard (and all [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) eligible). Rocha selected Beasts for most of his main deck business creatures, from the versatile (and potentially Dredge-hating) [Ravenous Baloth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Baloth) to the enormous [Spiritmonger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spiritmonger). With those Beasts comes a powerful synergy with [Contested Cliffs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contested+Cliffs); [Contested Cliffs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contested+Cliffs) provided Rocha a powerful method to control most creatures in the format without investing additional cards. I mean, who is going to beat up [Spiritmonger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spiritmonger)?


 



![Contested Cliffs](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Contested+Cliffs)

The most unusual thing about this deck might be the absence of [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)! Then next one in my mind is the selection of land in the sideboard. Jerret can find a land with [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish)... Rocha's sideboard features [Golgari Rot Farm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Rot+Farm), probably because if he needed to Wish for a land he would be anticipating missing multiple land drops (perfectly good reasoning). I think that the deck might get some benefit from sideboarding one of the four [Contested Cliffs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contested+Cliffs) in order to find that key card more frequently. While [Contested Cliffs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contested+Cliffs) in the sideboard neither produces both of the main colors in the deck (as [Golgari Rot Farm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Rot+Farm) does) nor implies multiple land drops, it is still another land that the deck can gain access to easily, and the potential upside is simply enormous.

Besides a solid amount of passive [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) resistance main ([Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder), [Ravenous Baloth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Baloth), and even the back side of [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy)), Jerret has four [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed)s in the starting 60 to mop up tokens. Dredge is fast, but it won't kill you before you can play a Deed *every* game! Additionally, Jerret can find either [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) or [Yixlid Jailer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yixlid+Jailer) main (depending on need); after sideboarding, he can supplement his strategy via four brutal copies of [Leyline of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+the+Void). All in all, especially for a methodical mid-range deck geared to dealing with other decks' monster threats, this one seems reasonably well prepared to fight Dredge. And really... What more can you expect of a deck that already has a solidly Rockin' proactive plan?
