GW Devotion at GP Miami
=======================



 Posted in **Daily Deck**
 on March 20, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/shuhei_authorpic.jpg)
By Shuhei Nakamura




Shuhei Nakamura is a member of the Pro Tour Hall of Fame, former Player of the Year, and is one of just six players with more than 400 lifetime pro points. 






Deck evolution can go several directions, and going slower is an option. I'm referring to decks that take a slightly slower route to fend off all the opponent's threats and come out a few cards ahead in the long run. But hey, if you feel confident enough, why not come out 20 cards ahead instead?



The mirror match of two Green Devotion decks in [the finals of Grand Prix Miami](http://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/gpmia15/finals-baumeister-cecchetti-2015-03-08) was painfully long. However, even against other decks, Green-White Devotion is exactly like this, going a little slower than the opponent.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mastery+of+the+Unseen)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mastery+of+the+Unseen)

Before I talk about why Green-White goes a little slower, I should explain about Green Devotion decks in general. On the one hand, Green Devotion succeeds in recruiting new cards like [Whisperwood Elemental](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whisperwood+Elemental) and [Ugin, the Spirit Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ugin%2C+the+Spirit+Dragon). On the other hand, this leads to costly win conditions—and don't forget the format is speeding up. In short, the deck was too slow for an aggressive field.



Green-White Devotion has found the balance in between. It maintains Green Devotion's strong points, such as mana creatures and huge monsters, while adding [Fleecemane Lion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fleecemane+Lion) to keep up with faster decks. The greatest addition from white is, of course, [Mastery of the Unseen](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mastery+of+the+Unseen). It's nothing in the early game, but it plays triple duty as (1) a replacement win condition to [Ugin, the Spirit Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ugin%2C+the+Spirit+Dragon); (2) a consumer of excess mana; (3) and a source of lifegain. Also, lowering the cost of the deck makes turning up manifest creatures easier.



This is a well-thought masterpiece of a deck. That's why it needs to consider the mirror match.
