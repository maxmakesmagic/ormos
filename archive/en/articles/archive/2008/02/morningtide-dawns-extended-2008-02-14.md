We've gone over Dredge decks basically every week so I won't bore you with the same details again. Numbers to note are Baumeister's two [Tolarian Winds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tolarian+Winds) (capable of a large graveyard dump on the second turn) and [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) (three). A card that bears mentioning out of the sideboard is [Crippling Fatigue](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crippling+Fatigue). This card is readily available via the Dredge mechanic itself (putting many cards in the graveyard and accidentally hitting [Crippling Fatigue](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crippling+Fatigue)). With that card down, Baumeister's Dredge deck can easily handle a [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) that might be pinning his [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return)s. The back side of [Crippling Fatigue](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crippling+Fatigue) is free, and out of the graveyard spells free card advantage.

![Reckless Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Reckless+Charge)

The unique elements out of this deck haven't been seen since "Frog in a Blender" maybe six years ago. [Reckless Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckless+Charge) replaces [Blistering Firecat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blistering+Firecat) for quite the surprise punch. [Blistering Firecat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blistering+Firecat) can be difficult to play around but at least you know when it's coming. [Reckless Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckless+Charge), though... The front side is so much more than three damage. No one that I know of is playing around this card so it should spell free [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) damage on the front side and some important top deck supplementation later in the game. [Reckless Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckless+Charge)'s buddy here is [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) proxy [Quirion Dryad](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quirion+Dryad), doing its best Miracle Grow impression. [Quirion Dryad](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quirion+Dryad) will acquire +1/+1 counters (see I'm on theme!) via anything but [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) and [Quirion Dryad](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quirion+Dryad) itself. Combined with [Reckless Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckless+Charge) and a fair amount of burn, this should make for an overall faster RDW clock.

The *Morningtide* update is... [Offalsnout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Offalsnout)! 2B for a 2/2 Flasher isn't actually a bad deal... It's just not something we are used to seeing, especially for a deck loaded up with [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage)s and [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)s. That Flash ability comes in handy with Evoke, though, doesn't it? Evoke allowed Ashford to "cycle" [Offalsnout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Offalsnout) directly into the graveyard to set up its triggered ability (probably targeting [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) or a [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) target); subtly the fact that *a creature is going to the graveyard* tap dances all over [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) in the process. [Offalsnout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Offalsnout) is just a great anti-Dredge card due to its speed and "splash damage" of simultaneously dealing with [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below). I expect to see more of this card as the season progresses.

... Don't forget that you might actually kill someone with your almost efficient 2/2 Flasher!

You accelerate to [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) with [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom), [Pentad Prism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pentad+Prism), and so on, then presumably win the game with your Epic copy per turn.

Typically the first enchantment to find is [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape), either that or [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) or [Form of the Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Form+of+the+Dragon) (if you are under pressure); *then* you get [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) to prevent the opponent from being able to deal with your enchantments. [Honden of Seeing Winds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honden+of+Seeing+Winds) keeps [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) going; [Boseiju, Who Shelters All](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Boseiju%252C%2BWho%2BShelters%2BAll) forces out [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) (but not the copies!) against Next Level Blue; [Form of the Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Form+of+the+Dragon) typically closes in five turns or fewer. [Boil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boil) out of the sideboard seems like a nice way to ruin a Blue mage's day, what with [Boseiju, Who Shelters All](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Boseiju%252C%2BWho%2BShelters%2BAll) already in the mix.


A card you might not have thought of in [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) is [Fire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Fire). This one is as flexible as ever... the main point of it here is killing [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) or some similar 2/2 Ideal hater (like [Ronom Unicorn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ronom+Unicorn)) to make going off that much easier... or even *possible*.

![Meekstone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Meekstone)

Mike Arenson's Spire Blue deck is slightly off-model, mixing Next Level Blue-esqe color splashes (but not the traditional [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)!) into the usually straight blue deck. The interesting thing to me is that [Meekstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meekstone) singleton for the [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage). [Meekstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meekstone) is a card that was once very popular but I think most people forgot was in the Extended environment at all. It seems like an interesting lockdown for common threats like [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) and a fine pair to [Vedalken Shackles](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vedalken+Shackles) that doesn't affect the Spire Blue side at all.

*Aside starring Jon Finkel...*


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/mtgcom/fcpics/swimming/mf185_Finkel.jpg)*Finkel facing off against Hernandez at US Nationals '00*The last time I saw a [Meekstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meekstone) in a competitive deck, it was actually in Jon Finkel's Top 8 match against Frank Hernandez at [the 2000 US National Championship](http://archive.wizards.com/sideboard/article.asp?x=USNAT00/welcome). The battle featured Napster (Jon) versus StOmPy (Hernandez), a matchup that greatly favored Finkel due to his "nine main deck [Perish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Perish)es" as Hernandez would later describe the four [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)s, four [Yawgmoth's Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will)s, and *one* [Perish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Perish) in Finkel's starting sixty. [Meekstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meekstone) is memorable... because it caused one of the rare big match mistakes of Finkel's career.


The play was simple. Jon drew a [Vicious Hunger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vicious+Hunger). There were two [Wild Dogs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Dogs) in play. One had [Rancor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rancor) on it. "Might as well make him spend the extra G," was probably what was running through Jon's head when be laid the [Vicious Hunger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vicious+Hunger) on the 4/1 [Wild Dogs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Dogs)... Of course he just ended up taking four [Reckless Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckless+Charge) style the next turn. The problem? Jon played "automatic" **Magic** rather than concentrating on what was actually going on in the game and realizing that *[Meekstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meekstone) was in play.* Frank had sided it in against Finkel's [Skittering Horror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skittering+Horror)s, [Phyrexian Negator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Negator)s, and so on. Because he had such a huge advantage over StOmPy, Jon probably just didn't realize that the 4/1 [Wild Dogs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Dogs) was going to stay tapped and that killing the other was a better short term play that saved the most life. The small mistake didn't end up costing Finkel the match or anything, and in fact few onlookers even noticed the mistake, but Jon did, and even pointed it out after the game.


So what *about* this Jon Finkel? I actually got an email from Jon this week! That isn't actually so exciting, except that this particular email was a response to [last week's article](/en/articles/archive/top-10-extended-decks-all-time-2008-02-07) on the top 10 Extended decks of all time. Now I've actually *seen* Jon's copy of *Deckade* next to his bed for night reading, but I didn't realize that the busy Hall of Famer kept up with *new* weekly Magic articles... until last week's email.


Past all of the personal insults to my taste in decks -- all warranted for certain -- Finkel insisted that I mention his Chicago 1997 deck as one of the best Extended decks of all time ("certainly way better than the Tax/Rack"). Jon has always considered this one his masterpiece; it bought him his first Pro Tour Top 8 after all. The thing that is really special about this deck is how well the mana was balanced. Jon played a total of four blue cards, and they all cost ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)... Years before [Flooded Strand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flooded+Strand) and [Polluted Delta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta), he was able to get the mana to work using his Tithe engine and only 17 lands!

This Prison deck is also the classic "switcheroo" transformative archetype. Jon started on an artifact-based combo lock deck ([Icy Manipulator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Icy+Manipulator) + [Icy Manipulator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Icy+Manipulator) + [Winter Orb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Winter+Orb) with [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) to speed things up was usually good for a win in 1997), but when his opponents sided out their [Swords to Plowshares](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares) for [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant)s, in came his four drop beatdown creatures, out on turn three thanks to [Sky Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sky+Diamond).


About two years ago we ran a promotional event for *Deckade* where Jon was a special guest, gunslinging with old decks I had designed. He was, predictably, supposed to be playing Napster but as a surprise for him I secretly slipped in his landmark Chicago deck because earlier in the week he had told me it was his all time favorite. Jon's [Winter Orb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Winter+Orb) "combo" lost only to then-modern combo deck [Heartbeat of Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartbeat+of+Spring), complete with the Finkel-foiling [Early Harvest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Early+Harvest), in the hands of future #1 Apprentice Asher "ManningBot" Hecht.

These Cloud decks are basically beefed up versions of The Rock with a legitimate finishing bomb ([Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) itself) and a host of Planeswalkers to help out with the dirty work. Garruk Wild speaker + [Golgari Rot Farm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Rot+Farm) can ramp up the deck's mana, and [Liliana Vess](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+Vess) can soften up an opponent to set up [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) or "[Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)" to find the Cloud itself. The main advantage this strategy has in the metagame is that it is basically unstoppable for Next Level Blue. The main disadvantage -- and it isn't a huge one given the [Putrefy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Putrefy) and [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed)s -- is that just as its gigantic mana costs make life difficult for [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance), Cloud doesn't play well against [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg).

Heartbeat seems, once again, to be an underrated Extended deck. It has the two-for-ones to out-last Rock / Doran style disruption, a reasonably fast combo kill, and is possibly the best combo deck for actually beating control in an attrition fight. More than any of that, [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) is just annoying for deck-of-the-hour Dredge, and [Moment's Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moment%27s+Peace) is basically two [Time Walk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Walk)s, completely immune to Dredge's disruption suite. The inclusion of [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) in current Dredge decks just makes it more consistent and resilient. Silver Bullet? [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) again. The GW Kithkin Advisor puts a real hurt on the four mana two-for-ones  

that give Heartbeat all of its oomph, and the scripted two drop destroyer [Engineered Explosives](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Explosives) is basically blank due to the X in its mana cost. That said, still an underrated and quite viable deck.
