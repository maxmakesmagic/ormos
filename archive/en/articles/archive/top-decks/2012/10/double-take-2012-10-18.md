Double Take
===========



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on October 18, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







We certainly live in exciting times!


 

Just this past weekend, David Williams took sole possession of the "player with most Team GP wins" moniker when he and his all-star squad of Matt Sperling plus Pro Tour Champion Paul Rietzl took apart the 1,700 player team event in San Jose, CA. Top Decks is, as a rule, tasked to competitive Constructed, but even we could appreciate the absolute *glee* expressed by so many who were at the massive event. Congrats to them all (but especially the singular achievement of Mr. Williams).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td217_precinct_captain.jpg)[Precinct Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Precinct+Captain) | Art by [Steve Prescott](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Steve+Prescott%22%5D)


Okay, back to our typical discussion of Top Decks: for this episode, we will spend the first half discussing exciting Standard decks featuring *Return to Ravnica* cards-slash-new set-enabled strategies. And then pop over to the Modern end to speculate on the same for the upcoming Modern Pro Tour this weekend.

This is a deck from last week's TCGPlayer 5K (though we saw some inheritors at some of the various 2012 State Championships). Azorius Aggro... Or "White Weenie" as we called it when I was a kid, is one of several fast, two-color, aggro decks that we are going to look at in this first block of decks.


Basically, the offensive white creatures are very good, and you can play a whole bunch of them together in a synergistic way to produce a pretty effective beatdown deck. The curve starts with [Champion of the Parish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish) or [War Falcon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=War+Falcon)... And the beats just keep coming. The majority of creatures in this deck are some kind of human, which is good for [Champion of the Parish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish); and there are plenty of Knights and Soldiers to keep [War Falcon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=War+Falcon)'s beak sharp for the red zone.

[Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) is one of the relatively few non-Human creatures in this deck... And is pretty much the same beat stick that you loved (or were terrified of) back in the long gone, nearly forgotten, [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) days of three weeks ago. As a hexproof creature, [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) is Azorius Aggro's most likely [Spectral Flight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Flight) target. When those two get together you have one of the best threats in the format... that no longer has the potential liability of being blocked. Or, a 4/4 flyer that makes another 4/4 flyer each attack; which together add up to precariously close to 50% of a player's starting life total.


[Lyev Skyknight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lyev+Skyknight) joins [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) at three mana, a fine "this is what detain can do" banner. It has evasion as well, but moreover it helps get Azorius Aggro's attackers through potential blockers.

Those attackers are quite specifically chosen, [Elite Inquisitor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elite+Inquisitor) is pretty good in terms of return on your mana investment, but particularly so in a world where Zombies is one of the top decks; ditto on [Knight of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Glory), now graduated to the main from the [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) sideboards of not-that-long-ago.


All-in-all, Azorius Aggro is potentially a very effective beatdown deck, that will likely prop up one wall of the emerging *Return to Ravnica* metagame.

The 2012 Massachusetts State Champion earned his title with Selesnya Aggro which is pretty much what it sounds like.


Starting on your choice of [Avacyn's Pilgrim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacyn%27s+Pilgrim) or [Arbor Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arbor+Elf), Selesnya Aggro grows up to make a second turn [Loxodon Smiter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Smiter), line up a [Silverblade Paladin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silverblade+Paladin), or eventually get to the lethal [Sublime Archangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sublime+Archangel).

From my own experience, I find Bant to be the most difficult deck in the current Standard to play and sideboard against. Like other U/W decks it has bombs like [Jace, Architect of Thought](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought); but unlike them you can't just go mono-[Izzet Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Izzet+Charm)s to defend yourself. Bant's five turn play might be a [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk)! The successful Bant player forces you to simultaneously respect its control elements and deal with its creature threats.

The basic strategy of the Junk Reanimator deck is this: Bring out an awesome threat!


You can start on a first turn [Arbor Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arbor+Elf), drop a second turn [Centaur Healer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Healer), annoy the heck out of your opponent with [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel), and then give your opponent a heart attack with [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) (all of this especially annoying if he is an aggressive deck).

Or you can play either [Mulch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulch) or [Grisly Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grisly+Salvage) to catalyze your graveyard. Ideally you want to flip over both an [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) (which costs only four out of the graveyard while you fix your mana) plus one of your juicy targets.

Your preferred target, [Angel of Serenity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Serenity), doesn't just humiliate your opponent's creatures (though it is great at that). Any other creatures you may have flipped over with that [Mulch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulch) or [Grisly Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grisly+Salvage)? You can stock up for later!


Obviously this is one of the most powerful strategies in the format, kind of doing better what many of the aggro decks try to do well, plus presenting a next level threat, often on turn three. It is the midrange deck that can play a bit of combo.


Some cards to note:


[Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman): You can open on this on turn one, then set it up for success with your second turn [Mulch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulch) / [Grisly Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grisly+Salvage). [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) can now make for your third turn [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites), luck willling. But in addition, it is one of the best options against an opposing [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) deck, and hell on a [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage).

All that in one card! Can one of the best creatures in *Return to Ravnica* make a great case not just in Zombies but all the way over to Reanimator? *I'm thinking probably, yes.*

One of the Modern format's favorite deck designers, Bronson Magnan, ran a red-splash version of Reanimator. His deck has much of the same strategy as the Pardon the Interrupt guys, but with [Faithless Looting](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faithless+Looting) and the defensive [Dreadbore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dreadbore).

It's a shame Magnan was not on the one drop creature plan, as [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) would make a heck of a two drop but I suppose [Avacyn's Pilgrim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacyn%27s+Pilgrim) is not the most powerful "BB1 on three" enabler.


Despite that criticism, everything that makes [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) potentially good makes it perhaps even better in this style of deck! Here you can actually use the color fixing to make a [Faithless Looting](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faithless+Looting) off the [Mulch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulch) or [Grisly Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grisly+Salvage) second turn. Ergo, if you opt for the four-color build, you might seriously consider that new one drop.

### Part 2: Some *Return to Ravnica* Cards to Watch


Next week we will do more of a wrap-up of Pro Tour *Return to Ravnica* (which returns us to the Modern format). Last time around, at the Players Championship, mid-range creature decks seemed to be the order of the day, with [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel)s blinking [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), and 187 two-for-one creatures (often played for free via [Æther Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial)) as some of the format headliners. What can *Return to Ravnica* add to this fascinating format?


Here are ten ideas:





![Deathrite Shaman](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Deathrite+Shaman)


 
We talked a couple of times about this card in the previous section. Imagine everything I just said... But factor in a first turn [Misty Rainforest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Misty+Rainforest).



I know.


*I know!*


Bonkers, right? The superior mana options in Modern make cards like [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) even more powerful. And when a deck based on flashing out [Eternal Witness](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness) was most recently one of the most successful builds? Or where there is a viable [Past in Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Past+in+Flames) across the table? I predict [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) finds a spot.





![Abrupt Decay](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Abrupt+Decay)


 
Standard doesn't have a lot going on to make the whole "this can't be countered" part of [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay) all that impressive. How about when you switch it into a format with [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) and [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) plus two drops like [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager), [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), and [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)?






![Ash Zealot](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ash+Zealot)


 
[Ash Zealot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ash+Zealot) hasn't made its mark in Standard yet but what about Modern? Critical mass of great burn: Meet critical mass of great creatures! As a follow up to [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide), Ash Zealout makes for a pretty scary one-two punch, in particular on the play. Anything that can soften up the opponent for the eventual burn flurry is welcome; and [Ash Zealot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ash+Zealot) is actually hyper efficient and features-rich for its cost, a red two drop almost worthy of the historic WW casting cost (it even has first strike!)






![Dreadbore](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dreadbore)


 
A year ago one of the most fearsome openings in Modern was the super fast [Karn Liberated](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn+Liberated). Today? A third turn [Karn Liberated](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Karn%2BLiberated) might not be as scary. You are still going to be behind, sure; but it's not going to be over, and chances are... The opponent might not have much left in hand. [Dreadbore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dreadbore) makes the printing of some powerful threats a bit more palatable, and gives beatdown decks options against big spell decks' most compelling openings.



Also... It is priced to please! This card can slot right into a Jund deck or it could play nicely with [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage). Your best days might be behind you [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate).





![Epic Experiment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Epic+Experiment)


 
I can't wait to see the red Ritual combo decks that try to play this as a neo-[Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire).






![Grisly Salvage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Grisly+Salvage)


 
(plus [Angel of Serenity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Serenity), and maybe [Lotleth Troll)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotleth+Troll%29)



Just like [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) is a mite better given more options in Modern, these Junk colored cards might also have some additional play. [Angel of Serenity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Serenity), if nothing else, can drop a 187 on some of its impressive competitors. [Lotleth Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotleth+Troll) is actually priced to make noise in Modern.





![Underworld Connections](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Underworld+Connections)


 
If the format has a large number of mid-range value decks as expected, what is better than a recurring card advantage tool? [Underworld Connections](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Connections) lets you choose if you want to draw (or not). So, for the cost of an additional land, it has the up-side of a [Phyrexian Arena](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Arena), but you can actually "turn it off" if you need to stay alive.






![Jace, Architect of Thought](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought)


 
I have learned not to underestimate a four mana Jace.






![Slaughter Games](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Slaughter+Games)


 
Cards like [Slaughter Games](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) have always held high end strategies in check. When [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction) was first printed, it forced [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) decks to diversify their top ends, and tangentially helped pave the way for an Affinity win. [Memoricide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memoricide) stared at itself in a mirror match for a World Championships. And this version? Like [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay), [Slaughter Games](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) actually gets to exploit its invulnerability to permission if played in Modern.



Some Pros tend not to like cards that don't affect the battlefield immediately, but I feel like [Slaughter Games](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) could be potential spoiler.
