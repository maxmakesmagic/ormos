Gone are the days of Red Deck Wins dominating the Swiss, with Goblins once again leading all Top 8s in terms of numbers if not qualifications. So even if it isn't the burn/utility powerhouse that took second place at the Pro Tour, a Red Deck is still the most popular opponent. What do you think Dan Paskins, author of "[Why Red is Simply the Best Color](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/feature/221)" would make of this Top 8?

That's right. SEVEN Red Decks: Four Goblins, a Fujita Sneak Attack (even if it looks and smells like some sort of dirtbag B/U deck), and two Red Deck Wins (including TOGIT standout and [Goblin Cadets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Cadets) advocate Pat Sullivan). SURELY a Red Deck would win that PTQ, right?


Nope.


Fresh off his first Pro Tour one week earlier, New York State Champion Michael Clair accurately predicted a field of all Goblins opponents. This version of Psychatog was co-designed with Scott McCord and Tim McKenna. Rumor has it that in testing, Clair started with three main-deck [Engineered Plague](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague)s, but that, time and again, McKenna handed him Dr. Teeth's head on a platter with his Warchiefs, Ringleaders, and Piledrivers deck: Enter that fourth [Engineered Plague](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague).


With [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and [Accumulated Knowledge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Accumulated+Knowledge), it is no problem for this deck to find ("mise") the *first* [Engineered Plague](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague) by turn three, fair and square. At that point, Clair would use [Intuition](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intuition) to get one of the remaining three [Engineered Plague](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague)s so that he could "combo his opponent out" with a double Plague draw. Although most Goblins decks can overcome a single [Engineered Plague](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague), almost none can beat two Plagues in play (their biggest creatures tend to be toughness 2), and even the decks siding in [Naturalize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize) present a card that, unlike their [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial)-driven Gobin threats themselves, gives the Psychatog player something to counter.


### Trends in 'Tog


Japanese master Itaru Ishida's [Ink-Eye Special](/en/events/coverage/japanese-powerhouse-invades-singapore) is reminiscent of Taylor Putnam's Gush-a-Tog, which the latter used for a second place finish at the recent Grand Prix Seattle.

The deck lists conform pretty closely... until you see that the Ink-Eye Special cuts [Sapphire Medallion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sapphire+Medallion), one of the key components of Putnam's deck, for better actual early game defense, while focusing even more effort on the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) aspect. [Force Spike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+Spike)s and an early game [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) compromise allows Ishida to cheat even further on land (down to 22!), where earlier (especially Standard) versions of Psychatog played 24 or more lands.


The [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-mana trick is an old one that has been used since *Masques* Block. [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) costs no mana, and in a queer, having-less-land-in-play-than-at-the-beginning-of-the-turn way, sort of makes free land drops. What you do, say on turn three or later, after you've missed a drop, is to float ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif) and [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush). You then play (or re-play) a land and make a play. Back in the [Rising Waters](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rising+Waters) days, the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) trick actually let you untap more lands, but these days, the Ink-Eye Special can follow up with a black for [Engineered Plague](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague) or Dr. Teeth himself, creating action without compromising drops (again, sort of).


An even more unusual take on Dr. Teeth is Jerret Rocha's winner from Chicago.

Reminiscent of Jeff Cunningham's deck from last year (or Team TOGIT's at PT Columbus), Rocha's deck is a three color Psychatog touching red for [Fire/Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire%2FIce) and [Isochron Scepter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter). [Fire/Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire%2FIce) on [Isochron Scepter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) gives Rocha a long game card drawing engine that is also powerful creature defense that works both on the tiny threats of Red Deck Wins or Goblins (Fire) with equal vigor as the giant Legends of Reanimator (Ice).


[Isochron Scepter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) + [Fire/Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire%2FIce) does a lot of things well. It is disruption that draws cards. It can tap blockers for a big [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) swing or draw cards to set up land drops or make [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) lethal. In some games, it helps to escape an opposing [Cranial Extraction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction), giving this control deck a way to win when Dr. Teeth is missing.

Well?

I honestly don't know how this deck works, or what it does. In fact, it seems pretty low powered to me. Sure, it can get a one drop advantage with [Grim Lavamancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer), [Mogg Fanatic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic), or [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial) and then play the [Standstill](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill)... but is that enough in today's competitive Extended? I speculate that the Swords let its small creatures dance past the surging Psychatog and Goblin opponents... but the counters don't seem very consistent to me against a an opposing deck with an actual long game. [Grim Lavamancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer) may not be "[Shadowmage Infiltrator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Shadowmage%2BInfiltrator)" or "[Meddling Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Meddling%2BMage)" but it fulfills the [Voidmage Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Voidmage+Prodigy)'s lifelong dream -- before now left unfulfilled -- to sacrifice other wizards to the whim of Kai Budde.


As I said before, I don't know how this deck works... which is probably one of its great strengths. Can you imagine sitting down across from Marshall Arthurs? On his first turn play, you'd put him on Red Deck Wins or Goblins and get your game plan ready, when all of a sudden, he's all "Island, [Cloud of Faeries](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloud+of+Faeries), [Standstill](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill), go!" and you're all "Huh? Wait a minute!?!" The power of rogue decks is often in their ability to make their opponents play badly due to their unpredictability, their inability to be categorized in existing boxes, and this deck seems to have that cache. While it might not be geared against legitimate long games, the current Extended is largely defined by short term gains in beatdown or combo decks, and the light counters of this U/R deck, reminiscent of Vintage's so-called "Fish," are effective there.


What I do know is that this deck ran the rounds in a 176 player PES PTQ... *and faced itself in the finals*! Arthurs went Hatchling-to-Hatchling with Mark Kelso in the U/R Standstill mirror for the slot! Losing little, will a deck like this be the face of the post-rotations new Extended?
