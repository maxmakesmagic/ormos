Innovation in Preparation
=========================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on August 14, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







Despite coming late in the season, [Grand Prix–Denver](/en/events/coverage/gerry-t-rocks-denver) this past weekend has revealed some very exciting new and different strategies, including at least one genuinely amazing cluster of innovations that can be utilized in the last weeks of this summer's Block Constructed PTQ season. Yes, yes; Faeries and Kithkin, too. But before we get into specifics, the Top 8:

These decks are quite similar—and share in a key finisher innovation designed to take on the onrushing metagame—but Thompson's deck has a couple of really something specials that set Justice Toast apart from previous five-color control decks in *Lorwyn* / *Shadowmoor* Block Constructed.


 



![Runed Halo](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Runed+Halo)

When I heard those two words waft into my ears I had to pick my jaw back up off the ground. The implications of this card—especially as a Constructed main deck spoiler—are as unbelievable as they are topical. You see, Grand Prix Denver occurred a week after the [U.S. National Championship](/en/events/coverage/jacob-re-ignites-great-chicago-fire), where Michael Jacob stood atop a heap of burned bodies, surrounded by unkillable Demigods. [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge)—[Rorix Bladewing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rorix+Bladewing)'s faster, somehow more unstoppable, little brother—is a serious problem for control decks. Many players talk about being able to beat one Demigod, but not the dreaded "Demigod draw" where multiple copies show up. The reason is that a control deck can counter *one* Demigod, but the second one—even if it is countered—brings the first one back, and it is all 5 power of haste smashing the control player in the face. How inconvenient.


In Standard, Mark Herberholz respected this threat so much that he even added [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) to his Quick 'n Toast deck specifically to help deal with [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) ([Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) puts the Demigod on the bottom of the opponent's library so it will not return to play when a second Demigod is played). However, in Block, while many of the same exciting cards that made Michael Jacob's deck so good—and so good all over the world the same weekend—there is no [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) to fight of [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) out of the sideboard.


But there *is* [Runed Halo](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Runed+Halo).


 



![Demigod of Revenge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Demigod+of+Revenge)

This card is so elegant. One copy is a perfect solution to all the Demigods the opponent might present, a kind of damage-preventing [Pithing Needle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle). Moreover, we saw in the Denver coverage that when the opponent has a [Tattermunge Maniac](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tattermunge+Maniac) draw, and the five-color deck is a little slow to start (as it often will be), [Runed Halo](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Runed+Halo) is a perfect second-turn spell to slow down the opponent's forward momentum and keep the 'Toast deck's life total nice and fluffy. This can give the control enough time to draw into the card advantage necessary to take over during the middle turns... instead of getting battered for 6 or even 10 damage, which would make opposing burn that much more immediately dangerous.

More than just red deck defense, there are some specific threats in Block Constructed that *really* don't like to mess with [Runed Halo](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Runed+Halo). To wit:


[Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique)... Not only are you safe from the typical Faeries deck's biggest beater, when the opponent successfully Champions [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique) into play... *it will have to tap its controller out!*


[Oona, Queen of the Fae](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oona%2C+Queen+of+the+Fae)... Not only will Oona be unable to damage you in combat, because [Runed Halo](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Runed+Halo) actually gives you Protection from [Oona, Queen of the Fae](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oona%2C+Queen+of+the+Fae), you can't be targeted by this card, so she can't point her [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) ability in your direction, meaning no danger from decking... and moreover no tokens, meaning no *attacks* from Oona tokens, either. Annoying!


The other main point of differentiation between the Justice Toast version and typical Quick 'n Toast five-color control is namesake [Archon of Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archon+of+Justice). [Archon of Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archon+of+Justice) is a versatile "tap out" control threat that also happens to be particularly effective against [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge). You can tap for [Archon of Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archon+of+Justice) on or about the same time the opponent can play [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge)... and the matchup between the cards is pretty favorable for the Archon, all things considered; an Archon can tangle with a Demigod, trade, and slow down the next one by removing a land from play (or remove a more relevant permanent from play, of course). Or big Justice can tangle with a pair of dreaded Demigods, trading with one in combat and removing the other permanently (with the loss of 5 life)... a little painful, but probably worth it. All-in-all, [Archon of Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archon+of+Justice) is a powerful tempo card that can give the offensive opponent pause, and cause him or her to reconsider the plan of action, even as it plays a little [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel).


The rest of the Justice Toast deck is built on a versatile control model... It boasts a hefty land count, multiple routes to card advantage (essentially all self-contained), a touch of speed thanks to evoke, sweepers in [Austere Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Austere+Command) and [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout), and a fair permission suite including the best of breed, [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command). Quick 'n Toast is a deck that can literally do everything. Unlike most modern control decks, it actually has recursive elements thanks to [Makeshift Mannequin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makeshift+Mannequin) (Antonino played all four), and even main-deck life gain thanks to [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) (De Rosa's deck bolsters the Finks—both ways—with [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command) out of the board). All in all, a superbly innovative and rich winning deck.

We have seen many Doran decks over the past several weeks, from Shaman-based decks with [Wolf-Skull Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wolf-Skull+Shaman) and [Leaf-Crowned Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leaf-Crowned+Elder) to robust mana bases that can support weenie-slaying [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout)s. This deck takes a very different—and compelling—route to board control. We often talk about **Magic** as being a game of great two-drops... Check out Burton's two-drop.


No, no, the other one:


 



![Scarblade Elite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Scarblade+Elite)

[Scarblade Elite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scarblade+Elite) is a nice little source of self-contained card advantage. If you're scratching your head saying "But there are no Assassins in this deck..." don't forget about changeling. In the graveyard, [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), [Crib Swap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crib+Swap), and [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) are all eligible out of the main deck. So Hunter could shoot down a [Boggart Ram-Gang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Ram-Gang) with a [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion), then tap his [Scarblade Elite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scarblade+Elite) to take out its friend [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger).


The rest of the deck is pretty self-explanatory; [Treefolk Harbinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treefolk+Harbinger) either gets [Murmuring Bosk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murmuring+Bosk) to set up colors or [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower) for beats (or [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), to plow through black).


Again we see the [Soul Snuffers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Snuffers) in the sideboard. Doran is very good at holding the ground—just check out the quality of creatures in Burton's deck—but even with [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher), it can potentially fall to a well-placed [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession); [Soul Snuffers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Snuffers) is a perfect solution to that part of the game, simultaneously knocking around the small Kithkin creatures. [Soul Snuffers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Snuffers) of course does double duty against Flame-Kin Harbinger, [Smokebraider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smokebraider), and much of the red-style Shaman suite in both the [Rage Forger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rage+Forger) deck and much of Elementals, simultaneously nixing opposing aspirations to persist.

This deck intersects Merfolk and changelings to great effect.


For example, Castor could play second turn [Stonybrook Banneret](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stonybrook+Banneret)... then untap to deploy [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus). This can be very surprising for an opponent anticipating the conventional Merfolk front line of generally tiny and inoffensive attackers. A lot of what we've said about the 4-toughness red (or really red-black) threats holds true for [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), too, except you *really* can't kill it with black removal.
