Bond ... Sanguine Bond
======================



 Posted in **Building on a Budget**
 on September 2, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 






Hello, and welcome to another edition of Building on a Budget. I received an avalanche of positive email regarding [last week's column](/en/articles/archive/building-budget/box-battle-2009-08-26), and I hope to do another column on the same subject matter each time a new set is released. This week I'm going to explore a couple different decks that abuse an overlooked rare from ***Magic** 2010*.

[Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond) is one of those cards that just demands to be played with. There are a bunch of different directions we could take with this deck and I'm looking forward to exploring each of those lists.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/boab/boab54_sanguineBondSplash.jpg)  
[Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond) suffers one of the biggest drawbacks a **Magic** card can possess: it does not actually do anything by itself. If we're going to build a deck around the new rare, we need to make sure the rest of our deck makes the card worthwhile.

![Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sanguine+Bond)  
The most common list that people have been sending me emails about is the all-black version of the deck. These decks rely very heavily on cards like [Tendrils of Corruption](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Corruption) and [Corrupt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corrupt). The mono-black version of the deck is extremely reliant on the [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond), often having no other win conditions. I don't really like decks like this. A lot of the time you fail to draw [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond) or your opponent gets a quick start. There are too many situations where the deck just folds. A common list for the mono-black version of the deck looks like this:

People seemed to take one of these two routes when they built their [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond) decks. I myself thought one of the biggest puzzle pieces was missing from both these lists, though. A lot of you are aware of my undying love for [Ranger of Eos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos). The white [Mulldrifter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter) seems especially well placed in a deck like this. [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden) is probably one of the best targets for our Ranger, and I'm fairly certain that a lot of casual decks would be shut down by this combination. I opened [Gatherer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Default.aspx) and went on a quest for cards that could fit into my new creation. I'll begin our adventure with an explanation of each card that I found.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/boab/boab54_theCardsHeader.jpg)  
**[Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond):** This is the card I'm attempting to build a deck around. I want to make sure all the cards in my deck have a good amount of synergy with my big enchantment.

**[Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden):** [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden) is quite good on its own these days. It seems like a very nice answer to [Bitterblossom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), and (in combination with [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond)) it punishes our opponents when they play problem cards for us like [Cloudgoat Ranger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger) or [Spectral Procession](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession).

**[Ranger of Eos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos):** Fetching up a pair of [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden)s seems like one of the most powerful things we can do with a deck like this. I wish there were more reasonable targets for the Ranger so I could play a fourth copy. Unfortunately, [Nip Gwyllion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nip+Gwyllion) just isn't exciting enough for me.

**[Kitchen Finks](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks):** This is just an incredible card. In addition to its stand-alone awesome factor, the Finks actually fits this deck very well. [Kitchen Finks](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) can cause an 8-point life swing before it ever enters combat if the game involves a [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond) hitting the table.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/boab/boab54_theCardsCross1.jpg)  
**[Tidehollow Sculler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidehollow+Sculler):** I wanted a reasonable two-mana creature, and I was stumped for a bit. [Knight of Meadowgrain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Meadowgrain) seemed like the obvious choice, but a budget mana base couldn't reliably cast the Knight on turn two. I ended up going with [Tidehollow Sculler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidehollow+Sculler). The card is extremely efficient and seems like an excellent dose of disruption in a deck that's trying to do as many zany things as this one.

**[Necromancer's Covenant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necromancer%27s+Covenant):** This card has gotten a lot of praise from the casual community since it was printed. It has a huge effect on the game. I usually find myself making at least five lifelink 2/2s every time I cast the spell. I decided this would be our late-game finisher.

**[Doom Blade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade):** I want our deck to have a good amount of efficient removal. It's important that my opponent's graveyard is fat when I cast [Necromancer's Covenant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necromancer%27s+Covenant). I also want outs to gigantic bombs like [Baneslayer Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/boab/boab54_theCardsCross2.jpg)  
**[Wall of Reverence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence):** Another great card that is currently underplayed. I remember testing this card before Pro Tour–Kyoto and essentially deciding that red decks could not exist because of its presence. Since then, however, we have had cards like [Anathemancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anathemancer) and [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) bring the color back. [Wall of Reverence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence) is still a card, and it seems like it fits perfectly into this deck.

**[Sign in Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sign+in+Blood):** This has got to be one of the cards to watch after the rotation. There doesn't really seem to be anything close to this in the card drawing department. Two mana for two cards is an incredibly solid deal. The fact that [Sign in Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sign+in+Blood) can actually be used to finish off your opponent when they're on a low enough life total makes this card especially awesome.

**[Tendrils of Corruption](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Corruption):** I understand that the deck doesn't maximize the amount of life it can gain with Tendrils, but that doesn't really matter. I've cast this card for 3 damage many times and been very happy. Its mana cost was clunky given our curve, but I wanted another spell that did something in this slot and thought Tendrils could be a good one- of.

**[Diabolic Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Tutor):** This deck seems very dependant on certain cards for very specific situations. The deck doesn't have a silver bullet package, but I thought it might be wise to increase the consistency of the overall plan a bit by adding a [Diabolic Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Tutor).

On my turn I draw another [Swamp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swamp), play it, cast another [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden), and pass. My opponent attacks with his team, and I Tendrils the [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf). Unfortunately for me, he has a pair of [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s, and the game is over.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/boab/2_1.jpg)  
I'm glad I finally gave the [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond) deck a look. It seems like it could be a fun choice for my casual group in the future. Unfortunately, I don't think this archetype can be tournament worthy with the cards in todays standard.

Zendikar is fast approaching and I may be pleasantly surprised with a number of cards that fit this archetype. Until then, however, I think this deck should probably stick with the casual room.
