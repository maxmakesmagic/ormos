Five Things About Modern
========================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on January 26, 2012 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











While we here at **DailyMTG.com** took the last couple of weeks "off" to start bringing out peeks and teasers toward the awesomeness that is—that will be—*Dark Ascension*, the competitive **Magic** world beyond the walls of Renton, WA, got Pro Tour *Avacyn Restored* qualifiers into full swing.

This season is Modern, the still-relatively-new, bigger-than-Extended home of old favorites and new combinations both. Modern resembles Standard in some ways (one of the top decks, for instance, is essentially a Standard transplant), Legacy in others (you can do anything you want, super cool, within the bounds of your mana base), but is ultimately an animal unto itself. Its size—the sheer number of cards available—gives Modern speed. The coexistence of *Zendikar* (and its [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa)s) and *Ravnica* block (and its [Breeding Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool)s) plow for Modern mana bases a wide road, ranging from single-color control decks to five-color decks touching hither and thither for all manner of flashbacks and splashes.

Here are five things I have learned playing Modern on **Magic Online**, talking to aficionados of the format (or at least PTQ participants), and perusing dozens and dozens of deck lists:

1. Modern's Large Card Pool Allows You to Play a Critical Mass of Cheap, Synergistic Threats

Spanning sets from *Scars of Mirrodin* back to regular-old *Mirrodin*, the Modern Affinity deck is a testament to speed, setting a benchmark for large-scale competitive formats. I mean, check out the average mana cost of this deck! With only fifteen lands—forty-five spells, therefore, in its main-deck sixty—Affinity tops out at a mere 56 total mana, with an average mana cost of 1.24! Essentially, if Affinity draws two lands, that is going to be enough to cast almost everything.

This is a deck that can set a pace offensively. First turn, you can lay out a couple of [Memnite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite)s and a [Signal Pest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest), maybe fortify with a [Steel Overseer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+Overseer) on the second turn, and then end it all in short order with [Galvanic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast) and [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast)s. The deck actually commands a fair amount of big burn (at decidedly not-big mana costs) that pack a *ton* of damage (4 and 5, respectively).

This PTQ-winning version of the Affinity deck spreads out of red for [Steelshaper's Gift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steelshaper%27s+Gift), which is obviously there to set up the still-mighty [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating). You know what's better than [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating)? [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) on an [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus). Nothing like getting all your guys killed, shrugging, and killing the opponent from 0 poison in one turn. I mean, *mise.*

No, I don't think Ramp;D was thinking about that, either, when they made [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) all those years ago.

While the strategy will never yield one of the "oops I won *on the second turn*" draws, [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) is awesome in its multi-faceted, redundant, infinite-ness.


> * When I say infinite, I don't mean that as an exaggeration, like when you have six cards in hand and your opponent asks you how many and you say, "I dunno, infinite I guess." I mean literally an unending amount of damage if you want. You get a [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) on a [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) and you can keep tapping and untapping until you are tired of it.
> * And the redundancy? You have not only [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) and [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) but [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) and [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) (or [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) and [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), or Kiki-Jiki and the 1/4, whatever you want). That makes it much more resilient when paired against stuff like [Memoricide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memoricide), [Surgical Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Surgical+Extraction), or even simple removal spells and disruption.
> * All that said, you still must attack. That is a little less sexy than some other combos that can't be stopped by, say, a [Ghostly Prison](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghostly+Prison).

This is a deck with many dimensions and many ways to crush an opponent.

First and foremost, it is just a [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) deck. Some decks really don't like the opening of [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) into [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks). "Block that... and that," is a perfectly good way to gain value and trade up.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Birds+of+Paradise)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)

You can scale up [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise), [Wall of Roots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Roots), [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), and into [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) (and further to the [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark)-into-Sun-Titan zone, if need be). You can also just go and get a pair of [Viscera Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer)s (and trade one up to [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast)) or... whatever. There are loads of ways to win when you are essentially drawing a card (or more) extra while netting mana.

Sacrifice [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) to [Viscera Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer); when [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) comes into play, get 2 life.

Sacrifice again.

Everything you can do with the [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), you can do that ten times with your [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap), in order to brain the opponent for two, For Two, FOR TWO, ten times.

It can suffocate you with [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), hassle your hand with [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique), re-buy the [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) with [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), and lock the game down with [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command). Conversely, it has not just the super-efficient [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), but the believe-it-or-not-sometimes-better [Burst Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning). There will be games where you can burn out your opponent from 7 or even 10.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cryptic+Command)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)  
But what I like about a deck like this?

Grand Prix Champion Wirecat pretty much broke all the rules with this one, right?

This is about the mid-rangey-est thing you can imagine: ponderous [Burning Vengeance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Vengeance) engine, breaking the intent of [Burning Vengeance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Vengeance) by clearing the opponent's hand with a [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) plus [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime) engine, braining him over and over... value on top of value, repetition after repetition.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

At the same time, this is a deck that has another cross-block synergy: [Haakon, Stromgald Scourge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Haakon%2C+Stromgald+Scourge) plus [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion). When you have a [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) down ([Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) is a Knight, after all!) you can go machine gun on your opponent's creatures, free removal spell after free removal spell.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

This deck can crush with planeswalkers... [Lily](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) and [Garruk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) dominate the opponent without having to spend mana (no probs, you have all kinds of stuff to do with your mana—don't worry). [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) forcing you to discard, yourself, is kind of a joke, as you have [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) and tons of ways to recoup. For that matter, playing a [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion)—getting a [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) into the graveyard—is a nice bonus for your [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). Tribal!

Oh yeah, this is *also* a fine [Smallpox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smallpox) and [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) deck. If you go first, play a land (maybe make the opponent discard) and follow your opponent's land-plus-guy with a land-plus-[Smallpox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smallpox) on your second turn. You both lose a land (your opponent now has none), your opponent loses a guy (you don't), and everybody discards... But! You are the one with [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam). Some turns later you have [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) plus [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) and all kinds of simple math-defying destruction.

Just a simple, hasty, straightforward Boros beatdown. Great use of landfall lets [Steppe Lynx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx) and [Plated Geopede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede) hit for tons of damage, backed up by versatile threats like [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny) and La Parka Diamond Cutters like [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide) and [Chandra's Phoenix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra%27s+Phoenix).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td179_threespc.jpg)  
The coolest thing of all?

[Boros Garrison](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Garrison).

Yes, my beloved readers, that is [Boros Garrison](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Garrison) in this PTQ-winning deck.

*Not only* is any kind of Karoo super cool (particularly this echo to the Budget Boros of *Ravnica* Block Standard), but in a *landfall* deck?

You get twice the mana, long-term, *and* you get to buy back your landfall.

All that, of course, results in a remarkably efficient beatdown. But again, not the oppressive or "dirty combo feeling" kind (for those of you who care about that kind of thing).

I'm thinking this is the start of something good.

And with *Dark Ascension* coming so soon?

Something *even better*.
