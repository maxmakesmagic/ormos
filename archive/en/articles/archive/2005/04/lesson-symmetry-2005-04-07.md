A Lesson in Symmetry
====================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on April 7, 2005 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic."

One of the things that Scott Johns asked me to do with this column, especially when we are off-format (i.e. not poring over Extended Top 8 deck lists every week) is to delve into the more theoretical aspects of **Magic**. Most of you are probably familiar with at least basic **Magic** theory. For example, you probably know that [Hymn to Tourach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach) (or descendents [Stupor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor), [Unburden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburden), and [Waking Nightmare](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Waking+Nightmare)) generate *card advantage*. That is, each of these cards spends just one sorcery in exchange for removing two cards out of the opponent's hand.



> ***Card advantage** is any process by which a player obtains effectively more cards than his opponent, for instance by [Ancestral Recall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Recall)ing, netting three cards for one, or by playing [Ensnaring Bridge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ensnaring+Bridge) so that single card nullifies several creatures.*  
> 
> -Eric Taylor


Though different theorists will disagree as to how we may exactly *count* card advantage, the concept itself is easy to understand. If I spend just one card to get more than one out of you, I'll be ahead, and over time, you shouldn't have much left to fight back. Similarly, if I draw lots of extra cards (for instance, one [Concentrate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Concentrate) from my hand trades for three new cards), I'll have a greater volume of cards with which to brawl even one-for-one; once again, he who has more cards will win, while Mr. Have Not will not have the "W" next to his name come game's end.


What is more difficult to understand is that group of cards with especially sweeping effects that affects *both* players, often in an identical fashion.


When I started playing **Magic** more than 10 years ago, I found these two cards to be very puzzling. Why would I want to play a card like [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God)? Wouldn't that card kill all of *my* creatures too?


My friend Jeff Wu quickly explained to me that maybe I could play a "creature-less" deck (a novelty I had not yet explored) and not have to worry about losing creatures (how to win the game was another question entirely).


[Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) was more problematic. I mean every deck, *every single deck*, in the history of **Magic** relies on land. Blowing up all the lands -- including all of your own lands, don't forget -- is not something to be taken lightly. Slowly I started to understand that maybe if I had something in play when the [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) resolved (hopefully something bigger than what my opponent had, if he had anything at all) the [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) would help to seal the game. Though it wasn't fleshed out very well, both of these notions -- not having any creatures in play when I cast a [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) and having a hopefully sizable creature in play when I resolved [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) -- served as building blocks for my understanding of *symmetry*.


Though [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) hasn't been present in the Core Set for some years, [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) remains one of the most important symmetrical cards. Calling it symmetrical is a bit of a misnomer, though, because most competitive decks that play [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) are, if not creature-less, creature *light* and don't suffer much ill because their own threats are destroyed. [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) is a pretty simple example because you can either hold back your creatures while the opponent plays out a couple – giving you the opportunity to generate card advantage with a two-for-one or so – or just bias your list in such a way that [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) doesn't affect you at all, even if you are planning to use it as a one-for-one.

![Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Death+Cloud)

[Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) is one of the trickiest cards to count in terms of symmetry and card advantage. The reason is that it actually rotates around four different elements:

* Life: Each player loses x life.
* Cards in Hand: Each player discards x cards.
* Creatures: Each player sacrifices x creatures.
* Lands: Each player sacrifices x lands.

Intuitively, it seems like you just can't win with [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud). You should always be sacrificing the same number elements as your opponent… *plus* you have to spend the [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) itself and a boatload of mana. With [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon), you can have fewer lands in play than your opponent, or just have a big [Erhnam Djinn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Erhnam+Djinn) in play so that it doesn't matter if you lost an extra land. Not so with [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud)… It's pretty difficult to end up with a legitimate threat in play after playing this card, even if you do manage to nuke all the mana.


The trick in breaking the symmetry of [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) is to bias your numbers in such a way that you wreck the opponent as badly as possible… but avoid as many of the adverse effects of [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) as possible yourself. The easiest way seems to be to play out as much mana as possible from your hand while having few (if any) creatures in play.


Look at Joshua Claytor's version. Claytor doesn't play very many creatures. Of his 12, [Sakura-Tribe Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) ramps up his mana count when it goes to the grave and [Kokusho, the Evening Star](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kokusho%2C+the+Evening+Star) has a very cooperative “[Fireball](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball)” synergy with an especially large [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) that forces its sacrifice. From the creature standpoint, Claytor may be nuking two or more of the opponent's creatures while losing essentially no creatures himself. By having fewer creatures in play than his opponent, Joshua breaks the symmetry of this element of [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud): the card affects his opponent's creatures much more significantly than his own.


 



![Kodama's Reach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Kodama%27s+Reach)

In terms of cards in hand, Claytor's deck is very good at turning its hand into board position. It doesn't have any true card drawing, and can play exhaustion **Magic** in the early game while ramping up mana with [Kodama's Reach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kodama%27s+Reach).

Speaking of [Kodama's Reach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kodama%27s+Reach), the goal of this card is to create an imbalance in terms of mana count. In fact, in concert with [Plow Under](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under), Claytor's [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) (hopefully the last card in his hand) can [Mind Twist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Twist) the opponent, kill all of his creatures, and destroy all of his land… while leaving Joshua himself with three or more lands. Winning off the top of his deck at that point can't be very difficult, especially if Joshua enters the late game with a three-land advantage while the opponent is struggling to string together land and spells. Note that if the opponent was stuck with cards in hand and Claytor had *only* the [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) (or at least fewer cards than the opponent, or fewer cards than the “x” of the [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud)), the hand is a prime place to start turning this symmetrical card into an opportunity for card advantage.


Thomas Wood's mono-black version takes a different look at [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud), but at the end of the day, has the same philosophy. While Claytor wants to have more lands left over after all is said and done, Wood's deck can accomplish essentially the same goal by playing [Chrome Mox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) and [Phyrexian Arena](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Arena). Despite its short-term drawback, [Chrome Mox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) is very similar to playing an extra land: it puts more mana in play. The goal here is to set up the board such that after [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) resolves, the black mage has mana left over… whether that mana takes the form of an extra land drawn out by a [Kodama's Reach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kodama%27s+Reach) or a [Chrome Mox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) isn't particularly important. Moreover, artifacts and enchantments are two rare types of permanents that [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) does not affect, and [Phyrexian Arena](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Arena) is a good card for drawing out of a low-resources position after the Cloud resolves in any case. Think of this as an [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) that leaves a couple of lands on your own side… maybe instead of that [Erhnam Djinn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Erhnam+Djinn).


The skills used in building decks that play on symmetrical elements can also be used to defeat those same decks when you are playing against them. Even if your deck relies on cards in hand, land, and creatures, here are a couple small things you can do with the cards you do play that are not directly related to the opponent's symmetrical effects to make the opponent's [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) less attractive.


* *Jockey for position in the land war*: There are many decks that play [Sakura-Tribe Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) or [Kodama's Reach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kodama%27s+Reach) just because “they are good cards.” Having more lands in play, even if it is just by slowing down the opponent's development via [Creeping Mold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Mold) or [Plow Under](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under), can take away much of the post-Cloud remainder that these players rely on to win.
* *Hide cards in plain sight*: There are cards such as [Conjurer's Bauble](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Conjurer%27s+Bauble), [Wayfarer's Bauble](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wayfarer%27s+Bauble), or [Sensei's Divining Top](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) that can sit in play, unaffected by [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud). Because recovering resources post-Cloud is so important, these cards serve a dual purpose of dodging the effects of the [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) themselves *and* giving you that extra land or card in hand after the worst has happened. Remember: if you have a [Sensei's Divining Top](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) but it is in your hand… that doesn't do you any good against a big [Death Cloud](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud).

Back when symmetry was just starting out as an itty-bitty baby theoretical concept, one of the main ways to fight the powerful symmetrical cards was to take away the imbalance that the opponent built into his own deck. It isn't hard to understand that if two White Weenie decks fight and one plays [Crusade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crusade) where the other just has four more good creatures, the [Crusade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crusade) player has no advantage, and may, in fact, have a disadvantage. Similarly, just running [Sky Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sky+Diamond) in your control deck a few years ago was often enough to take the oomph out of the opponent's [Winter Orb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Winter+Orb) strategy.


When you go up against an opponent who uses powerful effects, including beneficial effects like [Howling Mine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine) or [Heartbeat of Spring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartbeat+of+Spring), try to think about what makes that card a worthwhile component of the other guy's deck and fight your battles armed with specifically that in mind. Does he need to draw extra cards? Will his deck function at all without doubled mana? By removing or piggybacking the symmetry-breaking element the opponent uses to win, you may be able to reap the benefits of these effects, or at least avoid the elements that make them so deadly.


Understanding what makes a deck tick will also tell you if you should pick your fight with the symmetrical card itself. A misguided attempt to maximize card advantage often makes a player so gung ho to ride the opponent's free help or punish him via his own cards' downsides that he misses the fact that these are exactly the cards that he uses to win the game. Hopefully this introduction to symmetry will help you make the right decisions down the line.
