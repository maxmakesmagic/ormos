Journey into Mix
================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on April 24, 2014 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/astybs_authorpic.png)
By Adam Styborski




 Stybs has played Magic the world over, writing and drafting as part of the event coverage team and slinging Commander everywhere his decks will fit. 







The possibilities of *Journey into Nyx* are mindboggling. 

  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/2014/d09bq2szko_1.jpg) Battlefield Thaumaturge | Art by [Mike Sass](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Mike+Sass%22%5D)


 Just like the last set, *Journey into Nyx* is packed with five multicolor God enchantment creatures that can serve as commanders, and a slew of new cards that can change or update entire decks. That's what I've been working on since [everything was revealed](http://www.wizards.com/magic/tcg/article.aspx?x=mtg/tcg/journeyintonyx/cig#). 

And based on your feedback, you agree as well.

 New sets are always a time for the trials (and errors) of trying new things, and it's often the most dramatic changes that define what we remember about the newest cards. Focusing on rares—like we will today for Commander—is just one part of how a set plays out, and it's why I'm excited to hit up a local game store for [the *Journey into Nyx* Prerelease](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/TCG/Events.aspx?x=mtgcom/events/Prerelease-facts) this weekend. 

 It turns out commons and uncommons are also a part of new sets. [Who knew](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/magazine/archive.aspx?tag=limited%20information&description=limited%20information)? (Okay, okay... [I did](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1aBkAsOakPb3EtY8YctD0S8flx7un1PWC03AWUaI9VrM/edit?usp=sharing).) 

Today, we take a look at the things you've found exciting in the new set as well as a few decks to go along with them. I hope you're ready for a wild ride.

### How Enchanting


[It wasn't long ago](/en/articles/archive/top-decks/phenomenal-cosmic-powers-2014-04-10) that we first looked at Kruphix, God of Horizons, but I wasn't alone in the idea of building a deck with the inscrutable. 


> 
>  The card I am most looking forward to from *Journey into Nyx* is Kruphix, God of Horizons. He will fit perfectly in my [Intet, the Dreamer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intet%2C+the+Dreamer) deck that loves to ramp into big mana and cast big spells. His mana-storing effect is perfect for this deck's main plan for victory. Whether it is building a massive [Blue Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blue+Sun%27s+Zenith) or copying a huge [Clan Defiance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clan+Defiance), this deck is all about big spells and Kruphix is just what this deck needed.

It's a plan ripped right out of the dreams laid out in the preview article, although exactly where the God of Horizons fits in isn't yet clear. That's the beauty of discovery: You're never quite sure what you'll get when you try it out. Where would you slot in Kruphix here?


> 
> Every time a new set is being previewed, I enjoy perusing its Card Image Gallery and making my "shopping list" of cards I want. I'll list each card I'm interested in, and next to it the number of them I'd like to acquire and which Commander decks they're destined for. I usually wait until after the full list of cards has been published, but to answer this question I decided to start my wish list early.
> 
> 
> Even so, I found this one difficult to answer. It's not that I'm not excited about this set; even with the gallery not yet full, my list is quite long. The problem lies in the fact that most of the cards I want to insert into my existing decks aren't going to significantly change those decks.
> 
> 
>  I finally settled on my answer, though, and it's Bearer of the Heavens. I chose him because he has prompted me to resurrect a long-gone deck. I dismantled my [Gisela, Blade of Goldnight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gisela%2C+Blade+of+Goldnight) deck over a year ago. It was a chaotic, "crazy effects" deck that was never intended to win games, and I retired it because it had begun to bore me and just annoyed my fellow players. 
> 
> 
>

> 
>  In Bearer of the Heavens, I see the potential for a more competitive version of the deck. The Bearer's ability reminds me of [False Prophet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=False+Prophet), which was in the original [Gisela, Blade of Goldnight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Gisela%252C%2BBlade%2Bof%2BGoldnight) deck; sort of an "insurance policy." Combined with [Avacyn, Angel of Hope](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacyn%2C+Angel+of+Hope) or something tricksy like [Sudden Disappearance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sudden+Disappearance), I'll look to make it a one-sided table wipe... if I can remove all permanents but my own from the battlefield, with [Gisela, Blade of Goldnight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Gisela%252C%2BBlade%2Bof%2BGoldnight) doubling up my damage potential, it shouldn't take too long to wrap up games after the Bearer hits my graveyard.

Some of my list is obvious (new lands to use, like Mana Confluence) and some of my list is obligatory (Gods make strong commanders), but there's plenty I'm still working out exactly how I'll play.

* Harness by Force is a scaling [Act of Treason](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Act+of+Treason) that begs for the revival of my [Kresh the Bloodbraided](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kresh+the+Bloodbraided) or [Shattergang Brothers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shattergang+Brothers) deck.
* Pull from the Deep and Reviving Melody look like utility all-stars to add to a typical Riku of Two Reflections deck, a commander I've never built a complete deck around.
* Hour of Need can be both an answer to creatures more terrifying than a 4/4 with flying, but also serves as an end-of-turn upgrade to surprise an opponent with evasive upgrades of my own.
* Banishing Light and Font of Fertility are staple utility spells I'd recommend for every Commander player to pick up in multiples: Each will serve you well in almost any deck that sports the appropriate color.
* Dictate of Heliod and Dictate of Erebos are decidedly one-sided, but I think it's Dictate of the Twin Gods that will result in more players being defeated in the long run.
* Eidolon of Blossoms looks like another "value creature" to throw in alongside [Phelddagrif](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phelddagrif) or [Roon of the Hidden Realm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roon+of+the+Hidden+Realm), let alone a dedicated deck for recycling enchantments.

What's going on here? I focused on creatures with haste that had abilities that seem to get better with Iroas hanging around.

* [Archwing Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archwing+Dragon) and [Oni of Wild Places](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oni+of+Wild+Places) typically have the drawback of returning to your hand every turn. Here, with Iroas, God of Victory, the ability to remain protected from [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) effects means you'll always have a powerful attacker that can't die in combat and gets *better* after someone fires a board wipe.
* [Goblin Fire Fiend](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Fire+Fiend) is deviously clever: Iroas makes blocking much tougher, and requiring a 1/1 to be blocked will clear the way through most battlefields that aren't overrun by tokens. Since it won't die in battle, this little Goblin can go a long way in making someone else put more than they want into play.
* [Kamahl, Pit Fighter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kamahl%2C+Pit+Fighter) and [Felhide Spiritbinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Felhide+Spiritbinder) are usually too frail to survive attacking in a game of Commander. With Iroas around to protect them, we can safely strike at enemies: Kamahl's backup Lighting Bolt-on-a-stick ability means he can still put in work without an attack, and [Felhide Spiritbinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Felhide+Spiritbinder) will gladly attack into a superior force, as you'll just copy the best opponents have to offer.
* [Urabrask the Hidden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urabrask+the+Hidden) is already a strong card, but in combination with Iroas gets silly, as it makes it nigh impossible for opponents to get blockers going once they fall behind.
* [Falkenrath Marauders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Falkenrath+Marauders) is a card I've wanted to use for a long time, and Iroas, God of Victory makes it possible. Every hit that goes unblocked makes it bigger, but blocking Marauders means leaving something like [Skizzik](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skizzik) to get through. It's a painful choice opponents have to make.

 Of course, there's enchantments, artifacts, and creatures without haste to consider, too, which is why I'm still stumped to finish out an Iroas, God of Victory deck.
