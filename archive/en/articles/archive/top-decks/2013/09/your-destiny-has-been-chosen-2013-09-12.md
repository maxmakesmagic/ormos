Your Destiny Has Been Chosen
============================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on September 12, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_adamstyborski.jpg)
By Adam Styborski




 Stybs has played Magic the world over, writing and drafting as part of the event coverage team and slinging Commander everywhere his decks will fit. 







I don't believe in fate. 

  
  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_1_j1sfo99ud9.jpg)
[Wheel of Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wheel+of+Fate) | Art by [Kev Walker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Kev%20Walker%22%5D)


 The idea that all choices are merely illusory is unsettling. That everything—from birth through death—is predetermined by [the Moirai](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moirai) flies in the face of what many of us perceive. Not that it'd be inconvenient to lay blame for all our **Magic** losses at the feet of the Fates, but it's much more likely that *we're* the ones making all the mistakes, not a pseudo-godlike triumvirate. 

However, we don't live on Theros.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_2_j1sfov9ud9.jpg) Art by [Daarken](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Daarken%22%5D)



*Theros* is packed with plenty from Greek mythology, including its own riff on the ancient three that even the gods were beholden to. These are the Fates that look upon Theros:



![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/tcg/products/ths/obasdfkjw8324lz/iBiaW6Yymwd_EN_LR.jpg) Being in *Theros* was clearly destiny. 

### The Killing Moon

Triad of Fates, from where I sit, is modeled exactly from the Moirai of Greek legends:

* The Triad's first ability models that of [Clotho](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clotho), tying the thread (or counter) of fate to a creature.
* The Triad's second ability models that of [Lachesis](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lachesis_(mythology)), giving the length of thread (life to be lived) for that creature.
* The Triad's third ability models that of [Atropos](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atropos), determining how and when the creature dies.

 While the ancient Greek take on the Fates is far broader in their reach, Triad of Fates is intentional and deliberate (and a bit more fair). In fact, the [Oracle of Theros](https://twitter.com/OracleOfTheros) confirmed the Fates can be overcome. 



But I don't see why we wouldn't just put the Fates to work for ourselves. That's the beauty in choosing a creature like Triad of Fates as a commander: It's not all about finding the biggest maximum value, but exploring what a particular commander really wants to do.

Triad of Fates wants to manage the fate of the game, of course.

 Building around a theme is a nebulous idea. There's no set recipe or fixed way to go about it. But there are a few tricks I've found to smooth out the process. For me, the biggest element of building around a theme is distilling the theme to a specific goal. It's easy to look at [Niv-Mizzet, Dracogenius](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Niv-Mizzet%2C+Dracogenius) and think about building "a wacky deck of mad science" but it's almost impossibly broad to define what *that* is. 

 Triad of Fates is about one theme: controlling fate. I pivoted this into a straightforward goal: What are the things that interact with fate, in the **Magic** sense? 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_3_j1sfov9ud9.jpg) There's only one other card in the game that can put fate counters onto creatures: [Oblivion Stone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Stone). It's already a handy way to wipe down the battlefield, but it now serves another purpose alongside the Fates. The usual activated-ability suspects of [Illusionist's Bracers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Illusionist%27s+Bracers) and [Rings of Brighthearth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rings+of+Brighthearth) also double up Triad of Fates's fate-counter settings. Since we're activating a creature, [Lightning Greaves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Greaves) and [Swiftfoot Boots](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swiftfoot+Boots) speed fate along, although [Ring of Valkas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ring+of+Valkas) and [Helm of Kaldra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Helm+of+Kaldra) can do the same trick. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_4_2pdehtqikx.jpg) Knowing the fate of all things is what the Fates do, so things that let us scry into the future fit perfectly. [Crystal Ball](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crystal+Ball) and [Viscera Seer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer) are classics that will fit alongside new ways such as Temple of Silence. [Scrying Sheets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scrying+Sheets) is a fascinating way to peek, and by going all-in on snow-covered basic lands, [Terrain Generator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terrain+Generator) lets us control our mana in ways opponents often can't. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_5_e7jhbo8j4g.jpg) Controlling fate has its perks, among them the ability to tap into creatures that are already empowered. The destructive potential of [Massacre Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Massacre+Wurm) is always handy, and so is the "catch up on lands" ability with [Knight of the White Orchid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid). [Rune-Scarred Demon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rune-Scarred+Demon) is perhaps the new face of these types of value creatures, although [Sunblast Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunblast+Angel) and [Aegis Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aegis+Angel) are among my preferred choices as well. 

That's part of what's convenient about honing in on the goal for a theme: it makes it easier to choose just specific cards among the many you like. Of all the sets of Orzhov Commander cards sent my way, all of them had something that fit the theme for Triad of Fates.

I don't even care about [Deathbringer Liege](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathbringer+Liege)'s ability to pump my field; turning every ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) card I cast into creature destruction is the epitome of efficient value. Teysa scares people away from attacking you. Single creatures get blocked while swarms get nuked. The Primordials were designed for multiplayer play, and Luminate provides excellent creature removal on a nice-sized body. Finally, [Necropolis Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropolis+Regent) just ends games if it sticks.

[Karmic Guide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karmic+Guide) sounds like a card that could be in *Theros*, representing the guide who can bring you back from the Underworld. Cards like Rescue from the Underworld and Gift of Immortality will have to do instead. 


> 
> * [Angel of Despair](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Despair)—Hauntingly beautiful picture, useful ability, sizable flying body. What's not to love?
> * [Fleshbag Marauder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fleshbag+Marauder)—Most Orzhov decks revolve around the game's graveyards, and what better way to fill up your opponents' yards than by making them sacrifice something good? Add a [Sun Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan), and this guy gets REALLY silly.
> * [Karmic Guide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karmic+Guide)—You'll rarely pay the echo cost for this fine lady, but over the course of the game, you'll likely be able to resurrect several spicy targets.
> * [Mikaeus, the Unhallowed](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mikaeus%2C+the+Unhallowed)—There are some useful Humans in white and black, but in a deck that runs very few of them, Dark Mike shines the brightest. Sacrificing your own creatures for fun and profit just became much more fun and MUCH more profitable.
> * [Adarkar Valkyrie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Adarkar+Valkyrie)—Black and white are the colors of lots of things dying at once. [Adarkar Valkyrie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Adarkar+Valkyrie) lets you retain some sort of board after a Wrath, and if you target something that brings back guys (such as [Phyrexian Delver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Delver), [Sepulchral Primordial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sepulchral+Primordial), or the aforementioned [Karmic Guide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karmic+Guide)), your Wrath-type spells can turn the game completely into your favor.

![Sun Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sun+Titan)  
 Who could forget [Sun Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan)? With its handy appearance in *Duel Decks: Heroes vs. Monsters*, the big titan that could is an excellent choice to getting back other things. I heard it's featured in [powerful combos](/en/articles/archive/top-decks/it-takes-two-2013-08-29) too. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_6_f518kbh9a0.jpg)  
 Triad of Fates can flicker the lives of plenty of creatures, but having some help along the way to pull it off can't hurt. [Flickerwisp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flickerwisp) is great offensively or defensively, and the applications available with [Galepowder Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galepowder+Mage) and [Restoration Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel) are obvious too. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_7_rvb2xkuc8o.jpg)  
 Of course, there's more to controlling life than just creating. The fickle Fates can *end* it just as fast, caring about death as much as life. [Archon of Justice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archon+of+Justice) cares about dying, itself, and Reaper of Abyss is a loyal watcher of such events, but it's [Dark Prophecy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Prophecy) that tickles me here: predicting the death of something is much easier when you're the Fates in charge of things. 

 Going beyond the Fates for more ways to end the lives of creatures quickly leads to cards like [Martyr's Cause](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr%27s+Cause); [Attrition](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Attrition); and [Vish Kal, Blood Arbiter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vish+Kal%2C+Blood+Arbiter+)  paired up with the quartet of death-deniers in [Nether Traitor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nether+Traitor), [Tenacious Dead](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tenacious+Dead), [Reassembling Skeleton](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reassembling+Skeleton), and [Bloodghast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodghast). 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_8_ztqo2vyyu2.jpg)  
 To make all of this life-ending business matter more, adding creatures that create more creatures will spread the love of fate counters that translate into drawing cards. [Captain of the Watch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Captain+of+the+Watch) and [Myr Battlesphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Myr+Battlesphere) make plenty of dudes in a hurry, but the upcoming Abhorrent Overlord makes just as much sense, too. 


> 
> 
> 
> 
> * [Archangel of Thune](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Thune)—Orzhov excels at two things: gaining life and making lots of little dudes. The best thing to do with lots of little dudes is to make them not so little. [Archangel of Thune](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Thune) does this nicely, especially with the extort mechanic from **Gatecrash**.
> * [Sun Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan)—Orzhov also excels at sacrificing their little dudes for beneficial effects. [Sun Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan) lets you get those little dudes back, as well as having a significant body and vigilance to provide a sizable blocker. Better yet, the Titan doesn't even need to deal combat damage to bring creatures back.
> * [Hero of Bladehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold)—My friend has a [Teysa, Orzhov Scion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teysa%2C+Orzhov+Scion) token Commander that is the pride and joy of his collection, and he has long lectured me that the Hero is the one of the two best creature in his deck. The mass-production and pumping up of tokens is truly a force to behold.
> * [Grave Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan)—Another terror from my friend's Teysa Commander, I have long learned to cross my fingers and pray when this hits the board. As a diehard Riku player, I have little to no answers for this Zombie-spewing machine, aside from a copied [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), [Flame Slash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame+Slash), or a [Comet Storm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Comet+Storm). In my opinion, this is the best token-producer in the game, and one of the hardest creatures to deal with. What could possibly be better than this?
> * [Pontiff of Blight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pontiff+of+Blight)—Monsieur Le Pontiff, as my friends affectionately call him, is in my opinion the best creature in Commander. Extort makes each opponent lose 1 life, and you gain that much life. In a five-player game, extorting five times on a spell will gain you a staggering 20 life. Drawing a mana rock when you are in a tight spot turns from a dead draw into gaining life into the 50s, 60s, or even 100s, given the amount of mana available in Commander, as well of the number of players. His durability makes him a standout in Commander.

[Grave Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) is another powerhouse token-maker to add to our mix, but creatures alone may not be enough. [White Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=White+Sun%27s+Zenith); [Increasing Devotion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Increasing+Devotion); [Decree of Justice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Decree+of+Justice); [Entreat the Angels](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Entreat+the+Angels); and even the new Elspeth, Sun's Champion will help us quickly bring life we control onto the battlefield (for all those *dying* effects we have thrown in). 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_9_d0d6zui5jv.jpg)  
 The ability to pull back that which has passed is also in the power of the Fates, and cards like [Obzedat's Aid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%27s+Aid) and [Beacon of Unrest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beacon+of+Unrest) do it in style. Cashing in a creature that makes tokens, only to pull it back immediately, is a surprise opponents will learn to fear. More permanent ways to manipulate fate, such as [Debtor's Knell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Debtor%27s+Knell) and [Cauldron of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cauldron+of+Souls), work well too. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ct/ct264_10_3ms90y0di9.jpg)  
 While the fate of individuals can be the source of stories, the Fates also oversee how entire battles play out. Cards like [Hour of Reckoning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hour+of+Reckoning), [Austere Command](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Austere+Command), and [Solar Tide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solar+Tide) give us choices that only the most chosen can overcome. 

All said, following the flavor of the function of fate led me to combine your favorite Orzhov cards with my singular, focused theme.

 Is [Vish Kal, Blood Arbiter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Vish%2BKal%252C%2BBlood%2BArbiter) a "better" commander for this deck? Are there "stronger" or "more powerful" choices that can be made? It's very well true on both accounts. But what this deck does is follow through on its goal to influence and determine the fate of a game. 

 One of the questions I see pop up regularly is "Why did you choose so-and-so as the commander? Such-and-such is *totally* better in every way!" That *better* bit is a sticking point with me. Commander is meant for exploration and fun, with almost all of the legendary creatures in **Magic** around to make the most of things. 

 Whether it's [the destructive power of Polukranos, World Eater](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/ct/263&page=3)  or the [clunky but explosive potential of Anax and Cymede](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/ct/263&page=2), taking the time to discover what's hidden all across **Magic** is near-endless adventure. 

It's simply fated to happen.

### Up Against Your Will

While I don't place stock in any fate controlling our destinies, it seems there's always some new challenge facing us in life. Commander seems no different, and each week, difficult situations end up in my inbox.

 What I mean by *difficult situation* is one where a conflict happens in a game, and players end up dissatisfied as a result: 




* One player's deck is overwhelmingly powerful compared to others.
* Some players use the most efficient combos as others build Rube Goldberg machines.
* Someone insists on playing the same deck every game, regardless of whether everyone else wants a change or not.

 There are innumerous situations like these. I'm not interested in the situations themselves, but your successes in solving them. **What's a difficult situation you encountered playing Commander that ended up being solved by the group?**

* 200-word limit to answer the question
* Name and email required (non-personal information to be used in column)

Remember, having a situation isn't enough—we all have problems—but having a problem your group solved is what I'm after.
