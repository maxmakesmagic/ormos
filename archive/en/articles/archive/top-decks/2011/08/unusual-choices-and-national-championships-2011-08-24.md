Unusual Choices and the National Championships
==============================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on August 25, 2011 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











This past week showed the summer of 2011 a couple more National Championships. Their Top 8s featured a combination of established archetypes, new decks, and established archetypes trying new things and new cards.

Though Caw-Blade showed up multiple times in each National Championship Top 8, they were as a whole much more diverse than certain other Top 8s (say the [US National Championship](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/usnat11/welcome), with six Caw-Blade decks), and there seemed to be a great variation in the choices made even within the contexts of established decks.

Both Top 8s we will look at today ([Great Britain](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/gbnat11/welcome) and [Canada](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/canat11/welcome)) featured six different decks, with Caw-Blade and Red Deck variants doubling up in each. Let's see how they bore out!

The first thing that jumps out at us in this Top 8 is champion Daniel Royde's Mono-Green Eldrazi Ramp deck. Though we have seen a fair amount of action from [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) since the bannings of [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) and [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic), performances by onetime [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) peer Mono-Green Ramp have been fewer and farther between.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td157_ulamog.jpg)  
Royde's deck is absolutely chock full of mana: 28 of his 60 cards are lands, and he has a fair number of [Joraga Treespeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Joraga+Treespeaker)s and [Overgrown Battlement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overgrown+Battlement)s (and to a lesser extent, [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith)s) to boot.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Aside from individual customizations / modifications, Royde's deck functions at a baseline as many of these decks have for the last year or so: Early mana acceleration sets up; [Summoning Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Summoning+Trap) defends against [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s.

[Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)—whether hard-cast or a result of a lucky Trap—helps set up the specialty Eldrazi lands ([Eldrazi Temple](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Temple) and [Eye of Ugin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eye+of+Ugin)). With [Eye of Ugin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eye+of+Ugin), a Mono-Green Ramp deck is never truly out of threats. You can always go and get [Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ulamog%2C+the+Infinite+Gyre) or, in a pinch, the "humble" [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite).



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

But as we've already said, there are a fair number of customizations going on in this deck that make it an atypical Eldrazi Ramp...


> * [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman): Royde can go and get specialty cards on a whim. This includes [Obstinate Baloth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obstinate+Baloth) as either discard defense or against beatdown, [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) for Deceiver Twin, and [Viridian Corrupter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Corrupter) against artifacts. Out of the sideboard, the flyer-swatting [Stingerfling Spider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stingerfling+Spider)!
> * Customized one- and two-ofs, like [Beast Within](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beast+Within) (flexible answer) and [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) (potential threat, additional ramp)
> * [Artisan of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Artisan+of+Kozilek): a bit lower on the curve than we might be used to... [Kozilek, Butcher of Truth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kozilek%2C+Butcher+of+Truth) at ten mana is typically more common than this Eldrazi at nine. Though [Artisan of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Artisan+of+Kozilek) has a "mere" annihilator 2, when you are getting up there in mana costs, every last point matters, and the bridge between nine and ten mana can often feel like a delta of nine or ten mana.

Eduardo's Caw-Blade is more conventional relative to the current card pool. [Phantasmal Image](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image) is finding quite a bit of adoption in Caw-Blade lists, doing everything from copying the opponent's [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk) to pulling Caw-Blade players out of mana screw by pilfering a [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum)'s abilities.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td157_hawk.jpg)  
This sort of Caw-Blade has some respect for higher-end threats—[Emeria Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria+Angel) as well as some six-drops—but still runs main-deck [Timely Reinforcements](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Timely+Reinforcements) as a deterrent to Red Decks from the main. Note the presence of the near-ubiquitous [Dismember](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember) main, and the continued use of [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine) as the main-deck Equipment; Sajgalik supplements from the sideboard with a solo [Sword of War and Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+War+and+Peace).

Both decks are [Chandra's Phoenix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra%27s+Phoenix) variants. That means that they run a bit burn heavy relative to certain other red builds in order to have a higher chance of buying back a lost [Chandra's Phoenix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra%27s+Phoenix).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td157_phoenix.jpg)  
That said, neither deck is all-in on the [Chandra's Phoenix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra%27s+Phoenix) plan. Both ran a full eight fetch lands to help fuel [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer), and Penton went a little old school, doubling up the fetch land synergies by bringing back [Plated Geopede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede) (and, unrelated but still old-ish school, [Kargan Dragonlord](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kargan+Dragonlord)). [Ember Hauler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ember+Hauler) (as played by Harborne) has been the default Red Deck two-drop of choice since about March.

Both decks went with the full four copies of [Shrine of Burning Rage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrine+of+Burning+Rage)—maybe the scariest Red Deck card in Standard—and gave a nod to [Hero of Oxid Ridge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Oxid+Ridge), the consensus best solution to a world where white is defined by its ability to run [Timely Reinforcements](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Timely+Reinforcements).

William played arguably the most unique deck in either Top 8, a green-blue "good stuff" aggro-control deck combining. William's deck is basically good spells and better creatures, featuring mana acceleration and much the same Equipment advantage we see Caw-Blade trying to maintain, but also strange nuts like second-turn [Beast Within](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beast+Within)... your land.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td157_skinshifter.jpg)  
[Skinshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinshifter) is a card that has been getting rave reviews from Hall of Famer Brian Kibler, and we see that in the Dunn deck; note that [Skinshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinshifter) is a superb blocker even against Red, because you can always get the block in before going 0/8, even if the opponent has the burn spell. [Skinshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinshifter) in Rhino mode has trample, making it a fine Swordsman, and Bird mode is actually bigger than a [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Skinshifter)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinshifter)  
[Garruk, Primal Hunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk%2C+Primal+Hunter) helps to make up for the fact that William's deck plays only 14 creatures; Garruk actually *gains* loyalty while spitting out Beasts.

This deck, highly reminiscent of Aaron Nicoll's [Australia National Championship](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/aunat11/welcome) deck, features both a standard [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) engine, and also the [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) + [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) infinite combo.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td157_birthing.jpg)  
The advantage of this build relative to most other decks in the field (let alone other [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) decks) is the presence of a turn-three infinite kill (turn one [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise), turn two [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), turn three [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) the [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), make lots of 1/4s, etc.); the downside relative to some other blue-red-green lists (notably, the one advocated by Patrick Chapin and Michael Jacob) is that Twin Pod offers a generally inferior [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) strategy.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

As you can see, Marc only has one copy of most of his creature cards: one [Acidic Slime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Acidic+Slime), one [Frost Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) at six, one [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle), and so on. This is because he is taking up eight of his deck slots with combo pieces. So on the one hand, he can just come out and kill the opponent with a super sick two-card combination, but on the other hand, he doesn't have the elegant [Hero of Oxid Ridge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Oxid+Ridge) out-of-nowhere alpha strike kills, or the ability to lean on multiple [Inferno Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inferno+Titan)s in a creature mirror.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td157_3Cards1.jpg)  
Both strategies have their incentives—and the Exarch Twin combination is a big one!—so I think it is fair to say that neither blue-red-green Pod build is objectively superior to the other.

By contrast, Mani Davoudi played a fairly different Caw-Blade variant (while still falling under the macro umbrella of Caw-Blade). Mani ran main-deck [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) as a powerful aggro-control card (solid threat against combo and control, but cheap enough that it will typically resolve), and [Hero of Bladehold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold) as a four-of in the creature suite. [Hero of Bladehold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold) has been likened to the "seventh" Titan—albeit a Titan that costs only four mana—for the power of its token-generating ability. A fine Sword-bearer with 4 toughness, [Hero of Bladehold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold) can end games quickly for Caw-Blade, rather than relegating its master into a long, drawn-out battle of sometimes-enhanced 1/1 creatures.
