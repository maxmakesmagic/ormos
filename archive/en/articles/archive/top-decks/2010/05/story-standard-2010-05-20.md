A Story of Standard
===================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 20, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







So, National Qualifiers have supplanted the traditional Regional Championships. A cross between old Regionals and the old (beloved, but ultimately somewhat useless) State Championships, National Qualifiers seem to be an upgrade to the previous system. I, for one, was a big fan of being able to play a few minutes from my house this year, in an eight-invitation event!


So, how did National Qualifiers play out? Here are the stats—the known stats at the time of this writing, that is—for Qualifiers in the US:


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td91_chart.jpg)Some of what we know is surprising (but maybe shouldn't be), and other stuff is just, well, surprising!


### Jund Is Still the Deck to Beat


After several weeks of touting white-blue, specifically White-Blue Tapout variants, as the new dominant player in the Standard landscape, Jund battled back and killed all kinds of butt at National Qualifiers. Leading all decks in Top 8s, and leading all decks in invitations, Jund either never relinquished the top spot, or got it back last weekend.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td91_blightning.jpg)The cool thing about Jund, though—the cool thing about Jund *finally* maybe—is that it is no longer a homogenous erratic mana deck that relies on flipping a whole lot of [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning)s to win. Today's Jund—that Jund line item anyway—represents a fair number of decks with unique elements and styles of play. To wit:

This is the newest incarnation of the Jund deck. It plays more sacrifice lands to support a [Plated Geopede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede) attack starting on turn two. Between [Plated Geopede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede) and [Putrid Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech), this deck has eight different offensive two drops that can batter down a [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) and take big chunks out of the opponent's life total. You might notice the return of the four-of [Maelstrom Pulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Pulse). With so many planeswalkers in the format, Jund wants more ways to deal with those, too.


The other super cool, super special thing about this style of Jund deck is the sideboard. One basic [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) (which can be found by several main deck lands as well as [Borderland Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Borderland+Ranger)) allows the deck to sideboard in [Sedraxis Specter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sedraxis+Specter). Remember, against most blue decks, you are going to have an [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) in play anyway, thanks to the best blue card in Standard, [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas).

This is not quite the classic Jund, but also not far off. This deck, in contrast to the Geopede style, has *no* offensive two drop! No [Putrid Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech)! Unlike the deck that won the most recent Pro Tour, this deck has plenty of removal—[Bituminous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bituminous+Blast), [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), [Maelstrom Pulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Pulse), and [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate)—as well as a little acceleration.

Timothy's list seems fairly representative of the archetype as we find it today. The big defining element of this deck is the replacement of [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) (and often [Rafiq of the Many](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rafiq+of+the+Many)) with the [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara) and [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription) combination. With [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara) in play, you can hunt up [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription) mid-combat in order to hammer the opponent for a large chunk of bonus damage. The Conscription version has a hefty advantage in the mirror (you can just chump block a Rafiq-enhanced threat), whereas a trampler that is ten larger than it is meant to end negotiations fairly quickly.

I don't think I could have been happier than to see one of my best friends—Brian David-Marshall—qualify for the National Championships. He did so with a 32-creature attack deck designed and promulgated by my favorite **Magic** columnist and deck designer, Gerry Thompson.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td91_vengevine.jpg)Gerry's Naya deck, which we have been calling Naya LikeSaber (because it was promoted on Facebook, where you "Like" things) is an offensive atomic bomb. If you need to recover [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine), a [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) will almost always do it (only two total non-creature spells to flip). A [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) always does. The deck still has the [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) gas for [Sejiri Steppe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sejiri+Steppe) (and whatever else you might want land-wise) that has made Naya a premiere strategy for some months.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td91_2Cards1.jpg)A special something featured in this list is the presence of main deck [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage). The Sparkmage is hell on [Malakir Bloodwitch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Malakir+Bloodwitch) with the main deck [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) (which can be found by [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic)), but subtly, makes the Naya deck a huge favorite against [Polymorph](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polymorph). Go ahead and try to resolve that action.


Gerry's sideboard plays much like the [Sedraxis Specter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sedraxis+Specter) approach we saw in Geopede Jund. How do you cast [Vapor Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vapor+Snare) in Naya? Well, you have an [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island), several search mechanisms, and [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch) ... but the answer might as well be [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas). [Vapor Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vapor+Snare) not only comes online thanks to [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas), but you can spend the next several turns "cleaning off" your lands by bouncing them; such a cool addition to the deck by such a good designer.

With [Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine) and [Font of Mythos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Font+of+Mythos) in play, the opponent will be drawing three almost every turn; might as well do something that ruins that net-decker's day.


You know what's crazy? We barely scratched the surface in this one. There are dozens of new and different and viable decks [here](/en/events/coverage/2010-national-qualifiers-top-8-standard-deck-lists). Just as there are different looks at Jund, there are different looks at Grixis. Some decks play no creatures at all, where some hold creatures to be a core to winning. Some pick up [Gatekeeper of Malakir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gatekeeper+of+Malakir) with [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor), others lace up the two-for-one with [Staggershock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Staggershock). Me? I was lucky this weekend. This is what I did:
