Standard Metagame Breakdown
===========================



 Posted in [ARTICLES](/en/articles)
 on February 14, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/hero/news_default_wizards_logo_0.jpg)
By Blake Rasmussen, Nate Price, and Josh Bennett










Standard is about to change.


While every Pro Tour brings with it some shift in the metagame either great or small, Standard Pro Tours often shape the metagame in wild and sometimes unexpected ways. New innovations from the best minds in the game poke and prod the new set until something different comes out.


Sometimes that new thing is simply adding [Boros Reckoner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Reckoner) to the Blue-White-Red (UWR) decks that have been popular since *Return to Ravnica* came out, but sometimes that new thing is a game changer.


We have a bit of both here in Montreal. Compiling all of the archetypes is something like thirty percent science and seventy percent "What the heck do you call this deck?", but that's what the intrepid coverage staff is here for. That and our good looks.


What we found is pretty awesome: this is one of the most diverse Standard metagames we've ever seen.

Three already well-defined decks come out at the top – Esper Control, Jund Midrange, and UWR – and were the only decks to manage to make up even 10 percent of the field. UWR led the way, thanks in large part to how well [Boros Reckoner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Reckoner) slots into the space formerly held by [Geist of Saint Traft](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft).


But just behind them are a bevy of new aggressive decks made possible by the Boros and Gruul guilds joining the fray. Gruul Aggro, Saito Zoo, and Naya Humans each convinced 15 or more people to sleeve them up today. [Stomping Ground](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stomping+Ground) and [Sacred Foundry](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sacred+Foundry) were big reasons for that, but [Boros Reckoner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Reckoner) and [Burning-Tree Emissary](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning-Tree+Emissary) were fantastic additions to those decks.


A little ways down the list you'll notice Naya Aggro as well, which was very close to Saito Zoo, but often lacked the trademark [Gyre Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gyre+Sage). If you combine Naya Aggro with Saito Zoo, the numbers match up to Gruul Aggro. If you add Naya Humans to Naya Aggro and Saito Zoo, you end up with a whopping 43 variations on Naya aggro decks being played this weekend, which would put it on par with UWR.


A few new decks have popped up as well. The Aristocrats is a cheekily-named Black-Red-White deck that plays [Cartel Aristocrat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cartel+Aristocrat) and [Falkenrath Aristocrat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Falkenrath+Aristocrat) alongside a bevy of fuel to sacrifice to create a synergy that only backs up what is already a powerful suite of cards.


RUG Flash takes advantage of both [Stomping Ground](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stomping+Ground)s and [Breeding Pool](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool) to combine [Yeva, Nature's Herald](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yeva%2C+Nature%27s+Herald) with [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) and [Huntmaster of the Fells](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Huntmaster+of+the+Fells).


Human Reanimator remains popular thanks to [Cartel Aristocrat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cartel+Aristocrat). The card has a fantastic Game 1 win percentage against much of the field, and the deck has adopted other interesting twists since it was re-introduced to the world a few weeks ago. [Burning-Tree Emissary](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning-Tree+Emissary) gives it some interesting combo-ish play while feeding its mana, and cards such as [Borborygmos Enraged](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Borborygmos+Enraged) give it a solid backup plan.


A few other oddities:


Bore-os: Boros splashing [Dreadbore](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dreadbore) and other black cards


Mono-ish Black Control: [Mutilate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutilate)-based control decks splashing red, usually for Rakdos' Return


Esper Planeswalkers: We qualified any Esper Control deck that had at least five Planeswalkers here. Various flavors of Jace, [Tamiyo, the Moon Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tamiyo%2C+the+Moon+Sage), and [Sorin, Lord of Innistrad](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sorin%2C+Lord+of+Innistrad) usually made the cut.


Other: Every deck that had just one copy in attendance was lumped here, but aside from the odd (and awesome) [Omniscience](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Omniscience)/[Enter the Infinite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enter+the+Infinite) deck, most were variations on other known themes.


What do these decks look like? Here are a few very basic versions of the top decks (not attributed to any one player), not including sideboards.
