Havoc in Harrisburg
===================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on August 11, 2004 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Well we're back. Back from the *Ice Age*, back to that crazy world of twisted metal, *Mirrodin*. The relevant format is, of course, *Mirrodin* Block Constructed; if you want to go to the Pro Tour, this is the format to know. The decks we talk about in this column will be the decks you have to be prepared to beat, if not adopt yourself.

Before he handed the reins of [Swimming with Sharks](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/columnarchive&column=SwimmingWithSharks) over to me, BDM picked up one last set of PTQ deck lists. I have in front of me over 100 deck lists from the recent Harrisburg PA PTQ... but you only get to lay eyes on the most significant nine. Before we get too deep into the analysis, here are the lucky sharks who made Top 8 and how the Top 8 played out.

This Top 8 didn't have a lot of surprises as far as deck archetypes go. The Top 4 led off with the main expected decks of Red, Affinity, Red, and Affinity; along with another Red deck in the bottom bracket, we had the minority -- but not *too* different -- archetypes of G/R Land Destruction, Mono-Green Beacon, and U/W Angel.

I'm not going to go into the Affinity basics in this article. The chances are that if you had been following Swimming with Sharks before my taking over, you know very well how Affinity works already. These decks happened to be variations of Aether Vial Affinity -- just with no dedicated green sideboard. If you are preparing for GP New Jersey next weekend, Aether Vial Affinity is going to be your most important matchup. It looks like PTQ level players seem to be valuing the number of available sideboard slots they have more than overall card power. Both of the Aether Vial Affinity decks in this Top 8 elected to go with a red sideboard that didn't demand the dedication of a ton of [Tree of Tales](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tree+of+Tales); while [Electrostatic Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrostatic+Bolt) and [Shatter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shatter) aren't *quite* up there with [Viridian Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Shaman) and [Oxidize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize), neither Semion or Ben chose to sideboard more than one land.

The much more interesting story is the other dominant deck -- mono-Red. All four players ran four [Slith Firewalker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slith+Firewalker)s main, four [Magma Jet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Jet)s main, and three [Shunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shunt)s in the sideboard (more on that later). Two of the decks were very dedicated to an anti-artifact theme with 4 [Electrostatic Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrostatic+Bolt+) and 3 [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon) main and some combination of 6 [Detonate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detonate)/[Echoing Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Ruin)s/[Shatter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shatter)s. The other mono-Red deck played a more damage-intensive strategy instead, utilizing all 4 [Furnace Whelp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Whelp)s main, as well as [Beacon of Destruction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beacon+of+Destruction). Frank Mellert really likes his smoke. [Furnace Whelp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Whelp) might seem a little under-powered when compared with big cousins in other blocks (namely [Fledgling Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fledgling+Dragon) and [Lightning Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Dragon)) but the fact that he comes online for full pump on the next turn without need for Echo or Threshold makes Furnace Whelp a powerful short term threat. In a deck like mono-Red, Frank only needs one or two hits off of a [Furnace Whelp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Whelp) to put the opponent into point blank range. Not one of these mono-Red decks played with all 4 available copies of [Pulse of the Forge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pulse+of+the+Forge).

The Murphy and Helwig decks seem pretty standard versions of mono-Green and U/W to me; if you go up against these colors at GP NJ, don't expect the opponent's deck to deviate overmuch from the listed card choices. Murphy played with main-deck [Acquire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Acquire) and only half the Pulses he could have. With the format getting faster and moving away from big artifact creatures like [Darksteel Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Colossus), I don't know how good main-deck Acquire will be. [Pulse of the Fields](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pulse+of+the+Fields) is actually one of my favorite cards right now, and is doubly good in a deck with [Pristine Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pristine+Angel). The most surprising thing about Helwig's deck is the absence of [Blasting Station](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blasting+Station). Many mono-Green decks with [Beacon of Creation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beacon+of+Creation) have started using [Blasting Station](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blasting+Station) as "a green [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball)."

Last, but in this case, first, is Allen Jackson's G/R Land Destruction deck. Jackson's deck is essentially a bunch of good cards. He has cards that are solid in many different matchups: [Molten Rain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molten+Rain) against [Tooth and Nail](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail), [Magma Jet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Jet) and [Electrostatic Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrostatic+Bolt) for early game defense in a variety of match-ups. The really interesting thing about Allen's deck is that he has trump against everything but U/W. [Arc-Slogger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger) thrashes green, [Molder Slug](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molder+Slug) is a one card hammer and anvil against Affinity, and a big [Rude Awakening](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rude+Awakening) kills Red decks just about every time it resolves. The only thing I don't get about Allen's deck is the 15 cards off to the side. His is one of the only ones in the format that I've seen that has Forests main but not [Oxidize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize)s; I'm not sure where he brings in [Cosmic Larva](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cosmic+Larva). It sure is big, though.

| *MikeyP, Pro Tour Los Angeles ‘01* |

In Round 8, Mike was fighting a mono-Red mirror for Top 8. Not surprisingly, the decorated doctor was playing very tight mathematical **Magic** according to a paradigm defined only recently, called the Philosophy of Fire (basically applying advantage principles to assembling 20 damage rather than the traditional focus on card advantage) Mike's opponent Entwined [Grab the Reins](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grab+the+Reins) on his [Arc-Slogger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger), a play that would both rob Mike of his best threat and put him to nine. He thought for a bit and sent [Magma Jet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Jet) at the opponent's head. One of the top two cards from the scry was [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast). Mike put the [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) second from the bottom and repeatedly activated the [Arc-Slogger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger) such that he would kill with that [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) the following turn. Mike drew and went for the [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) kill... and bought an opposing [Shunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shunt).

He then untapped and followed up with a [Pulse of the Forge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pulse+of+the+Forge) for Top 8.

The lesson? MikeyP is without a doubt one of **Magic's** most decorated champions. It is pretty likely that he was the best player in the PTQ. He played tightly. But what is the difference between Mike's sideboard and the three that made Top 8? What was the card that won the matchup for Top 8 where Mike was playing so well?

At least according to this PTQ's success stories, if you want to run mono-Red, it looks like you should have -- and at the very least, respect -- [Shunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shunt).
