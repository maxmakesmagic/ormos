Content, Character, and Conley
==============================



 Posted in [Top Decks](/en/articles/columns/top-decks-archive)
 on December 1, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Do me a favor and chunk out the next thirty seconds.


*Thirty seconds*, okay?


Name three things you learned your freshman year of college (in class).


Go!


...


That's what I thought.


Okay now do me a different favor and chunk out the next *ten* seconds.


Just ten, okay?


Name three characters from the television show *Friends.*


Right?


Right!


Most people sputter and stutter on the first one. If you asked me cold, I could probably get out three things, but on a bad day... Probably not. Most people, though? Maybe they'll get one in thirty seconds.


*And this is something that cost your mommy and pops probably tens of thousands of dollars!*


On the other hand anyone who's anyone (of a certain age, I guess) can rattle out "Monica, Phoebe, and Chandler" lickety-quick.


You know why?


*Character* is infinitely more interesting and compelling than *content*.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_3Pics.jpg)*Tom Ross, Patrick Chapin, and Luis Scott-Vargas*  
Only decklist nerds like YT remember things like that Tom's Boss Ross deck had the [Basilisk Collar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) + [Cunning Sparkmage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage) combo *in the sideboard* (a combo that became so prevalent main deck). And while Patrick Chapin could rattle off the differences in Elves decks from Berlin (and why their group's was fastest), most fans just remember LSV. They remember LSV's run, or they remember LSV's come from behind and ultimate success.


*Character.*


Apologies, of course, if you 1) didn't [yet?] attend a freshman year of college, or 2) have never seen *Friends.* Feel free to fire off "Princess Leia, Luke, and Yoda" or "Kirk, Spock, and Bones" or any number of iconic three-person character sequences from movies or television shows literally decades old (and in many cases that predate the circumstances of our births). As a **Magic** player I am sure you have the creative capacity to imagine parallel questions, and see how easy, and easily contrastable, those questions are.


This line, the one between content and character, is the essence of what makes following **Magic: The Gathering** at a pro level so interesting.

All of those names are only three letters long, yet pack fireballs full of passion and fairy tales. I mean how many *Roberts* are there in the world? How many *Bob*s? Yet I can say "Bob" and those three letters conjure up so many meanings for basically every **Magic** aficionado.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_bob.jpg)Bob Maher, as seen in his invitational card, [Dark Confidant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)


  
For me they call up an image of someone tall and unstoppable. A player who could come out of retirement to win an Invitational—and give us the [Dark Confidant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)—or defeat the seemingly undefeatable Hat in the finals of a Masters Series event. Bob was a player so preternaturally confident that he could make the worst play in the room... but convince dozens of rapt onlookers that it was the best play available.


In a battle between content and character, content is a poor and whimpering common veiled by the long shadow of the mightiest mythic rare planeswalker.


That is what makes the Pro Tour so compelling: Cheering for our heroes and shaking our fists at the screen as Rich and BDM call the victory going the wrong way. And in the 2011 World Championships, the character of *Character* was played by master deck designer Conley Woods.


### Transformation Week: The Transformation of Conley Woods


The Conley of the 2011 World Championships seemed like a different man than the rogue deck designer we have all been cheering for the past couple of years. This Conley was sober stone at the feature match table, a calculating executor—and executioner—with only two losses over the first three days, both ultimately helping Woods teammates Luis Scott-Vargas and Paulo Vitor Damo da Rosa achieve Top 8 themselves. His quarterfinals save against Craig Wescoe will likely spark discussions for years to come.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_woods.jpg)*Conley Woods*  
More than his play at the tables, Conley—a player who made his fame in **Magic** by summoning [Sorin Markov](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sorin+Markov) in an Extended [Martyr of Sands](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr+of+Sands) deck, or by journeying to Magical Christmas Land with [Lotus Cobra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra), four [Violent Ultimatum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Violent+Ultimatum)s, *and four [Cruel Ultimatum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum)s—*dominated Standard as part of a ChannelFireball.com cadre, boasting a streamlined [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) deck as his weapon of choice.


*Could there be a correlation?*

Four members of Team ChannelFireball.com—Conley Woods, Luis Scott-Vargas, Paulo Vitor Damo da Rosa, and Josh Utter-Leyton—made the Worlds Top 8 with their [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) deck. [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) was one of the most successful decks of the most recent Block Constructed Pro Tour, and was flying under the radar of Standard metagamers. A powerful, extremely fast, deck with few main-deck predators, [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) has few rivals in the category of unbridled offense.


**How Does Tempered Steel Work?**


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_steel.jpg)[Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) | Art by [Wayne Reynolds](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22Wayne+Reynolds%22%5D)


While superficially it is a bunch of artifact dudes pumped up by the enchantment [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel), I think it is important to note how many evasion creatures this deck packs. [Glint Hawk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glint+Hawk), [Vault Skirge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vault+Skirge), [Glint Hawk Idol](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glint+Hawk+Idol), and [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) all fly. [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) kind of flies (on offense). [Etched Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Etched+Champion) doesn't fly... but then again the average flyer can't block it.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_3Cards1.jpg)  
So it's not just that your undercosted artifact dudes hit extra hard due to [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel); opponents also have a hard time blocking them.


The deck basically always has metalcraft, so [Dispatch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dispatch) and [Mox Opal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Opal) are super good. The deck is somewhat vulnerable to sweep spells, but a 3/3 (or even 5/5) [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) is good against tapped-out opponents.

As with many decks of the current era, the ChannelFireball.com version of [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) makes fine use of [Moorland Haunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt). Their four [Seachrome Coast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seachrome+Coast)s (with a [Glacial Fortress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glacial+Fortress) in the sideboard!) are basically dedicated to [Moorland Haunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt) activations.


...And yes, [Moorland Haunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt)'s spawn also fly.

**How Does Geist Aggro Work?**


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_craig.jpg)*Craig Wescoe*  
Craig's deck is an update on the White-Blue Humans archetype we have looked at a couple of times over the course of the season. A core White Weenie deck, Craig's plays fast drops and [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure) to pump them up.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Honor of the Pure | Doomed Traveler |

Some of those drops are extremely annoying, like [Doomed Traveler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomed+Traveler), which is basically [Liliana of the Veil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil)'s worst nightmare (at least at one mana). Others, like three-drop powerhouses [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader) and [Geist of Saint Traft](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft), are very good and extremely hard hitting... but genuinely frightening when there is an [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure) on the battlefield




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Mirran Crusader | Geist of Saint Traft |

[Geist of Saint Traft](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) isn't just a powerful threat with [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure), it is one of only a few blue assets (even if only half-blue) that Craig used to give his Humans / White Weenie a bit of panache and play. The other main one is [Mana Leak](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) (four total, after sideboards... a problem especially for defensive sorceries, but "all kinds of stuff") and the ubiquitous [Moorland Haunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt). [Moorland Haunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt) is simply one of the defining cards of this era, a great way for creature decks to recover card advantage in the face of sweepers and a way for them to win attrition wars and creature stalemates with other creature decks. As has already been noted, [Moorland Haunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt) tokens fly, and moreover—where this deck is concerned—they also get pumped up like Governator Ahnold by [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure).


For these relatively few blue effects, Craig ran the full set of eight white-blue dual lands and even a basic [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)!




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Oblivion Ring | Dismember |

While Craig ran the once-again-ubiquitous [Oblivion Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) and brought sexy back with the once-ubiquitous (but recently not so much) [Dismember](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember), one of the coolest wrinkles in his deck list is creature-based creature control. [Gideon's Lawkeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon%27s+Lawkeeper) is a redundant one-drop, and [Fiend Hunter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiend+Hunter) is kind of like an undercosted [Faceless Butcher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faceless+Butcher). Both work beautifully in the context of this mostly Human deck.

Richard Bland played a different white beatdown deck. [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader) and [Hero of Bladehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold) he had... but no [Moorland Haunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt)s. Instead, Bland harnessed the power of [Gavony Township](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township) in a Green-White Token Town build packed with Pilgrims, Paradise, planeswalkers, and punching power.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Hero of Bladehold | Gavony Township |

Bland's Green-White has tons of mana: 25 lands plus 4 [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) plus 4 [Avacyn's Pilgrim](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacyn%27s+Pilgrim). The accelerators can help him hit second-turn [Blade Splicer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blade+Splicer)s and [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader)s, or ramp up to the expensive bomb portion of his deck (planeswalkers or [Geist-Honored Monk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist-Honored+Monk)).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_geistWithTokens.jpg)  
A great many of Bland's cards produce tokens—the planeswalkers, obviously, and many of the creatures (Splicer, Monk, Hero)—but it is really studly that his *removal* does, too (hello, [Mortarpod](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortarpod)!). Interestingly, he chose not to play [Overrun](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) even with all those token creatures, instead opting for the off-mana (free, really) [Gut Shot](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gut+Shot) as a Phyrexian two-of.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td171_splickerWithTokens.jpg)  
Bland's acceleration and quality threats made it all the way to the finals before giving up the Worlds title to eventual winner Junya Iyanaga.

![Stromkirk Noble](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Stromkirk+Noble)  
Your opponent plays like a [Drowned Catacomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drowned+Catacomb)s or something, muttering quietly. You get in with the Noble and play [Stormblood Berserker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormblood+Berserker), you know, just like the script said you should. Your opponent plays a blocker and seems very happy about it.


Now you [Volt Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volt+Charge).


![Volt Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Volt+Charge)

But you [Volt Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volt+Charge). *Before* rumbling completes, your [Stromkirk Noble](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromkirk+Noble) is a 3/3 and your [Stormblood Berserker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormblood+Berserker) is a whopping 4/4!
