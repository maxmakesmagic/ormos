### PERILOUS RESEARCH


Standard and World Magic Cup Qualifiers!
========================================



![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)

August 21, 2014
 







Welcome back to Perilous Research! [Last week](http://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/pr/last-chances-making-them-count-2014-08-14), we took a look at the PTQ-winning Modern decks from the last few weeks in an effort to secure a win at one of the final PTQs of the season. Now we'll be shifting focus to Standard in preparation for the World *Magic* Cup Qualifiers that will be taking place in the coming weeks. I'd like to specifically focus on a deck that recently caught my attention. There's been whispering about a new black-green deck with the potential to be format defining. Last weekend, this mystery black-green monstrosity inundated World *Magic* Cup Qualifier Top 8s around the world. What's this newfangled tier 1? Let's check it out!


When approaching a newer format, even when there's already been a Pro Tour, it's important to recognize what the most powerful cards in the format are. In today's Standard, [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix), [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize), [Pack Rat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat), and [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) seem like the cards that have the biggest effect on games for their respective casting costs. It's easy to play [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) and [Pack Rat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat) together, but it's nearly impossible to cast these cards alongside [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation). The addition of [Llanowar Wastes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Wastes) from *Magic 2015* makes casting [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix) a lot less of a chore, though. And, suddenly, there's a new line we can take, where we get to play with [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix), [Pack Rat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat), and [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) at the same time.

This possibility was recognized by Japanese player Kazuaki Fujimura. Fujimura brought the deck to [Pro Tour *Magic 2015*](http://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/ptm15) and put up an impressive Constructed run. His drafts didn't work out as well and he didn't make Top 8, so his decklist risked fading into obscurity, which it did for the next two weeks. Then there was a quiet rumbling under the mountain and it was clear that a dragon had been awakened.


[Nissa, Worldwaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nissa%2C+Worldwaker) is quickly working her way into the Standard canon as one of the defining cards of the format. Black midrange strategies have been playing white for [Blood Baron of Vizkopa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Baron+of+Vizkopa) and [Obzedat, Ghost Council](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council) in an attempt to upgrade their [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gray+Merchant+of+Asphodel) into something that's better in more matchups. [Nissa, Worldwaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nissa%2C+Worldwaker) is about the best upgrade one could ask for if we're looking for a worthwhile five-mana spell for the archetype. [Nissa, Worldwaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nissa%2C+Worldwaker) is improved by the fact that black decks are very good at forcing opponents to tap their mana on specifically desired turns. For example, if we have access to five mana, we can cast [Pack Rat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat) and pass the turn with three mana open. We can simply make another Rat and go for the kill if the opponent doesn't interact with it, but in the situations where the opponent taps all his or her lands for [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict), we can untap and take over the game with [Nissa, Worldwaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nissa%2C+Worldwaker).


The most important addition the deck gets by committing strongly to green cards is [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix). The card is exactly what the Black Devotion deck was looking for. The current format seems to be dominated by [Rabble Red](http://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/united-states-wmcq-winning-decklist-2014-08-18) strategies, and [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix) is the best possible creature against those decks. Additionally, as a Black Devotion deck, we can fill our sideboard with [Pharika's Cure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pharika%27s+Cure) and [Drown in Sorrow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drown+in+Sorrow) to further push that match up in our favor.




[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Nissa, Worldwaker)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nissa, Worldwaker)


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Courser of Kruphix)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser of Kruphix)





The rest of the deck is exactly what you would expect from a midrange black strategy. Slower cards like [Underworld Connections](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Connections) have been cut, not that the deck has more velocity with mana acceleration and more complex threats like [Nissa, Worldwaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nissa%2C+Worldwaker). The deck still has access to four [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)es and four [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize)s in post-boarded games, so there's really no way to attack it efficiently if we're looking to push our win percentage above 60% against it.

The recent success of burn leads us to want a fourth copy of [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix) in our black-green main deck.

White-Black Midrange is the deck to beat in Standard. [ChannelFireball's The Pantheon](http://www.channelfireball.com/team-pantheon/) used the deck to great success at Pro Tour *Magic 2015*, and it doesn't seem like the deck will be going anywhere. Against this strategy, we want our deck to be as aggressive as possible. [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie) is very important on the turn before our opponent has access to mana for [Blood Baron of Vizkopa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Baron+of+Vizkopa). The deck has answers for everything, so it's important that we use our cards aggressively here. The opponent also has [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie), so it's usually correct to try to empty our hand of green creatures as quickly as possible before employing our own [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie) to try to get the opposing [Blood Baron of Vizkopa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Baron+of+Vizkopa) out of his or her hand.
