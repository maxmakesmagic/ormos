Blur
====



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on March 17, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






There are a lot of different ways to tell the story.

Affinity was an unbeatable *Mirrodin* Block deck, and so dominating in Standard that it not only took all eight spots in many a Regional Championships, but many of its components were later banned in that format ([Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager), [Disciple of the Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault), and even [Ancient Den](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Den), which had never hurt anyone) and others ([Æther Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial)). But at the time of the Extended-format [Pro Tour Columbus](/en/events/coverage/canali-crushes-columbus), it was just a deck that everyone knew was going to be one of the best possible options.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td134_ravager.jpg)  
[Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor) was legal.

[Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux) was legal.

You could play one [Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux) and find it with the [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)s you were playing already.

![Energy Flux](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Energy+Flux)  
So not long afterward everyone had [Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux)es. There were so many [Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux)es that it wasn't fun to try to play Affinity any more. So no more Affinity in the gauntlet; gauntlets. End of story, right?

Not even close. The [Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux)es left the sideboards! I mean, no one could possibly play Affinity. No one in his right mind, right?

... That's what everyone thought; almost everyone, anyway. It was like there was one big, giant, back office handshake. "Pinky swear that no one plays Affinity. Let's all play awful versions of The Rock, instead!"

Um. That's not what happened *at all.* Sure, there were tons and tons of terrible The Rock decks (none in the Top 8 though)... but rookie sensation Pierre Canali tore through the Pro Tour with his Affinity deck. It was Pierre's first Pro Tour, if memory serves. He had a good version of one of the best strategies, and defied the prevailing forces of [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed) at every turn. Canali had not yet acquired his PT win (obviously) or Rookie of the Year titles, but I, personally, was pretty sure he was going to win the Pro Tour when I saw him put about a googol modular counters onto one dude and smash through for lethal... with an [Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux) on the battlefield.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td134_counters.jpg)  
"You can't really be that surprised," Randy Buehler said from The Booth(tm). "I mean Affinity *is* the best deck in Standard."

We have seen considerable format crossover in recent weeks. White-Blue Caw-Blade was the best deck in Standard at [Pro Tour Paris](/en/events/coverage/ptparis11), then the same kind of deck started to perform in Extended. Why not its opposite number from the Pro Tour final table?

Nelson's deck has elements of both today's red-white deck and last year's Boros Bushwhacker. For those of you who don't quite remember that version of Red-White Beatdown, [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) can get a whole bunch of different one-drops: [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide), the formidable [Steppe Lynx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx), old favorite [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny)... and of course the singleton [Goblin Bushwhacker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker).



|  |  |

The [Goblin Bushwhacker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker) is an awesome beater, especially when the opponent is tapped for a [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) or similar. Every single [Plated Geopede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede) and [Steppe Lynx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx) will hit very hard—and immediately—so that you can play a bit like a combo deck.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td134_attacking.jpg)  
Nelson's deck is a mix of old and new. In addition to last year's Boros deck, he has a lot of this year's favorite cards. [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) can find the solo [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine), or marry [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage) to [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar).

All in all, a nice surprise out of the team of Red-White Aggro.

Speaking of an old strategy that you might not have expected out of Extended, Robert Withrow brought back White Weenie with little word or warning:

We haven't seen a deck like this in Extended... maybe in *years.* Even the performing White Weenie deck from Paul Rietzl's [Pro Tour Amsterdam](/en/events/coverage/ptamd10) was differentiated by its speed and [Steppe Lynx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx)es. Withrow's deck is a stone's throw from a Block Kithkin deck. Of its twenty-four creatures, eighteen are Kithkin, four are Kithkin-sympathizing [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger)s, and only [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) (who, can you know, find more and more Kithkin) is a legitimate off-brand beater.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Goldmeadow Stalwart | Cloudgoat Ranger |

The deck has some additional old favorites: for instance, [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) + [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights). [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) isn't as dramatic as it is in the Green-White [Summoning Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Summoning+Trap) deck, but it can still represent a savings against a [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger) (or just an extra card).



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Spectral Procession | Windbrisk Heights |

White Weenie—especially White Weenie with a ton of [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender)s—isn't what we have been conditioned to expect so far in the format, but it can certainly be strong in the metagame... especially if Red Decks rise and blur the line between Standard and Extended metagames as have some other builds.

The Wisconsin PTQ that showcased the above Boros and Kithkin decks also featured two essentially identical RDW decks.

Blue-centered [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle) decks still need [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) on the battlefield but are otherwise still awfully good at winning. The combination of [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder)s, [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain)s, green acceleration, and one-card combo wins via [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) is, in sum, still awfully good. [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) solves most problems, and the deck's toolbox including everything from [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) to [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command) can fill in a lot of holes.

It's nice to see a five-color deck back in Top 8 position.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td134_5Colors.jpg)  
At the beginning of the season, starting from Day Three of Pro Tour Paris, various builds of four- and five-color control were popular among the best players on Tour. We saw everything from [Sunblast Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunblast+Angel) to [Nicol Bolas, Planeswalker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nicol+Bolas%2C+Planeswalker) defining different builds. Gleicher's is pretty interesting.

Though it has the mana base and overall trappings of a Five-Color Control deck (starts on Vivid lands and [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool)s and plays everything up to and including [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum)), David battled with planeswalkers aplenty: [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant); [Elspeth, Knight-Errant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant); [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor); and even [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura)! This is a deck that seems like a five-color but probably plays like a Red-White-Blue Planeswalkers deck from circa last summer.

Zach Jesse ran what looks like the greatest hits of blue and red, with [Creeping Tar Pit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Tar+Pit) and [Crumbling Necropolis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crumbling+Necropolis) in the mana mix primarily for sideboard cards ([Countersquall](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Countersquall), [Anathemancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anathemancer)).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td134_demi.jpg)  
Yes, this deck has a lot of cool stuff going on. [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) in a control deck, big burn next to [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s... and a [Time Warp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp)! But I can't help but feel like some of it looks incomplete. It is a little surprising to see these colors, the potential to hit seven mana, and [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)... but no [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum). Even just one [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite)? You instinctively scour the deck list for a [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), but find neither.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Cruel Ultimatum | Time Warp |

Of course there is no reason you *have* to play [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) or [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin). The cards overall are very good, and [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) is probably even better in a deck that can draw into re-buys with Jace or [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) instead of just topdecking and crossing its fingers.

In addition to an upgrade to dual land (at least for the purposes of braining an opposing [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)) the biggest plus sign that the black deck has for it is [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek). In addition to giving the deck something good to do, early, [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek) is a superb long-game hold. You can keep it in your hand until you need to resolve something (checking for countermagic) or before you want to start attacking Jace (to check if the opponent has some kind of removal for your [Creeping Tar Pit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Tar+Pit)).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td134_inquisition.jpg)  
At the same time (excepting the loss of [Tectonic Edge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge)), the White-Blue-Black Caw-Blade deck, like the Red-White-Blue version, can do most everything that the original awesome deck did. While losing the Edge (and usually a goodly number of counterspells) can spell trouble against [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle), the black version can borrow [Memoricide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memoricide) to gobble up [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) a turn or two before the opponent can cast them.

Black looks to be the Caw-Blade build of choice at the moment—and given its Top 8 performance in the hands of a player of the caliber of Gerry Thompson, it is probably one that you should be aware of, if you are in fact planning to prepare for Standard.

If you are like me, you must be loving the ever-changing face of these metagames, across the different formats. Players jockeying for an edge while staying with an engine or deck philosophy they know works (White-Blue Caw-Blade to Red-White-Blue CawBlade to White-Blue-Black and so on)... and you can flip over to Extended and see from the dominance of Faeries, to the rise of White-Blue Caw-Blade (there too), to a PTQ Top 8 like Madison's where we see neither deck. Tons of opportunity, tons of chances to customize your deck and even "go your own way."

One thing's for sure: If you're preparing for Standard or Extended right now... You won't get bored.
