The Future Future is Now
========================



 Posted in **Latest Developments**
 on May 15, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/tom.jpg)
By Tom LaPille




Tom LaPille makes things. Some of the things he makes are card sets, like Dark Ascension and Born of the Gods. Sometimes he makes stories, too. Sometimes he makes unexpected things, like 16th-century Japanese clothing. He's probably making something right now. 







M**agic** developers care a lot about Standard Constructed. It is the most popular tournament format, and it's very important to us that the experience it provides be fun. The Future Future League is the name we have given to the format that we use to test Standard internally. At any given time, the Future Future League contains all the sets that will be in real-world Standard at the same time as the set that is currently in development. This means that at any given time the Future Future League is many months ahead of the real world. Today I'll talk about some decks I played while *Alara Reborn* was the newest set in the Future Future League.


During *Alara Reborn* development, I was still very new to **Magic** R&D. I spent much of the set's development cycle as more of a playtester than a developer, and for a few months I was building decks very quickly, often at a rate of a deck or more per day. Because of this, the decks I was playing were not often well tuned. However, the goal of early playtesting is to tell if cards are fun, not to produce tuned lists. Three extra games playing with a new card is more valuable to us than an hour spent worrying about the last two slots in a deck built around it, especially when the card might change at any moment.


The four decks I present today are very untuned. They are exactly the kinds of decks we make when we need to see how a card plays but don't have a ton of time to spend on making them perfect. This is, of course, not the only kind of Future Future League deck we make. We hold tournaments in which the decks we play are much more refined, and my tournament decks are much stronger than these. However, many more of the decks we make are motivated by what needs to be tested, and we need to test efficiently. Let's begin!


### [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain)


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/ld/ld38_sigilSplash.jpg)One reason that decks are built inside **Magic** R&D is that a playtester or developer identifies a card that they think is going to be fun for them to play with. This is exactly what happened the first time I met [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain). The card's Multiverse comments field was empty and it was hard to get a Limited deck that used it to its full potential, but I saw that there was some fun to be had. This deck was built to test [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain), so it has tons and tons of 1/1 creatures. [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger) and [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) each produce three tokens, and Elspeth creates one every turn. Also, [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny) and [Goldmeadow Harrier](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goldmeadow+Harrier) are naturally 1/1s on their own. I also included plenty of ways to leverage tokens other than [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain). [Knotvine Paladin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knotvine+Paladin) gets very large when he attacks while your tokens stay home, and [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) makes all of the tokens huge.

This deck was tons of fun. It's really fun to curve [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain) into [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger), which produces a lot of 3/3s. [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) produces one fewer 3/3, but three flying 3/3 creatures is quite a clock. There were also some amusing moments that happened thanks to the rules governing power and toughness setting. Power and toughness determining effects like [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) and [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny) apply before the bonuses given by +1/+1 counters, so it works with [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) the way you want it to. I had never controlled a 10/10 [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny) before, but thanks to two +1/+1 counters I can now say that I have. The interaction between [Knotvine Paladin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knotvine+Paladin) and the tokens was also lots of fun.


In hindsight, I think this deck probably wants a twenty-fifth land. It ramps up to five-mana spells, and [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) helps guard against mana flood. Also, [Goldmeadow Harrier](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goldmeadow+Harrier) likely should have just been [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring). However, details like that aren't that important when the goal is just to get a good sense of how fun a new card is. I knew after ten or so games with this deck that [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain) was fun, and I kept playing longer than that because of how much fun it was. At the end of the day I left a Multiverse comment on both [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain) and [Knotvine Paladin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knotvine+Paladin), and [Sigil Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Captain) made it to print unchanged.


### [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan)


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/ld/ld38_nulltreadSplash.jpg)Sometimes the lead developer of a set specifically requests that a card be tested with a specific subset of cards from earlier sets. In that case, a playtester must build a deck using the requested cards, whether or not he or she thinks the card in question is fun or powerful. The goal of these decks' construction is to discover how fun or powerful new cards actually are, and to identify changes that make them more fun and more appropriately powered.


In the case of [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan), someone remarked in a playtester meeting that no one had built a deck that used *Alara Reborn*'s green-blue cards along with cards from *Eventide*. Also, [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan)'s drawback was dissuading playtesters from using it in decks at all, so no one really knew how powerful it really was. I volunteered to build a green-blue deck with [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan) and started brewing. Here's what I made.

[Favor of the Overbeing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Favor+of+the+Overbeing) and [Shorecrasher Mimic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shorecrasher+Mimic) both asked for lots of green-blue cards, so I went to [Snakeform](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snakeform) and [Wistful Selkie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wistful+Selkie). I had an absolutely miserable afternoon playing with [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan). Over and over, I would play it on turn two and have it send back my first-turn mana creature only to see it die to [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw) or [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror). The card excelled most in play against red decks that could not easily deal with its sizable body, but once they learned that [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan)s were lurking in the mists they simply killed every creature I played so that I couldn't play it. Worse, they sometimes allowed me to keep exactly one creature but left mana open, threatening that if I did play a [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan) they would simply kill it in response and leave my Gargantuan to its death. Whatever [Nulltread Gargantuan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nulltread+Gargantuan) was, it wasn't overpowered.

This is a little bit of a goofball deck. [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara) demanded a toolbox of auras. [Elder Mastery](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elder+Mastery) was the best Aura I could find, but [Epic Proportions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Epic+Proportions) served as a backup Aura that I could both search for if I drew the [Elder Mastery](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elder+Mastery) and actually cast and [Lure](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lure) was there for goofy situations and because it looked fun. The rest of the deck was basically random cheap and efficient cards with the exception of a single [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) that I threw in for kicks.

I had a lot of fun with Sovereigns in this deck. I did not play it for very long because we already had the card somewhere we liked, so I never got the chance to tutor for anything other than the [Elder Mastery](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elder+Mastery). However, I did play two memorable games against playtester Mons Johnson in which I first defeated him with a surprise [Epic Proportions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Epic+Proportions) on a double-striking lone attacker, then by casting [Lure](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lure) on [Knotvine Paladin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knotvine+Paladin) to eat all of his creatures. I also almost managed to put together [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour), Rafiq, and Elspeth in one perfect attack, but my Rafiq died before I could declare attackers. That was a sad moment.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/ld/ld38_3CardsAcross.jpg)The Future Future League is a strange place for a **Magic** player to live. On the one hand, the cards in it are all well on their way to print. On the other hand, the cards that are the most important for us to test can change under a playtester's feet at any moment. Every day we use those cards to make decks much like these and play them against each other, and it is these decks that help us make our sets as fun as they can be.
