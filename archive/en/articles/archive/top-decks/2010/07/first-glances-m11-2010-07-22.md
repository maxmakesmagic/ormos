First Glances at M11
====================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on July 22, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Are you ready for your first big tournament featuring the all-new (or all-returned, new) cards from ***Magic** 2011*?


After reading this article you will ... well, you *might* be, let's say. There have been some cool implementations of the new (and "comeback") cards, and in some cases spawning new, different, and expansively diverse archetypes. So don't be surprised when a Mythic Conscription opponent can suddenly defend himself, or crumple—*too* depressed—when the [Flashfreeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flashfreeze) you've been sandbagging doesn't actually stop Jund's six-drop.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td100_wooden.jpg)  

  
  
 "*Failure to prepare is preparing to fail*."   
 —Attributed to Coach John Wooden


M11 has come, so far, in two flavors: New cards enhancing existing decks, and new cards spawning brand new ones. Let's look at some of the first group first.


### Mana Leak


These first two decks come from an Amsterdam Pro Tour Qualifier held in Chiba, Japan.

Pro Tour Qualifier winner Sakurai Taiki enhanced his Mythic Conscription deck with what will probably be the most played M11 card overall, [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak); additionally, he deftly navigated the shifting metagame by making his creature deck more resistant to [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) itself.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td100_manaLeak.jpg)  
How does [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) change Mythic Conscription?


[Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) was already a popular sideboard card in this archetype, particularly against [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment). The infusion of [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) in the format gave Taiki license to not only adopt that card (a no-brainer for many decks playing blue), but he gave his permission some redundancy by moving [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) to the main. 




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Mana Leak | Negate |

Make no mistake, the tools and the card choices look similar, but this is a completely different deck. Rather than *just* going for an explosive opening with [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) (possibly leading into a fast kill via [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara) fueling [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription)), Taiki's deck can actually *hold* a lead after he has gotten one. Traditional Mythic and Mythic Conscription stacks are typically accelerated "[Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker)" decks playing the beatdown role; this one is more of a Counter-Sliver; again giving it a better perch on the metagame clock against control. If there were a combo deck in the format, Taiki's deck would probably perform better against it than a regular old Mythic.


We mentioned that Taiki's deck not only plays [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), but seems more prepared for [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) itself. 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td100_trap.jpg)  
Note those three [Summoning Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Summoning+Trap)s. When he goes for a [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) second turn and the opponent smugly shows him a brand-spanking-new [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), Sakurai might just freebie out that [Summoning Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Summoning+Trap).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td100_3Cards1.jpg)  
... And if that means that opponent is facing down a turn-two [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel)—or for Akroma's sake a [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara)—it just is what it is; know what I'm saying?

The PTQ finalist was also a [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) miser.


With this deck we see essentially a wholesale return to the permission-based White-Blue decks from the Tap-Out / Planeswalker builds we have been used to for the past several months. Does Junichiro's deck ultimately win with planeswalkers? Yes. But the process of getting into a winning position is quite different.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td100_3Cards2.jpg)  
A permission deck will be far stronger against resisting "must-counter" threats like [Mind Sludge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sludge) on five, [Identity Crisis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Identity+Crisis) on six, and [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) on seven ... but all things considered, be an inferior defender against first turn [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide).


Especially given his complete lack of non-[Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) creatures, I find Junichiro's paucity of [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) effects puzzling (only two copies of [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment), no [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup)s at all), but [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) seems to be giving him some additional room.

Siira's deck is extraordinarily disruptive. Yes, he added [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) like almost every other blue mage. But in addition we have [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek), and, conditionally, the great [Esper Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Charm)!


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td100_3Cards3.jpg)  
So what did he cut?


Well there is not one [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) in either the starting sixty or the sideboard. Even more unusual: All manner of board-sweepers—a combination of not just [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) but [All Is Dust](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=All+Is+Dust), too—are in the sideboard. 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td100_graveTitan.jpg)  
Maybe that shouldn't be surprising ... Siira played a ton of creatures. And headlining those creatures was [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan)!


![Grave Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Grave+Titan)
  
Big, huge, 10 power to start; supplementing only a pair of main-deck [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel)s in this case, but a ton of walls; Siira used extra Walls where most players have sweep.


Speaking of [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan), the bad guys have also adopted M11's biggest threat.

This is a nice implementation of [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman) as a toolbox + card advantage engine. Obviously the deck can start dumping [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s with the ability to re-buy them at any point for free cards. Subtly, Robert could also go and get a [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos), which is basically like tutoring for a 3/2 [Concentrate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Concentrate) (or more, with [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)).

[Sphinx of Jwar Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx+of+Jwar+Isle) doesn't really hold up as a primary fighting flyer in the grand scheme of the format (it's terrible heads-up against a [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel), and given its cost is easily answered by any control deck); but playing the big six as a singleton in a [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman) deck is quite different than where it has been played in the past. To wit, Robert has a much lower chance of *drawing* the thing ... but if he wants a next-to-impossible-to-remove blocker (and eventually threat) aginst Jund ... he's got it.

Rather than going for [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle)s to kill the opponent, Shougo used his to set up—and recover from—what might be the most powerful, game-state-altering sorcery since [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum): [Destructive Force](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Destructive+Force).


I like the addition of blue, and in fact, I think [Frost Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) is a very appropriate supplement in this kind of a deck. [Frost Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) can help keep a creature down, and, along with [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), can help *resolve* [Destructive Force](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Destructive+Force) in the first place.

Instead of blue, we have white for [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant). Ajani can *also* keep a creature down and disrupt mana to get [Destructive Force](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Destructive+Force) to resolve. Playing white also gave Berg a full eight-pack of man-lands to go and get with his [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)s and vastly superior creature defense via [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment), [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens), and [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn).


And *whew.*


I'll say this to anyone preparing for a PTQ, Nationals, or other important Standard tournament this weekend: Be prepared for [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak). It's showing up in everything from the obvious control decks (white-blue, white-blue-black, and so on), to the beatdown decks—even main deck—as well as the big mana decks ... you have to admit there is something a little bit saucy about a ramp deck that can force through its [Wildfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire) upgrade with a [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)!
