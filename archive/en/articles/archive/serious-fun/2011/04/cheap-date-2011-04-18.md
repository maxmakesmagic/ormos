Cheap Date
==========



 Posted in **Serious Fun**
 on April 19, 2011 










First, let me take a deep, soothing breath. I have a tendency to be pretty intense about things, and I don't want to overwhelm you. So here goes: WELCOME TO COMMON WEEK!

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/sf/sf139_possibleImage.jpg)  
I don't know if there's a way to set fireworks and cannons off in Internet browsers, but I hope the all-capital lettering conveys how I feel about our most conspicuous of card rarities. I *love* commons. While there will always be room in my heart for the other cards in **Magic**, it's commons that I put at the very top of the pyramid.

Cards like [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine), [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker), [Doubling Season](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doubling+Season), [Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction), and [Sol Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sol+Ring) that always seem to reside at the top of "What is your favorite card?" response lists, but I'm here today to tell you to throw that to the wind. Scrap the swords. [Rootgrapple](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rootgrapple) the planeswalkers.

Drop a [Rare-B-Gone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rare-B-Gone) onto your world. All those pretty silver, gold, and cupric orange cards you grab first can be left behind for a little while.

Hold on; we're going into the world of Pauper **Magic**.

Say Hello to my Little Friends
Playing **Magic** with only commons—known affectionately as Pauper **Magic**—isn't a new concept. Like many of the ways to play today, it's one that has its roots in the ingenuity and exploration of players in **Magic**'s dark ages. What matters is that whatever the mode—on **Magic Online** or **Magic** on paper—creating and playing with commons is, actually, pretty common.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/sf/sf139_manaMorph.jpg)  
While card availability can certainly play a factor in the decision to go this route, it's a genuinely exciting and refreshing way to approach things. Even without the powerful cards and overarching effects that exist only in higher rarities, **Magic** still thrives.

While I've [shared](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/sf/64) that I'm a fan of "Peasant **Magic**," with rares and mythic rares excluded, it's really the commons that I find most interesting. In fact, I even built a Cube of all commons, which I [shared some game play](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/sf/80) from in this space, and you can [read a lot more](http://thepaupercube.wordpress.com) about building and shaping it if that sort of thing is interesting to you.

In that brief time I considered "cheating" and running a redo on what makes my Cube fun, I stumbled onto something a much more interesting: *Masters Edition III*. There was a little feature about the set that slapped me in the face and woke me up to something awesome.

Legendary creatures were included [as commons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?action=advancedamp;set=+%5B%22Masters%20Edition%20III%22%5Damp;type=+%5B%22Creature%22%5D+%5B%22Legendary%22%5Damp;rarity=+%5BC%5D) (something Tom LaPille [went into some details](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/ld/53) about).

As an adamant and vocal EDH [Commander](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/feature/119k) player and adamant and vocal supporter (for those [following me on Twitter](http://twitter.com/the_stybs) at least) of Pauper **Magic**, it was like something extraordinary happened.

Peanut butter, meet chocolate. Chocolate, peanut butter. *Serendipity.*

Leave the Rares. Take the Commons.
The vague recognition that, yes, common legendary creatures were in there didn't really register until I took that idea to Commander. In a world where the lists of awesome cards are dominated by the higher rarities I saw visions of commons flashing before my eyes. Taking the variant format I love most (Commander) and mixing it with the deck restriction I enjoy best (Pauper) looked awesome.

What makes this so exciting is that prior to *Master's Edition III* there were no common legendary creatures (and for Pauper, as long as something has been printed at common once, it's fair game).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/sf/sf139_banner1.jpg)  
[Jedit Ojanen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jedit+Ojanen) and [Tobias Andrion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tobias+Andrion) are the white and blue representatives provided for us, a classic color pair that usually plays for a longer game than not. However, without the usual tools that let a deck do that (namely, [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) effects), we can see a different side of the pair: action.

Creatures like [Esper Cormorants](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Cormorants) and [Deft Duelist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deft+Duelist) are great on the offense, with [Silver Drake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silver+Drake) and [Talon Trooper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Talon+Trooper) joining in with flying for the fight. To help out, there are a few uniquely white and blue cards that take care of creatures on the other side: [Curse of Chains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curse+of+Chains), [Minister of Impediments](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Minister+of+Impediments), [Plumes of Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plumes+of+Peace), and [Silkbind Faerie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silkbind+Faerie).

You can choose either of the legendary options to be the commander, but the goal of the deck is pretty straightforward: *swing*. With an array of flying critters, ways to keep them from being blocked, equipment to pump them up, and sticking towards the cheaper end of things you should have few issues getting your attacking groove on.

There are, of course, things that can be useful if the games become protracted:


> * [Arcane Spyglass](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcane+Spyglass) can convert extra lands you play into drawing more cards.
> * [Whispersilk Cloak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whispersilk+Cloak) can make any creature into an unblockable killer.
> * [Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead) can turn your Commander into a real animal.

Like many black and red creatures, having higher power than toughness makes them more like glass cannons than reusable juggernauts of aggression. While the white-blue deck above is aggressive, *this* will tear faces off. Eschewing the usual red route of burn spells—aside from the classic [Kaervek's Torch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kaervek%27s+Torch) and *Tempest* gem [Rolling Thunder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rolling+Thunder)—this deck aims to throw creatures around like candy.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Creatures with haste can do some surprising things, from catching players off-guard with an attack they weren't expecting to completely changing the amount of damage that's possible in a given turn. A veritable bonanza of creatures with haste exist in red, and for a slightly higher price-per-play on average you get to come out firing every turn.

Black is no slouch either, supplementing red's all-out attacks with a combination of removal (the whole [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror) clan and the like) as well as something other colors don't get a lot of: creature recursion. It's easy to understand how powerful [Living Death](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Death) can be, but once the mana is situated, cards like [Death Denied](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Denied) and [Grim Harvest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Harvest) become machines. And the impressive [Death Denied](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Denied) is like a reverse [Rolling Thunder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rolling+Thunder) just for you.
