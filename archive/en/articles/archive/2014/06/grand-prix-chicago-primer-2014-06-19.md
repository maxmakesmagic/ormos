June 19, 2014
 







Welcome back to Perilous Research, DailyMTG.com's exclusive *Magic Online* column!


This weekend, thousands of *Magic* players will be taking a break from cracking Moxes on *Magic Online* to compete with their Standard weapon of choice at Grand Prix Chicago. Today, we'll be taking a look at the most recent Standard results from *Magic Online* in an effort to ready ourselves for the big event.


Trending


We've seen a number of trends emerge online recently.


For example, over the last few weeks, we've seen the decline of control strategies in Standard. Azorius and Esper Control decks still exist in large numbers, but their performances have been less than stellar, especially in last week's *Magic Online* events.


White-Black Midrange seems to be the most successful deck right now. The deck matches up well against other Black Devotion strategies and the control decks.


Jund Monsters attacks the format beautifully at the moment. The deck may be a bit weak to the control strategies, but even those matchups are close. In particular, [Xenagos, the Reveler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xenagos%2C+the+Reveler) is great against the control decks and absolutely extraordinary against Black Devotion strategies and White-Black Midrange.


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/pr/2014/pr_wk24_xenegosthereveller.png)

Red decks of all shapes and sizes are coming out of the woodwork. Four distinct Red strategies have been performing well on *Magic Online* over the last week.


The decline of control also opens up the door for Blue Devotion to reenter the format and dominate the creature-based decks.


Setting the Standard


We can expect Grand Prix Chicago to offer up a diverse and exciting Standard format. Let's take a look at what the major archetypes will look like this coming weekend:

Jund Monsters has become a format staple. The deck combines mana acceleration with powerful threats and the format's best removal spells. [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix) helps stabilize the board while providing a steady stream of card advantage and lifegain. [Polukranos, World Eater](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polukranos%2C+World+Eater) does significant work against Blue Devotion and other aggressive creature strategies while serving as a healthy 5/5 for four mana with occasional upside in the other matchups. [Scavenging Ooze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scavenging+Ooze) punishes the Black-Green graveyard decks, outclasses other creatures very quickly, and gets the deck out of burn range. The deck is very good at forcing enemy control decks to have a [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict). [Stormbreath Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormbreath+Dragon) applies quick pressure after a board sweeper in control matchups and essentially forces the opponent to have a second copy of [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict). [Xenagos, the Reveler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xenagos%2C+the+Reveler) is the best possible threat against Black Devotion strategies and the control decks, providing a constant stream of pressure that requires multiple cards and oftentimes multiple turns from the opponent.

White-Black Midrange was initially adopted as a version of Black Devotion that was well-positioned in the mirror. Since then, the deck has evolved to include cards like [Obzedat, Ghost Council](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council). The deck is slow and is susceptible to being run over by the format's most aggressive strategies. However, especially on the play, the deck can chain two spot-removal spells, one after the other, to set up a chain of big spells.

Junk Midrange offers up great disruption, threats, removal, and one of the format's best card-advantage engines. The deck's versatility and high power level mean that it has no truly bad matchups, especially after sideboarding in [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie)s. [Blood Baron of Vizkopa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Baron+of+Vizkopa) is the best card against the Junk Midrange deck. As a result, this deck may not be very well-positioned in a field with a lot of White-Black. Still, Blood Baron can be [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize)d or [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie)d. The deck could make a big splash in Chicago this weekend if it's able to dodge the Blood Barons off the top of people's decks.

Selesnya Aggro applies huge pressure very quickly and is often able to punch through the slightly bigger creature decks with cards like [Banishing Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banishing+Light) and [Selesnya Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Selesnya+Charm). The deck has a lot of bad matchups, but it's able to capitalize on the smallest stumble from the opponent and completely run over him or her. I wouldn't recommend running this into a field full of Blue Devotion, but I would encourage Selesnya mages to include some number of [Skylasher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skylasher) and [Unflinching Courage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unflinching+Courage) in their sideboard if they insist on running the archetype.

All of the haste and [Boros Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Charm)s make the deck quite good against control. The deck might struggle with Black Devotion, but many of those players may be moving to White-Black Midrange.

Win conditions are overrated. In fact, the whole point of playing a dedicated control deck is dedicating one's self to controlling the game. Every win condition we draw is another card that doesn't interact with our opponent when it matters. As a control deck, we're winning with [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation). The card advantage accrued off the big spell will put our opponent into a spot where he or she can no longer win the game. [Elixir of Immortality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elixir+of+Immortality) ensures that the deck never runs out of cards while it *revelates* over and over. The major issue with this, the best-positioned control deck, is winning matches on time. We only have so much time to finish a match and winning a single game with this deck will often take well over 30 minutes, even if we're playing fast.

Despite lackluster performances in recent *Magic Online* events, this is still one of the more popular decks in Standard and we should be prepared for it, especially if we don't have any byes.

This deck was initially popularized by Ari Lax and friends before the release of *Journey into Nyx*. The deck aims to fill its graveyard with [Satyr Wayfinder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Satyr+Wayfinder), [Grisly Salvage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grisly+Salvage), and [Commune with the Gods](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Commune+with+the+Gods). A full graveyard powers up [Jarad, Golgari Lich Lord](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jarad%2C+Golgari+Lich+Lord); [Deathrite Shaman](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman); [Nighthowler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nighthowler); [Pharika, God of Affliction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pharika%2C+God+of+Affliction); [Shadowborn Demon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shadowborn+Demon); and [Whip of Erebos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whip+of+Erebos). The deck has become much more streamlined since then, and a lot of people seem to be battling with this particular list. The deck is exceptionally strong against other midrange strategies.
