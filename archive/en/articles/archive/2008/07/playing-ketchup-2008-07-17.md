Playing Ketchup
===============



 Posted in **Feature**
 on July 17, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







Do you mean playing *with* ketchup? 


This was always very confusing for me. I would just think of the little Heinz packets—okay they were probably some kind of non-Heinz generic *catsup* packets—in the cafeteria. You know, next to the little paper packages of salt or pepper for your fries? Do they still have those?


What the teacher meant was that some children were always behind, not paying attention behind their serial killer glasses, whatever, *had to "catch up"* ...playing ketchup.


That's what we're doing this week, not because we weren't paying attention, but more because we were busy paying attention to the near future of *Eventide*. However, *we are still in the middle of a Constructed PTQ season, damn it*, and there are two weeks of results to be analyzed.

What is clear from the last two weeks of PTQ results is that Faeries has gone from a legitimate contender to completely Constructed unplayable. Faeries seems like one of the worst possible choices to play in this metagame!


Certainly you are too clever to consider playing Faeries (its performance being so weak, with so few Top 8 appearances and a commensurately pitiable number of Pro Tour invitations acquired), but in the unlikely case that you might find yourself paired against someone five or six weeks out of date (remote as that possibility may be), you may want to consider some of the recent developments in this nearly forgotten archetype (you know, in the off chance that a black cat crossed some wretch's path and he accidentally found four [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom)s and had no other cards to play)... but probably not.

Main deck, Gavin went to 26 lands, up from the 24 of the early season (unless you count Robert Seder's accidental 28 in Hollywood). Additionally, he made the full leap, cutting all copies of the under-performing [Broken Ambitions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broken+Ambitions), replacing all four with [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize). [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) is superior in the Faeries mirror, allowing the active player to remove [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) from the opponent's opening hand even on the draw; perhaps more importantly, Osyp Lebedowicz points out that this swap is key in Faeries beating the rogue Green-White "Little Kid" deck ([Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) can get rid of [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) before he hits play, whereas [Broken Ambitions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broken+Ambitions) is just another card blanked by [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg)).


Out of the sideboard, Gavin played two different cards that are very effective in the Elementals matchup (Elementals being one of the most successful non-Faeries decks this season):


**[Faerie Trickery](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Trickery)**


Probably just the best option in the format for any blue deck against the card [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark). [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) has not yet been leveraged in an infinite combination in Block (unlike Standard), but it is still a potent source of card advantage, especially with the Faeries deck down in main deck permission; [Faerie Trickery](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Trickery) erases it forever.



![Faerie Trickery](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Faerie+Trickery)

![Puppeteer Clique](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Puppeteer+Clique)

**[Puppeteer Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Puppeteer+Clique)**


Basically the most impressive possible spell against the evoke mechanic. Draw two cards, eh? I'll draw two as well... and thanks for the body! Take 2!


All kidding aside, Faeries was hands-down the most successful deck of the last two weeks, and nothing seems to be in position to challenge it for top spot in *Lorwyn* / *Shadowmoor* Block. There is really only one deck that can claim a legitimate and sizable advantage in the Faeries matchup, with most other decks caught *playing ketchup* against the card advantage of a [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) and the versatile potency of [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command).


We've highlighted Faeries—and more or less *every* article regarding this format has highlighted Faeries—from the beginning... So let's focus on some of the other qualifying and all-around interesting decks from around the Internet.

Like many Doran decks, Hanson's has a nice synergy between [Treefolk Harbinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treefolk+Harbinger) and [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest) (specifically [Murmuring Bosk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murmuring+Bosk), which can help play the challengingly-colored Doran)... and [Treefolk Harbinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treefolk+Harbinger) and Doran itself.

[Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose) is a convenient way to help play [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower), and that two-mana cantrip effectively lifts this Doran to (a virtual) 25 lands.


A lot of players like Doran due to its ability to play lots of different kinds of instant speed targeted removal; [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) remains the gold standard at two mana, working nicely with both [Treefolk Harbinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treefolk+Harbinger) and [Wren's Run Vanquisher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wren%27s+Run+Vanquisher), but [Eyeblight's Ending](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eyeblight%27s+Ending) is the unusual black removal that can nix [Oona, Queen of the Fae](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oona%2C+Queen+of+the+Fae), and in a pinch, [Crib Swap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crib+Swap) crushes any and all.


A card that is seeing more and more play is [Scarblade Elite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scarblade+Elite). In *this* deck it is mostly a small but efficient body, but don't discount the long run ability to extract additional value from [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), [Crib Swap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crib+Swap), and [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) all.

On the subject of wild [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool) decks, check out Christopher Pauly's Five-Color Shamans!

This deck hybridizes many of the various Shamans synergies that we have seen in the past, both from the Red side (a [Rage Forger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rage+Forger) splash) and from the green side (every good green Shaman from [Wolf-Skull Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wolf-Skull+Shaman) to [Leaf-Crowned Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leaf-Crowned+Elder) to [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower)!). More-or-less everything is a Shaman in this deck, save [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher)... but we'll give Pauly a pass on that one seeing how good an instant two point [Hurricane](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurricane) is in the Faeries format. With so many Shamans, [Wolf-Skull Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wolf-Skull+Shaman) and [Leaf-Crowned Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leaf-Crowned+Elder) should have very solid batting averages, making for quite a bit of threat advantage.


Even though this deck is very decidedly "Shaman" in tribal theme, [Primal Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Beyond) has a lot of solid synergy. Elemental or no, it is a great combination with [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool), and several of the Shamans in this deck are either Elementals as well ([Fulminator Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fulminator+Mage)) or changelings ([Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion)), so [Primal Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Beyond) will rarely be stranded as a colorless producer... Anyway, Pauly's deck has nine more Elementals out of the sideboard.


Overall, I think this is my pick for the most exciting new deck.


...Though that title could easily go to Pat Chapin's Witch Solar Flare, a unique contraption if ever there was one in this format:

[Mistmeadow Witch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistmeadow+Witch) can point in either direction, but in a deck like this one, with all kinds of [Mulldrifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter)s, [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw)s, and [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark)s, the potential for card advantage abuse is considerable. Did you notice [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) plus [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower)? That's right, as busty as Doran is in the red zone, this particular Legendary Treefolk Shaman has *zero printed power*.


Playing Doran in this sort of a deck also gives Patrick the excuse to run [Murmuring Bosk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murmuring+Bosk). We think of [Murmuring Bosk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murmuring+Bosk) as a "green" card, but two of the three colors it produces are black and white, two colors both central to the traditional Solar Flare. In this case, green is mostly gravy.

Pro Tour superstar Olivier Ruel racked up yet another Grand Prix Top 8 with a Quick 'n Toast-esque [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool) control deck playing an interesting sixty-first card... [Platinum Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Platinum+Angel)! A pair of [Mind Shatter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Shatter)s started out up front, alongside a pair of [Pact of Negation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation)s. Not a lot of downside to those Pacts with [Platinum Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Platinum+Angel) in play; Ruel liked the seven drop so much, he sideboarded a second.


My favorite deck from the Buenos Aires coverage was the Mono-Red deck played by Tomoharu Saito and Shuuhei Nakamura; both Japanese players finished in the money, albeit out of the Top 8.

![Ashenmoor Gouger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ashenmoor+Gouger)

The defining card of this deck is clearly the [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger). Nakamura said that he would have rather run [Tattermunge Maniac](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tattermunge+Maniac) because the deck didn't have sufficient fast drops, but let's not discount the Gouger. Constructed is notoriously light on blocking, and [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger) is never going to offend from that side of the table. Ever. More importantly, he is invulnerable to [Slaughter Pact](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Pact), [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw), and [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror), and highly resistant to [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion), [Lash Out](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lash+Out), [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout), and [Incinerate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incinerate). Lastly, [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger) is bigger and faster in a red deck than the typical green play, often even up a mana. Combined with its myriad defenses against typical removal, [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger) seems like a fine offensive drop.

The "other" black card in this deck is [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge), probably the best threat in its cycle of highly impressive five-drops. Being black, [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) is resistant to many of the same removal spells, and having 4 toughness makes up a lot of the difference (as with [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger)). Unlike its 4/4 three-drop cousin, the jury is *not* out on [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge). This one just roars and roars until opponents are dead.
