The Other Side of Pro Tour–Berlin
=================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on November 6, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







Let's dial it back a week.


The column, [Expect the Unexpected Tomorrow](/en/articles/archive/top-decks/expect-unexpected-tomorrow-2008-10-30)...


The last line (from Patrick Chapin): "dude, just wait till you see what we brought... :)"


I think we paid off on both fronts.


Kind of.


I was obviously as surprised as most of you when the ElfBall ("Elves!") deck revealed itself to be the deck of the tournament—second-most played as well as locking down three quarters of Pro Tour–Berlin's Top 8—but on the other hand, the unexpected was the rule of the day... and Chapin's team surely earned that smiley emoticon with the unlikely win delivered by former U.S. National Champion Luis Scott-Vargas.


Why unlikely?


Luis met his worst matchup, the dreaded Tezzerator deck, in the first round of the Top 8... and opened up with the even more dreaded 0-2. Had this been a typical best-of-three, LSV would have had to suck it up with "just" the first Pro Tour Top 8 of his career... But it was a Top 8 best-of-five, and he battled through and took the next three games in a row.

 How does the Tezzerator work?


This deck is essentially a [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) deck... It gets its name from the big five-mana version of [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage), which can go and get three-mana artifacts, not just zeros and ones.

[Tezzeret the Seeker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tezzeret+the+Seeker) can find [Ensnaring Bridge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ensnaring+Bridge) to prevent the opponent from attacking. It can grab [Trinisphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinisphere) so as to slow down the acceleration of Storm, Elves!, All-In Red, or combo decks.


And all those [Great Furnace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Great+Furnace)s, [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox)es, and [Aether Spellbomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Aether%2BSpellbomb)s? ... Tezzeret's ultimate ability can just kill the opponent with a single grand swing.

One of the key cards in the Tezzerator—which can be found by the original [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) as well—is [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void). Set it to one? You prevent the dominant Elves deck from playing [Glimpse of Nature](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature), [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel), and [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid).


All in all, a mighty choice for a Pro Tour, and mighty tricky. It will be interesting to see if this roguish deck can survive through the PTQ season.


My guess is that it picks up more than one PTQ victory.


So after his unlikely victory against Öberg, Scott-Vargas found himself up against maybe the scariest player in the Top 8, reigning Player of the Year and Pro Tour Champion Tomoharu Saito, with the Dragon-Elves skew on the mirror match.


Saito's Elves deck works like most.


The core combination is to play [Glimpse of Nature](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature), after which every creature played will draw a card.


With the right configuration of Elves, Saito could "break even" on mana, and cycle rapidly through his deck.

Nettle Sentinel

[Birchlore Rangers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birchlore+Rangers) has flirted with tournament inclusion for years, but the All-Star that took this archetype to its present dominating level is [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel).


Assume you have played a [Glimpse of Nature](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature) and all you have in play is a [Birchlore Rangers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birchlore+Rangers) and a [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel). You can tap them both to produce ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif).


Now play any one-mana Elf.


Your [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) will untap. You have another Elf in play.


These two can tap to play the next one-mana Elf.


So long as you have one-mana Elves, you can continue to break even and cycle through your deck (drawing more one-mana Elves, ideally).

The deck can start to net mana with multiple [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel)s. If you have a [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid) and two [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel)s, you can tap the three to produce ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif). Play any green spell (ideally a one mana Elf). When you play a one-mana Elf (ideally!) you get to untap your two [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel)s. The same situation we described with [Birchlore Rangers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birchlore+Rangers) is applicable, except you have ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) still floating. You are generating a profit.


Saito's version has [Wirewood Hivemaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Hivemaster).


As you accumulate Elves you will produce Insects as well as mana. These Insects will want to be helpful, so once you have enough mass on the ground, you can tap them to play [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) for [Predator Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Predator+Dragon).


The Dragon is very hungry and devours however many Elves, Insects, whatever, to go lethal. It is hasty and puts a skid mark on the opponent's face immediately before the two of you shuffle up for the next game.


Saito's deck was very fast, and Saito, as we mentioned, is the reigning Player of the Year. Scary stuff, right?


Luis started his match 0-2 again!


No problem!


He won three in a row and found himself in another skewed mirror with Matej Zatlkaj, again with Dragon-Elves.


Zatlkaj's version is similar to Saito's, but he also has [Essence Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Warden). This allows him to accumulate a ton of life while either player generates Elves, making it harder to "go off" under the wrong circumstances... especially for a deck like Luis's, which ran a [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot) kill.

The really frightening prospect (of which Saito's deck was also capable) was that Zatlkaj could use his [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) not just when "going off" himself, but by piggybacking Luis's Elves with his [Wirewood Hivemaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Hivemaster) to set up a [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling).


The Chord could then produce an [Orzhov Pontiff](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orzhov+Pontiff) which would wipe LSV's board, undo all his hard work... and leave his opponent with an army of Insects!


But Luis, bereft of any tricky Pontiffs or Sharpshooters, soldiered through the Finals, and won 3-0 largely on the back of some sideboarded copies of [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize).

Luis could use [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) in the mirror to slow down his opponents by removing Glimpse—and subtly, to evaluate if they had the Chords necessary to break him up mid-combo—to improve his chances.


It helped that Luis had the fastest version of Elves in the Top 8, capable of winning on the second turn.

 Jan Doise's deck operates a little bit differently from the Dragon builds, though he has much of the same machinery.


Instead, Jan finished games with [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity). [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) actually turns all of Jan's Insects into Elves (Elves being one of the "all" creature types specified by [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity)), which would then be eligible to team up with [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid)... in order to create a bigger, potentially lethal, attack by leveraging the "X" side of the same ability.


Whatever was in play at the beginning of the turn—typically one [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) or thereabouts—could play the role of the [Predator Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Predator+Dragon), crashing into the Red Zone.

In addition, Jan could create actually infinite numbers, not just 200-power Dragons or "lethal" [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot)s. Because [Wirewood Symbiote](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Symbiote) becomes an Elf under [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity), it can be returned to hand like an Elf. Remember all the stuff about netting mana on cheap plays? Doise could do it over and over forever by untapping an active [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) and returning and re-playing (and re-Elfing) his Symbiote, making for limitless mana and life.


Just be careful that you don't deck yourself with [Glimpse of Nature](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature) on this one! This is a combination that doesn't actually require Glimpse (just having the right creatures in play), though practically speaking, you will probably have played Glimpse in most of the games where you can set up those creatures (there is only one [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) in the deck, for instance).

 Guillaume Baudois's version played twenty-three lands (running one [Unwilling Recruit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unwilling+Recruit) over the 24th land), but running four [Ghitu Encampment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghitu+Encampment)s main. [Ghitu Encampment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghitu+Encampment) is an interesting card. Certainly it has value as a potential offensive threat (especially post-[Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Wrath%2Bof%2BGod) or post-[Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) in a room where Cruel Control or some other Quick 'n Toast variant is popular), but it comes at a cost.



![Stigma Lasher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Stigma+Lasher)

When your fifth land that you just topdecked comes into play tapped... Your [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) sits waiting in your hand instead of screaming across the Red Zone angrily at your opponent's face. Just one small thing to consider.


On one, Guillaume ran [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic); this is not remarkable ([Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) has been a favorite of red decks since it was first printed in 1997 or so)... but the little guy has not been that popular in Standard as of late, finishing behind [Magus of the Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magus+of+the+Scroll) last summer, and largely behind [Tattermunge Maniac](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tattermunge+Maniac) right now.


Guillaume's two-drop is [Stigma Lasher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stigma+Lasher), which seems to indicate specific preparation for [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks). The other major contender for this position is [Vexing Shusher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vexing+Shusher), which obviously leans towards anti-Faeries or anti-Quick 'n Toast preparation.


The preference against [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) seems confirmed by [Puncture Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Puncture+Blast) in the main (over [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock), or in some lists, [Lash Out](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lash+Out)); [Puncture Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Puncture+Blast) is outstanding against [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) in that it removes that bane of the red deck immediately, with no persist shenanigans.


Not that the deck needed to win the PT–Berlin LCQ to get this notice, but obviously Demigod Deck Wins is going to be a major strategy come this weekend. Be prepared or be prepared to be burned out.

 So what do you get when you walk away from the red deck two-drop argument and cram a [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) and a 3/1 trampler into [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare)'s favorite mana slot? Incentive to play hot new card [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning), apparently.


Oscar's deck has a lot of the trappings of the conventional Demigod Deck, but more raw power thanks to dipping into a second color (and that dip does not seem overly painful by the way, thanks to the superb dual lands available in Standard, plus the fact that [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) has friendly color requirements).



![Blightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Blightning)

  

 [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) seems really exciting. Don't forget that cards along the lines of [Stupor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor) have been good enough before, many times... [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) is like a turbo-charged [Stupor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor) that kind of spits in the opponent's face while simultaneously kicking his butt. "Yeah, I'm getting card advantage here and everything, but on the other hand, you take 3 damage." Just a really elegantly designed (and quite adequately powerful and efficient) card.


The only caveat here is that the black-red [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) version is almost certainly a dog to the straight Red version of the Demigod strategy. The mana isn't overly painful, but every point matters when you are racing; [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) is a great threat, but it is a liability at least part of the time in the Faeries matchup, and I don't see that changing when we go Red v. Red with these competing builds. Still, this deck should be a contender on the back of [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning), if not the most popular archetype.

 These Elves seem to love a [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) (Bitter Awesome) much more than usual favorite / lord / etc. [Imperious Perfect](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperious+Perfect).


Elsewise, they are pretty straightforward decks, good creatures, good spells, similar to the decks that won last year's World Championships and the Standard Pro Tour–Hollywood.


In addition to these decks, I would make sure to prepare for Cruel Control. There has been some talk around the 'Net that Cruel Control doesn't need [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum), but it seems like the card I lose to most when I am facing that Five-Color Control deck. So basically, I'd love it if people stopped playing [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum). If you can comply with this desire and you are playing in the New York State Championship, it would be much appreciated!



![Cruel Ultimatum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cruel+Ultimatum)

  

 This is what you have to worry about:


There are many strategies for getting around a Cruel, such as sandbagging your [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) in Game 1, or siding in something like a [Hindering Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hindering+Light) in Game 2. However if your opponent is Cruel, he can play around this by waiting until he has nine mana in Game 1 ([Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate)) rather than firing prematurely, or siding in a really problematic card like [Vexing Shusher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vexing+Shusher) or [Guttural Response](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guttural+Response) (which is good like everywhere anyway). *That* is why you lose to [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum); because people can play well enough to actually resolve it through resistance!


So watch out. In addition to Faeries and the red decks (and possibly Kithkin / White Weenie), I think Cruel Control will be the most popular deck of the tournament. Do yourself a favor and review [Cruisin' for a Blue Again](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/8).


Good luck this weekend (unless you are playing me, of course).
