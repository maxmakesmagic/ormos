Right to Bear Arms
==================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on May 16, 2002 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_jaymoldenhauer-salazar.jpg)
By Jay Moldenhauer-Salazar











If you decide to build a deck around the *Judgment* sorcery [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate), I suggest you do the following:

Sit down at your friend’s dining room table, shuffling your green-sleeved deck and waiting for him to feed you. When he finally sits down, calmly hand him a folded slip of paper that says the following:

 "*Avoiding or Surviving an Attack*", by Scott McMillion  
* Make noise on the trail.
* Keep a clean camp.
* Never look a bear in the eyes.
* If attacked, don't fight, especially if you're alone.
* Play dead and make no noise if a bear begins to bite or maul you.
* Exception to above: if a bear attacks your tent, fight as loudly and as hard as you can. This lets the bear know you are not the easy meal he has mistaken you for.
* Know how to use pepper spray and test the can before setting out for the woods.
* Do not run from a grizzly bear.
* Remember, grizzlies can and do climb trees.”

When your friend gives you a quizzical look, shrug and say, “I just thought you should be prepared.”

Oh, come on... don’t you think that would be funny?

It was clear to me the second I saw [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate) that decks would follow. This card has a lot going for it. Specifically:

* It is one of only two spells with both flashback and threshold (the other is also in *Judgment*). This almost automatically makes the card interesting.
* It makes token creatures, and token creatures are fun.
* It puts anywhere from two to eight 2/2 creatures into play. Eight. For a single card. With four in a deck I am packing potentially 64/64 worth of bear. That’s a lot of bear.
* It is an uncommon, and so relatively easy to acquire. More than that, it is in my opinion one of the more intriguing, flavorful, and dynamic uncommons around.
* It fits into a bear theme deck. To be honest, this last point is the most important reason for me.

For those of you new to **Magic** and not up on slang, a “bear” is a 2/2 creature for 2 mana. These creatures are called “bears” because of [Grizzly Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Bears), a staple since **Magic**’s first Alpha release. Enough actual Creature-Bears have existed to make theme decks, but almost by definition they have thus far been generic and vanilla decks. To make bear theme decks interesting before *Judgment*, you needed to call it a “Pooh” deck and use [Drop of Honey](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drop+of+Honey) and [Unyaro Bee Sting](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unyaro+Bee+Sting) or make the ever-popular “Lions and Tigers and Bears... Oh My!” deck.

Plain old bears, though? They have been just plain and old. I care about these things because a) I like making [theme decks](/en/articles/archive/teme-legende-2002-03-07), and b) “bear” is such a common slang term in **Magic** that it just screams for a deck built around it.

So for those of you who enjoy creature theme decks, here is a look at bears and how you might take full advantage of the grizzled fate that *Judgment* has handed you.

### THE BEARS OF DOMINARIA

As I mentioned, the good news is that you can actually pack a bear deck with bears. Indeed, several of them are “bears”: [Grizzly Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Bears), [Balduvian Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Balduvian+Bears), [Bear Cub](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bear+Cub), and [Forest Bear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest+Bear). Even better, all of those cards are commons.

| --- |
| *Here's a question: How does Jay even know these cards exist?* |

Here’s a question: Why is a [Bear Cub](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bear+Cub) the same size as a full-grown bear? And have you seen the art on that thing? It just feels like [Bear Cub](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bear+Cub) should have been 1/2 for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) like [Woodland Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woodland+Druid). Ah, the wonders of continuity in a game with almost six thousand cards in it.

There are more interesting bears than just “bears.” [Striped Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Striped+Bears) are cantrip-creatures. [Golden Bear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golden+Bear) is fairly efficient, although it is probably inferior to [Razorclaw Bear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Razorclaw+Bear). The real reason to use the former instead of the latter is that [Golden Bear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golden+Bear) is common, while [Razorclaw Bear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Razorclaw+Bear) is a *Portal II* rare. Similarly, I would rather use [Pale Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pale+Bears) over [River Bear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Bear), but again there is that rare problem. One of my first ever theme decks was a bear deck, and it burned me to no end that I had to trade good cards for [Pale Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pale+Bears).

Finally, two bears stand out at nigh-exceptional. [Spectral Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Bears) gives you some actual beef for the same price as a normal “bear,” so they go right into the deck. [Werebear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Werebear) is also solid. The real problem with [Werebear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Werebear) is that bear decks haven’t really needed mana acceleration and have a difficult time reaching threshold if they stay in theme. Still, [Werebear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Werebear) at least has some good bear art, some silly bear flavor text, and is actually a bear, so it belongs in a bear theme deck.

Eleven different types of bear. That’s the good news. The bad news is that you have one of the least interesting foundations for a deck, like, ever. Those creatures aren’t going to keep you playing your theme deck game after game.

### BEARING THE WEIGHT: SUPPORT CARDS

Non-creature cards are the way to spruce up a bear deck. Before talking about the no-brainers to include, let me mention two general approaches you might take with a generic bear theme. These approaches aren’t mutually exclusive.

The “Bears Are Mean” approach suggests you should play up how angry and dangerous your bears can become. Cards like [Aggressive Urge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aggressive+Urge), [Feral Instinct](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Feral+Instinct), [Ferocity](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ferocity), [Overrun](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun), [Surge of Strength](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Surge+of+Strength), and [Wild Might](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Might) turn your sometimes silly 2/2's into raging beasts of white-hot terror. In addition, [Call of the Wild](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Wild), [Lure of Prey](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lure+of+Prey), etc., can cause a bear to jump out of the bushes and really surprise an opponent. You can argue that Primal Rage has a bear on it, although I personally would rather use [Primal Frenzy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Frenzy) or [Rancor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rancor).

The “Bears Live in Forests” approach, meanwhile, focuses on a bear’s dwelling of choice. [Barbed Foliage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barbed+Foliage), [Dark Heart of the Wood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Heart+of+the+Wood), [Deep Wood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Wood), [Dense Foliage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dense+Foliage), [Familiar Ground](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Familiar+Ground), [Gift of the Woods](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gift+of+the+Woods), [Harmony of Nature](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Harmony+of+Nature), and [Nature's Blessing](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Blessing)... those spells give some texture to your pack of bears. Heck, even [Wall of Wood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Wood) makes some sense to include under this approach.

Note that at least two cards fit both approaches: [Might of Oaks](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Might+of+Oaks) and [Sylvan Might](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Might).



|  |
| --- |
| *Two cards, twenty damage.* |

Before *Judgment*, you could find only a few crown jewels of bear-dom. The same critter on [Primal Rage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Rage) seems to have made its way to [Insist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Insist). Since theme decks can use a slanted lens when seeing things, I have decided that for the purposes of a bear theme, [Insist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Insist) has a bear on it rather than a wolverine or rabid hamster. The great use for [Insist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Insist) is as a way to cycle through your deck. Making creatures uncounterable is secondary to having something to do on turn 1 and getting you closer to threshold.

[Bearscape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bearscape), which turns your graveyard into bears (and “bears” for that matter) obviously belongs in a bear deck. What is frustrating about [Bearscape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bearscape) is that it works at cross-purposes to both [Wearbear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wearbear) and [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate). So whereas it probably sat as the centerpiece of bear decks after *Odyssey*, it now takes a bit of a back seat to [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate).

There is also [Winter Orb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Winter+Orb). Those are definitely bears on the art, albeit bears of the “Pale” variety. [Winter Orb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Winter+Orb) not only slows down an opponent, it is easy to use in a bear deck that relies on cheap creatures. [Winter Orb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Winter+Orb) makes the use of [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate) challenging, but since [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate) proves to be the only high-end spell in the deck and we can use [Werebear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Werebear), I don’t think it is too severe a problem.

Finally, [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate) has arrived and the Theme Gods rejoice. [Grizzly Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Fate) gives bear decks something just as scary to cast as [Overrun](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun). And now [Overrun](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) looks even scarier. Booya.

Below is the kind of bear deck I would probably take to a theme party/tournament. I’ve played with the bear theme in various ways in some other decks and even tried my hand at a [Tribes](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/tribe-blue-squid-2002-02-05) deck. My deep apologies to Stijn van Dongen for probably murdering the Tribes deckbuilding rules.

*Judgment* is almost here... get out your cans of pepper spray!
