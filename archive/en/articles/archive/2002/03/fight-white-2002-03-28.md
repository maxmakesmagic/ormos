Fight for White
===============



 Posted in **Feature**
 on March 28, 2002 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_jaymoldenhauer-salazar.jpg)
By Jay Moldenhauer-Salazar












Ideas for deckbuilding can come from the strangest places. Inspiration can bubble up from [new cards](http://archive.wizards.com/default.asp?x=mtgcom/daily/jm3), [weird cards](http://archive.wizards.com/default.asp?x=mtgcom/daily/jm6), [“bad” cards](http://archive.wizards.com/default.asp?x=mtgcom/daily/jm9), [accessible cards](/en/articles/archive/deckbuilding-diet-2002-03-14), and [flavor text](/en/articles/archive/clash-empires-2002-03-21). Sometimes, deck ideas can even come from the **Magic** Pro Tour.


For those of you who don’t follow the hardcore competitive side of the game, Pro Tour - Osaka occurred [March 15-17](http://archive.wizards.com/default.asp?x=sideboard/events/ptosa02). Two hundred seventy-seven people matched wits playing *Odyssey* Block Constructed. I write these articles fairly far in advance, so I can’t benefit from the scintillating analysis written since March 17th. I have seen the decklists from the event, however, and one thing is glaringly obvious:


Very, very few people played white.


Observe: 2296 [Swamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swamp)s, 1630 [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s, 1533 [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)s, 220 [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s, and (drum roll)... 43 [Plain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plain)s. The number of [Deserted Temple](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deserted+Temple)s? Ninety-three, more than twice the number of [Plain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plain)s.


Indeed, the only white card to show up in any capacity was [Mystic Enforcer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Enforcer), a gold card. Only four decks used other white cards. Wow. Our own Ben Bleiweiss sums up the phenomenon [quite nicely](http://archive.wizards.com/default.asp?x=sideboard/ptosa02/white). In Osaka, there was simply no white in sight.


All of this information produces a powerful smell. It’s a smell I can’t quite place... no, wait... I think that’s it... it smells kind of like... yes, in fact *exactly* like...


A challenge.


Green received the same diminished number of cards in *Torment* as white, and yet plenty of mono-green decks appeared in Osaka. White has access to good anti-black cards, and mono-black was one of the biggest decktypes around. So what’s the deal? Is white really that unplayable?


To the pros, the answer is clearly “yes.” But you may decide to build Block decks with your friends, and I would hate for you to think of the format as primarily built around four colors.


Personally, hearing that no one wants to create a base white deck gets my innovative juices flowing. I simply refuse to believe that all 82 cards in a 478-card format are useless. It must be the Underdog in me.


Below are some opportunities I think exist in the realm of white for *Odyssey* Block. These decks may not have won in Osaka, but they just might surprise the heck out of your local gaming buddies. At the very least, they might inspire you to think closely about the recently forgotten color of **Magic**.


### WHITE WEENIE


White Weenie (WW) is arguably the oldest and most consistent deck archetype in **Magic**’s history. The idea is to take little white dudes and beat your opponent with them. Usually the dudes get some help from a [Crusade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crusade)-like effect, first strike and protection from opposing colors. WW is the most obvious place to look for white deck ideas, and thus seems as good a place to start as any.


Low and behold, plenty of little white dudes appear in *Odyssey*  Block; [Crusade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crusade) appears in the form of both [Pianna, Nomad Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pianna%2C+Nomad+Captain) and [Divine Sacrament](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divine+Sacrament); [Patrol Hound](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Patrol+Hound) is a great example of a white first-striking weenie; and spells like [Devoted Caretaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devoted+Caretaker), [Mystic Crusader](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Crusader), and [Shelter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shelter) offer plenty of protection. Although not a particularly inexpensive deck to build, WW appears alive and well.


The fun thing about WW decks is that, because of their straightforward design, you can splash another other color to fill your weaknesses. Daniel Steinsdorfer’s deck in Osaka splashed blue for counterspells ([Rites of Refusal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rites+of+Refusal)) and card-drawing ([Breakthrough](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breakthrough)) that also speed towards threshold. Green gives potential creature-enhancing effects like [Sylvan Might](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Might) along with goodies like [Wild Mongrel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) and [Roar of the Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm). Black allows for creature removal via [Chainer's Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chainer%27s+Edict) and disruption via [Mesmeric Fiend](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mesmeric+Fiend), while red -- as always -- gives access to burn.

That is, as I said, the relatively obvious place to begin thinking about white. Yet numerous other white cards worth building decks around are currently feeling undervalued:


MASTER APOTHECARY

![Master Apothecary](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Master+Apothecary)

The more clerics, the more annoying your deck becomes with [Master Apothecary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+Apothecary). Versus green and red decks especially, the Apothecary can be hugely frustrating. What I particularly like about him is that his ability can be used the same turn he comes into play.


The problem inherent in a [Master Apothecary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+Apothecary) deck is clear: All of those clerics may be good at preventing damage, but they suck at dealing it. How the heck is an army of clerics going to kill your opponent?


As with any deckbuilding challenge, there are probably several ways to sort through a win condition. You could splash blue for [Iridescent Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Iridescent+Angel) or green for [Mystic Enforcer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Enforcer). [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) would be a funny way to end the game. For those people who scoff at fast games, [Ambassador Laquatus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ambassador+Laquatus) would allow for a [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) strategy.


I’ve focused below on a recurring way to deal damage. [Caustic Tar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Caustic+Tar) would fit here, but I like the idea that [Kamahl, Pit Fighter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kamahl%2C+Pit+Fighter) can be protected by the Apothecary and his gang. Splashing red also allows for a small amount of burn to make its way in the deck, further shortening the game. The deck avoids any chase rares, which is great.

CANTIVORE

![Cantivore](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cantivore)

I seem to be obsessed with the *Odyssey* ‘Vores. I can’t help but think of decks built around each one. [Cantivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cantivore) is hands down the most difficult one to use, but that just adds to the challenge.


Some white cards in *Odyssey*  Block make sense in a [Cantivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cantivore) deck: [Auramancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auramancer) has great interactions with enchantments. [Kirtar's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kirtar%27s+Desire) slows down creatures and becomes a [Pacifism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pacifism) at just about the time when you would like your [Cantivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cantivore) to attack. [Testament of Faith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Testament+of+Faith) is mana-hungry, but is essentially a good wall. [Strength of Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strength+of+Isolation) is decent if you think you will have enough creatures in your deck to suitably enchant. And then there’s [Karmic Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karmic+Justice), which turns anyone wanting to kill your tasty enchantments into a puddle of mush.


Unfortunately, that’s probably not enough meat for a monowhite deck. But wait! Other colors have enchantments too! Imagine green with [Still Life](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Still+Life) and [Squirrel Nest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squirrel+Nest). Imagine black with [Zombie Infestation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zombie+Infestation) and the aforementioned [Caustic Tar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Caustic+Tar). Imagine a kooky red land-destruction deck with [Price of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Price+of+Glory), [Steam Vines](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steam+Vines), [Pyromania](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromania), and [Burning Sands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Sands).

Other examples abound: Make a [Reborn Hero](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reborn+Hero)-[Devastating Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Dreams) deck, a [Major Teroh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Major+Teroh)-[Alter Reality](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alter+Reality) deck, or even a deck built around [Soulcatcher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soulcatcher), [Transcendence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Transcendence), or [Graceful Antelope](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Graceful+Antelope). Show everyone that mono-black, squirrels, and blue-green madness may be powerful choices, but they can’t quite compete with [Possessed Nomad](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Possessed+Nomad).
