Thoroughly Modern Metagame
==========================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on June 20, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







Quick question: Name a Hasbro super brand. Go!


  
  

**Magic: The Gathering**, huh? Well... I guess I could have guessed you would say that. Got another guess? 


G.I. Joe, huh? Yes, yes I suppose recruiting The Rock (The Rock that inspired Sol Malka's The Rock deck no less) and Bruce Willis might do that.


What I was thinking about in the context of Modern Week was actually my favorite childhood Hasbro brand: Transformers.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td252_prime.jpg)
[*Transformers: The Movie*](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformers:_The_Movie) is to this day one of my favorite movies. My kid sister and I still chuckle over the opening line "The year is 2005...." Back in 1985, that was far enough into the future that the idea of flying cars, moon bases, and giant orange cities that could shift and tumble into defensible fortresses would seem plausible. Hot Rod's fumble, costing Prime his life, was soul-shattering (although I was glad to have had "the Touch"). 


What does this have to do with Modern?


Modern is this great format that has transformed, tournament to tournament, to look like many different formats implying any number of different attitudes and intentions from on high, although always with the same intent. For a non-rotating format, Modern really feels like a format that is a car one day, a giant robot the next, then a tape deck, a fighter plane, a jaguar-slash-cassette tape: you know, diverse.

 Modern at its first Pro Tour didn't really know what it wanted to be... but it seemed to have a good idea of what it *didn't* want to be. The idea with the extensive banned list of past flagships was intended to foster format diversity. Then-recent Extended tournaments were being won hither and yon by [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) and [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor). Nobody but nobody (except presumably Sam Black and Paulo Vitor Damo Da Rosa) ever had any emotional reaction but horror and revulsion at a second-turn [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom). Modern first time around would have none of these flagships!

 It was interesting that both methodical cards that encouraged long games ([Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic); [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom); [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top); [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)) and super-fast cards like [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) and [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) were banned preemptively.

 Some pundits called Modern a Legacy without [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) as a safeguard. 


 The solution at that point was to invoke the first of Modern's many transformations; and cheap search cards [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) and [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) joined the ranks of [Glimpse of Nature](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature), [Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis), and [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep) on the banned list.

[Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) and [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) might have gone the way of the dodo in Modern, but one one-mana cantrip that was left in place was [Serum Visions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serum+Visions). 


 Add that together with a then-freshly released new eight-pack of *Innistrad* cards, [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) and [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), and you get a ferocious strategy for the much-decorated Antonino De Rosa. Again, Modern showed us a radical shift... from midrange to a blue tempo deck with control elements. If the stated goal of all those bannings was format diversity, I think we can *say mission accomplished*.

### The Jund Axis


 Jund variations had been posting Top 8s for months, but they really rose to prominence at the [2012 **Magic** Players Championship](/en/events/coverage/watanabe-wins-magic-players-championship). Yuuya Watanabe took down the streaking Shouta Yasooka in the sixteen-man finals, showcasing a combination of the flexibility of the Jund deck and the power of one [Olivia Voldaren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Olivia+Voldaren) against an opponent relying on largely cheap utility creatures.

![Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lingering+Souls)
  
 The finals of [Grand Prix Chicago](/en/events/coverage/jacob-wilson-first-second-city) saw two similar Jund-decks-with-[Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) face off in the finals. [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) was not just another card in the same vein as the rest of the Jund powerhouses... it was a card that was good *against* the rest of them. [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) could take the edge off of [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf). It was fabulous against [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) ("Sure, make me discard this"); and as it has been in formats various, [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) was a great stop sign against aggro decks. Those four spirit tokens just *lived* to die in front of [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) or [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) in order to buy time for the Jund deck to set up its more powerful single card efficiencies.

 At [Grand Prix Toronto](/en/events/coverage/edel-tops-toronto), Willy Edel pivoted Jund again. If [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) was the answer to the Jund mirror... what was the answer to [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls)? 


![Thundermaw Hellkite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Thundermaw+Hellkite)
  
 Willy dug for the *literal*, scripted, answer to [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls). 


What about the Dragon designed specifically to get past that card?


 And to get him there? Willy added a couple of [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra)s. With [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) on the battlefield at the start of turn three, one [Marsh Flats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Marsh+Flats) could send him straight to five mana!

 My favorite deck from Grand Prix Toronto was Collin Morton's. A straight WU board control deck with—*gasp*—no counterspells main deck. What does it say about a format, especially one that started with a stack of [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension)s and [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Deceiver%2BExarch) [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Splinter%2BTwin)s, to play a deck optimized to beat Jund with the classic [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)... and absolutely no combo coverage to start? 


I would actually say it was a good thing.


Collin knew how focused the format had become on Jund, and Jund-versus-Jund, and crafted an anti-Jund that rewarded him with a Grand Prix Top 8. All signs pointed to Jund... so why worry about combo?


Why Indeed?

 Stanislav Cifka made his own brilliant set of predictions at [Pro Tour *Return to Ravnica*](/en/events/coverage/ptrtr12) a short time earlier. Cifka returned not to Ravnica's, but to Modern's roots, as a format where combo can thrive. 


 Without [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder), without [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain), Cifka crafted a super-fast combo deck that ran a very different set of one-mana search cards (his Stars, Spheres, and Spellbombs). He rode the redundancy provided by [Faith's Reward](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Reward) over just [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise); knowing how popular decks like Jund were, he played a deck that could dodge all their [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate)s and most of their [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay)s.

*Modern*.


See what I said about transformation up top? Giant robots into jaguar-cassette-tapes?


 That's what Modern means to me. This was—and remains—a format that was built, engineered even, for diversity. It is a huge format that has always aspired to letting players actually play the many different strategies afforded by its many different cards. This diversity has been more-or-less granted, such that even when our overlords in Renton, WA, have brought the banhammer down on this strategy or that, players have always found enough sunlight for their various [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls), Eggs, and Ajanis.
