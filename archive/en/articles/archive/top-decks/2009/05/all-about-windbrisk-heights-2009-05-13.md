All About the Windbrisk Heights
===============================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 14, 2009 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td38_bdm.jpg)
> *"It seems like if you're not playing [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) right now you're not maximizing the value of your **Magic: The Gathering** collection ... [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) is clearly the top card."*  
> *–Brian David-Marshall*

Hello everyone and welcome to our pre-Regionals Top Decks look at [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) that is *also* a look back at the recent **Magic Online** 2009 Championship and a handful of PTQ results (yes—we're already in the next PTQ season!), all geared towards one lofty goal: Acquiring invitations to this year's U.S. National Championship ... which will be up for grabs the day after tomorrow.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Windbrisk+Heights)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights)  
So let's look at BDM's comment (which was made on last week's Top 8 Magic podcast); I hadn't thought about it like that. If you had asked me before the conversation I probably would have said that [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) is the best card in Standard, despite the obvious decline of Faeries in the face of [Volcanic Fallout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Fallout), a.k.a. Volcanic Blowout.

But Brian was right!

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130614080448im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/td/td38_wbSplash.jpg)  
Looking over the results from the **Magic Online** 2009 Championshps, plus these three or so PTQ Top 8s that have ushered in the first couple of weeks of the PT Austin Qualifier season, we can see a clear trend: [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) is the tops.

Why [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights)?

Looking back on a card that is now legal in its second year of Standard play, a card that has busted out [Biorhythm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Biorhythm) in Extended (some guy was kooky enough to do this with [Rith's Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rith%27s+Charm) months before the advent of [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) decks), plopped out [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger) a.k.a. Siege-Goat Commander in Block Constructed, and is now featured in Mono-White, Red-White, Black-White and Green-White attack variants in Standard, I actually wish this were a preview card for *Lorwyn*.

What would I have said about it?

Could I possibly have had any inkling of how good this land would end up being?

Probably not.

You see, [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) is a combination of two different cards that Mark Rosewater told me, on separate occasions, would never see print again ... but with no loss of card advantage; plus, it's a land.

**[Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) is an [Impulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse).**

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Impulse)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse)  
**Magic**'s Head Designer once told me that "[Impulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse) is too much selection; we don't want players to see that many cards ... not at that cost." And here is the Heights! Same selection, but with no cost at all! I am just ribbing Mark, of course; unlike [Impulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse), [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) does not put the card you want into your hand where you would ostensibly have immediate access to it. Instead the card is time-delayed (the land comes into play tapped) *and* its being played *ever* is contingent on your getting a halfway decent attack. Yes, today's decks with their [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession)s, [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger)s, [Siege-Gang Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander)s, and [Marsh Flitter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Marsh+Flitter)s (all cards that produce three eligible creatures immediately) *can* and *do* get the Heights going, but they don't do so with the same immediacy of an instant-speed Impulse.

**[Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) is a [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker).**

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Tinker)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker)  
A [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker)? Well, it doesn't have quite the length on the search (only [Impulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse)-level searching, not the whole deck), but it *does* give you any amount of mana. Like I mentioned before, the first thing I tried to do with [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) was to play [Biorhythm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Biorhythm) with it. You see, [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) (and all the Hideaway lands, really) can produce an arbitrarily large amount of mana. Sure, sometimes you get stuck with a Knight of the Meadowgrain underneath, but lots of the time you are cheating out four or five mana worth of threats at the cost of two lands short term, a bargain if you actually had to play the card from your hand .... Obviously just gravy from a cheap cheater.

The card is good; it gives you immediate selection for almost no cost (kind of like one mana due to the card being tapped) and doesn't take up a spell slot! No wonder it has become so popular. How popular? Check out these stats:

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td38_stats.jpg)  
*Twenty-eight*[Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) (out of a possible 32) by the most recent Top 8? These Hideaway lands have got to have something going for them (and to be fair, the odd deck out had four of its own ... just the wrong color). Just so you don't have to scroll all the way back up to the quote that we opened up with again, I'll reiterate it for you:

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td38_bdm.jpg)
> *"It seems like if you're not playing [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) right now you're not maximizing the value of your **Magic: The Gathering** collection ... [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) is clearly the top card."*  
> *–Brian David-Marshall*

*Clearly.*

So with this sweeping change in what is apparently the best of the best of the best, it seems like Regionals preparation is going to be—largely—a discussion of the various [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) options. Now we have written on many occasions about the Red-White Boat Brew style of deck (starting way back when it was first revealed, and Brian Kowal didn't even have [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) yet), so even though Red-White remains quite a viable choice based on the tournament results, we are going to focus on the other two main [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) decks, Green-White and Black-White Tokens.

This deck is a Green implementation taking advantage of the [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) / [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) synergy. The synergy doesn't stop there! The Green-White deck also borrows from the Green-White Little Kid deck, pumping creatures like [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) and [Steward of Valeron](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steward+of+Valeron) with the powerful [Wilt-Leaf Liege](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wilt-Leaf+Liege) (the Liege also doubles as [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) defense!). I have always chuckled at the prospect of playing a second turn [Steward of Valeron](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steward+of+Valeron), attacking with it behind vigilence, *and then* playing a post-combat Liege. 

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td38_overrunwind.jpg)  
One of the chief incentives of the Green-White Tokens deck is its extremely fast offense. You can start off with [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch) and get an accelerated [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession); your Processions are offensively better with [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) than most decks' because you can pop [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) during combat and just kill the opponent on the spot (or at least represent twelve points of trample).

This deck has a couple of additional cool things going for it. Both are related to Planeswalkers. While not as impressive as in the Black-White Tokens deck (due to that deck's steeper commitment to redundancy), [Ajani Goldmane](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Goldmane) is nevertheless impressive with both [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) and [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession)... everything, really, but those cards in particular due to the potentials for card advantage.

[Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) is a good performer in particular due to the presence of [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup). [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup) is a fine token producer in a deck that can generate a good many offensive advantages via token production, but the synergy with Garruk, that ability to untap lands in order to produce more mana and add a relevant counter in order to waltz through the Red Zone *next turn* thanks to Garruk's faux [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) ability (conveniently powered up by helping to play the [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)) represent some absolutely filthy efficacy.

Despite very few changes to the bulk of the cards in the main deck, today's Black-White Tokens is a very different deck than we have looked at previously. 

Yes, the deck has all the [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger)s and [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession)s to generate token advantage on the board, in turn setting up [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights). Yes, Black-White has probably graduated to the format's premiere [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) deck, jumping [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) Beatdown and Fae; but what is really interesting about this strategy today has nothing to do with the tokens part of the deck!

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130614072616im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/td/td38_3CardsAcross.jpg)  
The modern Black-White Tokens presents a slow infinity. Its persist creatures can keep coming back as long as [Ajani Goldmane](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Goldmane) can keep putting up +1/+1 counters; you see, these counters counteract the -1/-1 counters associated with the persist ability, and both counters vanish. So instead of coming back as a 2/1 creature once, a [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) can continually reset; it's even worse on a Redcap, which is actually dealing damage! What ends up happening most of the time, while the Black-White deck is jacking up an impregnable board position, is that a stray [Mutavault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault) ends up getting pumped up to 7/7 or so and is—vigilantly—the actual tool of Black-White's success. 

In a format that seems to be defined by [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) setting up the mighty [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights), the obvious question becomes, *which is the best [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) + [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) deck?* We would argue that the answer is Black-White Tokens.

Why?

It's the story of Jon, Jon, and Johnny.

Consider this card:

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Traumatic+Visions)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Traumatic+Visions)  
This is Jon Finkel's favorite card; or, at least, it does everything Jon Finkel could ever want to do. [Traumatic Visions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Traumatic+Visions) counters spells, draws cards, fixes his most abominable mana bases, and ultimately keeps Jon playing lands. It's perfect.

Now consider this card:

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Pale+Recluse)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pale+Recluse)  
This card does everything Jon *Becker* would ever want to do. It goes and gets a [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest). It goes and gets a [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains). It therefore helps to address (this) Jon's chronic mana difficulties. It blocks flying creatures. It's a Spider (Becker's favorite creature type). Ever a lover of a big butt, Becker can even appreciate the body on [Pale Recluse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pale+Recluse) once it is in play.

Finally, consider this card:

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Zealous+Persecution)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Persecution)  
This card does everything a Black-White player would ever want to do. Black-White—if we think about the flavor of the strategy as a descendent of Orzhov—is one about incremental advantage, attrition, milking out a little bit of value. Heck, there was even a card that we all loved from *Ravnica* block that did only *half* of what [Zealous Persecution](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Persecution) does, [Orzhov Pontiff](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orzhov+Pontiff). And at the time, many a Black-White player swore by the Pontiff.

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130614063920im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/td/td38_weThreeKings.jpg)  
Today, in a world where [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) is king, [Zealous Persecution](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Persecution) is your incremental [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) (for a fraction of the mana) *that also kills all the opponent's tokens.* It is bad enough for little 1/1 flyers, but think about how poor Green-White is also laden with [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch)s and [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves). In a word: blowout.

The more I play, the more impressive [Zealous Persecution](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Persecution) looks given the metagame. More than one capable player has suggested *splashing* this simple spell in other, non-Black-White Tokens decks (such as Green-White tokens). The Richmond PTQs last weekend, in fact, featured multiple Black-White *Kithkin* decks touching black purely for [Zealous Persecution](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Persecution) to enhance the interactions their Kithkin aggro decks were capable of presenting.

So here is our rogue deck of the day.

The only deck to win one of the tournaments we looked at that *didn't* play four [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights).

And an exciting list to boot!

So how does the [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) deck work?

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Finest+Hour)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour)Really there are multiple synergies. The deck can play it straight ... First-turn [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) (or better yet [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch)), offense via [Rhox War Monk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rhox+War+Monk) or [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) on the second turn, actually crossing the Red Zone starting with three, dealing 3, maybe even gaining 3.

But the reason this deck came about was the critical mass of green-blue spells. These spells, in turn, start making [Shorecrasher Mimic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shorecrasher+Mimic) worth playing for the first time. [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) is one of the cards you might have to check on [Gatherer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Default.aspx). This is a new card that conveniently costs blue and green mana for [Shorecrasher Mimic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shorecrasher+Mimic), that can *also* turn even the most innocuous threats on the board into brutal murderers.

Consider a teeny tiny [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)—the smallest creature on the squad—with [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) in play. Attack! [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) clocks in at 1/2; the opponent takes a bee sting. Now the Birds gets to attack *again.* And Exalted operates *again.* It is almost like attacking with a [Nomadic Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nomadic+Elf) this time. Long story short, the smallest animal on the team just did as much damage as a [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession)! All by its lonesome. Now consider a real threat made by a Rafiq, acting alone. That is, between his personal double strike and the free [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) attack (plus any and all exalted) ... the two cards can easily produce 20 damage.

This seems like a very powerful rogue strategy. Rogue decks tend to be weaker than average (but can fill nice niches in a metagame) ... and you can see a possible chink in Ashton's armor with the inclusion of [Jhessian Infiltrator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jhessian+Infiltrator) just to make [Shorecrasher Mimic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shorecrasher+Mimic) better. But this time we see one with a tremendous offensive up side that can also prove hell on red decks. Not only are there all kinds of Finks and War Monks starting, Tommy has more in the sideboard, along with [Captured Sunlight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Captured+Sunlight) to make beating down difficult.

So what is the lesson here?

You know me. I would never say that you have to play [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) even if BDM says you probably should. In fact, we *know* from Ashton's recent win that Standard is plenty open enough for effective new ideas. But the success is still in the preparation. So if you're not going to play with [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights), I would suggest that you figure out what you're going to do about it if someone else decides to lay one down across the table from you.

With that, I leave you with a few more threats that you are bound to see at the gaming tables, day after tomorrow ....
