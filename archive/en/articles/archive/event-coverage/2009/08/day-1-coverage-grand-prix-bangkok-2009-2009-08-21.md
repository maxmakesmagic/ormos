Day 1 Coverage of Grand Prix Bangkok 2009
=========================================



 Posted in **Event Coverage**
 on August 21, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/wizards_authorpic_larger.jpg)
By Wizards of the Coast











![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/gpban09/01_intro.jpg)Day one has drawn to a close here at Grand Prix Bangkok, with 413 players narrowing down to 99, as the players with 6-2 or better records advance to day two. Sitting at the top of the standings, undefeated, are Kurt Stockbrugger from Canada, Ruud Warmenhoven from the Netherlands, Ryan Luo from Singapore and Thatiland's Chokchai Jettanachewchankit. Right behind them on 7-1 there is a veritable sea of pro players, just waiting to get their drafts on tomorrow, including Matteo Orsini Jones, Shingou Kurihara, Sam Black, Gaudenis Vidugiris, Shuu Komuro and Yuuta Takahashi to name just a few. Of course, there are still plenty of local players battling it out for a chance at the title and defending their home turf, so keep it locked here at Magicthegathering.com while we crack open the draft packs to see who will be the 2009 Grand Prix Bangkok Champion.

Saturday, August 22: 9:02a.m. – Last Chance Qualifier Decklists
---------------------------------------------------------------


*by Ray “blisterguy” Walkinshaw*

I feel like I start every Grand Prix by waxing lyrical on the value of a trio of byes to get your tournament off to a good start, but it bears repeating, because they're just that good. Whether you think they're fair or not, having byes at the start of an event like this is a hefty advantage. Not only do you not have to play the first three rounds, but your tiebreakers are that much better for not having persons who happened upon losses in rounds one, two or three. Having to go a mere three wins and two loses, perhaps even as low as 2-2 to make day two is a lot easier than rattling off five or more wins, and the players know it. Throughout the region, in the lead up to this event, people have been flocking to Grant Prix Trial tournaments to compete for the hallowed three bye prize, and those who missed out there, were queuing up here yesterday to battle it out in the Last Chance Qualifiers. As the lights were being switched off in the venue last night after a hard day's Magicing, I could be found hunching over my laptop (I really must work on my posture) hammering out the winner's decklists, for you, our faithful readers.

Saturday, August 22: 10:52a.m. – The Sealed Section, with Olivier Ruel and Martin Juza
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Ray “blisterguy” Walkinshaw*

If you're looking for any advice of Magic 2010 sealed deck, you'd be hard pressed to do better than the finalists of GP Brighton two weeks ago, Martin Juza and Hall of Fame Road Warrior, Olivier Ruel. Compared to Ruel, who has been knocking out Grand Prix top 8's for what seems like forever, Juza is a relative newcomer, but has certainly been putting up some strong results over the last year or two. Looking through his pool, Juza was quick to dismiss his Green, White and Blue cards. Not because the Red and Black were that good, but more because those other colors were considerably lacking. Juza held up a pair of [Consume Spirit](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consume+Spirit)s and a [Tendrils of Corruption](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Corruption), along with two [Child of Night](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Child+of+Night). “I might play my [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond),” he joked, “there's not another single playable color.” He toyed with the idea of going Blue for some fliers and a [Twincast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twincast), to combo with his removal spells, but eventually opted for his Red cards, that at least had a [Fireball](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) and a [Pyroclasm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyroclasm). “I think it's better to splash these and a couple of stupid Red creatures over the Blue cards, I want to maximize my Black for all these,” he explained, pointing to his [Tendrils of Corruption](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Corruption) and [Consume Spirit](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consume+Spirit)s. Juza pushed the [Sanguine Bond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanguine+Bond) to the side, before admitting “I think it's okay,” with a nod, meaning his deck, not the Bond. As I was leaving the table though, I heard him say, “Maybe I will play it, just for fun.”


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/gpban09/01_sealed_juza.jpg)*Martin Juza, will he play Sanguine Bond “just for fun”?*


Ruel however, was in a much better position. He too, had discounted some of his colors immediately, both Red and Green, not because they were no good, but because his Blue, Black and White were so strong. “I'm quite sure it's Blue White from what I saw, but let's double check,” he said, laying out the cards, revealing doubles of [Blinding Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinding+Mage), [Divine Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divine+Verdict) and [Sleep](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sleep), along with a [Guardian Seraph](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guardian+Seraph), a [Serra Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel), and a seemingly out of place [Clone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clone). “I gotta go for the combo,” he clarified with a chuckle, pointing at the [Guardian Seraph](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guardian+Seraph), “it's also an answer to some bombs.” Ruel also pushed a couple of [Elite Vanguard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elite+Vanguard)s into his playables. “Not sure about this guy, but with double [Sleep](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sleep) I think I need as many guys as I can.” Wanting to make sure Blue White was the correct color combination for him, Ruel swept aside the White cards for a moment to pair Blue with Black, revealing the powerful [Mind Shatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Shatter), backed up by a pair of [Mind Rot](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Rot) and a trio of [Kelinore Bat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kelinore+Bat)s. “[Sleep](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sleep) is not as good in Blue Black,” he noticed, “the thing is, I think I'm going to want [Mind Shatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Shatter) against Green decks every round. My Blue White has a better curve and synergy, but my Blue Black deals with bombs better.”


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/gpban09/01_sealed_ruel.jpg)*Frequent flier, Olivier Ruel.*


We then discussed playing or drawing in M10 sealed. “I definitely would want to play with my Blue White, and draw with my Blue Black. That's another reason to go with the Blue White. Most people will choose to draw, and then it's as if I won the flip anyway.” Ruel favored aggressive Blue White decks late in Shards of Alara block draft, but while he has a predilection for that archetype, he admitted it's not as effective in M10 sealed. “There are not as many bad draws in this format, people should be two color. In Shards, people draft decks that don't work.” Ruel continued to ponder his choices, growing less sure of his Blue White. “Building a deck that dies to bombs, I'm not sure that's so smart,” he said with a frown. In the end, he decided on the Blue White anyway, “it's too late to change now. I'll definitely be testing Blue Black with my byes, though.” As he counted out his mana, he also had this to add about his 17 lands. “It's not often I play less than 18 land in this format, I'm generally only ever land screwed than flooded.”

**Saturday, August 22: 2:55p.m. – Round 4: Tomoharu Saito vs Makihito Mihara**
------------------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Ray “blisterguy” Walkinshaw*

“Bad deck,” Grand Prix Singapore Champion, Tomoharu Saito exclaimed, gesturing to himself as he sat down. “Good deck,” he continued, pointing to 2006 World Champion Makihito Mihara.


Both players said something to each other in Japanese in a manner that I assume meant they were wishing each other good luck. Saito kept and was quickly taking chunks out of Mihara's life total with an [Elite Vanguard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elite+Vanguard). Mihara had a [Seismic Strike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Strike) for a [Lightning Elemental](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Elemental), but the Vanguard kept coming. Mihara summoned a [Centaur Courser](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Courser) to hold it off, and then dropped a [Mind Control](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Control) on Saito's recent arrival, a [Capricious Efreet](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Capricious+Efreet), only to recoil in surprise as Saito destroyed it with a maindeck [Solemn Offering](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Offering). Mihara then summoned an [Enormous Baloth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enormous+Baloth), but the Efreet ate that during Saito's upkeep, while more and more small White creatures filled out Saito's board. Mihara eagerly traded his men with Saito's, if only to ensure the Efreet wouldn't cause so much harm to his team. A [Sleep](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sleep) tapped the attackers down for a turn, while Mihara looked for answers. He summoned an [Air Elemental](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Air+Elemental), and waited with baited breath to see what Saito rolled with his die during his upkeep. Sure enough, the Efreet ate the Elemental, and Mihara was scooping up his cards.


Saito 1 – Mihara 0


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/gpban09/01_R4_Saito.jpg)*Tomoharu Saito sideboarding a considerable amount of cards.*


Saito's first two lands were [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)s in Game 2, perhaps explaining the extensive sideboarding he'd undertaken, while Mihara got his mana just right with a [Borderland Ranger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Borderland+Ranger), suiting it up with an [Oakenform](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oakenform) and smashing in for 5 while Saito filled up his hand with a [Divination](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination). [Cancel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cancel) trumped a [Stampeding Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stampeding+Rhino), and Saito wasted no time slamming a [Mind Control](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Control) of his own on the [Oakenform](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oakenform)‘d Ranger on his fifth turn, having played only [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)s so far this game. Mihara rebounded with an Awakening Druid, but a [Gorgon Flail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gorgon+Flail) wielding [Veteran Swordsmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Veteran+Swordsmith) looked like it would put pay to that. That is, until Mihara played his own [Mind Control](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Control), and then a [Siege-Gang Commander](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander). Having taken a great deal of damage early in the game, Saito was quick to pack up his cards.


Saito 1 – Mihara 1


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/gpban09/01_R4_Mihara.jpg)*Makihito Mihara eyes up Saito's meager life total.*


With much less sideboarding this time, Saito instead went into the tank over his opening seven, before finally electing to throw it back for one less. Mihara did the same, saving a great deal of time, thanks to the M10 rules on the matter. Both players kept their six. Saito had a turn two [Veteran Armorsmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Veteran+Armorsmith), while Mihara tidied up his mana with a [Terramorphic Expanse](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terramorphic+Expanse), and then a [Borderland Ranger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Borderland+Ranger). With only [Plains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains) on his side of the battlefield, Saito was still getting in there, summoning a [Stormfront Pegasus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormfront+Pegasus). Saito sent his Armorsmith in to battle with Mihara's [Cudgel Troll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cudgel+Troll) while he lacked the mana to regenerate. Mihara called the bluff and the Armorsmith was sent to the sin-bin. A [Wind Drake](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wind+Drake) traded with the Pegasus, while the Troll evened up the life totals. Saito tired to stall long enough to draw some [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)s by Excommunicating a [Stampeding Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stampeding+Rhino), but was soon sweeping up his cards in defeat.


Makihito Mihara defeats Tomoharu Saito 2 – 1

Saturday, August 22: -4:31p.m. – Round 5: Lost in Translation
-------------------------------------------------------------


*by Ray “blisterguy” Walkinshaw*

Veteran Japanese level 3 judge, Shindo Yoshiya has kept the peace in many a Grand Prix and Pro Tour event in his time, but his contributions to the game of Magic don't end there. Shindo moonlights as a translator for Wizards of the Coast, translating English Magic cards into Japanese. Shindo regaled us yesterday about how he couldn't get any of this work done in his hotel room, because the judge sharing a room with him could read English, and might get a glimpse of some of that top secret, tasty spoiler information that we're all on the edge of our seats for at this time of year. If he was after the Inside Scoop on Zendikar however, he'd be sorely disappointed, because that went to print months ago. Right now, Shindo is working on the first set of 2010, Worldwake! Okay, so perhaps “disappointed” is not the word I was looking for there. There are people out there who'd sell their Grandmothers, or their eye teeth, or even their Grandmother's eye teeth (if she still has any) to get a peek at some Worldwakery goodness, but Shindo is a tight-lipped man, who could only chuckle as he admitted that it wasn't uncommon for players to buy him drinks to see if they can't get his tongue wagging.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/gpban09/01_R5_shindo.jpg)*Japanese level 3 judge and Wizards of the Coast translator, Shindo Yoshiya*


But this weekend, we're playing with Magic 2010, and while Shindo can't tell us about anything that hasn't been released yet, he certainly has a story or two about the latest Base Set. “In Japanese,” he explained, “all the card names need to be Nouns. Usually, if something has been given a Verb name, it's easy enough to change it to a Noun, but [Regenerate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Regenerate) gave us some difficulty, because there was already a card called Regeneration! In the end, we gave it a new name with a similar meaning, something like ‘a new life' I think.” One can only guess they're hoping a card never gets printed with *that* as its name. Speaking of verbs and nouns and cards ending up with the same name, apparently they ran into a spot of bother a few years back when Mobilize and Mobilization ended up with the same card name in Japanese. Thankfully, one of those has only shown up in Portal so far, so it's unlikely that there'll be any decklist confusion, but Shindo has assured me that they now have a comprehensive database that will help ensure that never happens again.
