All Your Lightning Bolts Are Belong to Us
=========================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on July 8, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Over the course of the current Standard "season" I have covered as wide a variety of different—but successful and viable—decks and strategies as I could in this column. This really is one of the most vibrant and diverse Standard formats ... well ... um ... *ever*. I think that idea is borne out in the many variations and configurations of good cards that we have seen (and continue to see) in Top 8s from Pro Tour Qualifiers to Open events to real life Premier Events.

### Is There a Best Deck?

Honestly, it might still be Jund. Despite the many different flavors of [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) decks, ranging from Grixis to Bant colors, to four colors, Jund has continued to rack up Pro Tour Qualifier wins essentially every week. Undeterred by [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s and [Extractor Demon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extractor+Demon)s, Jund's [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning)s have continued to perform—and devastate—despite the shifting format's new brand of punishing disincentives.

If it isn't Jund, the most successful deck might be Mythic Conscription. What a great success story that deck has been over this run! Mythic went from a very good Pro Tour deck, care of Zvi Mowshowitz, to a week one winner in the hands of Lauren Lee (one of Zvi's real-life play test partners). But when the already-good Mythic joined with the [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara) and [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription), we witnessed the Voltron-ing of a good stack into one of the truly sublime deck concepts in Standard. The ability to tap for a one-time throwaway six drop and transform any creature—often a usually inoffensive [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) or even [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)—into a huge, annihilating, trampling, player-killing machine has changed not just Mythic, but other decks as well.

Over the course of this format we've seen White-Blue decks take up the [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription) banner.

[Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) is just that damn good.

Or at least it was.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td98_Prophetic_Prism.jpg)  
The people say that necessity is the mother of invention, and the resourcefulness of the villianous Jund players has shown us that the old adage continues to be true in modern **Magic**. Once upon a time it was too scary to play even [Borderland Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Borderland+Ranger) (what if all of my [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s, [Savage Lands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savage+Lands), and [Raging Ravine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raging+Ravine)s are *all* covered by [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)?). [Prophetic Prism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prophetic+Prism) has in large part unlocked the [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) stranglehold. Jund might not be able to operate optimally under a Seas barrage, but the two Seas draw is no longer a death knell. Subtly, Jund can play [Prophetic Prism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prophetic+Prism) no matter how many [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) are currently plaguing their mana bases.

We have seen Jace in service to giant Eldrazi creatures, and Jace teaming up with *Zendikar* Block buddies [Oracle of Mul Daya](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oracle+of+Mul+Daya) and [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) to produce dozens of 0/1 Plant tokens. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Oracle of Mul Daya | Avenger of Zendikar |

We have seen the rise of White-Blue control as an early leader through Jund, and the ongoing evolution of those colors from permission to tap-out to adding different colors and layers as disparate as [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) and [Abyssal Persecutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abyssal+Persecutor). We've seen the deck go big, and we've seen the deck rediscover [Emeria Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria+Angel)s ...

But what we haven't focused on much is the simple strategies that this most diverse of formats *also* allows for.

Because while it might seem, sometimes, that whoever draws more copies of [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) in a control mirror wins—it just isn't true. In fact, the best performing decks in recent weeks have not been based on lots of colors and mythic rare planeswalkers at all! The recent king—all due respect to Jund and Mythic Conscription—has been the Red Deck.

Katsuhiro Mori, already a Japanese National Champion and World Champion just added to his long list of titles with—you guessed it—the Red Deck.

Mori's Red Deck is reminiscent of Mispagel's, but tuned—at least according to Katsuhiro—to beat any kind of deck.

The main decks are pretty close, with Mori cutting most of the [Staggershock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Staggershock)s for a 25th land, main deck [Quenchable Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quenchable+Fire), and an extra [Earthquake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Earthquake). [Earthquake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Earthquake) is a deceptively effective burn spell against Bant opponents. It mops up all their little [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch)s and [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra)s while staving off next turn's attackers.

| --- | --- |
| Earthquake | Quenchable Fire |

What about [Quenchable Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quenchable+Fire)? What's so fantastic about that four-drop, that Mori would consider playing all four copies?

Against many decks (primarily Jund) [Quenchable Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quenchable+Fire) is one more mana for a [Ball Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning) that can't be blocked. It is a [Ball Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning) that doesn't get stopped by a [Doom Blade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade). It is a Ball Lighting that doesn't have two-thirds of its damage soaked up by [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens), or a [Ball Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning) that isn't completely lapped, outclassed, and generally humiliated by a [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel).

But what [Quenchable Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quenchable+Fire) mostly is, is a big 6 point packet of damage; you know, like a [Ball Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning).

Six damage is a lot for a deck that only really does one thing—take the opponent from 20 to 0 as efficiently as possible. It is like two [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s; it does the same damage as all three of Mori's [Burst Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning)s!

Mori's sideboard in particular is full of goodies that we don't necessarily associate with Red Decks. [Dragon's Claw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragon%27s+Claw) is a nice card that was played in Joey's StarCityGames.com Open winner as well as the Japan National Champion deck, but what about [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) and [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage)? Mori borrowed Tom Ross's Naya combination—some of the best cards in the format for dealing with removal-poor threat decks like [Polymorph](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polymorph) or Dredgevine, and put them into a deck that is usually considered much less tricky and elegant than some of the format's more colorful options.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Cunning Sparkmage | Basilisk Collar |

It is important to note that these successful Red Decks *don't* have something that we *have* come to associate with the deck style: [Goblin Bushwhacker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker) and [Devastating Summons](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Summons). It is by removing that (sometimes) explosive combination that the Red Decks have rediscovered Standard success, and their place near—or even at—the top of the format's, err ... *[Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain).*

Speaking of Tom Ross, the Boss Naya deck designer recently won himself a Pro Tour Qualifier on the back of [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) himself. *One* [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) that is!

This is literally just Tom's Boss Naya deck reinvented for Standard ... but in a different way than the Gerry Thomspon-led [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine) squad. You'll notice *that there aren't any [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s!*

The deck in particular started off as some "extra" **Magic Online** cards. Tom played them together and did well enough without four [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s. He [made a Top 8](http://www.wizards.com/magic/Digital/MagicOnlineTourn.aspx?x=mtg/digital/magiconline/tourn/1305133) and just [missed a Top 8](http://www.wizards.com/magic/Digital/MagicOnlineTourn.aspx?x=mtg/digital/magiconline/tourn/1305246). Even after finding some [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s, he decided he liked the old ways—and the flexibility of [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos), in particular—better. And it was without [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s that Ross took down his PTQ, with his signature deck.

![Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lightning+Bolt)  
One thing you might notice is how many singletons are in this list. One [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), one [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) ... maybe more notably one copy each of [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant), [Elspeth, Knight-Errant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant), and [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura). Ross had an interesting and non-intuitive (if you consider how the rest of the format is treating these cards) outlook. With only one copy of each of his color's planeswalkers, Tom could not accidentally draw multiple copies, clogging up his hand with relatively slow threats!

The great thing about the success of the new / old no-Devastating Red Decks, and of the Boss Naya throwback—a Pro Tour Qualifier where Ross *lost* every single flip and die roll, incidentally—is how players can be competitive with even more different decks and colors. Some of the strategies aren't even directly related to adding [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) to Jund or Naya! The [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) school is as effective as any of the options with fancier mana bases, and in the case of the Red Deck in particular, might just be that mythical best deck.
