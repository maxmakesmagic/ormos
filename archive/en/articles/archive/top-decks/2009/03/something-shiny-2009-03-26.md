===============



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on March 26, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic."

I hope you don't mind. This week we have a triumphant champion, a deck I openly wish I made myself, a deck I secretly wish I made, and as a shiny visit from the Great Glass-Spinner. Let's go!

This deck represents a fair departure not just from the more familiar Zoo / Domain Zoo decks that we have seen and discussed for some months, but also from the more recent Naya *Burn* decks popularized by Adam Prosak via Luis Scott-Vargas and others.

While it may be Naya colors and look like a Zoo deck, I don't think that that is how Saito's deck really plays out. The Zoo decks of recent years have been all about playing the strongest possible cards regardless of colors and / or pushing the boundaries of the available domain cards such as [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames) or [Might of Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Might+of+Alara) (especially when combined with double striking creatures such as [Boros Swiftblade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Swiftblade) or [Viashino Slaughtermaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viashino+Slaughtermaster)). 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Might of Alara | Viashino Slaughtermaster |

Separately, the so-called Naya Burn decks are really improved three-color takes on the [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) Deck. They usually play the [Keldon Marauders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Keldon+Marauders) but replace the quick-but-expendable [Spark Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spark+Elemental) with a recurring source of 3 damage for one mana in [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl). But even though they find room for one of the best offensive two-drops in history (and I speak naturally of [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)), Naya Burn still preserves the spirit of the [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) Deck with [Sulfuric Vortex](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sulfuric+Vortex) and lots of burn spells.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Wild Nacatl | Sulfuric Vortex |

Saito's deck, though, isn't really that deck. Limiting to three colors, he doesn't really push the envelope with every [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant), and he also doesn't keep the spirit of the burn deck close to his heart... Saito's spell selection includes not only main-deck [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte), but [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) (an efficient removal spell, sure ... but one that does no damage). His creatures are ... interesting. [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) over [Keldon Marauders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Keldon+Marauders) is possible thanks to Saito's modified mana base (more on that in a minute); less aggressive, but quite saucy in the face of a [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) or even a more egalitarian [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command). The singleton [Isamaru, Hound of Konda](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isamaru%2C+Hound+of+Konda) as a first-turn redundancy over [Kird Ape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape) and [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl) is quite cute, and in fact, made possible by that aforementioned change in the mana base. Looking at this deck my friend Brian David-Marshall joked that Saito probably played the Hound first turn off the one [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains)—at least once—and had his hapless foe convinced if for a moment he was up against White Weenie.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Gaddock Teeg | Isamaru, Hound of Konda |

Wooly Thoctar takes the place of [Sulfuric Vortex](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sulfuric+Vortex) at the three, rounding out a general philosophy of decidedly *not* extreme deck design. Saito's deck is about playing good creatures and flexible spells. While it *appears* simple, it is my opinion that this is a deck for a master, not a neophyte.

Although not as punishing as Domain Zoo in the early turns, the mana base of Saito's Naya is one that will consistently present challenges with regards to mulligan decisions or which path to follow on a [Windswept Heath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windswept+Heath), [Bloodstained Mire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodstained+Mire), or [Wooded Foothills](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wooded+Foothills). There is enough burn to give Saito a "hot hand" victory a couple of times per tournament, but not nearly enough to lean back on "stupid burn" wins enjoyed by the [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) Deck and to a lesser extent Naya Burn. Instead, this deck allows a strong player to hose some opponents with [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg), pressure most with efficient threats (3/3 on turn one, \*/\*+1 on turn two, 5/4 on turn three) with enough spell support to get those threats through—whether that support is in the form of just getting a blocker out of the way or closing the final 3-6 points outside the red zone.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td31_VolcanicBlowout.jpg)So why this kind of a deck over Zoo? It is certainly lower on the power curve (no [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)) and potential explosiveness (no dramatic [Might of Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Might+of+Alara) for 14 on turn three). The answer is in the mana base. Zoo's mana base is quite un-fun to execute upon sometimes, whereas Saito's deck can actually *not* take extensive damage sometimes (whereas Zoo so often starts on 14). The ample basic lands in this deck make it relatively friendly with [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile), a concern for most people in the room at this point.

I was a bit surprised at the utter lack of Affinity hate in Saito's sideboard (one [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) only), but I quite like some of his choices anyway. Of particular interest is [Volcanic Fallout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Fallout) (a.k.a. Volcanic Blowout), a "get out of jail free" card of sorts, at least against some fluttering foes. Look for this card to displace other Fae-fighting options in decks that have a fair number of 3-toughness creatures (and this deck has many, even on one mana).

Adding [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) to Extended Zoo (or Naya) is an idea that has been burning in the back of my mind since seeing the Ranger gather [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl)s ... in Legacy*.* As you can see, Bill's deck is similar to a sideboarded version of Saito's, but with a dedicated Ranger configuration instead of main deck [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile).

So how does this deck work? 

Painted with a broad brush, Bill's deck is a lot like Saito's, a flexible offensive creature deck with burn support rather than a burn deck that treats its creatures like more efficient burn cards (a la Naya Burn). Like Saito's deck, Bill's has some [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Umezawa%2527s%2BJitte)s and some burn, but those are there for support. Stark has got the Wooly Thoctars, and of course, it goes even further up the mana curve with the four-cost [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos).

[Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) is a good creature even when all it is doing is getting a couple of [Kird Ape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape)s to reload after an opposing [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God). But in this case, it is also a precision tool. Bill has the singleton [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender) to foil red spells, and a singleton [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny) (which can grow up to be a 4/4 or even larger threat). When you need a "burn spell" it can even fetch [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) (as it often does in Standard).

The swap Blair made was to abandon the equipment hallmark of the Bant strategy in favor of [Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kira%2C+Great+Glass-Spinner). [Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kira%252C%2BGreat%2BGlass-Spinner) is a brand new rediscovered interactive offensive weapon, and a special one given some of the traditional vulnerabilities of the strategy.

[Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kira%252C%2BGreat%2BGlass-Spinner) plus [Ethersworn Canonist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethersworn+Canonist) is a lock against the Storm combination decks, and pretty rough for Elves. More interesting, though, is what [Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kira%252C%2BGreat%2BGlass-Spinner) will do to a single [Vedalken Shackles](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vedalken+Shackles). Basically, [Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kira%252C%2BGreat%2BGlass-Spinner) turns that particular annoyance completely off.



|  |  |

| Kira, Great Glass-Spinner | Vedalken Shackles |

[Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kira%252C%2BGreat%2BGlass-Spinner) is fundamentally powerful given the right basic set of parameters; that is, the Great Glass-Spinner helps out everyone on your team so long as someone is somehow out to get them .... She is going to be turning off Shackles and generating card advantage even when the opponent is playing optimally. But because she is seldom seen, opponents will be surprisingly prone to making errors while she is in play. All those little mistakes that people make when playing on autopilot will occur more and more often. I can already imagine myself trying to "finish off" damaged creatures with burn spells that are about to be countered, maybe tossing [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Umezawa%2527s%2BJitte) counters into the primordial muck. You can probably imagine yourself doing the same, hopefully chuckling. If we are both very honest, we probably know that we would do it, and do it again, in the same game. Again even.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td31_kiraAndFriends.jpg)[Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kira%252C%2BGreat%2BGlass-Spinner) is cool but playing with her does come with a price. Blair's deck is never going to win a [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Umezawa%2527s%2BJitte) fight, because [Kira, Great Glass-Spinner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kira%252C%2BGreat%2BGlass-Spinner) and Equipment don't get along. Similarly, there is no [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) free win against Fae. Perhaps worst of all, the deck is a little slower on the offense when playing against control for the same reasons. Put simply, his creatures are good ... but that's about as good as they are going to get. 

However the counterbalance of playing with a card that can generate so much advantage given the right opponents just might be worth cashing in the [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Umezawa%2527s%2BJitte)s. Certainly something worth looking at if you are interested in the Bant, as many strong players have been in recent weeks.

Jonathan Loucks, one of the designers behind last year's popular [Makeshift Mannequin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makeshift+Mannequin) deck, was the actual winner of the Seattle PTQ where Bill Stark made Top 8. In a star-studded Top 8, Jonathan defeated former [Magic Academy](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Archive.aspx?author=jeff%20cunningham&description=Magic%20Academy) writer and Grand Prix Champion Jeff "ffej" Cunningham in the finals.

So how does this cool new deck work?



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker | Pestermite |

In essence it is a hybrid combo-control deck. The "obvious" combination is [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) + [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite). [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kiki-Jiki%252C%2BMirror%2BBreaker) copies a [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), a [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) token comes into play, untapping [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kiki-Jiki%252C%2BMirror%2BBreaker). Rinse and repeat to set up essentially infinite damage (you can just keep untapping [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kiki-Jiki%252C%2BMirror%2BBreaker) with incremental [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) tokens so you are not limited to 20 damage as you are with some combo decks).

The hybrid wings work like this: Notice the power on [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kiki-Jiki%252C%2BMirror%2BBreaker) and [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite)? That's right. They are just powerful enough to hang with [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark). [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) (and the numerous strategic singletons) also work well with [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven). Now imagine we live in a dream world where the opponent would do what I am about to describe (for illustration's sake).

You play [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) and find [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker), [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), and [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double). The opponent buries the combo pieces, leaving you with [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) and [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double). You can untap, play your fifth land and the [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox), evoke [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), and go ahead and kill with your combo. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td31_giftsChoice.jpg)  
On top of all of this, Jonathan's deck is also a [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) deck, and a pretty good one; Spellbombs, Explosives, [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void), and so on are easily available. The [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) can even go [Civic Wayfinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Civic+Wayfinder), grabbing [Great Furnace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Great+Furnace) for red mana, or [Ancient Den](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Den) for white (remember: if you are using [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) to fix your mana, you probably already have a blue source).

So why play this kind of a deck over some of the faster, harder-to-counter combo decks such as Elves or Storm? The reason is pretty simple .... This is a two-card kill *that you can actually cast.* Imagine just running out a [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) on the opponent's turn, then untapping, playing [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kiki-Jiki%252C%2BMirror%2BBreaker), and winning on the spot. While a bit little slower than Elves, and a little more open to interaction than Storm, the sheer ease of this combo makes it attractive in a deck that can also play a "regular" game of progressive card advantage.

My inclination was—at least originally—to make this a faster, more consistent, combo deck. I was going to cut the [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), but ....

[Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), while seemingly out of place in this build, offers an important backup plan. Unlike many combo decks, this one can actually present problems—and ride card advantage—even when its most flamboyant way to win has been cut off. In the finals, Cunningham's modified Bant deck played Cranial Extracion taking [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kiki-Jiki%252C%2BMirror%2BBreaker) out of the equation. Jonathan was able to regroup and win with [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) beatdown (after landing [Pithing Needle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle) on [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) of course); it was just Finks, [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage)s, and [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark)s in the red zone, winning essentially a fair fight—but one where Loucks had all the routes to card advantage.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td31_fairFight.jpg)  
All in all, I feel like Jonathan's is one of the most inventiveand surprizingly flexible – PTQ winners to have come out this season. It is a rare deck that both has a potential turn-four kill *and* can play essentially The Rock-style **Magic** well enough to win an ordinary long game against decks that either don't demand a fast combo or are sitting on a [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic).
