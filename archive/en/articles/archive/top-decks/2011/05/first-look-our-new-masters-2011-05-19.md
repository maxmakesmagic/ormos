The First Look at Our New Masters
=================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 19, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






The StarCityGames.com Open Series event in Orlando, Florida gave us our first big event look at our brave New Phyrexian world.

Some stuff stayed the same, some not-that-old stuff dusted off the cobwebs, some players continued to excel with both old and new decks, and, as predicted, there is an all-new power casting a long and combo-tacular shadow across the state of Standard.

But first, the Top 8:

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td143_chart.jpg)  
### Caw-Blade

The event was won by Star City Games mainstay Edgar Flores with—surprise, surprise—White-Blue Caw-Blade. While some other notable players slid back into DarkBlade mode, splashing black to handle the anticipated Exarch Twin and / or [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) combo decks, Flores stayed straight and true to his two-color build.

Edgar is *so* straight white-blue he didn't even dip into Phyrexian black mana for [Dismember](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember), as some players have started doing, regardless of their "true" deck colors.

| Batterskull | Sword of War and Peace |

[Batterskull](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull) and [Sword of War and Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+War+and+Peace) are obvious additions to Standard [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) decks. One is a supplemental Sword—not [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine), but with much more game impact than [Sword of Body and Mind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Body+and+Mind) usually has—and the other makes for a fine replacement for the sometimes (and originally played) [Sylvok Lifestaff](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvok+Lifestaff). Another thing to keep in mind is that [Sword of War and Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+War+and+Peace)—for the first time—gives a Caw-Blade player the chance to run roughshod over another Caw-Blade player, even if the latter has access to [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk)s. Previously you could hide behind a succession of [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk)s while setting up your own board / Sword position, but depending on which Sword the opponent has gone for, this may no longer be a defensive option.

Edgar plays Caw-Blade a bit differently than most players. You will note that he only has two copies of [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) (both in the sideboard), and does not play [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura) at all. Instead, Flores plays tons of super fast one- and two-mana plays ([Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn), [Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce), [Oust](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oust)) and a slightly lighter land count relative to most other Caw-Blade players (26 this week, up from 25).

[Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil) is another card that not all Caw-Blade players run, but given the diverse new threats that the *New Phyrexia* reality is presenting us with, [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil) seems like a fine choice. Of course you can live the dream against a [Precursor Golem](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Precursor+Golem) (bounce all their Golems and draw three cards), but it is also a wide enough defensive card that it can be applied to [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) (four toughness) or even [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) (same).

![Into the Roil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Into+the+Roil)  
*Why* you might want to hit one of those, we will address in a moment.

### DarkBlade

Despite Edgar's win, most of the top-performing Caw-Blade decks were all playing DarkBlade (that is black-splashing) variants. These decks retain the core of Caw-Blade ([Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) in a White-Blue Control shell), but add cheap black discard to shift more towards proactive disruption rather than flexible control.

Kitt Holland ("Mister" [MTGMom](http://mtgmom.com/)) and newly-minted GP Top 8 competitor Bertoncini played identical DarkBlade decks.

Like Edgar Flores and his white-blue version, these DarkBlade decks played both [Batterskull](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull) and [Sword of War and Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+War+and+Peace) as main-deck Equipment additions. That is where they are the same as Caw-Blade. Where they are different, they are very different.

Where Flores played a semi-traditional package of "just" the eight two-drops, Holland and Bertoncini added [Emeria Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria+Angel). [Emeria Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria+Angel), of course, can produce one [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk)-equivalent per turn (or even more, with [Marsh Flats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Marsh+Flats)).

![Emeria Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Emeria+Angel)  
Instead of four [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s and four [Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce)s and three copies of [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil) as a final "Get Out of Jail Free" card, the DarkBlade decks played a mere two permission spells and one [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil). Instead, they supplemented instants with [Go for the Throat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Go+for+the+Throat), which is often much better than [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil) (but not when you are fighting a [Precursor Golem](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Precursor+Golem) and have four mana untapped).

![Go for the Throat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Go+for+the+Throat)  
The real incentive to play DarkBlade, beyond getting to upgrade your Jace-fighting creature-land to [Creeping Tar Pit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Tar+Pit), is the black discard. In addition to four copies of [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek)—the leader of cards of this type in Standard, as it can grab anything from a [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide) to a [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)—Kitt and Alex added [Despise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Despise). This new discard spell can take out any planeswalker where [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek) can nab [Jace Beleren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren) only, and both can nab [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch).

![Inquisition of Kozilek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek)  
And that is really why many good players shifted to black: The approach of the [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) combo decks (see more below). Their sideboards reinforced the disruption, adding [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) to the discard, as well as [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) as a proactive, preemptive answer to [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin). As a two-drop 0/4, [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) can block early while you set up your Swords, or even carry one (it might be built like a Wall, but [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) can still attack).

Extra [Batterskull](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull)s out of the sideboard can help dominate beatdown decks.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td143_dismember.jpg)  
[Dismember](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember) is a great way to showcase Phyrexian mana. Yes, the DarkBlade deck can tap for black... but the flexibility of this spell in particular is that you can have only white- and blue-producing lands in play... *and still cast [Dismember](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember)!* For ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)!

![Dismember](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dismember)  
While the specific capabilities of these decks is a bit different (more or less permission, more or less removal, discard or no), from an offensive standpoint, it is important to realize that all four of them can slam down a second-turn [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic)l, and / or sit behind [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor).

### The Exarch Twin Decks

The basic combination:



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Deceiver Exarch | Splinter Twin |

Cast [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) (usually at the end of the opponent's turn); untap and cast [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) on it.

[Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) can tap to make a [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), which will untap the first [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) so it can tap to make another [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)... roughly infinity times.

*Ta-da!*

Because [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) and [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) are only two cards, and one of them is Blue, the combo can be grafted into a number of different decks, cheaply (Tim Landale didn't even play the full four [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin)s in his RUG).

This Grixis Exarch Twin deck is a combination of disruption, permission, control elements, and specifically chosen creatures.

[Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) is obvious in its inclusion... and in the long run, [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) might have the same caption. [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) buys a deck like this the time necessary to set up the two-card combination, and can defend [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) from removal. In addition, in a mirror match, [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) can "steal" someone else's [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) to disrupt the combo.

Lots of cool one-of functionality cards in Strunk's sideboard...


> 
> * [Combust](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Combust): It's not just for [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel)s anymore! [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) has four toughness, making it hard to kill with red instants... but still easy prey for [Combust](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Combust).
> * [Twisted Image](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twisted+Image): Cute against [Precursor Golem](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Precursor+Golem), awesome against other people's [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite)s.

So this deck still gets turn-two [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra). It still gets to screw around with [Oracle of Mul Daya](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oracle+of+Mul+Daya) and go very large with [Inferno Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inferno+Titan).

> * [Demon of Death's Gate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demon+of+Death%27s+Gate): The 6 life is negligible if you were, for instance, going to be hit with a 6/6. Little guys... Get big!

Ah, [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td143_valakut.jpg)  
[Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) itself is maybe the most overpowered, undercosted cardboard in Standard (I mean look at the card's Extended performance). But most players have somehow (maybe willfully) forgotten [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut)'s former scary-monster position in a rising firestorm of Jace-ire (that maybe really should just be Caw-Blade-appreciation).

So how does this deck, once a proto-boogeyman, now largely ignored due to the rise of [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine), struggle once again into a position of relevance?

The answer seems to be [Nature's Claim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Claim).

![Nature's Claim](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Nature%27s+Claim)  
Mana-ramp-into-[Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut) (or [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan), which itself propels into [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut)) is still one of the fastest, most powerful, things you van do in Standard. It is just that the increasingly low curve of white-blue, with its second-turn [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic)s and [Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce) backup, has positioned itself as such a flawless foil.

So what does [Nature's Claim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Claim) do?

This instant is like [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut)'s [Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce). Conditional... but quick (and backbreaking when it hits). For all its strength and speed, Caw-Blade still needs to devote maybe four mana on its own turn for an optimal Feast and [Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Famine) run. [Nature's Claim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Claim) can disrupt and destroy the card that made the deck and reduce the best strategy to a tapped-out 1/1 swing. Wow.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td143_claim.jpg)  
How about Exarch Twin? [Nature's Claim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Claim) is very much the [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut) answer to [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek) here. For one mana, elbowing its way main deck, it's very good at disrupting the pretty new combo (kill your [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin)).

Last gasp at relevance? Maybe an overstatement, but consider the mission accomplished, and the door is wide open. Many top players hate running reactive stuff like [Nature's Claim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Claim) main, but in Miguel's case, it seems like a good mite of pre-sideboarding, helping to facilitate the [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut) comeback.

So there you have it, our first look.

Despite the mighty Edgar's win (probably player much more than deck, here) my guess is that the mono-dominance of Caw-Blade will soon give way to increasingly awesome Twin decks, Grixis and RUG being just the beginning. So that's where we'll leave it...

*New Phyrexia*: The Beginning.
