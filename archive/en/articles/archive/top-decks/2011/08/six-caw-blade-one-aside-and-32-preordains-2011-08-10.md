Six Caw-Blade, One Aside, and 32 Preordains
===========================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on August 11, 2011 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores












> **Brian David-Marshall:** How did you like the [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage)s in LSV's sideboard?  
> **Me:** HOW DO YOU THINK I LIKED THEM???
> 
> 

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Azure+Mage)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage)  
[Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) is kinda sorta a perfect card.

  
[Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) is a 2-power beater for two mana. Anybody who is anybody ([Meddling Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meddling+Mage), [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant), [Voidmage Prodigy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Voidmage+Prodigy), etc.) packs 2 power into two mana, along with a relevant special ability ([Wild Mongrel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) in Green-Blue Madness, [Gaea's Skyfolk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Skyfolk) in Miracle Grow, or [Ironclaw Orcs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ironclaw+Orcs) in the original Sligh deck... wouldn't want to accidentally block). This is known.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

The unlikely [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) has the power to transform the tone of a game or match-up.

First and foremost, [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) is a 2/1 for two, so it can put on the beats. If you're positioning yourself, say, against a control deck *as* a control deck, the fact that there is a 2/1 on the battlefield on turn two can be tremendously challenging. If you were anticipating an aggressive deck, that would be one thing, but what are you supposed to do if your deck is set up for White-Blue? When are you supposed to tap? How many beats for 2 are you supposed to take?

Just getting in for damage can be great. As we've said, anyone who's anyone, ever, has been some kind of 2/X attacker for two mana; and there's a reason for that. "Bears" put pressure on opponents and create situations where the opponent is losing mana and options. What is great about [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) is that, come turn four, it generates more options *for you.*

As we've hinted at already, there may be no good play for an opponent being pressured by [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage). "Oh well." Maybe you have a one-turn window; maybe you don't have a relevant card because your hand is full of spells that are supposed to combat [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura). Is your most realistic option really turn four? Against 3+ [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s and 2+ [Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce)s? Might want to look up the definition of *most realistic* there. If you are doing nothing and biding your time, come four mana, [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) is going to do what distinguishes [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) (you know, the thing [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) was already doing, and not for four mana), exactly.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

[Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) really seems like a perfect **Magic** card.

Two-power two-drop: Check.

Relevant ability: Check.

Not grossly overpowered: Check.

Seems ingenious when someone actually plays it?

I think that is the most wondrous thing about [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage), and part of the reason I so admire its recent inclusion in the [US Nationals](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/usnat11/welcome) Top 8 deck lists. Lots of us have talked about how much we like [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage), but no one—no one until Luis, Ochoa, Owen, and company that is—had the Stone(forge)s to actually run one. [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) is great because it is opportunistic, and requires a pinch of bravery. [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) last weekend was a sign of banished indecision and good positioning; no, it was not a "main deck" card (at least not so far, nor in the decks it actually appeared), but a lot of really high-impact stuff is initially relegated to sideboard real estate. No harm there.

***Aside on Sideboarding in General***

One thing many players fail to realize is that sideboard games are statistically more important than regular games.

Imagine your matches generally reach reasonable conclusion (that is, you don't accumulate a rampant number of draws). In each match you will play a Game 1... *and* you will play a Game 2 (sideboarded). However in some matches, you will also play a Game 3 (which is also sideboarded). Ergo over the course of a tournament you will generally play more sideboarded matches than Game 1s.

Having powerful, effective, or somewhat unexpected sideboard cards (like [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage)) can give you an edge during these generally more significant games.

***End Aside***

Yes, yes... The consensus best player in the United States played two copies of [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage) in his US Nationals Top 8 deck list. How about the other 73?

Not to spoil the ending overmuch, but there were six total Caw-Blade decks in the Top 8, and half of those were of the same mold as Luis's list. David Ochoa ran the same 75, and Owen Turtenwald ran one fewer [Scalding Tarn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scalding+Tarn), and one more [Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce).

|  |  |

The ChannelFireball crew, which in this Top 8 included Player of the Year standout Turtenwald and US Nationals finalist Ochoa played what can nominally be called "Caw-Blade" but incorporated a number of specific alterations and innovations. Among them:


> * Slightly higher land count: LSV and Ochoa played 27 lands, which along with 4 [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain)s, functions as approximately 48% mana (see also [UB Trippin](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/daily/mf38)'); remember that some Caw-Blade decks of the recent past have dropped to land counts as "low" as 25 lands.
> * Creature configuration: "Classic" Caw-Blade decks played four [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk)s and four [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic)s, plus maybe one [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan). More recent lists have gone the route of [Hero of Bladehold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold) or [Blade Splicer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blade+Splicer). Based on the Channel[Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) configuration, it looks like [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx) is going to be standard in Standard as a double. Amazingly, even the blue-red combo deck had two [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx)es (if in the sideboard)!
> * Two Swords, two [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment), three [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura)... only one [Jace Beleren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren). Jace's card drawing may be counterbalanced by the sideboarding of [Azure Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azure+Mage).
> * [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep)s; actually, five of six Top 8 Caw-Blade decks (and six of eight decks overall) played three [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep)s in the sideboard. The other three Caw-Blade players in the Top 8 ran only two copies of [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura) main.
>

His creature configuration was quite a bit different... only two [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx)es but two [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan)s. Obviously [Torpor Orb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Torpor+Orb) is a different card from [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite); one can hose a number of different "enters the battlefield" creature strategies, while the other blocks on the ground, can hose Poison's pump, etc.

Caw-Blade in the post–[Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) Standard era is a bit of a different beast than Caw-Blade at its height. If you want to equip a Sword, you need to spend the full five mana to cast and equip it (though if you get a hit in with it, you will typically more than make that action up).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td155_timely.jpg)  
Generally, Caw-Blade has the ability to play aggro-control (creature, possibly Sword up, defending the lead with countermagic backup)... but it can also play a kind of true control. All these decks play a variety of planeswalkers, so they can go planeswalker control, especially leaning on two or three copies of [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura) per deck as defense. While none of them specialized in defense in particular, [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) was a part of all their sorcerous vocabularies. Expect access to this breaker if you are thinking about swarming the table.

The printing of [Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe) promised to bring with it a new age of [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension), and the reprinting of [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) in ***Magic** 2012* simply solidified that possibility.

[Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) in 2012 lacks the capacity for infinite turns (or infinite *anything*) based on how James configured his deck, but the huge number of cheap draw spells—[Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe), [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain), [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder), and maybe even [See Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=See+Beyond)—helps the deck put two counters on [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) and get a critical mass of cards once that key enchantment is online.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

From that point, winning is relatively simple. Every spell you run—say a [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain)—doubles up by itself, so one-mana draw spells turn into major-league digging and card advantage, and [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) and [Burst Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning) become lethal very quickly (it doesn't take too many turns once your [Burst Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning)s start hitting for 8).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td155_pyromancer.jpg)  
I mentioned previously the presence of [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx). One of the potential problems with [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) is that configured like this, it is very dependent on actually having the Ascension in play. Especially in sideboarded games, keeping Ascension on the table is anything but automatic. [Celestial Purge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Purge), [Vampire Hexmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Hexmage), even [Nature's Claim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Claim) can make winning... inconvenient.

Say the opponent left in [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) (one of the relatively few main-deck-able cards that can interact with a [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension)). If that same opponent wants to apply the Ring to [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx), it's going to mean shipping you two cards first... *and* you are playing a deck with [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil)!

This build of Blue-Black Control echoes some earlier lists (a mix of planeswalkers, but tops up on [Karn Liberated](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn+Liberated)), with light countermagic and several different flavors of creature removal... but it has some newer features as well.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Solemn+Simulacrum)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum)  
[Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum) very much fills the "[Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)" slot in this deck. It costs four mana, helps Ali get a bit ahead on cards, and can defend the battlefield, if not decisively. [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum) is well known for the four-to-six, allowing Blue-Black Control decks to go straight from four mana to a place where [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine), [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx), and [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) are fifth-turn possibilities.

Ali played a few unusual cards in his sideboard (the total mix included eleven different spells); some of them bear a mention:


> * [Despise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Despise) and [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress): Here to bolster the main-deck quartet of [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek). One-mana discard spells improve the flow of information and can trade with much more significant stuff.
> * [Peace Strider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Peace+Strider): Because sometimes you want to gain 3 life.
> * [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine): Because sometimes you want to gain 6, 12, 18, or even 24 life.
> * [Memoricide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memoricide), [Praetor's Grasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Praetor%27s+Grasp), [Surgical Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Surgical+Extraction): Sequence-breakers all.
> 

So... What next?

It seems pretty clear that Caw-Blade is going to continue to prove an über-popular deck despite the recent bannings. [Karn Liberated](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn+Liberated)'s Blue-Black Control, on the radar via Gerard Fabiano and Reid Duke since approximately the TCGPlayer.com event in NYC (prior to the bannings), is going to pick up in popularity if simply because of Ali's win.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td155_sphinx.jpg)  
[Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) continues to help people draw lands early and spells late. Make sure that you can handle two [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx)es, because just about everyone has a pair nowadays.
