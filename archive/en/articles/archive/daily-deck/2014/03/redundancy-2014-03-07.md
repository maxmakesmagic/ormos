Redundancy
==========



 Posted in **Daily Deck**
 on March 10, 2014 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











Over the past couple of weeks, we talked about how some cards are better than others in specific ways.

  
While few people would argue that...

  
...is a better card than...

  
 ...most players with a few years under their belts probably recognize the viability—and in some formats strong impact—of a card like the former. ([Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), of course, is a cross-format All-Star and possibly the best red card of all time that, few, if any, players have problems recognizing as desirable.) 

 Now speaking of [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), we know that... 



|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
|  |  |  |

  
*[Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) offers a more powerful effect than [Shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock), at the same cost.* 

And



|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
|  |  |  |

*[Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) offers the same effect as [Lightning Strike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Strike), but at lower cost.* 

 There are implications of both [strict superiority](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/lo/289) and efficacy around [casting costs](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/lo/288) in both examples. 

However!

We will often find ourselves playing cards like...

[Shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock), or... 

[Lightning Strike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Strike), or... 

[Searing Spear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Searing+Spear), or even... 

[Volcanic Hammer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Hammer)... 

 ...even in wide formats that offer clearly superior cards like [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt).

Andy Burden's PTQ winner has many seemingly strange cards.

[Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) we get. [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) is great! But... 

[Lava Spike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Spike) is a [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) that (a) can only be cast on your own turn and (b) can only hit players. 

[Shard Volley](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shard+Volley), though... here we have most of the text of a [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), but at grievous cost. 

Ouch.

Why?

How?

How could a deck with so many weird and clearly inferior cards have won such a tough kind of event as a large-format Modern PTQ?

 Even the cards that are not strictly inferior to [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) are kind of nudge-nudge/wink-wink worse. [Rift Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt), for instance, is just a slow [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) that gives up information on the first turn, and then an inflexible three-mana [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) later in the game. 

[Burst Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning) and [Bump in the Night](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bump+in+the+Night) both have interesting upsides... but only when you have a ton of mana. In most games, they are [Shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock) and [a black] [Lava Spike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Spike), respectively. 

What's going on here?

*Redundancy.*

 In competitive Constructed **Magic** we have what is called The Rule of Four. 

 That is, excepting particular rule-breaker cards (e.g., [Relentless Rats](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relentless+Rats)) you can only play a maximum total of four cards of any particular name. But who hasn't wanted to play a deck of "all" [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s?

 Wouldn't it be fun to play—can't you easily imagine the power of playing—a deck of "all" [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s [and lands]? The problem is the pesky Rule of Four says that you can only play four cards named [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt). So if you want to play more than four... you need to branch out to probably inferior versions... *that largely do the same things.* Which is how we get decks like Burden's.

 Although he wasn't allowed at the time to play [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) itself, you can see that legendary **Magic**ian and Hall of Famer Jon Finkel tried very hard to play a deck of "all [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s" himself. 

[Spark Elemental](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spark+Elemental), [Lava Spike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Spike), and [Rift Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt) would all have been cards that tried to get at the spirit of paying ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif) to deal 3 points of damage directly to the opponent; [Incinerate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incinerate) (better than the yet-to-be-printed [Lightning Strike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Strike), maybe) was essentially a two-mana [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), Jr., here... I hope you get the idea. This deck was not about picking the very best cards for their functions; it was not about evaluations like we talked about last week; rather, this was a deck that had an idea of what it wanted \_*a*\_ card to do... and tried to pick many cards that had similar outputs at reasonable (and similar) casting costs and jammed them all together. 

 Redundancy isn't a concept that works exclusively with burn cards. Where there is a function on a **Magic** card, there is a possible avenue for finding similar cards, presuming you want more than four copies. Check out this deck that Hall of Famer Huey Jensen played a few months back...

Simply, the deck really, really wanted to play a turn-one 1/1 mana acceleration card... potentially into that turn-two [Elvish Archdruid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid)! Many considerations of this deck center around the desire to maximize playing 1/1 acceleration creatures for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif).

 Because of this, Jensen played every single such creature that was available for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) in the format. *Every single one.*The presence of the three similar creatures—[Arbor Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arbor+Elf), [Avacyn's Pilgrim](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacyn%27s+Pilgrim), and [Elvish Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Mystic)—actually gave structure to the deck as a whole. 

 Now, what happens when you play a deck like this is that you hope to get similar draws consistently, draws that have a 1/1 acceleration creature for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) in your opening hand. This allows you to get more mana rolling—and quickly—than other decks in the room. Hopefully, you can do something explosive with that mana, like playing huge threats *a la* [Garruk, Caller of Beasts](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk%2C+Caller+of+Beasts) and [Kalonian Hydra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kalonian+Hydra), quickly.

[Elvish Archdruid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid) and [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) are two other cards that turn a large volume of usually small creatures (especially Elves) into winning attack forces.

 Duke dug really deep... but at the same time knew what he wanted to play. You might be more familiar with [Elvish Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Mystic), but Reid played both [Fyndhorn Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fyndhorn+Elves) and the original, [Llanowar Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves). *And...* he played the combination of [Green Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) and [Dryad Arbor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dryad+Arbor). On the first turn of the game, a [Green Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) fetching a 0 CMC [Dryad Arbor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dryad+Arbor) is quite similar to a [Llanowar Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves)/[Fyndhorn Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fyndhorn+Elves).

 With the tools and examples from this article, you can probably see that Cuneo built a deck that was *redundant* in its counterspell composition in the same way that Jon played lots of [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s and Huey and Reid played lots of 1/1s for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif). 

 About one in four of Andrew's cards was some kind of counterspell (including four copies of the original [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell), of course). He played the next best thing in [Dissipate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dissipate), and the [card advantage](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/lo/286)–bestowing version [Dismiss](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismiss) as well. But this deck was redundant not only in that it played lots of cards *that could potentially counter one of the opponent's spells* but paid attention to *when* it could do so. 

 Andrew decided he wanted to play not only four [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s, but that *he wanted to be able to counter the opponent's play on turn two.* [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) might be the most obvious way to do that... but he was still constrained by the Rule of Four. However, by adding those two [Power Sink](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Power+Sink)s, he was able to increase the redundancy by which he could counter on turn two... when the opponent tapped out, [Power Sink](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Power+Sink) could do much the same thing.

 This is Anssi Alkio's deck from the Top 8 of last month's [Pro Tour *Born of the Gods*](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptbng14/welcome). The central strategy of this deck is to enchant [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) with [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin). A [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) so enchanted can tap to produce a [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) copy that will untap the original (enchanted) one... allowing it to tap to make another copy, which will in turn untap the original, which in turn can be accomplished any number of times. 

 The opponent then finds him- or herself attacked by a seemingly infinite number of [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)s.

 Now, as this is the ultimate goal of Alkio's deck, he played four copies each of [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) and [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), maximizing the chance to pair the two... but that was not enough for Alkio. *In addition*, he played two [Pestermite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite)s as the *redundant* "fifth and sixth" [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)s. [Pestermite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) has only 1 toughness; one of the things a [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) might do is contain a [Bitterblossom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) or [Wild Nacatl](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl), buying time to pair with [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin). [Pestermite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) is not as good at that kind of stuff... but is equally good at making infinite attackers. 

 Alkio also played [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) as a "fifth" [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin). [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) can kill an opponent with a summoning-sick [Pestermite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), but is otherwise much weaker than [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin). It costs more than [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), costs a third red mana, and—unlike [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin)—can be stopped by a [Shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock). But when you decide you want to play with more than four copies of [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin)... you might dip into the five-mana 2/2 version.

Redundancy can be a tricky arrow to add to your quiver. For players who want to play the best version of each kind of card—the best attacker, the most flexible removal, the most efficient card-draw—redundancy kind of flies in the face of that. It is therefore not something you will use all the time, but rather a tool that you draw on when it seems appropriate.

When, exactly, is "appropriate?"

 Redundancy works on a range; you don't have to push it to the extreme of playing *an entire deck* where most of the cards have a single similar function, although clearly you can. Sometimes, you can just play just a *fifth* copy of a card (like Alkio's [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker)) and that is redundancy, too. To use this tool well, you will need to know what you want your cards to do, and figure out how many cards you want to do those things and how much they cost.
