The New Standard
================



 Posted in [Perilous Research](/en/articles/columns/perilous-research-archive)
 on October 8, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 





Welcome back to Perilous Research, DailyMTG's exclusive *Magic Online* column. *Battle for Zendikar* Prerelease events start tomorrow on *Magic Online*! We're just one day away from our chance to be among the first to own and play with the new set online. Today, we'll be talking about what the new set's online release will do to Standard.


*Battle for Zendikar* Standard is already proving itself to be a playground for innovative deck builders. The combination of the battle lands from *Battle for Zendikar* and fetch lands from *Khans of Tarkir* gives players the opportunity to get extremely creative with their mana bases.


Standard is still very new, though. It's hard to pinpoint which decks are actually good or find a strong list for any particular archetype when there's such a small sample of actual match data. Luckily, there were some big tournaments this past weekend that might give us a glimpse of what's actually going to see play when the set releases on *Magic Online*.


*Magic Online* is sure to hypercharge these decklists. In a very short amount of time, we'll be seeing the less impressive decks fall out of favor while the best performing archetypes will quickly be sharpened into unstoppable killing machines.


There's sure to be a lot of competition, too. With Pro Tour *Battle for Zendikar* just one week away, we'll be seeing the world's best *Magic* minds put their hard work to the test against the online community. Who will find Standard's newest tier 1 monster? Will it be you?


Today, we'll be looking at the decks that performed well at last weekend's StarCityGames Standard Open and discussing what made those decks successful. By doing so, we'll be able to focus on the most powerful things we can be doing in the new Standard for when we start playing it on *Magic Online* in the coming days.


Let's take a look at some decklists!

The most exciting creation to come out of last weekend is likely Gerry Thompson's Five-Color [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) deck. The deck has many angles of attack and should be able to overpower other decks that are trying to play a long game. When developing evenly with an opponent through the early turns, Five-Color [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) can just introduce its opponent to a parade of [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino)s by casting [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) and [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino) on subsequent turns to produce large amounts of pressure very quickly. However, the toolbox available allows the deck to trump opposing Rhinos and Dragons with [Gilt-Leaf Winnower](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilt-Leaf+Winnower), wipe the board clean with [Crux of Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crux+of+Fate) or [Languish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Languish), or draw unreasonable amounts of cards with [Ugin's Insight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ugin%27s+Insight) or planeswalkers.


What's most impressive about this deck is its versatility. We could jam whatever spells cost five or less in here that we want. The world is our oyster. We just need spells that complement one another with the best efficiency. Sure, we could take Thompson's route and simply play a pile of powerful cards, but we could also set up a lot of synergies. The trick is finding the best four- and five-cost spells to search up for your particular deck, and it's especially powerful to find cards that are strong in multiples. [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino) is the obvious pick, but there's a lot to be said for a card like [Whisperwood Elemental](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whisperwood+Elemental) if it's only taking up one slot.

[Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) may have been the most talked about deck, but the real story of week one was the success of super aggressive decks like Atarka Red and Green-White Megamorph. Atarka Red as [played to victory in Indianapolis](http://www.starcitygames.com/events/031015_indianapolis.html) by Brian DeMars caught a lot of people off guard. The deck applies huge amounts of pressure early and has the power to finish people off out of nowhere with the help of [Temur Battle Rage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temur+Battle+Rage) and [Become Immense](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Become+Immense). These two cards in conjunction with one another are going to win the game the majority of the time they're timed correctly.


Sure, an opponent might just have a removal spell at the ready—but try leaving open mana for removal when you're getting beat down by an aggressive red deck! Red may have lost a lot of its favorite toys, but the suite of aggressive red spells available is definitely worth taking note of. It's also important to remember that red decks won the last two Pro Tours and, as a result, the Pro Tour at large may overcompensate to do well against aggressive strategies at the big show.


However, [Become Immense](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Become+Immense) is better than ever because a lot of the format's best instant-speed removal is rotating out. The card seems difficult to contend with unless you're severely warping your deck to do so. Becoming large and in charge may just be one of the best approaches to the new Standard. There are other aggressive red strategies that use cards like [Retreat to Valakut](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Retreat+to+Valakut) and more landfall creatures. Those decks are capable of even faster draws, but their consistency isn't quite as tight as something like DeMars's version of the deck.

Green-White Megamorph surprised a lot of people with a second-place finish at the Open last weekend. Streamlined green-white decks tend to over-perform early in Standard formats, where it's difficult for the reactive decks to arrive at correct numbers for their spells and proactive strategies like Green-White Megamorph or Atarka Red are usually more lenient with card choices. The deck has a lot of play, though, and utilizing [Evolutionary Leap](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evolutionary+Leap) and [Tragic Arrogance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tragic+Arrogance) out of the sideboard forces opponents to be thoughtful in a scenario where they'll likely be trying to tap out every turn. [Deathmist Raptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathmist+Raptor) seems to have gotten better against the format's answers, but worse without [Elvish Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Mystic) by its side. [Hangarback Walker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hangarback+Walker) is still one of the best things we can be doing in Standard, and it makes sense to play a full four here—especially with the [Tragic Arrogance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tragic+Arrogance) plan out of the sideboard.

Esper Dragons was one of the more established decks to show up last weekend. The deck still combines powerful removal with some of the format's most impressive threats. In the coming weeks, we may see more versions of this deck start playing [Hangarback Walker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hangarback+Walker) as a way to better leverage early mana usage. The deck turns the corner very quickly for a control strategy, and [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time) combined with strong answers remains one of the best ways to lock up the game in Standard. We can be sure that [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time) will be the primary engine of control strategies at Pro Tour *Battle for Zendikar*.

If Gerry Thompson is playing five colors, then we must certainly be able to play four. Jeskai decks are some of the most streamlined tempo strategies available in the current Standard. Without [Stoke the Flames](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoke+the+Flames), the deck needs to apply hasty pressure elsewhere, and [Mantis Rider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mantis+Rider) happens to fill the role beautifully. [Mantis Rider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mantis+Rider) with [Kolaghan's Command](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kolaghan%27s+Command) packs a serious punch and punishes opponents for playing with planeswalkers that match up well against the rest of the format. [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) is quickly proving itself to be the best card in the new Standard. We can expect [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) decks to be powerhouses in Standard for the coming months. This deck gets to play with [Treasure Cruise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise) because its general inexpensive cost and offensive nature rewards us for playing with the less blue-intensive delve card.

Abzan Control lost [Elspeth, Sun's Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion) and [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize), but cards such as [Gideon, Ally of Zendikar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon%2C+Ally+of+Zendikar), [Despise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Despise), and [Gilt-Leaf Winnower](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilt-Leaf+Winnower) are all impressive additions. [Despise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Despise) may not seem like it's the missing [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize), but it functions similarly in the current Standard and I wouldn't be surprised to see three main deck copies in a lot of the midrange decks. [Despise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Despise) is worse than [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) at protecting our threats, but better at dealing with aggression in most cases. Either way, it seems that [Despise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Despise) is at least a passible replacement for [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize). I was worried about the black mana requirements of Abzan strategies with [Ruinous Path](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ruinous+Path). Usually, I wouldn't want to play a 1BB card without at least eighteen sources of black mana. This version of the deck is almost there thanks to three main deck Swamps and a copy of [Scoured Barrens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scoured+Barrens). I would probably go so far as to cut one plains for another copy of [Scoured Barrens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scoured+Barrens) if I were to play a similar deck at a tournament next week.


I'd also like to see a bit more of a top end available to a deck like this. A single copy of [Ugin, the Spirit Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ugin%2C+the+Spirit+Dragon), [Greenwarden of Murasa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greenwarden+of+Murasa), or something else that lets us go very large seems like a very nice inclusion here. [Painful Truths](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Painful+Truths) is also a card that likely needs to be explored more extensively. Drawing three cards for three mana is a very nice deal, and I'm sure that a good number of players will be looking to combine it with inexpensive cards they can unload.
