I looked over my pool and after reading all of my fresh new *Scars of Mirrodin* cards I noticed that I had strong cards in green, black, white, and blue—but not too much in red, except for maybe a [Galvanic Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast) that I could splash.

Even though I really wanted to play my copies of [True Conviction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True+Conviction), [Razor Hippogriff](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Razor+Hippogriff), and [Glimmerpoint Stag](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpoint+Stag) (which would have gone excellently with my [Skinrender](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinrender) I might add) I felt like my white just wasn't deep enough to run.

My blue was similarly lacking in depth when compared with my black and my green, which were highlighted by excellent removal spells like my two copies of [Slice in Twain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slice+in+Twain), a [Skinrender](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinrender), and a [Grasp of Darkness](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grasp+of+Darkness).

I wasn't totally happy with the pieces that I had for the infect deck, but ... well ... I was at the Prerelease and I really wanted to poison some people to death. The fact that I had [Hand of the Praetors](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+the+Praetors) (the infect lord) just made it all the more tempting.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/li/li110_infectedStrike.jpg)  
I wasn't sure how aggressively people would block my infect creatures. Would they take a few hits early and accumulate a number of poison counters before finally deciding to lock down the board and stop my infectors? 

Or would my opponents (correctly) realize that it was dangerous to start picking up poison counters early and go out of their way to block, bounce, or burn my infectors? 

I figured that if players were willing to get dangerous and take a few poison counters before they tried to lock down the board, then I would probably be in pretty good shape with my deck. But if my opponents went out of their way to stop me early I would probably be in trouble. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Cystbearer | Blackcleave Goblin |

Turns out, people were up to my tricks and I was having a lot of trouble winning by poisoning my opponents out.

The biggest problem with the infect version of the deck is that it was very fragile. The next biggest problem is that it was too slow. Combine the two problems and you have a perfect recipe for disaster. When you throw in the fact that this version didn't have a very good end game (I had cut a lot of the higher end stuff for cards that could help me break through and poison my opponents to death in the middle of the game) and you have an absolute delicacy. 

If you think you might be interested in seeing this type of deck in action—the type of deck with a bad early game, a fragile, mediocre mid-game and a bad late game—then you need to look no further than the loser's bracket of your next Sealed Deck tournament and find the guy or gal who looks dejected because he or she opened a shoddy pool.

Me, I subjected myself to this type of a deck because I really wanted to poison some people.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/li/li110_counters.jpg)  
I kept playing with this version of the deck until I won a game with poison counters. Once I finally inflicted the tenth poison counter upon a helpless foe I was ready to fix my deck up.

So I switched a few cards around and put together a non-infect black-green deck.

Then I topped out my curve with [Alpha Tyrranax](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alpha+Tyrranax), [Saberclaw Golem](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saberclaw+Golem), a couple of [Golem Artisan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golem+Artisan)s, and a [Chimeric Mass](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chimeric+Mass).

All in all, I had a solid black-green deck with a very light splash. While I didn't really have any humongous bombs (except for [Skinrender](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinrender)), I had a lot of very good cards, including a lot of good ways to deal with my opponents threats, a lot of good ways to gain card advantage, and enough big threats to reliably put my opponents away once I developed an advantage.

### In Defiance

By the end of the day I realized that I should have played my second [Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance). As good as [Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance) is in Draft—and it's pretty good in Draft—it's even better in Sealed where your opponents will, on average, have access to a lot more artifacts. 

  
Every time I drew my [Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance) I had something really good to do with it. From having my 3/2 survive a combat against a [Snapsail Glider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapsail+Glider), to protecting an attacking [Chimeric Mass](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chimeric+Mass) from a [Sylvok Replica](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvok+Replica), to detaching a [Grafted Exoskeleton](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grafted+Exoskeleton) from my opponent's creature (killing the equipped creature in the process).

![Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance)  
Yes, in case you didn't already realize, you can use [Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance) on your opponent's creature to detach any Equipment that is currently equipped to it (note also that if you [Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance) your own creature it will detach any Equipment that it has on it). 

While [Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance) probably won't be a first-pickable card, I can see myself being very happy to pick it up 4th-7th pick. For two mana, it does a lot.

### Coming Equipped

While it didn't look like much to me while I was building my deck, fellow Daily MTG columnist [Brian David-Marshall](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Archive.aspx?author=Brian%20David-Marshall) told me that I should definitely take a closer look at [Infiltration Lens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infiltration+Lens)—especially in infect decks. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/li/li110_lens.jpg)  
At only one mana to cast and one mana to equip, you really don't have to take much (or necessarily any) time off to cast it and use it. If your opponent never blocks your creature, then you've gotten a pretty good value paying only two total mana for a piece of Equipment that stops your opponent from blocking a creature.

![Infiltration Lens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Infiltration+Lens)  
If your opponent ever does decide to block, you will have gotten an even better value when you draw two cards for only two total mana and you still have the [Infiltration Lens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infiltration+Lens) left around to give your opponent headaches.

If I could go back and rebuild my deck I would definitely make room for my [Infiltration Lens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infiltration+Lens) and the second [Tel-Jilad Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Defiance)—probably in place of a [Silver Myr](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silver+Myr) and a [Snapsail Glider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapsail+Glider).

### Some Initial Thoughts on Metalcraft

Over the course of the next few weeks and months, players are going to develop a much better understanding of how to make the most of metalcraft. But at the Prerelease, I was just starting to figure out ways to best take advantage of [Rusted Relic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rusted+Relic) and friends.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/li/li110_relic.jpg)  
The first, and perhaps most obvious, thing that you need to know about metalcraft is that you need a ton of artifacts to be able to reliably turn it on. And you want as many of them to be cheap as possible so you have a decent shot of turning metalcraft on early.

This means that you should go out of your way to pick up mana Myr—even off-color ones—for your metalcraft decks. It also means that you want to grab as many spellbombs as possible as they will help you turn on metalcraft early, and you will be able to cycle them later (once you have a bunch of artifacts under your control) to dig for something more relevant.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Carapace Forger | Rusted Relic |

There are also a ton of other good, cheap artifacts that might not look particularly flashy, but become pretty great when they provide some extra value. This helps you turn on metalcraft and makes your [Carapace Forger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Carapace+Forger)s 4/4s and your [Rusted Relic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rusted+Relic)s 5/5s.

For example, [Perilous Myr](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Perilous+Myr) might not look like much at first, but this little Myr is [shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=shock)ingly good—especially in metalcraft decks.

![Perilous Myr](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Perilous+Myr)  
Then there are the cards that really take advantage of the fact that you have so many artifacts—cards like [Vedalken Certarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vedalken+Certarch), [Myrsmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Myrsmith), and [Golem Foundry](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golem+Foundry). 

Yes, [Golem Foundry](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golem+Foundry).

![Golem Foundry](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Golem+Foundry)  
[Golem Foundry](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golem+Foundry) is a card that really caught my attention at the Prerelease. Combined with [Throne of Geth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Throne+of+Geth), [Thrummingbird](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrummingbird), [Contagion Clasp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contagion+Clasp), or the already devastating [Contagion Engine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contagion+Engine), you can start pumping out 3/3s at a somewhat alarming rate. Just fill your deck with artifacts, pick up a couple of cards with proliferate and you are good to go!
