Thing of Many Decks
===================



 Posted in **Latest Developments**
 on October 29, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/tom.jpg)
By Tom LaPille




Tom LaPille makes things. Some of the things he makes are card sets, like Dark Ascension and Born of the Gods. Sometimes he makes stories, too. Sometimes he makes unexpected things, like 16th-century Japanese clothing. He's probably making something right now. 







Making **Magic** is one of the most fun jobs I've had, but it can sometimes be lonely. We spend hours and hours working on cards, trying to sculpt environments and make **Magic** as fun as we can. We often have a good idea what will happen with cards before we make them, but in many ways we work inside an echo chamber. For us, there is a long delay between finishing work on a card set and seeing what the printed cards do in the real world. We love the feedback that we get from the audience, and waiting several months to get it can be frustrating.


As you might expect, some of the most fun we have from watching the real world happens soon after a set releases. When you do things that we expected to happen, we get to feel smart, which is fun. When you do things that we didn't expect to happen, we are surprised, and most of the time it's fun to see what happened. Occasionally you discover something that we didn't expect, and most of the time we're excited to see what you found. Sometimes there's something that we all thought was both strong and cool, and you don't collectively latch onto it, which can be sad. No matter what happens, though, it's fun and instructive for us to watch it.


I can't speak for other developers, but looking through tournament deck lists and results often triggers memories of developing the set in question. Today, I'll take a walk through some recent deck lists and tell some related stories.


First, we'll go to some **Magic Online** Daily Event Standard deck lists.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ld/ld114_pyromancer.jpg)We did not think of the five *Zendikar* Ascensions as Constructed cards during development. We understood that [Luminarch Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Ascension) would likely show up as a sideboard card at some point, but our attempts to find [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) and [Beastmaster Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beastmaster+Ascension)-based decks that were strong enough for Standard or Block Constructed did not succeed. I always thought that was too bad, as having a rare cycle of cards with a tight naming convention that has no Constructed-strong card in it is a great recipe for player disdain. "The Ascensions? Yeah, those all suck." On the other hand, one or two flag-bearer members of the cycle can save everything. For example, many players have positive associations with the cycle of [Oaths](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&name=+%5Boath%5D&set=%5B%22Exodus%22%5D) in *Exodus* thanks to [Oath of Druids](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oath+of+Druids) and to a lesser extent [Oath of Ghouls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oath+of+Ghouls) even though the other three didn't see much play.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ld/ld114_beastmaster.jpg)I was quite relieved, then, to see [Beastmaster Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beastmaster+Ascension) stake a strong claim to status as one of the strongest decks in *Zendikar* Block Constructed at Pro Tour–San Juan. I suspected at the time that we would be seeing more of [Beastmaster Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beastmaster+Ascension) in Standard after *Shards of Alara* block rotated out, and **Magic Online** results indicate that this is the case. I was even more relieved at U.S. Nationals this year to see [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) become a real deck. Although [Beastmaster Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beastmaster+Ascension) is part of a relatively normal creature-based deck, [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) is a deck so far off the normal Standard map that it feels like you're playing a totally different game. Although I don't want the "different game" to become the norm, I like that it's available in Standard if you're looking for that. It feels very similar in its role to the Seismic Assault / [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll) deck from a little over a year ago, and I'm glad that there's a deck out there that does that.

There are a lot of things going on here that make me happy.


I was part of the Vampire team for *Zendikar* development. [Blade of the Bloodchief](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blade+of+the+Bloodchief) evolved out of a card that we made, and I was always a little sad that a card that screamed "VAMPIRE!" so loudly was ignored by actual vampire decks. With [Viscera Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer) and [Bloodthrone Vampire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodthrone+Vampire) though, it's good enough.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ld/ld114_3Cards1.jpg)[Captivating Vampire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Captivating+Vampire) is a totally sweet card to me. Mind control as a trope has been associated with vampires in many places, and **Magic** has a rough time with that because control effects are often blue. Here we managed to get that onto a Vampire, but it didn't show up in Standard immediately. With the rotation, though, he's good enough, and Vampire decks are playing enough Vampires to activate him. That's awesome.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ld/ld114_captivating.jpg)The details of this deck indicate that the answer cards we're putting in are good enough to be worth using. [Bojuka Bog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bojuka+Bog), [Tectonic Edge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge), [Vampire Hexmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Hexmage), [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), [Memoricide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memoricide), and [Brittle Effigy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brittle+Effigy) were all built to let people solve weird problems in decks that might not otherwise be able to, and this deck contains all of them. That tells me that our cards are working.

When *Scars of Mirrodin* was handed from Design to Development, there was a red theme of sacrificing your own stuff for benefit. This theme is still present in cards like [Barrage Ogre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barrage+Ogre), [Kuldotha Rebirth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kuldotha+Rebirth), and [Furnace Celebration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Celebration), but there used to be much more. There were also cards that read "When you sacrifice CARDNAME, do something." We found that it wasn't particularly fun to have a huge mass of cards that asked you to throw your own cards away. We also discovered that to many players, there's not much difference between "When you sacrifice CARDNAME, do something," and "Sacrifice CARDNAME: do something," which internally caused the same sort of confusion that caused arguments to break out when I played **Magic** in grade school. 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ld/ld114_perilous.jpg)Rather than fight that confusion and general lack of appeal, we reduced the theme in number and changed some cards from "Whenever you sacrifice CARDNAME" to "Whenever CARDNAME is put into a graveyard from the battlefield." (That's where [Perilous Myr](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Perilous+Myr) came from, if I remember correctly.) However, we kept a few cards, like [Barrage Ogre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barrage+Ogre) and [Furnace Celebration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Celebration), so that the theme could come up in the right quantity. Mark Rosewater was sad that his theme was reduced in number; I was happy, but hoped that it would still show up enough to add texture. Both of us were happy when I was able to email him this Limited deck, as it shows that Mark's vision is still present at a high enough level to matter.

There are once again several things going on in this deck that I like.


White attack decks are popular, baseline strategies that many players gravitate toward when deck building. When we aren't careful to sculpt an environment so that such decks are playable, they often aren't. Based on how many decks there are that look like this in the **Magic Online** results, this is a fine thing to be doing.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ld/ld114_dayOf.jpg)Both as a player and a developer, I like attack decks to have lots of play into the late game. I find it frustrating to be left with nothing to do after a [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) or two and have to sit there miserably. I find it sad to watch other people have to do that. For a deck with four [Ornithopter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter)s and four [Memnite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite)s, this deck has shocking amounts of play into the late game with [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk)s and equipment found by [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic)s.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/ld/ld114_venser.jpg)One of the cards I had the most fun with in the Future Future League while we developed *Scars of Mirrodin* was [Venser, the Sojourner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Venser%2C+the+Sojourner). For quite a long time, I didn't see any results that included decks with Venser, which had me worried. I'm not usually attached to one particular card or deck being powerful, but I knew from playing Venser that he was really fun. We err on the side of making things that we identify as being extra fun a little stronger, and Venser was something that I thought was totally sweet. As it turns out, finding the right deck for Venser just might have been tricky. On the surface, he seems to want to be in a base white-blue deck. Perhaps it could look something like this deck, which I played in the Future Future League at the time of *Scars of Mirrodin* development:

Michael Svein might sneer at my deck, as he has a strong claim that a Venser deck is supposed to be base blue-red instead of base white-blue. Although I'm maximizing Venser a little more, which is appropriate when I'm playtesting the card, he won a State Championships tournament. At this point, I'd say that he has the chips.


As I said at the beginning of this article, we love seeing what everyone does with the cards we make. Although we love hearing back from you in articles, tweets, and emails, the best feedback we get from you is seeing what you actually do when you play, be it through reading your tournament results or from traveling to events and playing against you in person. Both of those things are fun for us to do, and we couldn't do it if you weren't all out there playing. Thanks as always, keep up the good work, and we'll keep putting out more cards for you to explore.
