The State of Modern
===================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 22, 2014 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 







Welcome back to Perilous Research! Last weekend, we all had the opportunity to watch [one of the most exciting Pro Tours](/en/events/coverage/chapins-journey-takes-him-pro-tour-title) in recent memory. Now, we're all clamoring to qualify for the next Pro Tour so we can be a part of the action. With the [upcoming PTQ season](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/TCG/Events.aspx?x=mtg/event/protour/qualifierlist#huey) featuring the Modern format, now is a good time to take a big look at the format. 

Modern is a huge format with countless viable deck themes. The sheer volume of information we need to process to play optimally against all the decks can be daunting, but learning the necessary nuances will put us far ahead of the curve going into our next Modern event. Today, we'll be discussing the current state of Modern by analyzing the decks that seem to define the format going into the PTQ season.

 Jund is becoming one of the most successful decks in the format again, despite the loss of [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman). The combination of [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek) and [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) makes it easy to have an uncontested [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) or [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) take over the game. This version of the deck plays [Prophetic Flamespeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prophetic+Flamespeaker), an exciting haymaker of a card from *Journey into Nyx*. [Prophetic Flamespeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prophetic+Flamespeaker) allows the deck to replace stray slots—slots that might traditionally be occupied by Planeswalkers or situational spells—with creatures. [Prophetic Flamespeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prophetic+Flamespeaker) works especially well alongside [Jund Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jund+Charm) and [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine). It's clear that the combination of strong discard, strong removal, and strong creatures is a recipe for success no matter how many cards get banned.

[Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) has risen to power and has quickly become one of the best decks in Modern after its victory in [Grand Prix Minneapolis](/en/events/coverage/parks-great-scape-minneapolis). [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) aims to counter key spells and kill key creatures while developing its mana. Once the deck gets to seven lands, it performs the one-card combo of casting [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) to search for six Mountains and [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) to deal the opponent 18 points of damage. Is 18 not enough? Then we can wait for our eighth land and find two [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) and six Mountains for 36 points of damage. The deck put up a lackluster performance at [Pro Tour *Born of the Gods*](/en/events/coverage/mclaren-weaves-victory-valencia) because of the popularity of [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon), but the format has moved in a different direction and it's clear that the [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) deck has what it takes to be a tier one contender.

 Faeries is a powerful tempo control strategy that excels against Combo and Control strategies. [Pack Rat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat) is a new addition to the deck that combos nicely with the [Mutavault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault)s and discard. The unbanning of [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) put a target on Faeries's head that left it floundering, but the current format allows for players to have a lot of success with this archetype, especially now that Burn, Red Deck Wins, and Zoo are on the decline in popularity.

 Tarmo-Twin is a tempo/combo strategy that uses strong removal and disruption alongside [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), and [Scavenging Ooze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scavenging+Ooze) to apply consistent pressure. The strategy often puts opponents into a situation where they're forced to tap out. When they do, the deck can [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) its [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) or [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) and win the game out of nowhere.

 Kiki-Pod is a resilient midrange/combo strategy that aims to out-value the opponent with its [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) before combining [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) with [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch). The deck can go off out of nowhere with an active [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod). For example, let's say we have a [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod), a [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), and a [Wall of Roots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Roots) in play; we can sacrifice the [Wall of Roots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Roots), finding [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), untapping the [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod), then sacrifice the [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), finding [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel), flickering the [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), untapping [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod), then sacrifice the [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel), finding [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) to make infinite [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Deceiver%2BExarch)s for a lethal attack.

 Melira-Pod is a midrange/combo deck that has access to a lot of disruption. The deck eventually seeks to combine [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast) with [Viscera Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer) and a persist creature for infinite life via [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) or infinite damage via [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap). The deck can also make its creatures infinitely large and gain infinite life by combining [Archangel of Thune](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Thune) with [Spike Feeder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spike+Feeder). The deck can grind opponents out and win games without its combo in a lot of situations. This is widely considered the best deck in the format, but its current weakness to [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) means that it's not as dominant as it once was.
