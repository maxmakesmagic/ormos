A Surprising Premiere
=====================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on July 7, 2005 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







The summer's *Kamigawa* Block PTQs for Pro Tour Los Angeles are now underway. As we've done with the past couple of Constructed formats, Swimming With Sharks is going to provide you with the Top 8 deck lists every week in order to help you prepare for your own local PTQs.


Many players approach these lists as "the decks to play," but I think that they are most useful for predicting what your opponents will be playing across the table, for giving you a leg up on the competition rather than putting you in the exact same position. Each week we will not only provide the deck lists themselves, but also tally up the decks to show which archetypes are showing up and advancing. The competitors most adept at playing the metagame will be able to use these tools to tailor their choices and make for the most potent archetype decisions... or even give birth to new rogue options.

### Surprise, Surprise


Two things surprised me about this Top 8 set. The first one was that there were only three copies of the card [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) out of fifteen deck lists. Contrast that with the [Top 8 of PT Philadelphia](/en/events/coverage/pro-tour%E2%80%93los-angeles-qualifying-season-top-8-decklists) where more than three times the number of [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) in half the decks, and where [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven)-based engines dominated the whole show, bashing everyone else before meeting in the finals. Interestingly, the only copies of [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) at the PTQ Top 8s level showed up in a new Mono-Blue deck, piloted by Lawrence Watts in Little Rock.

![Jushi Apprentice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jushi+Apprentice)

This deck sure can draw a lot of cards. With early game [Jushi Apprentice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jushi+Apprentice)s and late game Legendary Lady of Scrolls, Watts's deck is superb at winning long attrition wars. Assuming he makes it to the late turns intact, the top of Watts's curve matches the predicted opponent's Meloku, and goes one better with Keiga. This deck tries to do something no successful deck out of Philadelphia did: counter its opponent’s spells. In the Top 8, Kenji Tsumura's two measly [Time Stop](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Stop)s were the only main deck permission in the room, but Watts uses his card advantage to set up [Hinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hinder), [Oppressive Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oppressive+Will), and even [Disrupting Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disrupting+Shoal) starting... with six more counters in his sideboard. This strategy works well at collapsing some of the ponderous threat setups of the *Kamigawa* Block, and benefits from the relatively weak beatdown wing of the format to date.

Though the deck occupies a position of power because of its ability to answer threats, there are some odd interactions between Kira and the equipment. Thought it can draw lots of extra cards, Watts's list lacks the positional speed of the typical green mage, and does not seek to break [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top). It will be interesting to see if Mono-Blue catches on as the format progresses.


The second thing that surprised me was the complete absence of [Sosuke's Summons](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sosuke%27s+Summons). [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) may have been the most successful deck of PT Philadelphia, but Snakes was the most populous... Neither deck made a splash in the qualifiers we looked at, this week at least.

![Hokori, Dust Drinker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hokori%2C+Dust+Drinker)

Hokori was one of the strongest [Time of Need](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+of+Need) threats that the TOGIT-style decks could grab against a slower deck such as [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction)-based control or [Sway of the Stars](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sway+of+the+Stars), and could constrict those trying to gain card advantage with repeated [Sosuke's Summons](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sosuke%27s+Summons) as well as those trying to lock down the game with [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven). The opponent could have more cards, could better finesse his library, but without the mana to use these better resources, that advantage could fall beneath a North Tree or Yosei assault. [Yosei the Morning Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yosei+the+Morning+Star) holds a special position in the *Kamigawa* Block metagame; everyone plays cards like [Meloku the Clouded Mirror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meloku+the+Clouded+Mirror) and half a dozen other Legendary creatures; Yosei is a potent threat that actually avoids the wrong side of the new Legend Rule; though the Orlando Top 8 looks stacked with White Dragon Legends due to the dominance of TOGIT-style 3 Color Control at that particular tournament, there is essentially no other archetype that runs Yosei, making for a "safer" threat in a format where everyone is playing Legendary cards like [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) just to run over the other guy's copy, already in play.

Tony Menzer's deck from Little Rock is a *four*-color control deck reminiscent of the TOGIT build, but adding Swamps and four [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction)s – and it ironically plays more copies of that *Champions of Kamigawa* chase rare than most of the straight black decks. Hybridizing the best elements of the TOGIT list, and even incorporating late game Honden and Myojin action, Menzer's deck seems to give up nothing to add its black cards... until we realize that he has cut what some considered to be the defining card of the format pre-Philadelphia, [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) itself; Andre Mueller played a similar deck in the PT Philadelphia Top 8.


The White Weenie decks that showed up this week were relatively straightforward. As in Philadelphia, they dueled well, but none has taken a first place so far.


In *Kamigawa* Block, White Weenie takes one of two shapes: straightforward attack with efficient drops, or a slightly de-emphasized offense that incorporates [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) components. Jones uses [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) to tutor up [Indomitable Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Indomitable+Will) or [Cage of Hands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cage+of+Hands) using [Lantern Kami](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lantern+Kami), [Kami of Ancient Law](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kami+of+Ancient+Law), [Terashi's Grasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terashi%27s+Grasp), or one of his Arcane combat tricks. He doesn't need to play the maximum number of any of these cards to generate an advantage; often it is sufficient to slow down one of the slow control decks with a single [Cage of Hands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cage+of+Hands) or save a threat from lethal combat damage or [Hideous Laughter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hideous+Laughter) with a carefully placed [Indomitable Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Indomitable+Will). Because the [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) is often being set up with a lethal effect on the stack, a single [Blessed Breath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blessed+Breath) or [Shining Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shining+Shoal) will often save more than one creature, anyway.


A new weapon that some of the White Weenie players have embraced is [Charge Across the Araba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Charge+Across+the+Araba). Better in the offensive swarm decks than the [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) finesse White Weenie despite being Arcane, [Charge Across the Araba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Charge+Across+the+Araba) trades land drops for a potential [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun). Though not reach per se, [Charge Across the Araba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Charge+Across+the+Araba) is a finisher that White Weenie has been waiting for, allowing the deck to bust through board positions that would be impossible to breach otherwise, even after long exhaustion wars, as well as pre-empt [Ethereal Haze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethereal+Haze) locks in the absence of the Dust Drinker.


### The New Blood


 



![Hand of Cruelty](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hand+of+Cruelty)

The most important new deck has to be Mono-Black Aggro. In just one week's worth of results, this deck has posted three Top 8s and a victory already. The three decks that showed up all had different features, but all seem enabled by the new *Saviors of Kamigawa* card [Hand of Cruelty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Cruelty). Put aside for a moment the strength that a disruptive attack deck has against ponderous faux control decks and consider just the next best contender in this format. [Hand of Cruelty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Cruelty) single-handedly shuts down White Weenie. That deck's options are pretty narrow against a single [Hand of Cruelty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Cruelty), with typically just [Lantern Kami](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lantern+Kami) to get through. [Hand of Cruelty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Cruelty) doesn't step aside for [Eight-and-a-Half Tails](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eight-and-a-Half+Tails), doesn't accept a [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp)'s [Cage of Hands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cage+of+Hands), and always knows which incoming attacker is wearing [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte).

[Hand of Cruelty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Cruelty) is just miles better than his White counterpart in the Black-on-White Weenie matchup. Black creatures like [Takenuma Bleeder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Takenuma+Bleeder) and even [Ogre Marauder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ogre+Marauder) can provide some resistance to [Hand of Honor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Honor), and cards like [Distress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Distress) and [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction) can pre-empt the Protection from Black version much more effectively than White can answer [Hand of Cruelty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Cruelty). To a lesser extent, this card's low cost and sometimes evasion can also set up ninjutsu, an important element given that all of this week's Black decks sported Ninja cards.

If Black Hand decks catch on, Reason's route may be the preferred one... 3/3s and 5/5s might have some downsides... but they sure match up well against a similarly minded opponent sporting [Wicked Akuba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wicked+Akuba) and [Throat Slitter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Throat+Slitter).
