 on November 3, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 







Hello and welcome to Infect Week here on **magicthegathering.com**. Last week I presented some [fun ideas for Game Day](/en/articles/archive/building-budget/building-affiliation-2010-10-27). A lot of you put together one of the decks from last week's column and played them to impressive finishes.

All the positive feedback made me take a second look at this poison deck that pursues quick victories with reckless abandon. Let's take a look at the list.

It's beautiful in its simplicity. You play poison creatures. Attack. Play lots of pump spells. Then, well, you win.


There's something so wonderful about winning on turn three the first time you play with a deck. A warm fuzzy feeling. The last time I did this was with my Extended Elf Combo deck from last winter.


I played some more matches and was continually impressed with the deck. There were a few problems, though, that I felt could be fixed through some informed tweaking. Mono-red decks seemed to pose the biggest challenge. They burned all my infect creatures and I was unable to stick one for a nice big swing.



![Prey's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Prey%27s+Vengeance)

 

Two cards were always less impressive than one might expect. [Putrefax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrefax) and [Primal Bellow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Bellow) never seemed as good as the rest of the cards. I decided that I should try to play with some more infect creatures to make the deck mulligan less. I also wanted to play a few games with [Prey's Vengeance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prey%27s+Vengeance) and see how it went. Here's the list I played.

I didn't need the super high land count to help cast [Putrefax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrefax) anymore.


The deck was certainly performing better against the red decks. [Corpse Cur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corpse+Cur) does an excellent job making a constant stream of infect creatures. The deck continued to perform very well against [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle) and Blue-Red-Green variations, especially before sideboarding.



![Corpse Cur](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Corpse+Cur)

 

It still didn't feel right, though. [Cystbearer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cystbearer) was terribly unexciting and [Corpse Cur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corpse+Cur) was very bad in the non-red match-ups. I also found myself wishing that [Prey's Vengeance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prey%27s+Vengeance) was [Primal Bellow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Bellow). To be fair, the new version did not seem that much better than the original. I wanted the deck to be more oriented to winning as quickly as it possibly could.


One of my opponents suggested that I try playing with [Smoldering Spires](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smoldering+Spires). I played a few games with the following list in an effort to test that particular land.

I decided that I was already playing red and I could easily play with some copies of [Copperline Gorge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperline+Gorge) because they're fairly easy to acquire and the current list is completely devoid of rares at this point. [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) continues to be one of those cards that I put into decks without asking any questions. [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) loses a lot of value when you're playing Infect, though. Normally, aggressive decks need to have one question in mind during deck development, "How quickly can I reduce my opponent's life total from 20 to 0?" [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) is usually an easy 15%. Don't get me wrong; I'm not telling you to spew turn-one [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s at your opponent's face, but when it's turn four or five. .. that face looks like a very nice target. I still felt like [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) was worth the slot, even without this late game ability.

I drew another [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest), played it, cast [Boar Umbra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boar+Umbra) on my [Blight Mamba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blight+Mamba) and attacked for four poison counters. One of the best parts about the deck is how well it takes advantage of opponents that don't know how to play against it. It's often hard to imagine that a 1/1 infect creatures is going to kill you if you don't block.


The next game is my favorite thus far.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/boab/boab115_fauna.jpg)I had won the roll and my opening hand had no land and I was forced to mulligan into [Cystbearer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cystbearer), [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest), [Copperline Gorge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperline+Gorge), [Vines of Vastwood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vines+of+Vastwood), [Prey's Vengeance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prey%27s+Vengeance), [Prey's Vengeance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prey%27s+Vengeance). It seemed very hard to win the game with those six cards so I decided to go down to five. My five-card hand was entirely land and I was forced to mulligan down to four. I kept [Copperline Gorge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperline+Gorge), [Copperline Gorge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperline+Gorge), [Blight Mamba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blight+Mamba), [Groundswell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Groundswell). I drew [Smoldering Spires](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smoldering+Spires), played it, prevented [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman) from blocking, cast [Groundswell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Groundswell) on my [Blight Mamba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blight+Mamba), cast [Assault Strobe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Assault+Strobe) on the [Blight Mamba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blight+Mamba), and attacked for ten poison counters.


These games may seem crazy, but these are the types of things that can happen when you mulligan aggressively with this type of all-in deck.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/boab/boab115_koth.jpg)I lost the roll and kept [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest), [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest), [Ichorclaw Myr](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorclaw+Myr), [Blight Mamba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blight+Mamba), [Vines of Vastwood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vines+of+Vastwood), [Flame Slash](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame+Slash), [Giant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Giant+Growth). My opponent led with a [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide) and I got a free [Teetering Peaks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teetering+Peaks) in my hand. I drew [Copperline Gorge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperline+Gorge), played it, cast [Flame Slash](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame+Slash) on the [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide), and passed the turn. My opponent played a land and passed the turn. I drew another [Vines of Vastwood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vines+of+Vastwood) and decided that I needed to slow-roll the two drop if I wanted to have a shot against a burn heavy deck. [Corpse Cur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corpse+Cur) comes in handy after board, but we can't try to have more creatures than our opponents have burn spells. I played the [Teetering Peaks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teetering+Peaks) and passed the turn. My opponent played a third land, cast an [Ember Hauler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ember+Hauler), and passed the turn. I drew a [Groundswell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Groundswell), played my forest, cast my [Ichorclaw Myr](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorclaw+Myr), and passed the turn.


My opponent attempted a [Burst Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning) on the [Ichorclaw Myr](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorclaw+Myr) on my end step, but I used [Vines of Vastwood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vines+of+Vastwood) to give it shroud. My opponent thought for a bit and cast [Koth of the Hammer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Koth+of+the+Hammer), he untapped a [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain), attacked for 6, and passed the turn. I drew an [Assault Strobe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Assault+Strobe) and stole the game out from under my red opponent.


This last game is an interesting example of how you need to play when you're wielding an all-in style deck like this. Sometimes the only way to win is for your opponent to make mistakes. You need to put them in a situation where their mistake seems like it's probably the best play. Waiting a turn to play the two-drop ensures that my opponent will likely have a very strong spell they need to opt not to play if they want to burn the infect creature. Opponents in the know will likely make the right play, but most opponents have probably never had to face this type of strategy.


The deck loses the majority of games against mono-red. I used this game as an example of an opening you can use to steal the first game.


I continued to play games with this version of the deck and I was repeatedly impressed with its ability to steal games from the best decks in Standard. I still had a lot of trouble with red decks and I decided that I needed a good sideboard.


Unfortunately, sideboarding with this type of deck is very scary. It's hard to take cards out when you have such a focused strategy. The sideboard may look mismatched, but I'll explain my choices afterward. Here's the full 75 I would play today:

These sideboarding plans let you keep the deck's very straightforward plan intact. Your deck becomes slightly tweaked to have an edge in specific match-ups without actually changing the overall plan.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/boab/boab115_topsOfTwos.jpg)I said it last week, but I feel I should reiterate how important mulligans are when you're playing a deck like this. You absolutely need a two-drop in your opening hand. If your hand lacks one of the twelve two-drops in the deck then it probably needs to be shipped back. I would follow this rule all the way down to five cards, where I probably keep any combination of lands and spells.


The deck is an exciting rollercoaster for sure. There are many games where you feel helpless, but that is to be expected from such an aggressive deck.
