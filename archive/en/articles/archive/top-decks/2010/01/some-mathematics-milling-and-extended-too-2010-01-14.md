Some Mathematics of Milling (and Extended, too!)
================================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on January 14, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






For the Top Decks contribution to Milling Week, I wanted to cast a wide (and hopefully entertaining, and hopefully enlightening) net ... a little bit of historical retrospective, some general **Magic** strategy on the subject of deck exhaustion, and finally a look to the current Extended format. Is a "milling" strategy viable? Why, why not, and what kind of Transmute action might ruin your day?


Let's go!


### Part I. A Brief History of Milling


"Milling," as we've decided to entitle the week's theme, takes its name from [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone), a card whose ability, while not entirely unique at this point, is iconic to a degree that few cards can claim. Our understanding of "card advantage" has for some stretches blocked [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) out of Constructed popularity, but there is no denying that the [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) deck is one of the fundamental pillars upon which modern competitive **Magic** rests.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td73_millstoneSplash.jpg)  
Specifically, there were actually *two* versions of [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) decks in the Top 8 of the very first Pro Tour ... and one of them won!

The Loconto and Hammer decks used [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) as a primary route to victory. Neither deck used it as the *only* route to victory (Hammer played [Mishra's Factory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factory) to outwit [Feldon's Cane](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Feldon%27s+Cane) players, and Loconto added [Blinking Spirit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinking+Spirit)s, and an inventive [Hallowed Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Ground) combination with his Mishra's Factories ... for ... you know ... even more [Blinking Spirits](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinking+Spirits)), but both decks won the majority of games by running opponents out of their libraries.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td73_3Cards1.jpg)  
[Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) itself waned in popularity in subsequent months—for some years—due to a combination of metagame changes and a greater understanding of the game by the average player. Mono-black [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) decks quickly took over Standard, and these decks with their [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual)–driven mana and built-in nineteen-card bonuses (which the damage-poor [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) decks could do essentially nothing to punish) were specifically very good against these white-blue decks.


In the abstract, players became more and more aware of what, today, we consider basic card advantage principles. On the one hand, [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) was "just" a "[Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball)"—that is, just a way to win (not even giving players the option to defend themselves, as [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) does). Every time you drew a [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone)—a card that had essentially no effect on the board, it was thought—you would be "down a card" while the opponent would be setting up the advance of a victory plan. Sure, you would have "something" to do with your mana, but it wasn't like having a "real" card ... like a creature. Creatures, of course, are both a way to win *and* a way, potentially, to defend yourself.


Had [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) been played in the early 2000s, its "card advantage" stat line would have looked bleaker and bleaker, with cards like [Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glory) and [Roar of the Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm) printed in the *Odyssey* block (but by this point, [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) would have already had, and finished up on, its second run in Constructed). Instead of just going down a card yourself, activating your [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) might actually set the *opponent* up with *more* cards!

| --- | --- |
| Glory | Roar of the Wurm |

But returning to the mid-to-late 1990s, [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) decks, even *without* the bias against their "card advantage" dead weight issues, would have had serious problems with the post-[Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence+) Standard metagame. *Tempest* gave rise to [Jackal Pup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup)s and [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland)s, and a red deck seven to ten times faster than the white-blue [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) decks (at least) ... and, surprisingly, with more and more efficient routes to economic advantage thanks to the recurring action of a [Cursed Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll) or the sublimely slick mana of a [Fireblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireblast).


There was, though, that brief period around 2001 where [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) made a significant reappearance in a white-blue control deck:

The card resurfaced on the basis of pure speed. Speed? Really? Absolutely! That is, when facing up with other blue control decks, the one with [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) (rather than [Blinding Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinding+Angel), [Evil Eye of Orms-by-Gore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evil+Eye+of+Orms-by-Gore), or, ironically, [Nether Spirit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nether+Spirit)) could stick a significant threat (that is, the [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone)) on the second turn, often sliding right under a [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) wall. The opponent would probably have no way at all to deal with this kind of permanent, making victory inevitable some twenty turns down the line. "Card advantage" be damned! (You're just going to draw a bunch of [Vendetta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendetta)s or [Dominate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dominate)s anyway.)


As a side note, I get a lot of credit for convincing blue players to tap mana on their own turns to, you know, *block* with a [Keiga, the Tide Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Keiga%2C+the+Tide+Star) or the equivalent, but Zvi and Scott Johns had it figured out for the summer of [Fires of Yavimaya](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fires+of+Yavimaya). Their deck started out a [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) deck, but they played a cantrip–[Mahamoti Djinn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mahamoti+Djinn) transformative strategy that made for a smooth and silky turn six.

That Zvi was one smart Mahamoti-[Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) Magician, wasn't he? Did you know he has [a book](http://www.top8magic.com/store/zvi-mowshowitz-my-files-volume-1/)?

### Part II. How [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) Just Might Ruin Your Day, or at Least Your Deck


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td73_trapSplash.jpg)  
There were indications with a couple of different Standard Red Decks that [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) was a complete monster against them. The first, most obvious, one was Standard [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle). That kind of deck would generally be forced to try to win with a [Siege-Gang Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander) or whatever, because one [Harrow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Harrow) and it was usually done. A single [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) would often pull out enough [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s to gravely neuter the Molten Pinnacle.

For sake of argument, consider Nick Stadtmiller's Valakut Ramp deck. This deck has twelve [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s. Valakut requires five [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s *on the battlefield* before it does *anything* besides tap for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif). While it is very rare that an [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) will eliminate every remaining [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) that you haven't drawn yet (though that is a possibility, especially in long games), it will with significant regularity make Valakut thoroughly uninteresting as a card, certainly demoting it to a couple of points of damage at the most, rather than a looming long-game threat.

However, even more poignant was testing with a Barely Boros–type red-white burn deck.

Wait! What's the problem?


There is no *direct* card advantage relationship between [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) and the opponent's deck. Let's look at Petr Brodzek's actual 5-1 deck from the [2009 World Championships](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/coverage-2009-world-championships-2009-11-12). The deck has 4 [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa)s, 4 [Naya Panorama](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naya+Panorama)s, 4 [Scalding Tarn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scalding+Tarn)s, and 4 [Terramorphic Expanse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terramorphic+Expanse)s ... sixteen opportunities for the opponent to play a free [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap), but seventeen near-legitimate sources of white mana to set up [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant). The importance of [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) in the Jacerator matchup cannot be exaggerated; simply, if the red-white deck sticks its planeswalker, it will usually win. If not ... not so much.


But with a single swoop of [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap), the Jacerator deck can actually rid itself of any and all possibilities of an Ajani-driven [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) attempt. How?


Let's suppose you draw seven cards. One of them is a [Scalding Tarn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scalding+Tarn). You open up on [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide) (hooray!). You have a hand for a second-turn [Plated Geopede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede), and plan to get your opponent's mana tapped with a heavy attack on turns three and four. You've got [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) in your hand, and you know that sticking Ajani will make winning much easier.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Goblin Guide | Plated Geopede |

The problem? You opened on a [Scalding Tarn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scalding+Tarn), and before you have the opportunity to go and find your singleton [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains), your opponent strikes!


You have 52 cards in your deck at this point, more than a dozen ways to find your white source ... but only one actual [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains). If your opponent hits that [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains)—which will occur about a quarter of the time at this point, and much more later—that's actually pre-emptively countering each and every [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) you will inevitably draw in the future. It's actually worse than that (for you). Your opponent doesn't *have* to actually counter. In fact, [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) will have very quietly [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)ed the best card in your hand ... and you might not even know it!


Think about this for a moment. A full quarter of the time the opponent doesn't even have to think about your best card. It is a gimme. A freebie. Your plan was to go Vengeant, and instead you have to play fair against a prolific defense, a slight edge in the Expedition heads-up, and a backbreaking quartet of [Kabira Crossroads](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kabira+Crossroads). Believe me, this isn't nothing.

It might look a little bit strange, but the deck is actually quite serviceable. On the embarrassing side, I kept losing to Rubin Zoo decks after stabilizing around 10-16 life, due to being outmaneuvered by multiple copies of [Cursed Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll)—that is, [Punishing Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Punishing+Fire). However the matchups across a great many decks are quite favorable.


I originally made the deck, in mirror to the hatred [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) had for Valakut in Standard, to be a [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) hater. It is very difficult to lose Game 1. The math holds; if you stick even one [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap), winning can be very difficult for the [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) deck, and the presence of those Snares helps stick more than one.

Similarly, Dredge is a slow pitch right over the plate. This might initially seem counterintuitive because the [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) deck is trying to put a lot of cards into the opponent's graveyard (and the Dredge deck is *also* trying to put a lot of cards into the Dredge deck's graveyard), but consider the *necessity* of playing [Ravenous Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Trap) and all four copies of [Relic of Progenitus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+of+Progenitus) in this kind of a deck. For one thing, your opponent might be Dredge. Interjecting in that hunt for a [Watery Grave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Watery+Grave) is actually horrendous if you don't have a way to keep your opponent from demolishing you out of the graveyard. You *have* to play [Relic of Progenitus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+of+Progenitus) (and see the venom regarding [Punishing Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Punishing+Fire), above). You may have noticed that while the deck doesn't have any real card drawing, it is chock-full of cantrips like [Repeal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Repeal) (for [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths)) and [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) (for [Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis)); [Relic of Progenitus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+of+Progenitus) is perfectly suited to this squad.


Most rogue decks are power-low and rely on the opponent's lack of familiarity with a strategy in order to win. The [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) deck is actually quite powerful, and has—if not *as* stupid a draw as [Urborg, Tomb of Yawgmoth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urborg%2C+Tomb+of+Yawgmoth); [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize); [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths); [Vampire Hexmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Hexmage)—a pretty stupid draw with [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) (sometimes two) and a [Trapmaker's Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trapmaker%27s+Snare) on the second turn.

This deck plays 21 lands, but only seven mana-producing lands. A multiple [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) swoop on the first or second turn can not only lock out a color (which is quite possible) but take out a huge percentage of mana-producing lands. You can extrapolate from the [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) discussion, above, how hitting the right lands will, in this case, create dead draws in the form of cards that can't be cast ... and to a lesser degree, non-mana-producing lands that can no longer go and find other lands! So long as the opponent doesn't have [Punishing Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Punishing+Fire), it is pretty easy to win attrition wars with the remaining fast response cards in the deck, or at least buy enough time for the [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel)s to take over (even if you don't end up winning via deck exhaustion).


The [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) deck has a large number of very good matchups, consistent with a combo-control positioning, but some dismal ones as well. I don't think it has a very good chance against Faeries, and I got absolutely demolished by a white-blue rogue deck with [Muddle the Mixture](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Muddle+the+Mixture). After setting up an impressive flourish of Traps the first couple of turns—unfortunately flipping roughly one million [Momentary Blink](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Momentary+Blink)s along the way—I watched my opponent cast Transmute for a [Jötun Grunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=J%C3%B6tun+Grunt), and ... well ... It might have been tougher than Fae.

Like I said, working on the Traps deck began as a purely intellectual exercise, but it turned out to be a lot more competitive than I originally anticipated. So for all of you who have been asking for the list, there it is!


I actually learned a lot from playing it, and am probably going to end up playing a similarly structured (if more conventional in terms of kill cards) white-blue deck when I get to my first PTQ.


I leave you this week with one of my favorite library exhaustion decks of all time. It isn't actually a "[Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone)" deck, but you don't lose the game by having no cards in your deck ... You lose when you can't draw one! Anyway, it features the work of one of my favorite deck designers of all time—and, whether you know it or not, one of yours, too.
