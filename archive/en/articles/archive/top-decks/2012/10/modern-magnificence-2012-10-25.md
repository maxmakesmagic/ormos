Modern Magnificence
===================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on October 25, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







We certainly live in exciting times! 


  
  
 Seattle, Washington, near the home of **Magic: The Gathering**, was the site of last weekend's [Pro Tour *Return to Ravnica*](/en/events/coverage/ptrtr12). *Return to Ravnica* is proving to be one of the most popular releases in recent memory, and the big event that shares a name with this epic set did not disappoint in either its storylines or its deck quality. 


 Here is how Pro Tour *Return to Ravnica*'s Top 8 shook out: 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td218_top_8_chart.jpg)  
### Second Breakfast


If you followed the PT coverage at all last weekend, you know that eventual champion Stanislav Cifka put on a singular performance: 2–0 after 2–0... winning the first *fifteen* rounds of the swiss part of the tournament, and then going on to win it all. 


 It was no surprise to anyone, then, when, after the dust settled on Day Three, that it was the dominating Cifka left standing atop a mountain of *Eggs*. He did his Top 8 magic **Magic** with this Eggs descendent, dubbed "Second Breakfast."

 Second Breakfast is a direct reference to the chattering, hungry hobbits of Peter Jackson's *Lord of the Rings* films. In **Magic** terms, combo decks often have breakfast or breakfast cereal names (like Trix; Fruity Pebbles; Wheaties; or, of course, Full English Breakfast); the ancestor to this deck used analogues like [Skycloud Egg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skycloud+Egg) or [Darkwater Egg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkwater+Egg) (not Modern legal, and not so synergistic with [Reshape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) as [Chromatic Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Star), anyway), which led to a pretty equally breakfast-themed nickname. "Second," of course, comes from the card [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise)... one of the key components of this combo deck.

| --- | --- |
| Second Sunrise | Chromatic Star |

 Cifka's deck is fairly unique in competitive **Magic** history; how many other decks do you know that can put away pressure-filled PT matches *without playing land on the first turn?* In fact, Cifka won lots of matches while missing a land drop on turn one (or even for several consecutive turns)! 


 This deck can miss land drops, instead laying a [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) or two as a future combo catalyst. 


 On the turn Cifka wants to "go off" (let's say turn four, having suspended a pair on turn one), he can warp in whatever [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom)s are ready, and then chain small card draw after small card draw. [Chromatic Sphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Sphere), [Chromatic Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Star), [Elsewhere Flask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elsewhere+Flask)... these cards draw cards cheaply, often while fixing colors *and* going to the graveyard. 




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Lotus Bloom | Chromatic Sphere |

 Going to the graveyard is a big chunk of what gets Second Breakfast moving. Remember, the deck takes its name from the spell [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise). When you play [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) (or [Faith's Reward](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Reward)) you get to bring back all the toys you sacrificed this turn: that means you get back your Chromatic This or Chromatic That, you get back [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom), and you might even get back lands! Second Breakfast has a nasty habit of [Ghost Quartering](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quartering) *itself* to search up land and then get back both the [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) and the originally targeted land once [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) hits. 




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Faith's Reward | Ghost Quarter |

 Second Breakfast doesn't progress the same way every game, but because so many of its cards do similar things (basically artifacts that draw cards in a relatively inexpensive fashion given some setup time), we can predict where he will get to... *eventually*. The deck has one [Pyrite Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrite+Spellbomb) to win with in Game 1. If you draw it early you can sacrifice it to draw a card like many of the other "Eggs" in the deck. But once you have drawn through your entire deck (generally via multiple [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise)s and [Faith's Reward](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Reward)s and many, many sacrificed [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom)s fueling your progression as you draw up) you will find yourself with no deck at all... and that is exactly where you want to be. 


 When you have no library whatsoever it is easy to create a loop of *just* 
[Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise)/[Faith's Reward](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Reward) via [Conjurer's Bauble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Conjurer%27s+Bauble). If you have two [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom)s (one of which you could have accomplished via [Reshape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) somewhere along the line to drawing your whole deck), [Conjurer's Bauble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Conjurer%27s+Bauble) makes [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) your only card—which you cast via [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom). ### Jund


 Jund was far and away the most popular deck of Pro Tour *Return to Ravnica*. Almost one-third of all the players in the Pro Tour brought the iconic [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) deck to the battlefield, with three of them ending up in the Top 8 itself. 




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Bloodbraid Elf | Lightning Bolt |

 Jund in Modern relies on many of the same cards that made it famous in Standard a couple of years ago. [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) and [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) were hanging out together way back when... but in Modern they are joined by substantially improved creatures like [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant), and [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). Jund has value written all over it; it is a midrange creature deck but chock full of two-for-ones. It commands elite removal from the aforementioned [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) to a variety of flexible options like *Return to Ravnica* newcomer [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay) to longtime robots-smasher [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge).

[Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) is more or less my favorite kind of combo deck in formats like Modern. 


 It can play a basic midrange value game plan... it can start off with [Search for Tomorrow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Search+for+Tomorrow) on the first turn, plus block-and-ramp with [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) to get going. Tian's build has two-for-one defense in [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) and [Electrolyze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrolyze), and can buy all kinds of time with [Izzet Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Izzet+Charm) and [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand). In sum, it is "a deck" and it can "do all kinds of stuff you might want to do." 


...


 Or, it can blow you all to dead with one [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift)! 


 The whole point of all that ramp, that [Search for Tomorrow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Search+for+Tomorrow), that extra land from [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder), is to get sufficient lands in play to pop a [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) that will put one or both copies of [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) into play along with [Stomping Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stomping+Ground), [Steam Vents](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steam+Vents), or regular old [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) to be lethal in one shot.

 GU Infect is a different kind of deck that seems like multiple decks crammed together. Infect attacks. It has offensive one-drops like [Glistener Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glistener+Elf), and it has evasion like [Blighted Agent](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blighted+Agent)... but it isn't a traditional beatdown deck. Unlike your StOmPy, Suicide Black, or White Weenie, Infect can use its attack phase like a vector for a combo-kill of its own!

 WU Control in Modern today isn't quite the "WU Control" of Brian Weissman and his [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel)s from the dawn of **Magic** strategy. This deck is much more an enters-the-battlefield creature deck that relies on the bonuses on [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) and [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), tied with a nice bow of additional value thanks to [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel).

More, and more of the same.
