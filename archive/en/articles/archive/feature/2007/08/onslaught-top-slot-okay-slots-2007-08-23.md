The *Onslaught* Top Slot... Okay, *Slots*
=========================================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on August 23, 2007 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






White was the unconditional king of *Onslaught* tournament play. I would argue that the most enduringly strong cards from the set—and, when you get to cards like [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%2C+Angel+of+Wrath) from *Legions* or [Decree of Justice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Decree+of+Justice) and [Eternal Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Dragon) from *Scourge*, the whole shebang—are the poster children of this block. White in *Onslaught* was a flashing neon sign that advertised utility and unconditionally useful and mana consistent spells, a refreshing reversal of the previous [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel)- and [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)-centered *Odyssey* mechanics-driven strategies. White's best card—and arguably the best card in the set if not *Onslaught*, *Legions*, and *Scourge* together—remains one of my absolute favorite spells.

**Winner Winner:** [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance)

![Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance)Most main sets have a [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) wannabe, for example [Winds of Rath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Winds+of+Rath), [Rout](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rout), or [Kirtar's Wrath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kirtar%27s+Wrath). Those cards are generally staple in block play, and often claim market share in Standard. *Onslaught*'s [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) proxy was special in that it was actually a *[Nevinyrral's Disk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nevinyrral%27s+Disk)* update... It's just that few noticed because [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance) came to the ball all accessorized with ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif) next to its four colorless mana in the top corner instead of a plain brown border.

Besides a certain single, narrow, linear strategy and its *Onslaught* Block components, [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance) might be the most widely successful card of the set. Not only did this big sweeper dominate at and ultimately win Pro Tour–Venice, it took top honors at the following year's World Championships, contributing to numerous decks in between. At the Grand Prix level, [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance) helped to bring Bob Maher yet another title (some half a decade after the height of his game), and unlike the expensive block [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) replacements found in most other supplemental sets, actually made it to Extended play.

Part of the reason [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance) was, and remains, so good is that the *next* Block gave tournament sharks the Ravager Affinity deck. A well-built [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance) deck can stall Affinity and then blow up *everything*, lands included. In (*Onslaught*) Block play, or many Standard matchups, [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance) played as a multiple enchantment sweeper against creature-poor decks (even traditional [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) would not necessarily have been effective against, say Rift / Slide). 

Few people would cite *Onslaught* as a powerhouse block. It doesn't compare, fundamentally, to *Mirrodin* and its Affinity, nor *Odyssey* with its [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel)s and [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)s. However, even fewer would discount the enduring effectiveness of [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance), an expensive and ponderous sorcery actually worth its six mana.

**Runner-up:** [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide)

[Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) was something special. It was in a sense a supplement to (see below) [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift) in the default deck of *Onslaught* Block, something persistent with which you could gain (even more) value from cycling cards. However, unlike [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift), which was kind of a stack of [Shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock)s, [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) offered an essentially unique effect. Not only could it help defend against creatures of any size (where [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift) could mostly defend only against small(er) creatures), [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide), pointed at your own cards, could prove very beneficial. In Osyp Lebedowicz's many red-white decks, [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) set up [Exalted Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel)s on turn four (play the Angel face down, block, Slide it out with a one-mana cycling card, be ready to attack with a 4/5 lifelink flyer the following turn. Julien Nujiten won the 2004 World Championships with an [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) deck focused on re-seeing his [Eternal Witness](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness) every turn; with nothing else online, that two-card combo would help Julien to cycle through his own deck and return his cyclers; a second [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) would allow the champ to go bonkers.

Runner-up: [Exalted Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel)

Bob Maher once told me he thought this was the best overall *Limited* card in *Onslaught*. The edge on this card over Visara, Rorix, or one of the other incredible six-drop threats was that while [Exalted Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel) was a six-drop, it wasn't really. You could pay *seven* mana but have it fully online on turn *four.* The combinations with [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) were of course fantastic. I found it very difficult to beat tuned red-white decks with, say, Goblins because this card was so good at racing. Unlike certain other Block or linear superstars, [Exalted Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel) has had a fine career in Extended, making a Pro Tour Top 8 in Nick West's NO Stick and beating people up in a variety of roles to this day.

Eugene Harvey, second; Josh Ravitz, third;Tony Tsai, eleventh; Osyp Lebedowicz was the big loser with a Top 32 twenty-ninth. Five pilots, four money finishes, including two in the Top 4... and it took Bob Maher on a Brian Kowal deck to beat the Eugenius. Most people would point at his Pro Tour winning build of Red-White as Osyp's signature Slide deck, but it was this deck, from Grand Prix–Detroit, that is the crown jewel of his deck design resume, a clear break in the format rather than just a great individual finish.

Osyp was in love with [Wing Shards](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wing+Shards) from the first time he saw it. "You wouldn't believe the deck I could have played in Venice if I had that card available, Mike..."

"Why? Because you could have done so much better?" (Osyp won that Pro Tour.)

Well, it looks like he could!

### Blue

Blue was like the opposite of white in *Onslaught* Block, unanimously considered the almost unplayable stone dregs. 

Many commentators see *Onslaught* Block as a grand experiment. What would the world look like if we didn't have [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)? Would people play expensive, gigantic, creatures? Would they be good? Well, in the absence of [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell), people did, indeed, play big creatures, hard-casting eight-drops in the Top 4 of the Pro Tour. Goblins ended up ruining it to some degree, but at least it wasn't blue: blue wasn't ruining much of anything.

If you compare *Onslaught*'s top blue cards with the top cards from other colors, you will easily be able to see how weak the color was, by comparison, in tournament play; cards like [Arcanis the Omnipotent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcanis+the+Omnipotent) and [Callous Oppressor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Callous+Oppressor) are serious candidates for top billing! This is not to say blue didn't have a place, but it was not a powerhouse like white or its challenger red, and it lacked the enablers of green. Consider:

**Winner Winner:** [Chain of Vapor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Vapor)

![Chain of Vapor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Chain+of+Vapor)Is this really it? I looked over the list of blue *Onslaught* cards and checked it twice, scratched out my initial picks, wrote and re-wrote... and I think this minor singleton / sideboard card is the best that blue had to offer to the set. I guess somebody has to be the worst in every set, and blue usually gets to be the best.

[Chain of Vapor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Vapor) has been relatively enduring, playing a very specific role in combo decks all the way to Vintage. While most decks use it to get rid of problem permanents like [Ivory Mask](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ivory+Mask), some storm decks actually pick up their own permanents to keep their single turn counts up!

**Runner-up:**
[Future Sight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Future+Sight)

This card is very interesting. I think that sometime before it rotates out of Extended we may again see it in a key role in a very good combo or control deck. 

In the right deck, [Future Sight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Future+Sight) is like a personal [Howling Mine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine), albeit much more powerful given a favorable top of a deck. Its only downside -- and it isn't that much of a downside considering the fact that without [Future Sight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Future+Sight) you don't get *anything* extra -- is that [Future Sight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Future+Sight) is not particularly synergistic with reactive cards, limiting its usefulness in non-explosive combo decks.

All that said, to me, the quintessential [Future Sight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Future+Sight) deck is Andrew Cuneo's, which he used to win the 2003 MTGO Worlds Qualfier. Zev "[Standstill](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill)" Gurwitz would eventually adopt Andrew's [Future Sight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Future+Sight)s in the [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) deck he used to qualify for U.S. Nationals.

**Runner-up:** [Annex](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Annex)

When I first saw [Annex](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Annex), I thought of it as "a Control Magic for lands," which according to both cost and functionality, it essentially is. This card has been hot and cold over the years, everything from overrated to a killer sideboard card in control-on-control. Last year, [Annex](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Annex)-[Wildfire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire) was a key anti-[Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) Standard deck, and [Annex](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Annex) has been a middling-to-very good sideboard card in various UrzaTron decks, used both for disrupting other powerful, if slow, decks and completing 'Tron in the mirror. 

In the red-and-white world of *Onslaught* Block, this card was never popular.

**Winner Winner:** [Patriarch's Bidding](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Patriarch%27s+Bidding)

![Patriarch's Bidding](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Patriarch%27s+Bidding)This card is elegant and brutal at the same time... It is sort of like a fatal car crash involving a newly shined classic European sports coupe. Bidding decks have served Beats, Goblins, Zombies, and even Elementals! The most famous sort of Bidding deck is of course a Goblin deck, which generates critical mass with [Skirk Prospector](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skirk+Prospector)s and [Goblin Sharpshooter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Sharpshooter)s, jacking mana, dealing damage, untapping, eventually playing the Bidding (getting back [Goblin Warchief](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Warchief), of course) and doing it again until the opponent is dead. The masters of Goblin Bidding could win seemingly out of nowhere, starting with a single [Goblin Matron](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Matron) but ending the game the very same turn.

Visara online is hard to beat on turns six and seven, when she's been played at retail. On turn *two*... The opponent had better be cheating, too.

Incidentally, Visara is my pick for best open in *Onslaught*... I once had both an [Exalted Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel)*and* a [Rorix Bladewing](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rorix+Bladewing)*in play* and lost to this monster.

**Runner-up:** [Smother](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother)

Though it wasn't universally played at the onset, [Smother](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother) grew into one of the set's key role players. Why? Think of the context that [Smother](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother) found itself in when first printed for Standard. What were the defining cards? The defining *creatures?* Though ineffective against many signature *Onslaught* threats, like Visara, Rorix, or Arcanis, [Smother](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother) put a real hurt on [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) and [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog). For this reason, [Smother](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother) has remained popular in Extended, though in recent years, it has lost some market share to [Putrefy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrefy) (which can also fight [Isochron Scepter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter)) and [Sudden Shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sudden+Shock).

### Red

Red was superb in *Onslaught*. Some players will disagree that white was the strongest, and they will do so because of red; red, in the form of [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift) and sometimes [Starstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Starstorm) and [Slice and Dice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slice+and+Dice), was an integral part of the default white deck. On top of that, red had its own color, its own strategy: Goblins. As a strategy, Goblins is the most enduring to come out of *Onslaught* (Block); it is one of the most popular decks in Legacy, and still sometimes played in Extended.

**Winner Winner:** [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift)

![Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lightning+Rift)I think that second-turn [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift) decided more games in the *Onslaught* Block Constructed PTQ season than any other factor. Though [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance) was an almost incomparable staple, to my recollection, [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift) was the actual best card in the format. If you ever find yourself in a dark alley with [Sneak Attack](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sneak+Attack), you should make it a point not to get hit by any creature attached to a [Blazing Shoal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blazing+Shoal)... But a hasted [Dragon Tyrant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragon+Tyrant) is liable to kill you outright.

**Runner-up:** [Goblin Piledriver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Piledriver)

![Goblin Piledriver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Goblin+Piledriver)I was torn as to which *Onslaught* Goblin should get the nod. If you take all of the past four years into consideration, multiple Goblins could reasonably be considered more significant than one or both of the previous red cards... But [Lightning Rift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Rift) was the best in Block, and continues to inspire even in Extended, and Rorix is arguably the best six-drop creature ever, a superstar of cheating, and, like Akroma, good enough to see play at retail. We could arguably have considered...*[Goblin Sharpshooter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Sharpshooter):* The centerpiece of Goblins and Goblin Bidding critical mass... Besides once played in a cute [Kamahl, Fist of Krosa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kamahl%2C+Fist+of+Krosa) theme deck for land destruction purposes, the Sharpshooter has seen little or no play out of the core *Onslaught* linear archetype, and even there, often shows up only as a bullet one-of.

*[Sparksmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sparksmith):* A truly abusive Limited card (especially at common), [Sparksmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sparksmith) has been a component in the Goblins decks as a one-of bullet, all the way to a four-of suicide cannon in creature-heavy formats. Like Goibin Sharpshooter, this card serves a unique and powerful function... but not one that is necessarily core to the Goblins strategy.

*[Skirk Prospector](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skirk+Prospector):* This card is sort of the hand of the Goblins decks. This is the deceptively little tool with which Goblins performs all of its mischief. There is no Siege-Gang / Sharpshooter critical mass without [Skirk Prospector](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skirk+Prospector), and even the vanilla broken Piledriver draws all start with [Skirk Prospector](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skirk+Prospector), if just to get Warchief into play on turn two. It has been a four-of in nearly every serious Goblins deck since the debut of *Onslaught*.

As popular as [Skirk Prospector](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skirk+Prospector) has been in this and related archetypes, the All-Star has to be [Goblin Piledriver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Piledriver). People kind of forget that the only third-turn kill in Block (second turn [Goblin Warchief](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Warchief), third turn three or even four Piledrivers) needed a Prospector assist... To them, it is a trinity of 7/2s striking them in the face while they are tapped for [Astral Slide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) or [Explosive Vegetation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Explosive+Vegetation). [Goblin Piledriver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Piledriver) has raced second-turn Akroma and cemented Goblins as *the* deck of Legacy. It is the icon of offense, and, as far as I can remember, has only been cut once, when the metagame was all Goblins mirrors and Affinity. There are countless successful decks with one Sharpshooter, one [Sparksmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sparksmith), four Prospectors, and four Piledrivers... ### Green

I think that green in *Onslaught* is the hardest color to discuss. It wasn't completely flaccid like blue, and it wasn't overflowing with format dominance like white. It had numerous cards that did certain things well, or uniquely ([Explosive Vegetation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Explosive+Vegetation)); seductive, blue-like effects that got creative juices flowing ([Krosan Tusker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker)), and newly repositioned staples ([Naturalize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize))... but none of those do it for me, not for what we are discussing, really. The Vegetation deck was exceptional at the Pro Tour, but had numerous structural flaws that were exposed over the course of an evolving metagame, and Green-White Silvos was never seen again come three-set PTQ play. [Krosan Tusker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker) is *still* brimming with potential because no one has utilized it outside of a niche role in Slide decks or as a supplemental fixer. [Naturalize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize) consistently finishes second behind whatever other option is available—[Wear Away](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wear+Away), [Rending Vines](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rending+Vines), [Krosan Grip](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Grip), even [Seed Spark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seed+Spark)—and has never really succeeded in fulfilling the popularity of the original [Disenchant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant). Consider the following:

**Winner winner:** [Ravenous Baloth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Baloth)

![Ravenous Baloth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ravenous+Baloth)Do you realize there was a time when green-white decks were willing to play [Nettletooth Djinn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettletooth+Djinn) on the Pro Tour?

Look at how far green 4/4 creatures have come!

[Loxodon Hierarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch) has marginalized [Ravenous Baloth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Baloth) somewhat in Extended (*Ravnica* lands make the white splash too easy), but [Ravenous Baloth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Baloth) still sees play as a redundant four-drop and is preferable in Beasts themed or sub-themed decks, helping to give those decks additional value on [Spiritmonger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spiritmonger)s, [Indrik Stomphowler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Indrik+Stomphowler)s, etc.

**Runner-up:** [Wirewood Savage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Savage)

Surprised? In the right circumstances, this card will allow you to draw your deck for essentially no mana! [Cavern Harpy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy) is a beast. I mean sure, [Cavern Harpy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy) was awesome in *Invasion* Limited, but she is *also* actually a *Beast*. You can play [Cavern Harpy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy) as many times as you want. She has to return a blue or black creature when she comes into play... Why not herself? With [Aluren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aluren) out, she comes down for free. So [Aluren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aluren) + [Cavern Harpy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy) + [Wirewood Savage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Savage) allows you to draw your deck without any mana cost... Once you've done that, you have by definition seen all your toys and you should probably be able to win. Might I suggest gaining infinite life with [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden), then dealing 20 (or however much) to both players with [Maggot Carrier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maggot+Carrier)?

### Lands

Everyone knows that the real winner winners from *Onslaught* were [Bloodstained Mire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodstained+Mire), [Polluted Delta](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta), [Flooded Strand](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flooded+Strand), [Windswept Heath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windswept+Heath), and [Wooded Foothills](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wooded+Foothills). These cards remain better, even today in Extended and Legacy, than any other cards from the set or block. They are played in red decks to fuel [Grim Lavamancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer) and [Fledgling Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fledgling+Dragon), splash Kataki in [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) decks to beat Affinity, marry [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)s to [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel)s in Madness love-fests, find [Forest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s (or [Forest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest) dual lands) to power up [Kird Ape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape)s in nongreen decks, and thin out Goblins draws because everyone knows that you don't win by actually paying retail. 

All that, and they make sure that you have the right mana to pay for your spells in the early game. I have this cycle ahead of the *Ravnica* duals (both staple cycles), and everything short of the originals... and [Graven Cairns](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Graven+Cairns) (that dual is awesome).
