The scariest cards in this group were 1) [Magma Burst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Burst), and 2) [Pollen Remedy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pollen+Remedy). If you weren't playing during *Invasion* Block Limited-particularly Team Limited-you might not remember the savagery of [Magma Burst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Burst). [Magma Burst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Burst) was so effective in Limited play it made me *believe,* believe that a four mana spell doing three damage might be good enough to make the 60 card break (it wasn't)… But it remains one of history's most feared common cards. Just call up Wizards of the Coast headquarters in Renton, WA and ask for **Magic** bigwig Aaron Forsythe; ask the old man how he feels about [Magma Burst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Burst). Better yet, [read on](http://archive.wizards.com/sideboard/article.asp?x=PTNY01%5C725fm12).


The reason [Pollen Remedy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pollen+Remedy) was scary-despite the fact that it was only a marginally played Draft card-is that, well, look what it does to some poor Joe Aaron who goes all in on [Magma Burst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Burst).


Next up is a fair number of cards that actually were, are, or could-or should-be role players in Constructed **Magic**.



![Agonizing Demise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Agonizing+Demise)

### [Agonizing Demise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Agonizing+Demise)


Dave Williams played two copies of this card in his Top 8 deck from Pro Tour-Tokyo. [Agonizing Demise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Agonizing+Demise) is a classic example of a card that is a first pick in Limited and barely playable in Constructed… But in Block, "barely" is still on the playable side of the binary. Destroying a creature for four mana is not really what you are looking to do in sixty card competition, but a limited card pool will force inventive deck designers-in Dave's case Wisconsin's Brian Kowal, if memory serves-to find the tools for the job. I don't know how often the Kicker came up, but just the *thought* of sticking it to a [Shivan Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shivan+Wurm) or [Jade Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jade+Leech) with that little extra oomph gets my heart racing and my palms all sweaty.


### [Ana Battlemage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ana+Battlemage)


I am not sure what deck [Ana Battlemage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ana+Battlemage) will eventually find himself in, but I have got to believe that this card will make an impact (primarily for the blue kicker). [Stunted Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stunted+Growth) was a legitimate game ender-much like [Plow Under](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under) was a year or two ago in Standard-and [Ana Battlemage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ana+Battlemage) on six offers a similar effect for just one more mana, additionally leaving a 2/2 body. The black kicker is something we can reasonably imagine as relevant as well, though probably just conditional gravy.


### [Bog Down](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bog+Down)


Like [Agonizing Demise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Agonizing+Demise), [Bog Down](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bog+Down) was played in Dave's Tokyo Top 8 deck. Its main function was as a kind of [Stupor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor) for +1 card advantage, though the kicker was-surprisingly-played a fair amount as well (at least in PTQ-level play), usually on the draw. Playing even might not seem like the greatest utilization of resources, and going down two lands is the kind of thing that *the opponent* would pay five or six mana to accomplish, but the ability to knock down three cards can't be underrated in some matchups, and can be quite useful when you have the lead, or perhaps just too much mana.


### [Breath of Darigaaz](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breath+of+Darigaaz)


I actually think this card is weaker than the next-up [Canopy Surge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Canopy+Surge), even though [Earthquake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Earthquake) (the card it was obviously modeled after) has consistently been a Constructed staple. The reason is that [Breath of Darigaaz](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breath+of+Darigaaz) is a poor man's [Earthquake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Earthquake) in a context of numerous red elimination cards ([Flametongue Kavu](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flametongue+Kavu), [Ghitu Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghitu+Fire), [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames), etc.), whereas [Canopy Surge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Canopy+Surge) offered a somewhat unique option for its color. An awesome first pick in Limited, Breath never accomplished very much in Constructed tournament play.


### [Canopy Surge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Canopy+Surge)


One of the reasons [Stonewood Invocation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stonewood+Invocation) has become such a scary threat for control is that green has long been thin on the finishers; [Stonewood Invocation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stonewood+Invocation) makes a control player think, and play differently as well, maybe using bounce cards on his own turn rather than waiting for the opponent to commit mid-combats. Other colors have things like [Meloku the Clouded Mirror](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Meloku%2Bthe%2BClouded%2BMirror), [Haunting Echoes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Haunting+Echoes), and [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire) to bring about the end times, but green has had to settle largely for [Hurricane](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurricane). [Canopy Surge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Canopy+Surge) is a disciple of the old [Hurricane](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurricane) (obviously) and can fill many of the same roles. It is flying defense, yes, but perhaps more importantly, it can kill opposing magicians when green has the lead.



![Citanul Woodreaders](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Citanul+Woodreaders)

### [Citanul Woodreaders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Citanul+Woodreaders)


[Horned Turtle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Horned+Turtle) [River Kaijin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Kaijin) helped Antonino De Rosa earn a Grand Prix title in the face of [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte)… [Citanul Woodreaders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Citanul+Woodreaders) is the [River Kaijin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Kaijin) that can potentially do so much more. Perfectly positioned in a color that can produce the requisite kicker mana, my guess is that this card will be a surprisingly effective role player at some point down the line. Don't be surprised if mono-green mana ramp / board stall ends up a rogue option for *Time Spiral* Block PTQs.


### [Desolation Giant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desolation+Giant)


The most unexpected thing about this card in my opinion is that it doesn't see *more* play, especially in blue-red-white Angelfire sideboards as a redundant [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God). [Desolation Giant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desolation+Giant) has almost no pedigree, but it is long on potential.


That leaves us with…


### The 20 Greatest Kicker Cards of All Time


### [Desolation Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desolation+Angel)


Possibly the strongest kicker card of them all on pedigree, [Desolation Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desolation+Angel) was part of a deck that, in late *Invasion* Block Constructed, defined the format like no other deck since High Tide in Extended some years earlier. The Angel deck attacked the opponent on a resource level-smashing his hand with [Gerrard's Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gerrard%27s+Verdict) and sometimes [Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Probe), blasting his mana base with [Recoil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Recoil) and [Vindicate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate)-then reloaded with [Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction) and ended it all with a juggernaut of a 5/4 Armageddon. Angel put up an amazing string of wins on the PTQ circuit and essentially *was* late GP play. Here is a look at the archetype care of future PT winner Rickard Österberg:

### [Dismantling Blow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismantling+Blow)


Arguing over this card (versus [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant)) got me a legendary lecture from arguably the greatest deck designer of all time, Zvi Mowshowitz. Zvi and teammate Scott Johns ran [Dismantling Blow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismantling+Blow) main in their blue-white deck at U.S. Nationals 2001 when one of the chief threats of the format was [Saproling Burst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saproling+Burst) out of [Fires of Yavimaya](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fires+of+Yavimaya). The reason? [Dismantling Blow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismantling+Blow) on your own [Tsabo's Web](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tsabo%27s+Web) is a heck of a lot more effective in the mirror than a "dead" [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant)! This version of blue-white has two interesting bits of trivia associated with it, which I have probably touched on in this column in the past: 1) the sideboard [Mahamoti Djinn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mahamoti+Djinn)s are an important precursor to the modern "tap-out" blue strategies advocated by myself and others, and 2) it was full of so many cantrips, Scott Johns actually suggested going to 61!

### [Jilt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jilt)


Once upon a time there was a card called [Barrin's Spite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barrin%27s+Spite) and it was awesome. It was one of the rares players were largely overjoyed to open. [Barrin's Spite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barrin%27s+Spite) was a first pick that defined a player's draft and color combination. Some players would flirt with running the Spite in Constructed because it was so good on tempo, removing two creatures from the board, allowing the future Dimir colors to get in there on a great attack, setting up a ton of damage and preserving tempo…


And then they printed [Jilt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jilt) - an *instant* - at common. In a Limited format defined largely by bear (2/2 for two) fights, [Jilt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jilt)'s "deal two" was nearly as good as the Spite's sacrifice… and [Jilt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jilt) let a man *aim.*


Ultimately, [Jilt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jilt) became a go-to card for sideboard space, specifically in blue-red-white [Opposition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opposition) or madness decks. It was great on the mana and controlling the game's tempo (just think about this card with damage on the stack, blocking with a [Flametongue Kavu](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flametongue+Kavu)) but more than that… [Jilt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jilt) was *blue* direct damage.


### [Kavu Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kavu+Titan)


[Kavu Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kavu+Titan) is a great card we don't think about much because of its timing. When it first came out, one of the big enemies for green-red was Rebels, and Rebels had [Parallax Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Parallax+Wave), and this creature comes back from the wrong side of reality as a 2/2 regardless of how much mana you spent on it; that made [Jade Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jade+Leech) the safer bet in general. None of this is poor [Kavu Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kavu+Titan)'s fault.

### [Phyrexian Scuta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Scuta)


An obsession for numerous players over the years has been "replacing [Juzam Djinn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Juzam+Djinn)." Numerous pretenders, including green cards like [Blastoderm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blastoderm) and [Jade Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jade+Leech) have made the rounds, yet ultimately started their own lineages. The reason I like this card (which has never been particularly good for me) is the pedigree of its posterity. [Phyrexian Scuta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Scuta) was a kind of Juzam played in the first big deck by a future big deck designer and one of my favorite human beings:

One of the underrated cards coming out of Masques Block Constructed was [Nether Spirit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nether+Spirit). The [Nether Spirit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nether+Spirit) control deck never had the numbers that Rebels could command, and was admittedly lacking in certain card advantage elements, but it had a lot of good spot removal and an almost unstoppable solitary threat. Enter Invasion, and enter [Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Probe).


Even without the kicker, [Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Probe) was a great combination with [Nether Spirit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nether+Spirit)… That much should be obvious (Probing out a [Nether Spirit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nether+Spirit) at ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161225105850im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless2.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif) was basically the same as playing one for the same converted mana cost, but you also got to dig, draw, and profit short term). [Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Probe)-Go started to see play as early as Champs, largely in straight black-blue form. By Worlds 2001 and Apocalypse, the mana base was there for a black-blue-white version that could play [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God). ### [Prohibit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prohibit)


People will play whatever "counter target spell" variant you give them, and [Prohibit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prohibit) is proof of it. At two mana [Prohibit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prohibit) is fine, particularly in a format based on braining each other with 2/2 Bears, but in the kicker range, it starts getting a little expensive (but hey, you do what you've got to with the tools you get). Brian Kowal used this card to good effect, winning the first *Invasion* Block Constructed PTQ with black-blue-green.

### [Rushing River](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rushing+River)


This card was *the* bounce spell of choice during the reign of Madness in Standard, also earning spots in Extended Madness decks. At one point, [Rushing River](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rushing+River) was a go-to bounce spell in combo for its ability to simultaneously deal with, say, an [Elvish Lyrist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Lyrist) and a [Seal of Cleansing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seal+of+Cleansing). It has since been at least moderately supplanted by [Echoing Truth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Truth) in bigger formats.


Here is an example of a Trix combo deck utilizing [Rushing River](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rushing+River), designed by Top 16 Dragonmaster Brian Kibler:

### [Sunscape Battlemage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscape+Battlemage)


This was a minority sideboard card in numerous green-white or green-white-blue decks. Why? It could kill [Blinding Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinding+Angel), which would consistently lock those decks-strong on the ground but poor against dedicated white board control elements like [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) &co.-giving green-white a shot where it would have otherwise been locked.
