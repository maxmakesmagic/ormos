Trial and Error – Last Chance Grand Prix Trial Winners
------------------------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

As the **Magic** season drags on, players are on a constant quest for whatever small scraps of information they can use to give them an edge. With Grand Prix-Washington DC just barely in the books, the players preparing for Grand Prix-Sendai haven't had much time to adjust things based on the information they gleaned from the results in the States. However, within the meager two weeks, the ripples from DC have grown and affected the format in some interesting ways.


Amidst the decks that have become staples of the most recent incarnation of the format (
 [Vengevine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)
 Naya, Mythic Conscription, Jund, UW Tapout, and UWR Planeswalkers), a few less well-known decks have surfaced here in Sendai. The first that caught my eye as I scanned the winning lists was an Allies deck piloted by Takuya Maekawa. It's a hyper-aggressive red/white/green deck relying on the cheap allies of those colors to beat opponents into submission. In addition to the efficient beaters,
 [Kabira Evangel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kabira+Evangel)
 provides a path through blockers, while
 [Harabaz Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Harabaz+Druid)
 provides some mana acceleration and fixing. Outside the Ally shell,
 [Ranger of Eos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos)
 can be used to refill his hand or provide him access to the copies of
 [Goblin Bushwhacker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker)
 to enhance his team further. Lastly, since virtually every creature in his deck has an effect upon entering the battlefield,
 [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf)
 is always a hit, especially if it snags a
 [Naya Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naya+Charm)
 to clear the way for attackers.


Another deck that caught my eye was an updated version of the graveyard decks that were mostly experimented with at the beginning of the season. Youichi Nagami relied on the power of
 [Hedron Crab](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hedron+Crab)
 ,
 [Merfolk Looter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merfolk+Looter)
 , and the new
 [Enclave Cryptologist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enclave+Cryptologist)
 to sift through his deck, while relying on creatures that return from the graveyard to provide the muscle. Just in case things get dicey, an
 [Eldrazi Monument](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Monument)
 can lift the team to the air or provide an impenetrable wall of creatures for defense. Cards like
 [Bloodghast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodghast)
 and
 [Vengevine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)
 pull double duty here, acting as sources of damage, excellent discard to the various looters in the deck, and recursive sacrifices to the Monument. If things need to be cleared out of the way,
 [Sedraxis Alchemist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sedraxis+Alchemist)
 can push it out of the way, which is a great way to deal with an
 [Eldrazi Conscription](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription)
 or maybe even the one doing the conscripting-
 [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn)
 .

Feature Match Round 3: Submission - Kenji Tsumura (UW Tapout) vs. Kazuaki Arahori (Esper Control)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

I usually expect to have a few rounds to work on some other projects since most of the action tends to begin when the players with three byes come to battle in round four. This time around, I was given a treat! In 2001, the first Grand Prix-Sendai was held no more than ten miles from this very spot. Kazuaki Arahori clawed his way to a victory in only the second Extended tournament in Japanese history. Here, nearly a decade later, he has come to regain his title in this third Grand Prix-Sendai.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsen10/fm3Shakehands.jpg)The man he would have to defeat this round on the way to his goal was a Japanese legend in the **Magic** community. Kenji Tsumura, once one of the most feared players in the world, was forced to take a long hiatus from **Magic** in preparation for his University entrance exams. With them successfully completed, he is now able to once again travel and play the game he loves. Tsumura is famous not only for his lightning quick play, but the lightning quick smile that always seems to appear on his face while playing. But don't let the smile fool you, he is as dangerous an opponent as they come, even after his time off.


Arahori won the roll and went first. His Esper-control deck fed him a hand of white control cards and a couple of
 [Plains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains)
 to start the game out. Tsumura, piloting a UW Tapout deck, helped to fix Arahori's mana with a
 [Spreading Seas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)
 on a
 [Plains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains)
 . Unfortunately, Arahori was a bit light on the mana side. He managed to find an
 [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)
 for his third land on the fourth turn, but soon returned to failing to draw lands. Even a
 [Wall of Omens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens)
 turned up no help.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsen10/fm3Extra.jpg)*Wanna kill her then you better put O Ring on it.*
Tsumura, on the other side of the table, had been consistently making land drops, as well as Mistfield Borderpost drops, enabling him to cast an
 [Elspeth, Knight-Errant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant)
 . Arahori managed to lock her in an
 [Oblivion Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring)
 , but Tsumura put a Ring around the Ring, returning her to play. Arahori added yet another
 [Oblivion Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring)
 to lock her away, as well as one for the
 [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)
 that followed her. Tsumura had a huge advantage by this point, though, and a
 [Mind Spring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring)
 for five refilled his hand. With plenty of gas in the tank, Tsumura was able to seal the deal with a second copy of Jace. At the end of the game, Arahori had still only managed to get three lands into play.


Kazuaki Arahori 0 – Kenji Tsumura 1


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsen10/fm3Arahori.jpg)*Kazuaki Arahori is no stranger to Magical success in Sendai*
Arahori's start for the second game was considerably better. He had a
 [Plains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains)
 ,
 [Arcane Sanctum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcane+Sanctum)
 , and
 [Glacial Fortress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glacial+Fortress)
 , all left above the
 [Spreading Seas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)
 . This diverse mana allowed him to draw two cards with his
 [Esper Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Charm)
 at the end of Tsumura's turn before untapping and
 [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)
 ing him. Tsumura wasn't having any of that and
 [Negate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate)
 d it. After Tsumura tapped out for an
 [Everflowing Chalice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice)
 for two, Arahori took advantage of the opportunity to draw a couple more cards off of another Charm and sneak a
 [Malakir Bloodwitch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Malakir+Bloodwitch)
 into play. True to the name of his deck, Tsumura tapped out on his turn to trump the Bloodwitch with the incredibly resilient
 [Sphinx of Jwar Isle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx+of+Jwar+Isle)
 .


An
 [Elspeth, Knight-Errant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant)
 for Arahori started got to add one Soldier token to play before it was eaten by Tsumura's Sphinx. When Tsumura attempted to
 [Mind Spring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring)
 for a new hand on the following turn, Arahori stopped it with
 [Negate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate)
 . With a handful of cards to Tsumura's three, Arahori began the arduous process of climbing back on top of the game. He used a
 [Spreading Seas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)
 to shut off Tsumura's
 [Celestial Colonnade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade)
 , attacked for five, and added a
 [Creeping Tar Pit](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Tar+Pit)
 to his team.


Tsumura drew for his turn and added a
 [Jace Beleren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren)
 to his squad. Jace is an incredibly potent card in blue-based control matches like this, not only for his ability to draw cards, but for his ability to destroy what is probably the most important card in the match up- opposing
 [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)
 s. Tsumura used the planeswalker to allow both players to draw a card and left his Sphinx home to run interference.


Arahori cycled a
 [Spreading Seas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)
 on a
 [Sejiri Refuge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sejiri+Refuge)
 before adding a
 [Baneslayer Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel)
 to his team and passing the turn tapped out. Tsumura took advantage of the window to cast a
 [Martial Coup](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup)
 for six, netting him a massive army and the only creatures on the table. He removed a counter from Jace to draw a card and passed the turn. With Jace dropped to four loyalty, Arahori took the opportunity to off him with an attack from his
 [Celestial Colonnade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade)
 . A
 [Wall of Omens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens)
 followed, drawing him a card.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsen10/fm3Tsumura.jpg)*For those of you newer Pros out there, this is Kenji Tsumura. Memorize his face and be afraid if it's across from you.*
Unfortunately, the Wall had little effect on the swarming Soldiers of Tsumura's army. With a little enhancement from Elspeth, Tsumura's team rumbled over. A
 [Doom Blade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade)
 on the enhanced token saved Arahori some damage, but he was dropping fast. His Tar Pit and Colonnade teamed up to deal with the Elspeth, but another soon replaced it. With Tsumura's team taking four-point chunks out of his life total, he couldn't continue to play this planeswalker-destruction game for long. This time, rather than expend all of his mana to attack the Elspeth, he kept his team home. Tsumura lifted a Soldier with Elspeth and attacked in with his whole team. Arahori traded his
 [Celestial Colonnade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade)
 for the bigger Soldier, but not before tapping it to try to draw into an answer with
 [Esper Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Charm)
 .


With Arahori now tapped out, Tsumura used a
 [Mind Spring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring)
 to completely refill his hand. A
 [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)
 was met with
 [Negate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate)
 , leaving Arahori with only an
 [Esper Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Charm)
 , a land, and a
 [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)
 in hand. The Jace hit play, immediately
 [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm)
 ing a way out of this tight spot. The little
 [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm)
 ing session paid off in spades, as Arahori was gifted with an
 [Oblivion Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring)
 for the Elspeth, as well as a
 [Mind Spring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring)
 for him to fill his hand up on a later turn. Unfortunately for him, Tsumura had already gotten to fill his hand up, and from the depths of his giant hand, he managed to find an
 [Oblivion Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring)
 of his own to bring his Elspeth back for lethal damage.


Kazuaki Arahori 0 – Kenji Tsumura 2


 

Feature Match Round 5: Never Ever Ever - Martin Juza (Mythic Conscription) vs. Shuu Komuro (Jund)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

As he sat wandered over to the feature match area with the look of a loss still fresh on his face, Martin Juza said to me, "You really should have picked someone else. I never win any more."


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsen10/round05b_shakehands.jpg)For someone who "doesn't win anymore," Juza's first two turns were pretty absurd. After winning the die roll,
 [Noble Hierarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch)
 ,
 [Lotus Cobra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra)
 , and a
 [Verdant Catacombs](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Verdant+Catacombs)
 allowed a
 [Dauntless Escort](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dauntless+Escort)
 to hit the table on the second turn. Juza thought for a second about which of his three drops to play, eventually settling on the Escort over the

*by Nate Price*

Good versus evil. The weak against the strong. The oppressed facing their oppressors.


The most memorable and inspiring stories of human history have always been about the struggle between a hero up against insurmountable odds. From ancient Greece to modern Japanese anime, the hero myth has always served as an example of how continuing to struggle against the odds can lead to unimagined success. Part of the reason they're so successful is that we all secretly wish we were the hero. We want to feel the crushing despair of an unwinnable situation. We want to feel the claustrophobic pressure of insurmountable odds. We want to feel these things because when you hit rock bottom, it feels that much better when we come out on top. We want to do the impossible.


Well, what if I told you that you could?


Enter [Archenemy](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/feature/91). We previewed it for you in [our San Juan coverage](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptsj10/arcfeature), so hopefully you're at least a little acquainted with it. For those who aren't, here's a crash course. You are one of two forces—either a maniacal force bent on the destruction of the world, overwhelming in power, or a team of intrepid heroes, struggling against the might of your diabolical foe. As the oppressively powerful Archenemy, you obviously have a much greater vitality and higher life than the pitiful gnats standing up to you. The sources of your power, such as your laser-sharks, moon base, and mind control powers, are represented by oversized (though clearly not large enough to convey the true depth of your awesome power) cards called schemes. These generally have some ridiculous abilities, such as untapping all permanents during each untap, putting creatures directly into play from your hand, drawing all the cards in the world, or blowing an opponent up. Completely. With this overwhelming power, the Archenemy is far too strong to be taken on by a solitary challenger. Instead, the upstart mortals must gang up in an attempt to equal your power.


Fools.


As the challengers, it's up to you and two of your friends to pool your resources to overcome the evil Archenemy. You contribute everything you can individually to the teams efforts—creatures, enchantments, spells…whatever you can scrape together. This is a desperate fight, and you must treat it accordingly. Once you've assembled your army, you and your friends must stand up and attack your Archenemy until he topples over dead. You get to be the hero, the savior.


Just as in San Juan, players in attendance would be blessed with the opportunity to become the hero and stand up to the true evil whose true name cannot be comprehended by the measly worms surrounding him. To those who fear and despise him, he is merely known as Ron Foster.


After watching Ron get demolished by a quartet of valiant heroes, I decided to try my hand at villainy. The world brought some of its best to attempt to stop me, as long-time ***Magic*** pro Alex West and San Juan Top 8 finisher and tcgplayer.com columnist Noah Swartz were among my opponents. I let them choose the method of their demise, and the masochists thought they could stop me from assembling the doomsday machine. After scheming and plotting for about ten turns, their confidence was proven well-founded. Under an annihilating tsunami of Eldrazi and their conscripts, all of the pieces of my glorious doomsday machine were washed away. Triumphant, the challengers departed with their spoils, waiting until another Archenemey would rise to endanger the world. Another chance for them to be the hero.
