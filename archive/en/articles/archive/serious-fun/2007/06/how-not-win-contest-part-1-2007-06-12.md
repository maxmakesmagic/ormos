How Not To Win A Contest, Part 1
================================



 Posted in **Serious Fun**
 on June 12, 2007 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_theferrett.jpg)
By The Ferrett












So the "Most Powerful Card In *Future Sight*" contest sent well over two hundred and thirty emails tumbling into my inbox as everyone made their case for their favorite.


Most of you were passionate, giving me decks and long essays and funny stories. Some of you got disqualified early by not following the rules. (Hint: if you didn't have the exact words "Future Sight Powerful!" in the subject line of your email, you were out; my spam filter is strict but firm about such matters.) But when the dust cleared and my eyes stopped burning from staring at the computer screen, I could choose only one.


Let us look at your collective votes. And then let us discuss the reasons that some were not picked.

The interesting thing about these lists is that the same errors kept getting made; those who suggested [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) tended to leave out the same vital facts that could have turned "an interesting email" into a "contest-breaking winner," and the folks who punted for [Chronomantic Escape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chronomantic+Escape) had their own omissions. So we'll walk down the Top 10 Most Popular Cards, and discuss the merits of both the cards and their entries.


But it seems a shame to leave out the people who amused me, so let's start with the three most amusing entries:



> 
> *The most powerful multiplayer card in the* Future Sight *expansion is...*
> 
> 
> *[Steamflogger Boss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steamflogger+Boss)! Not only does it pump [Mistform Ultimus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistform+Ultimus) and other copies, but you get two contraptions for the price of one. Two! How could any opponents outmatch that card advantage? If you have multiples out, a single contraption maker would instead create sixteen! With [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter), [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double), and [Clone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clone) in standard with the Ultimus and the Boss himself, who could stand up to him? Just look at this sample decklist that proves its worth.*
> 
>

**[Thunderblade Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thunderblade+Charge)**



> 
> *The Most Powerful Mutiplayer Card in* Future Sight *is probably often overlooked as a "Bad [Hammer of Bogardan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hammer+of+Bogardan)." I guess a hammer is hammer, though.*
> 
> 
> 
> ![Thunderblade Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Thunderblade+Charge)
> 
> *[Thunderblade Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thunderblade+Charge) is every bit as good as its ancestor in multiplayer. I can't think of the number of times my last Lightning Bolt was buried in my deck and some new threat was threatening the board. With [Thunderblade Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thunderblade+Charge), every creature you poke through to hit a player is carrying a Sword Of Fire But Not Ice, assuming you have an extra five mana. Reusability has always been a strong characteristic in multiplayer, and this effect can be repeated ad infinitum. Of note, a lowly 1/1 can sneak through to deal a single point to an opponent, only for the Thunderblade to burn off someone else's threat. Blue [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) guy is gaining card advantage with his [Thieving Magpie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thieving+Magpie)? Send a Goblin to the green mage who's seeing the worst of it. I know I wouldn't mind taking a single point (or sometimes two or three) to let someone burn down a [Windborn Muse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windborn+Muse) or a [Shadowmage Infiltrator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shadowmage+Infiltrator) or a [Nacatl War-Pride](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nacatl+War-Pride), even.*
> 
> 
> *Of course, with double strike or an extra combat step, your [Thunderblade Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thunderblade+Charge)s can do more than three damage in a single turn, something the original [Hammer of Bogardan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hammer+of+Bogardan) couldn't.* (But at a horrendous mana cost – T.F.)
> 
> 
> *Good cards in multiplayer answer threats, have repeatable effects, and can abuse the political nature of the multiplayer environment. [Thunderblade Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thunderblade+Charge) does all three.*
>

> 
> *[Muraganda Petroglyphs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Muraganda+Petroglyphs) is the most powerful multi-player card in* Future Sight*, and I'd like to tell you why. A Muraganda deck won't make combo players feel that you're the greatest threat. If you play with something that's flashy, you get taken out—[Nacatl War-Pride](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nacatl+War-Pride) is a great example.*

> *Sometimes, however, you want to play something that looks terrible but isn't. People may forget that you can make instant creatures that don't have flash. > 
> 
> *Its greatest advantage is the confusion factor, wondering what you're up to, but not being feeling threatened directly. You're also in color for [Privileged Position](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Privileged+Position), which doesn't give an ability to your creatures so much as lay down some rules to the other players. So one card that makes all of your creatures giant-sized all the time and lets you take less heat from the political side of the game? Pretty nice, I say.*

**The Top 10, in Reverse Order**



![Imperial Mask](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Imperial+Mask)

**[Imperial Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperial+Mask) (5 votes)**  
[Imperial Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperial+Mask) is a pretty good choice for the most powerful card; okay, it won't win you games, but it will shield you (and your partners) from spells aimed directly at your face—which means that most combos and burn decks and discard just flat-out fizzle.


That said, [Imperial Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperial+Mask) is merely a protective card. It doesn't help you to *win* the game, it merely makes it harder for you to lose—and that only qualifies if nobody has a [Naturalize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize). And some strategies don't give a dang about being targeted; if I have a big green Stompy deck that wins via attacking, [Imperial Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperial+Mask) is all but a dead draw against me, making it a risky choice unless you know your local metagame down cold.


Thus, while I really dig the effect of the Mask, I have issues calling a card "the most powerful" when it doesn't win the game and it doesn't actively interfere with your opponent's plans.


It's good. Just not good enough.


*The Problem With The Submissions: Rules Knowledge*


Unfortunately, two of the five submitters believed that "your teammates" could be defined as "Whoever I like at the time" (and one was a great Jim Carrey gag, as outlined above, which made me giggle but wasn't going to take the prize). That's not the case, sadly—even if you've temporarily allied with someone, he may still try to kill you later and thus is counted in the rules as an "opponent." So you can't give him a token, even if you really really like him and he bought you a malt down at the ol' ice cream shoppe. Sadly, given this misunderstanding, the emails they sent me regarding [Imperial Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperial+Mask) had to be discarded.


That said, Christopher L.S. pointed out that [Imperial Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperial+Mask) and [Chain of Smog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Smog) can turn quite ugly, assuming that the guy you hit wants to hurt other people. Ouch.



![Korlash, Heir to Blackblade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Korlash%2C+Heir+to+Blackblade)

**[Korlash, Heir to Blackblade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Korlash%2C+Heir+to+Blackblade) (6 votes)**


I've already gone on record as stating that mono-black decks are an *excellent* choice for an untested metagame. Many groups are creature-centric, and their decks fizzle awfully if they can't amass an army—and spells like [Innocent Blood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Innocent+Blood), [Mutilate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutilate), [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation), and [Barter in Blood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barter+in+Blood) will stop that plan cold. If you have a control player or two, they hate discard, and a [Mind Sludge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sludge) or two (or a [Persecute](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persecute), if that pesky *Ravnica* play-with-all-colors spirit hasn't infested your group) will take anyone down a peg. And lastly, [Cabal Coffers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Coffers)-fuelled [Corrupt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corrupt)s and Drain Lifes will yank pesky opponents right out of the game while hoisting your life total into a safe zone where you have time to refuel.


So Korlash comes with an excellent pedigree; he's practically *made* to drop into mono-black decks. I'll say that he's got a good shot at the top, and leave this for later.


*The Problem With The Submissions: Insufficient Delineation*


Yes, Korlash is very awesome and powerful and a horrendous finisher. But is he *more* awesome and powerful than, say, other classic mono-black finishers like [Skeletal Vampire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skeletal+Vampire), [Visara the Dreadful](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Visara+the+Dreadful), [Spirit of the Night](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spirit+of+the+Night), [Avatar of Woe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avatar+of+Woe), or [Mortivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortivore)?


I mean, he's very good. And maybe he *is* the most powerful. But when you drop him in a powerful black deck where almost *any* large, tough creature could serve as a finisher, it doesn't showcase Korlash to his best ability. When I see a deck and go, "Yeah, that's pretty standard mono-black... Oh, and there's Korlash," it doesn't excite me.


Perhaps exciting isn't the name of the game, I know. But then it's much harder to pick a winner when everyone's got the same essential idea.

![Nihilith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Nihilith)

**[Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) (8 votes)**


[Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) is one of those cards that, like [Avatar of Woe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avatar+of+Woe), is all but guaranteed to come down early and often in multiplayer. Because if you haven't read the card, kindly note that [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) doesn't care *where the card comes from*. Whether that card is discarded or destroyed, [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) has a counter yoinked off of it as long as that card goes to the graveyard.


The potential is obvious. Second turn suspend [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith), third turn [Mindlash Sliver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindlash+Sliver), sacrifice it, six players discard a card, attack. (Or even "First turn [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual), suspend [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) and play Mindlash, second turn discard and attack.") Or even, as Chan Jeff said:



> 
> *A very nifty (and craftier) use of the [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) is instead of using it with mass creature / land destruction, you use it with mass* hand*destruction. A nasty combo of [Ill-Gotten Gains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ill-Gotten+Gains) followed up by [Delirium Skeins](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delirium+Skeins) (returned via [Ill-Gotten Gains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ill-Gotten+Gains)) will strip your opponents of your hands* and *get those [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith)s into play! If you've had Nihilii die earlier, you can even grab them back with your [Ill-Gotten Gains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ill-Gotten+Gains)!*
> 
> 
> 


But there is a drawback to the [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith). Did ya see it?


*The Problem With The Submissions: Is That All There Is?*


I like the idea that [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) comes out of nowhere, and very early. But the problem that I had with it, and the submitters didn't seem to anticipate this, is that a 4/4 with fear on turn three is scary, but not *insurmountable*.


It may well be that you have the kind of card-light group where a 4/4 fear guy is going to kill everyone at the table. But at my table, we have [Diabolic Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Edict)s and Judge Unworthies and [Temporal Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temporal+Isolation)s and even red burn. A mostly vanilla 4/4 guy has to attack five times before it destroys *one* player, let alone two of them—and if you're playing with the sort of people who have no answers in ten turns for a 4/4 creature, you probably would wreck your local metagame with a [Craw Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craw+Wurm).


You get speed. That's good. But you also don't get a lot of bang for your buck when it arrives, and nobody said, "Okay, and here's what you do on turn four to *break [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) in half*." They just sort of.... stopped.


(I don't think anyone even suggested [Mindslicer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindslicer), which would have at least give the [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith) a chance against an empty-handed field.)


There might be a good [Nihilith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihilith)-based strategy out there somewhere. But it has to involve more than the attack phase.



![Barren Glory](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Barren+Glory)

**[Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) (8 votes)**


Chris Romeo summed the case for [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) up the best:



> 
> *The correct answer is [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory)—because it says right on it that "you win the game." No other card in* Future Sight *simply wins you the game. A lot of cards help you deal 20 damage or, like [Whetwheel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whetwheel), help you deck your opponent. However, no other card says that you just flat out win the game. [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory)'s right up there with [Coalition Victory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Coalition+Victory).*
> 
> 
> 


And that's true. [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) has a very potent effect. But it's a got a darned steep price! And hence we come to the problem.


*The Problem With The Submissions: The Glory Of The Perfect Draw*  
[Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) is the perfect Johnny card: it wins, it wins big, and it takes a lot to set it up. But the people who shilled for [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory), by and large, assumed that everything would go perfectly. Or, to quote one submitter named Jonathan:



> 
> *You can win fifth turn regularly with this card. Put it in a red-white deck with four [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon), four Barren Glories, four [Ignorant Bliss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ignorant+Bliss)es, and four [Lost Auramancers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lost+Auramancers). These cards are the base of the deck.*
> 
> 
> *First turn: [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) and suspend [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon). Second turn, either land. Third turn, either land. Fourth turn, have to have two plains and play [Lost Auramancers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lost+Auramancers). Fifth turn, play only a creature, artifact, or land. Sixth, play only a creature, artifact, or land. Seventh, [Auramancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auramancer)s goes away—bring [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) in. Eighth, at the beginning of your upkeep, play [Ignorant Bliss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ignorant+Bliss) to remove your hand from game, sack everything to Gargadon, and that's the win."*
> 
> 
> 


But you know what I *didn't* hear? The *other* possibilities. Things like:


Fifth turn: Josh [Ichor Slick](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichor+Slick)s my [Lost Auramancers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lost+Auramancers).  

Seventh turn: Dmitri plays [Naturalize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize) on [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory).  

Eight turn: I still haven't drawn an [Ignorant Bliss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ignorant+Bliss).


Or, as is far more likely:


Sixth turn: Since I still haven't played the slightest bit of defense, everyone's attacking me because they can and I'm down to Lightning Bolt range. Ooops.


[Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory)'s a challenging card, but the problem is that a deck designed to make sure of [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) needs a perfect draw and little disruption. It is, as they say, difficult to pull off. Though Bram V.B. gave it a darned fine shot:

That's probably the most bulletproof [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) deck I've seen; it has early defense that's friendly, and a pretty solid combo engine ([Academy Rector](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Academy+Rector) into [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) is very nice, particularly if you can get a [Mindslicer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindslicer) off beforehand), and even *then* it's reasonably tricky to pull off.


Still, for it to be the most powerful card, I tend to think that a *variety* of powerful decks have to be built around it. One very powerful deck does not mean that [Barren Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Glory) is the most powerful card in *Future Sight*; it means, most likely, that it's the most potentially powerful card in *Future Sight*, but then again you could say that [Mortal Combat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortal+Combat) is potentially a powerful card, too.


Thus, I shall regretfully pass.



![Akroma's Memorial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Akroma%27s+Memorial)

**[Akroma's Memorial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Memorial) (8 Votes)**


I'll admit that I bought a couple of copies of this sucker, and it's quite good in the right kind of deck. Of course, Akroming all of your creatures (assuming that is a verb) is a darned good thing, too. Can't argue with the benefit this little artifact brings.


Yet there is a large problem with the Memorial that Akroma herself does not have... And that problem is [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant).


(Oh, all right. You newer kids need another word, so I'll say that, too: [Naturalize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize).)


*The Problem With The Submissions: Insufficient Defense*


I love the Memorial, but it leaves your creatures hanging out to dry at a moment's notice. Let's say we have this scenario, which I have seen come to pass:


"I will attack with all of my hasted, flying, first-striking, trampling, protection from red and black critters over your head for the win! *Die, mortal*!"


"I will spend two mana and remove all of those abilities. Your creatures are now landbound and much more defenseless. Now I will make some devastating blocks to make you regret that card."


Akroma was nice, but if she got shot down you had to deal with her. [Akroma's Memorial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Memorial), on the other hand, can get removed at an inconvenient time, making you do two sets of math for every attack phase: If the Memorial stays in play, and if it doesn't.


(Plus, at seven mana, it's hard to get out. This has been brought to you by the Department of the Bleedin' Obvious.)


The people who submitted the Memorial as their choice tended to talk about its power, but not have any backup plan in case Memorial hit the bin. No shield for the artifact itself, no idea of what to do with your guy, no way of ensuring that the Memorial didn't croak the turn you cast it. That's difficult.


When Memorial pays off, it'll pay off in spades. But it's also a large investment for a tough gamble: win or die. That, I think, makes it a hard case to argue as the most powerful card... because part of the definition of a powerful card is that it has to be able to protect itself in some form (or enable strategies that do so easily).

Many people enjoyed my preview card, mainly because of its effect on the board. As Scott H. said, "It is not powerful by itself, but creates a game state that encourages creature combat and discourages control and combo decks."


Many others thought that there were good ways to get around its drawback—mostly by playing all-creature decks. And since there are so many good comes-into-play effects, why not?

*The Problem With The Submissions: But What About The Drawback?*


Here's what Alexander D.R. had to say, and he is correct:



> 
> *One final note: if everyone decides to gang up on you, [Heartwood Storyteller](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartwood+Storyteller) is terrible. If the game turns into you against a three-man alliance, every removal spell that alliance casts against you lets you draw a card, but it lets them draw two more cards! This is essentially an unwinnable situation for you, and it is the Achilles' heel of the Storyteller. Fortunately, it's unlikely that a 2/3 creature with no damage-dealing abilities will provoke a three-person team up.... Unless that creature wears the crown of BEST MULTIPLAYER CARD IN* FUTURE SIGHT*!*
> 
> 
> *(The crown is made of the bones of a [Nacatl War-Pride](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nacatl+War-Pride), which coincidentally becomes much better with [Heartwood Storyteller](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartwood+Storyteller), because opponents play fewer removal spells and more creatures.)*
> 
> 
> 


Okay, I love his description of the crown, but the man is correct: You're not just playing [Heartwood Storyteller](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartwood+Storyteller), you are playing [Jedit Ojanen of Efrava](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jedit+Ojanen+of+Efrava) and [Nacatl War-Pride](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nacatl+War-Pride) and [Spectral Force](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Force) and what-have-you. Assuming you get into *any* win position at all, that Storyteller will turn against you in an instant... And nobody submitted a deck that could overcome that "I'm in the lead, and now Storyteller is hurting me!" problem.


Thus, I'm afraid, I cannot give it the crown. Made of bones of War Pride though it may be.
