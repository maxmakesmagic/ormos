Ten Things I Learned from Worlds 2010
=====================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on December 16, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







This past weekend in [Chiba, Japan](/en/events/coverage/theres-no-jace-guillaume) gave us, perhaps, the most exciting cap to a year of professional **Magic**, ever. The crowning of Guillaume Matignon via a star-studded Top 8 was awesome ... But the weekend (and the process of making Top 8) was much more than just picking a blue-black deck. Over the course of poring over each and every letter and syllable of story and Hagon-podcast has taught me a ton. Here are the top 10 things that I took away:


### 10. The Right Number of Creatures to Play in Standard is *Six*: Three Sea Gate Oracles and Three Grave Titans


I mean look at the [Top 8 decks](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/2010-magic-gathering-world-championships-top-8-standard-deck-lists). Of the Top 8, five of them were Blue-Black Control decks using exclusively [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) and [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle) for main-deck creatures; of those five, three non-Guillaume players had three copies of each. *You can't argue with science.*


Well ... I guess a different man might make the argument that the Guillaumes meeting in the finals with their three [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) / two [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle) configurations might make for a different conclusion. But the point is the point: [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle)s show you the path to take with your [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan)s, if what you want is to win.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td121_gravetitan.jpg)The other three decks in the Worlds 2010 Top 8 were White-Blue Control, Vampires, and a throwback Mono-Green Eldrazi Ramp. The undefeated Day One decks were two copies of Blue-Black Control (both of which made Top 8), Erich Froehlich's Vampires (which made Top 8), one-time Rookie of the Year Pierre Canali with Elves, and ascending Hall of Famer Brian Kibler with Caw-Go (White-Blue Control with [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk)).


Put another way, there were no copies of [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) at either the 6-0 finish line or in the Top 8.


### 9. Grave Titan is a Top 10 Card


Prior to Worlds 2010 Patrick Chapin and I were discussing re-vamps to the Top 10 cards in Standard; Patrick presented a list with [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) extremely high ... Just after [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) and [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) if I recall correctly.



![Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)
![Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Primeval+Titan)

I questioned the presence of [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan)*at all*. I mean [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) is a fine card with roots in last summer's Jund and sporadic play in Extended Four- and Five-Color Grixis decks, but a Top 10 card in Standard? What do you think this is, *Frost* Titan?


I am not 100% sure how the entire Black-Blue universe all decided to forego [Frost Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) in favor of [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) this weekend past, but I know where it originally came from; when he was first brewing Blue-Black Control, Nick Spagnolo told us that as good as [Frost Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) was, it was basically irrelevant against an opponent with a resolved [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) and a little mana; [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) at least left behind some little buddies to go try to beat on Jace.



![Frost Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Frost+Titan)

Look at the shape of this metagame; the Top 8 was five-eighths Blue-Black Control; the undefeated decks on Day One were two-fifths Blue-Black Control. Blue-Black Control has been thrust into a very different metagame position, in terms of popularity and success, than it once commanded; as such, it is not just a matter of figuring out how to *play* Blue-Black Control, but increasingly, to beat it.


How do you do so without severely disrupting your card pool?


What is Blue-Black Control about, anyway?


Winning the mirror will often be about winning a Jace war. As we said, [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) is better at going after Jace than [Frost Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan). Then there is the matter of point removal. Every single Blue-Black deck in the Top 8 played at least two copies of [Doom Blade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade), and in a couple of cases, three. When you are facing off against another Blue-Black deck, would you rather present [Frost Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) (expensive to hit but still more appropriate than a [Creeping Tar Pit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Tar+Pit)), or a [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan)?


Let's have a look at the 2010 World Championship deck, shall we?

There are all different ways to make a Blue-Black deck, but I think that when you fire up the queues in **Magic Online**, you will want to be aware of this build's capabilities ...



> 
> **Main deck sweep.** Many iterations of this deck have, in the past, lacked any kind of [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) effect ... Matignon's had [Consume the Meek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consume+the+Meek) main deck.
> 
> 
> **Light discard.** In addition to [Memoricide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memoricide) to fight [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) (and other Black-Blue decks) the Worlds winner has [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek) both main deck and sideboard (up to three copies).
> 
>

Speaking of 6-0 Extended performances, this is another deck you should have in your testing arsenal.


Kind of base-Grixis control, this deck gains a good deal from its white splash:



> 
> **[Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens).** Improved early game mana stability, as well as [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide) (et al.) defense.
> 
> 
> **[Esper Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Charm).** Probably the biggest overall incentive to the white splash, this card gives an otherwise Grixis deck resistance to [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) and [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) without having to delve into vulgar removal spells on the order of a true [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant)—main deck, that is. Obviously instant-speed card drawing is also useful for a control deck.
> 
> 
> **[Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment).** Obviously a unique and highly desirable effect for a control deck.
> 
> 
> **[Runed Halo](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Runed+Halo).** The spoiler for a deck boasting 20+ nonbasic lands (name "[Anathemancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anathemancer)"); also very useful against [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle).

So ... Who needs [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) (or in this case [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment))? Apparently not Guillaume! With [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command), he could "[Fog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fog)" the opponent's entire squad, lining them up for [Sunblast Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunblast+Angel)! What a beating for creatures! To my mind that is the most exciting thing setting this version of the popular control strategy apart from the rest of the room, but it's not like Matignon didn't have a legitimate sorcery sweeper *at all* ...



![Hallowed Burial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hallowed+Burial)

While it costs one more mana, [Hallowed Burial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Burial) has some very nice incentives over [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment). For one thing, [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) is essentially an inferior [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) (for the same cost) ... It's not like we don't see creatures that can walk away from a Day. Even if we don't see a lot of regenerating creatures in modern Extended, there are still lots of creatures you don't want to fight with [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment):



> 
> **[Sprouting Thrinax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax) in Jund.** Leaves just as much power on the table *after* you kill it.
> 
> 
> **[Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) in various decks.** Persist makes conventional removal much less attractive.
> 
> 
> **[Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine).** Kind of like a bigger [Sprouting Thrinax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax), but delivered with the spirit of [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks).
> 
> 
> **[Anathemancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anathemancer).** As if dealing with it the first time were not painful enough.
> 
> 
> **[Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine) and [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge).** They'll be back.
> 
> 
> 


[Hallowed Burial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Burial) isn't perfect against [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), but it is a heck of a lot better against [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) if the opponent hasn't already got a jazzed graveyard; plus flat-out awesome against the entire Thrinax / Finks / Wurmcoil / graveyard shenanigans families.

With [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) and [Cultivate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cultivate) to help it escape [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), plus [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)s of its own, the [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) strategy has some decent defense. Factor in a wide variety of sideboard options—[Great Sable Stag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Great+Sable+Stag) for Faeries, [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) for beatdown—and it has some very attractive B-plans.


What's that? You don't necessarily know what Plan A is?


There is a reason there is an exclamation point after the one-word title to this subsection!



![Wargate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Wargate)

So what do you get with this piece? Ideally ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20160829100442im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/x.gif) is two, and the card is [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen); although theoretically [Wargate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wargate) can be just another ramp spell (X = 0 and you can go and get a land).


What does [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) do for you? Well every land is a [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain), so that makes for quite the combo with [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle). The coolest thing is that you can play a [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) getting a bunch of [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle)s, and they all count one another as [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s; so with just six lands in play you can kill the opponent with a [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift).


Another option is to just go [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) + [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle).. Every land you play from this point on is essentially a [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) (or better ... as with a [Misty Rainforest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Misty+Rainforest)). You can win an incremental value game quite consistently, especially with all the ramp in the deck.


The combination of mana consistency, raw power, and relatively quick combo clock, should make this deck popular.

### 2. There is More to Legacy Than Survival of the Fittest


No one is saying [Survival of the Fittest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest) isn't great, but I for one was happy to see that neither of the top Team Championships trios was fronting the [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman) predecessor. Ivan Floch of the Slovak Republic ran a White-Blue [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) / [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) control deck with the [Sword of the Meek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+the+Meek) + [Thopter Foundry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thopter+Foundry) also-combo.

I am not of the camp that Extended control decks have to play [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) 100% of the time, but Ali has no analogue at all. No [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder), no [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle). In fact, he has no [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)! Heresy of heresies!


Of course we see the [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s and [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)s, and thanks to the white splash four [Esper Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Charm)s. However the creature suite is also quite different, almost hearkening back to summer 2009 Standard with [Plumeveil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plumeveil) and [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel). But also Amsterdam with the [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan)s.


In terms of finishers, in addition to a pair of [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum)s, Ali has the biggest bad guy of all: [Nicol Bolas, Planeswalker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nicol+Bolas%2C+Planeswalker)!


I mean Extended really is magical Christmas land when you can get away with playing an eight-drop in the same format that other people can command a turn-three kill.


2010 was a great Worlds, and the cap to a fine year of **Magic: The Gathering**. Enjoy the holidays!
