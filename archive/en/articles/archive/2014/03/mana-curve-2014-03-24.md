Mana Curve
==========



 Posted in [NEWS](/en/articles?source=MX_Nav2020)
 on March 24, 2014 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Edgar has what he thinks is a pretty good opening hand. He gazes down and sees rare after shiny mythic rare card. Land, land, land... a [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) (big game), his [Ætherling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86therling) (his unstoppable finisher), and a pair of [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict)s (destroy all monsters). *I can't lose*, thinks Ed, to himself. *Certainly not to a creature deck.*


Gabby opens up with a Swamp and a tapped [Tormented Hero](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormented+Hero).



![Tormented Hero](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?size=small&type=card&name=Tormented%20Hero&options=)

*What a silly card*, thinks Ed, to himself. *It doesn't even enter the battlefield straight.*


Ed plays his first land and passes.


Gabby slams for 2, and plays a [Thrill-Kill Assassin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin), unleashed, off a freshly-dropped [Mutavault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault).


*That isn't even a real card* thinks Ed, drawing a [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin). *I'm really going to DESTROY Gabby with this!*


Gabby plays a third land and lays down a [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie). There goes that [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin) (theoretically great against black... but also a white creature)! Gabby slams for 4 damage as Ed draws a [Dissolve](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dissolve) and plays his third land.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Fiendslayer+Paladin)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lifebane+Zombie)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie)
Gabby activates [autocard]Mutavault[autocard] and swings for a handful. *Very nearly two full hands full.*


*Why couldn't Gabby have played a spell?* Ed asks himself, staring at that [Dissolve](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dissolve).


Like clockwork, Ed has his fourth land... unfortunately it is a [Temple of Enlightenment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temple+of+Enlightenment).


Even if his fourth land had properly entered the battlefield untapped (so that he could have played [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict)) the game probably wouldn't have gone very well for Edgar, thanks to Gabby's lead and the follow-up threatened by that mighty [Mutavault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault).



![Mutavault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?size=small&type=card&name=Mutavault&options=)

What could Edgar have blamed this on? His cards were sweet and sweeter; rares, creature crushers, and big battle cruisers. It is very difficult to say Gabby's cards were "better" than Ed's in this game.


Ed also wasn't mana screwed... he hit all his land drops! He didn't mulligan, didn't fall behind in [card advantage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=card+advantage), or stumble in any obvious way; in fact, if he had been able to pull off his turn-four [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) (presumably into a later [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation)), Edgar would have had a huge lead in cards! Yet Edgar not only was never in this game, but he managed to lose to a [Thrill-Kill Assassin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin) and an unenhanced [Tormented Hero](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormented+Hero) while drawing a [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin)!


Quite simply, Edgar's opponent Gabby made great use of the notion of *mana curve*... and Edgar didn't.


The mana curve is a concept that entered the **Magic** canon shortly after the first Pro Tour. Mana curve's original implementation was closely related to [redundancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=redundancy)—the idea of playing a land every turn and tapping all your mana every turn. In its original form, mana curve asked players to look at the cards in their decks not (just) as unique spells, but around their collective and similar casting costs.

In this 1996 deck—the first deck really designed with mana curve in mind—would have asked you to think of [Brass Man](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brass+Man), [Dwarven Trader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Trader), and [Goblins of the Flarg](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblins+of+the+Flarg) all as a single class of offensive creature cards that you could play on the first turn; [Ironclaw Orcs](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ironclaw+Orcs), [Orcish Librarian](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orcish+Librarian), and [Dwarven Lieutenant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Lieutenant) together as creatures you could drop for two mana; and [Brothers of Fire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brothers+of+Fire), [Orcish Artillery](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orcish+Artillery), and [Orcish Cannoneers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orcish+Cannoneers) together at three.


[Dwarven Trader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Trader)? [Goblins of the Flarg](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblins+of+the+Flarg) *in the same deck* as [Dwarven Trader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Trader)? What probably strikes you as most obvious looking at this deck is how far creatures have come over the last eighteen years! Paul Sligh really bent over backwards to run some one-drops!


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dwarven+Trader)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Trader)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Goblins+of+the+Flarg)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblins+of+the+Flarg)
What ultimately ended up being the useful takeaway around mana curve is this: Play with some lower casting costs, especially in order to take advantage of your early-game land drops, then tap that land to get utility out of it.


In our opening story, how did Gabby take advantage of mana curve while Ed didn't?


Gabby played land #1 and used it for the [Tormented Hero](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormented+Hero); in the broader redundant sense, tapping for [Rakdos Cackler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Cackler) would have sufficed.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Tormented+Hero)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormented+Hero)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Rakdos+Cackler)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Cackler)
Gabby played land #2 and followed up with the [Thrill-Kill Assassin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin). Again, the hungry mana curve would have been satisfied with [Pain Seer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pain+Seer) or [Pack Rat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat), too.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Pain+Seer)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pain+Seer)
Gabby played land #3 and tapped all three for [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie). [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie) was a table-snapping elbow drop against Edgar's freshly-drawing [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin), but the mana curve might have been happy to see Gabby drop a [Herald of Torment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Herald+of+Torment).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lifebane+Zombie)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Herald+of+Torment)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Herald+of+Torment)
Even though nothing got cast on turn four, two of Gabby's lands were committed to a huge 9-point attack, being [Mutavault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault) and a Swamp pouring a point into [Mutavault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault).


Put simply, Gabby played a land basically every turn, and used all that mana (getting a [two-for-one](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=two-for-one) on the way, natch).


What about Edgar's side of the table?


Ed played a land every turn... but did not tap any of those lands. Edgar might have been able to tap three for a [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin)... but for that [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie) two-for-one. But even if Edgar had had the opportunity to play what should have been a board-dominant [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin), he would not have been able to cast the [Dissolve](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dissolve) he drew... with three mana tapped for that conjectural [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin), he wouldn't have had three loose for the scrying counterspell.


The simplest way of looking at this is just that Gabby's cards were cheaper... so even with a limited amount of mana (say, one Swamp), they got cast. Edgar's cards were relatively clunky. They were all good, powerful, cards... and not a one got cast.


It would be needlessly superficial to say that Gabby's deck was active and Edgar's reactive and explain everything away that way; controlling decks can take advantage of this concept and tool as well. Such decks can take steps to lower their curves, for example by sideboarding in one-drops like [Soldier of the Pantheon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soldier+of+the+Pantheon); or tap all their mana for a turn-two [Syncopate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Syncopate) (in answer to the opponent tapping most or all of *his or her* mana).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Soldier+of+the+Pantheon)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soldier+of+the+Pantheon)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Syncopate)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Syncopate)
How different would our imaginary game have been if Edgar took the first hit from [Herald of Torment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Herald+of+Torment), but just [Syncopate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Syncopate)d the [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie)? Edgar might have taken maybe one more hit from [Thrill-Kill Assassin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin) before turtling up behind say one [Azorius Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azorius+Charm) or [Last Breath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Last+Breath). He might not even have had to cast [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict), just hide behind the [Fiendslayer Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiendslayer+Paladin) until he had the mana for a nice [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation).


Edgar had mana.


Edgar had cards.


But given our scenario... he just didn't tap his mana to play many cards (while his opponent curved out perfectly).

This famous blue deck, played by Hall of Famer Randy Buehler, very nicely illustrates how a reactive deck can still take advantage of mana curve principles. Randy played cards like [Force Spike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+Spike), so even if he was going to be the slower deck in almost any matchup, he would have defensive play—even on turn one. Usually, a one-mana 2/1 creature like [Jackal Pup](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup) would stick and be good for a beatdown deck in those days... but if he got to go first, Randy would be able to answer it with [Force Spike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+Spike). Of course, later in a game, [Force Spike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+Spike) would be relevant any time the opponent would tap out.


Further, Randy played a ton of two-mana permission spells, from the iconic [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) to the time-buying [Memory Lapse](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memory+Lapse). He very frequently had a reactive spell to play on turn two, so his deck, with really only the one [Rainbow Efreet](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rainbow+Efreet) (plus lands) to win, would not be so far behind from the first and second turns.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Force+Spike)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+Spike)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Counterspell)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)
What might be subtle is that even though Randy's main tops up on only four mana, cards like [Whispers of the Muse](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whispers+of+the+Muse) allowed him to tap six or more lands in a turn, consuming big chunks of mana later in games, rather than letting it sit around untapped while he waited for something to happen.

George Baxter's Good Stuff deck was the most aggressive deck from the first Pro Tour.


If we graph the casting costs in Baxter's deck we come up with something like this:


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/lo/2014/lo_wk12_292_chart1.jpg)
Baxter's deck cashes in a zero with Zuran Orb but its ten one-mana spells are actually fairly deceptive. [Barbed Sextant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barbed+Sextant) doesn't really do anything, [Dark Ritual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) doesn't do anything by itself, and [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) is entirely reactive. Unless Baxter had the (admittedly dreaded) [Dark Ritual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) + [Hypnotic Specter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypnotic+Specter) opening... he probably wasn't going to do much on the first turn.


Now, between [Hymn to Tourach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach) and his eight copies of [Knight of Stromgald](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Stromgald) and [Order of the Ebon Hand](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Order+of+the+Ebon+Hand), Baxter had a ton of things he could do—offensively and aggressively—on turn two; but turn one would pass without a play more often than not.

Contrast Baxter's curve—the curve of the most aggressive deck of the first Pro Tour—against Rietzl's 2010 model (for purposes of this illustration, in this deck [Spectral Procession](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) is always three mana):


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/lo/2014/lo_wk12_292_chart2.jpg)
Paul played *nineteen* one-mana spells; twelve of which were [Figure of Destiny](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny), [Student of Warfare](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Student+of+Warfare), and [Steppe Lynx](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx)—all very dangerous plays at that point.


In stark contrast to most other decks, Paul would (or at least could) make an aggressive play on the first turn in most games. Just as with our opening narrative of Gabby and the [Tormented Hero](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormented+Hero), Paul could "get the jump" on his opponents starting on turn one, which could be a substantial difference-maker.


The mana curve of a successful deck doesn't necessarily have to look as tall-on-the-left, nor as steep, as Paul's.


What is important to take away is that decks with generally inexpensive cards can make plays earlier in games and take advantage of their resources and options in ways that decks with lots of four-mana spells never will. Building a deck with attention to the mana curve is just one way of thinking, but it is a technology and way of doing things that can potentially increase your flexibility by giving you access to more resources: both mana, that might have otherwise gone unspent, *and*the utility of playing cards that might otherwise have been stuck in your hand.


Mana curve is generally a deck-building principle, rather than a game-play one... but the general notion of tapping all your lands each turn tends, all other things held equal, to be good **Magic**.


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/lo/2014/lo_wk12_292_vs.jpg)
All other things held equal, if you have the option of *unleashing* a 2/X [Rakdos Cackler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Cackler) versus a 2/X [Thrill-Kill Assassin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin) on an empty board, you probably want to tap for the more expensive creature. Other things might influence your decision (like having a [Thrill-Kill Assassin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrill-Kill+Assassin) already down against a WU player), but paying more on this turn for similar output can be helpful in many ways. *(For example, the next turn you could make your land drop, play the [Rakdos Cackler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Cackler), and still attack with the [Mutavault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault).)*


Like anything else in Level One, the idea of making your land drops and tapping as much mana as you can is meant to be a useful guideline, not the be-all/end-all. Deep in the midgame, if you draw a land and it is your only card, you might want to hold it back to bluff, for instance.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=%C3%86therling)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86therling)
Or if you have the choice between tapping all six for your [Ætherling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86therling) versus tapping four for [Jace, Architect of Thought](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought)—but leaving two open for [Negate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) in a similar deck matchup—(or even *bluffing* the [Negate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate)) I would guess your long-run results would be better waiting on the [Ætherling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86therling) in an overwhelming number of games.


But in the absence of specific information? Making your land drops, then tapping those lands—tapping out every turn if you can—while applying pressure to the opponent, is a *much* better model than not having a specific model at all.
