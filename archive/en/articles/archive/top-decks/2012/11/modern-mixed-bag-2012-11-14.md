Modern Mixed Bag
================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on November 15, 2012 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











This past weekend saw quite the diverse Top 8 for [Grand Prix Chicago](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/gpchi12/welcome) and its featured Modern format. In fact, there was only one duplicate archetype in all the Top 8! 

Like so:

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td221_chicago_chart_2.jpg)  
Jund
 Jund, the most popular deck of the recent  [Pro Tour *Return to Ravnica*](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptrtr12/welcome), was the only deck to show up twice in this Top 8. Two things to note about this particular Jund-phenomenon: (1) this is not your father's Jund deck, and (2) it's probably pretty good given that it faced off against itself, first-against-second.

 The only difference between champion and finalist was the [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains) in Josh Utter-Leyton's deck (over Wilson's second [Swamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swamp)). 

 By now, after its longtime boogeyman status in Standard two years ago and recent performances in Modern tournaments, you probably know how Jund works. This is a deck that combines fast drops like [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) (turn one), [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), and [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) (two of **Magic**'s most well-considered two-drops), with a toolbox full of utility answers. Jund has great one-mana interaction in [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) and [Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek) and can remove all kinds of permanents with [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate), [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay), [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge), and [Maelstrom Pulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Pulse). All of it is held together by the hasty [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), which is both an initiative-grabbing attacker and a pretty good utility spell setup man.

*That's nice... So, what's different?*

 Utter-Leyton and Wilson played a little something called [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls). Previously, we have seen [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) spawn two different White-Blue [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) variants in Standard, solidify the grinding White-Blue [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) archetype in Legacy, and piggyback the graveyard-filling momentum of [Mulch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulch) for games when [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) was not immediately available. Now, this white card with the black flashback is breaking new ground in making a pretty successful black-red-green deck just a bit better. 

  
 Affinity is less of a problem for the Jund deck that can flip a [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls). The card makes two, and then four, 1/1 flying tokens... half—even a quarter—of a [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) outclasses a [Memnite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite) or trades cleanly with a [Vault Skirge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vault+Skirge). And hey! You can *all of a sudden* block a [Signal Pest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest)!

 Other Jund decks being quite popular (and quite good at trading cards and coming off having pocketed a little value), [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) can give you the edge in the mirror. [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) puts you in a position where your [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) has quite the advantage against your opponent's [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). In a fair matchup, it gives you fliers to race or a string of chumpers to block. 

All in all, a pretty groundbreaking innovation for what could otherwise have been a stale—if colossally popular—archetype.

 "Affinity" here has really only one card with affinity for artifacts ([Thoughtcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtcast)), but that was a strong signal for Majlaton to go with a solo basic [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) in his land count. In addition to [Thoughtcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtcast), Majlaton could summon [Master of Etherium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium) with his blue. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

  
 The strength of Affinity, of course, is in its non-reliance on colored mana. [Memnite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite) and [Ornithopter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter) may not look like much... but they also don't cost very much. The deck has tons of high impact one- and two-mana spells, including [Signal Pest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) and [Steel Overseer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+Overseer) (which can buff your numerous cheapies); the big bomb twos are [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) and [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating). [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) is effective with so many artifacts, but especially so with lands like [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) and [Blinkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinkmoth+Nexus).

 The Modern Pod deck is a deck with many options... which should be obvious. In addition to namesake [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod), the deck has [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) to go find whatever creature on demand. Ergo, you can quickly set up an [Ethersworn Canonist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethersworn+Canonist) or a [Glen Elendra Archmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glen+Elendra+Archmage) against combo; [Avalanche Riders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avalanche+Riders) to break up the Urzatron; or [Linvala, Keeper of Silence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Linvala%2C+Keeper+of+Silence) to stop another deck with Birds and Elves, perhaps disrupting the mirror. Hovis had [Izzet Staticaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Izzet+Staticaster) to mop up little 0/1 and 1/1 creatures. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

  
 But what gets you to the final tables is [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker)! 

 This deck can combine creatures at three, four, or five with [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki) to deal infinite damage (convenient for [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod), allowing for catalysts at two, three, or four). 

* [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)—Untap [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker), allowing it to make a copy of [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) (which will untap [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki)), rinse and repeat.
* [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel)—"Blink" [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki)... which returns to the battlefield untapped so you can copy [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel) (which will in turn "blink" [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki), essentially untapping it... rinse and repeat).
* [Zealous Conscripts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Conscripts)—As with [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)... just because you already control [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki) doesn't mean you can't run this routine over and over again with successive [Zealous Conscripts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Conscripts) tokens.
 Both [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) and [Zealous Conscripts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Conscripts) give you the ability to use [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) more than one time in a turn... which might be important to jump mana costs or get you to the combo you need.

 Exarch Twin can put together similar [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki) combinations as [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod), with either [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) or [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) as the Mirror Breaker's pick-and-roll partner. However, in addition, it can go ho-hum three-into-four-into-infinite by enchanting either [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) or [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) with [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), again to set up an infinite damage attack.

 Rather than playing the midrange toolbox Plan B of [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), Simon's deck has the shell of a blue-red control deck. He has some removal and he has some permission. I like how he can pick off, say, a combo containing [Soul Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden) with [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer). [Cyclonic Rift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cyclonic+Rift) is in a sense the ultimate trump. It can short-term answer almost any permanent (and a deck that can set up an infinite damage attack doesn't need a huge window)... and given sufficient mana it can do anything and everything from "making the opponent discard cards in excess of seven at the end of turn" to "making an opposing infinite attack deck pick up, say, 999 attacking [Zealous Conscripts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Conscripts)." 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

  
 In addition to [Cyclonic Rift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cyclonic+Rift), Simon incorporated [Mizzium Skin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mizzium+Skin) as a new helper from *Return to Ravnica*. [Mizzium Skin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mizzium+Skin) gives a creature hexproof, so it can be like a one-mana counterspell that can protect a key combo component from removal... even when that removal is, say, [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay)! 

 Simon's sideboard has quite a few of his deck's most impressive gems. A [Stomping Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stomping+Ground)s powers up [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge), a [Watery Grave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Watery+Grave) is his point discard, but the most powerful of them is [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon). Look around the mana bases of some of these decks. They will not do well with all basic [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s! Simon's [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) can slow down the majority of decks in Modern and completely work over a deck like [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) or anything Urzatron. Meanwhile, Simon's own deck requires but a single blue mana to go infinite.

 This deck can do a little of everything and can do a fair number of things pretty well. Most of its cards are solid on their own... but in concert with one another they can set up synergies ranging from empty-handed lockdown to fast, [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker)-like (and oppressive) beatdown. 

[Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) is the card that laces together many of the powerful combinations in McDermott's deck. You can play Gifts as a pure-value two-for-one (and then use [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) to recoup something if you wanted it more, perhaps even putting [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) in the [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven))... Or you can put together specific combinations of cards to gain disproportionate advantages. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

  
* [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) + [Iona, Shield of Emeria](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Iona%2C+Shield+of+Emeria) (or [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) + [Elesh Norn, Grand Cenobite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elesh+Norn%2C+Grand+Cenobite)). In this [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) package you actually "fail to find." That is, you literally only find the [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) and the creature you want... your opponent has to put both into your graveyard. You play [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) from your graveyard to reanimate the creature that is now in your graveyard so you can lock down either a color or kill a ton of guys/keep them off your back. Note the average Infect deck can't possibly win if [Elesh Norn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elesh+Norn) is in play, and it doesn't matter how many creatures [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) can make if they have 1 or 2 power... because [Elesh Norn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elesh+Norn) will cancel out all that power. [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki) himself is no friend of [Elesh Norn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elesh+Norn) on the other side of the table.
* The above, but with four cards. Unlike when White-Blue Tron tried the [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) + X package last year, this version of [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) can actually tap for black mana easily... It might not be a thing to step the four-mana [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) into the five-mana [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) (depending on which cards go where). Essentially, this is like the previous option that might net you two more cards.
* [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime) + [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) (and probably + [Urborg, Tomb of Yawgmoth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urborg%2C+Tomb+of+Yawgmoth)). [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) by itself is a huge card-drawing engine. It has synergy with cards like [Verdant Catacombs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Verdant+Catacombs); this deck wins with [Creeping Tar Pit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Tar+Pit) anyway, and it can recoup those if they get killed. [Tectonic Edge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge) and [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) (in particular against opponents with few basics, like Affinity) have great long-term partnerships with [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam). Turn after turn, if you are casting [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) while not under pressure, you can not only recoup the lands that went to the graveyard "naturally" but you can dredge a land or two into your graveyard each turn as well. It is essentially "[Landcestral Recall](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Landcestral+Recall)." When combined with [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime) and plentiful black mana, you can just knock out the opponent's hand. You simply play [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime), get it into the graveyard, and then play successive [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime)s using the retrace alternate... with [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) giving you three+ Retraces per turn. Most opponents can't afford to be [Fugue](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fugue)d every turn.
[Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven)—this intricate version with mostly one-ofs in particular—appeals to a certain kind of mage. Do you like to make lots of decisions in a game, some of them very difficult? Do you like having lots of different angles of attack and defense? Do you like to suffocate the opponent's strategy, maybe poking him or her 2 points at a time with [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman), more than dropping an unfair hammer yourself? 

Gifts might be the most fun (and still effective) deck for you!

 This is a deck with only twenty lands that can pop out one of the most expensive Planeswalkers in the game, or casually tutor up [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn). [Ancient Stirrings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Stirrings), [Expedition Map](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Expedition+Map), [Sylvan Scrying](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Scrying), and even "cantrip" effects a la [Chromatic Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Star) are what gets this deck the lands it needs to dominate. Again, once it does, all the Tron deck's plays seem degenerate (you know, like lacing together the Urzatron and playing [Karn Liberated](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn+Liberated) on the third turn). 

 Once [Karn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn) is on the battlefield, it is a dominant force. You can crush your opponent's hand over several turns, then reset the game (but with a massive preexisting advantage) or you can cripple the opponent's development, [Sinkhole](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sinkhole)-style. Imagine you went first and played an [Urza's Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Tower) and a [Chromatic Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Star). On your second turn, you drew, made ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif), drew again, played an [Urza's Power Plant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Power+Plant), and finished the triumvirate with a [Sylvan Scrying](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Scrying). On your third turn you played the [Urza's Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Mine) that completed the set and cast [Karn Liberated](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn+Liberated) (of course), at which point *you removed your opponent's second land from the battlefield*.

To be clear:

* You: Insane mana engine + [Karn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn) online.
* Opponent: One land
 The alternative plan is to find [Eye of Ugin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eye+of+Ugin), power that up, and then hard-cast [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn). You take the extra turn, attack the opponent with [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul); it's about as easy to interact with for some decks as it sounds. 

And yes, only twenty lands in this one... but boy does each one pack a value!

 Edgar lifted this deck from Emanuel Sutor and his recent performance at [Grand Prix Lyon](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/gplyo12/welcome#1). Essentially, this is a midrange white-blue creature deck with a little enters-the-battlefield, a couple of control elements, and ultimately topping up on [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel). 

[Blade Splicer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blade+Splicer), [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens), and to a lesser extent [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique) and [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) ("lesser" in this deck, at least) combine well with the blink enters-the-battlefield on [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel). [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft), given sufficient time, is elite on offense; [Blade Splicer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blade+Splicer) is elite on defense. The spell slots kind of tie everything together...

[Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) is a bit of card advantage and an important hard counter in a world of fast kills, multiple routes to infinity, and the grinding advantage of [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) or [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls). [Dismember](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember), [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), and [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) are the catch-alls. None of them are overwhelmingly powerful but they are good at breaking up hyper-focused plans, have generally good coverage across the various realistic threats in a format, and—above all—are fast (you'll note that the design choices like one [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) in Affinity in part anticipate the presence of a [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) in the format). [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare) is fast and essentially always trades up against [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)s, [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)s, or [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager)s; it might not be a perfect long-term solution to [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam), but [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare) can certainly buy a body a turn. 

 It is interesting that the more restrictive [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) has taken the place of traditional ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161225105850im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless2.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif) sweepers in a deck that can muster the blue mana... If you aren't worried about having a single ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif), it is certainly an upgrade to [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment).

[Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel),  [once called the best large creature of all time](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptaus09/decktech8)  by a Hall of Famer upon taking down his first Pro Tour win with it at the top of his curve, does similar work in Edgar's deck. [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) is the catch-up card, clean-up crew, and best thing possible to tap out for. 

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td221_baneslayer_angel.jpg)[Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) | Art by [Greg Staples](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;artist=%5B%22Greg%20Staples%22%5D)

 Make no mistake: WU Control is not as powerful as some of the other strategies. It can't go infinite like the [Kiki-Jiki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki) decks, and although it can grind, it lacks much of the individual card efficiency of a Jund (especially in topdeck situations). But if you want to play an interactive game with a traditional game plan, a deck like this one proves that the strategy can still perform in the face of Modern's mighty mixed bag.
