Confessions of a Gunslinger
===========================



 Posted in **The Week That Was**
 on February 6, 2009 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20170709072651im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Brian David-Marshall











I was the guest gunslinger this past weekend at the Los Angeles Prerelease hosted by Glenn Goddard and Sun Mesa Events. Also known as the Champions Challenge, gunslinging is essentially the player equivalent of an artist signing. People sign up to play against you, and if you lose they win a booster pack. I was quite honored to be invited and took the task of protecting the precious packs—and whatever tattered reputation I have as a player with some modicum of Limited skills—very seriously. I was fortunate to receive a deep pool of cards to play with in the first flight of the event and at the Champions Challenge station. 

I still have no idea if I built my deck correctly despite leaving more packs in the box of than out of it at the end of Saturday (and most of the packs that left the box did so with my second pool of cards—more on that deck later). Here is the deck I played, as well as all the cards that were left in my sideboard, built from a pool of three *Shards of Alara* boosters and three *Conflux* boosters.

I quickly identified that I had opened a handful of synergistic rares in [Wall of Reverence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence), [Meglonoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meglonoth), and [Spearbreaker Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spearbreaker+Behemoth). Potentially [Where Ancients Tread](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Where+Ancients+Tread) could have been lumped in there as well but I did not have enough "cheap" 5-power creatures to make that worthwhile. One of the cards that should have been included when doing the Naya math was [Nacatl Hunt-Pride](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nacatl+Hunt-Pride). I left it out of the deck because I felt like I already had enough on the high end of my curve, but my deck could have easily accommodated it and probably needed one more fatty—especially one with a rock-solid set of abilities. 

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130615004014im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/twtw/twtw24_cards.jpg)  
With the [Knotvine Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knotvine+Mystic) in the mix there was little doubt that I would play a base-Naya deck. There was a lot of discussion among the players at my table about whether or not [Knotvine Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knotvine+Mystic) was any good in this format. The camp that held a negative opinion about the card felt that it was fine if you could play it on turn three, but it required three different specifically colored mana to play. Getting to three different mana on turn three is not very hard in the post-*Conflux* Limited format. I think that cards such as [Knotvine Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knotvine+Mystic)—and predecessors from *Shards* like [Rhox War Monk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rhox+War+Monk), [Sprouting Thrinax](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax), and [Woolly Thoctar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woolly+Thoctar)—will all go up in draft value due to the multicolor lands and basic land cycling. 

Speaking of those lands ... I went back and forth on the inclusion of [Rupture Spire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rupture+Spire) in my deck, but in the end was very happy that I decided to put it in. The cost of "summoning" the Spire on turn two or three is not very different than spending your third turn to play an Obelisk in order to fix your mana for a [Woolly Thoctar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woolly+Thoctar), or having to wait a turn to crack your Panorama for the right assortment of [Tower Gargoyle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tower+Gargoyle) mana. I heard stories about players with multiple Spires in their card pool drawing unkeepable two-land hands but overall I was very happy with the card, which was far more helpful than harmful in my experience with it on Saturday.

**A Dark Day**I have had to share bad news in this column before (often related to changes in the game), but the news I have to deliver today really puts into perspective how insignificant those things are in the grand scheme of things. 

After playing in a Pro Tour qualifier for Honolulu two weeks ago, Robert Cash and Kenneth Ellis were involved in a car accident driving home from San Diego to Modesto. Robert was killed in the crash and Kenneth was seriously injured. My deepest condolences go out to Robert's family and friends and my wishes for a speedy and complete recovery go out to Kenneth and his family. 

If Kenneth's name sounds familiar to regular coverage readers, he was featured in a [video deck tech](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V4f1uo5SywY) from a PTQ at [Pro Tour–Hollywood](/magic/magazine/events.aspx?x=mtgevent/pthol08/welcome), showing off his "Ten Commandments" deck that caught the attention of players and everyone on the coverage staff.

Wizards of the Coast has set up a [a special forum](http://forums.gleemax.com/showthread.php?t=1145451) for people wishing to share their condolences and memories.

Even now I am not sure if I would play the Hunt-Pride or the red-black enchantment. [Suicidal Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Suicidal+Charge) has turned out to be a splash-worthy card with a powerful game-changing ability that works on a number of levels. It sweeps away pesky 1-toughness utility guys, can be used on either offense or defense to whittle down the size of an opponent's army, and, most importantly, can be used to make opposing creatures crash into [Meglonoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meglonoth) for fun and profit, and leave your opponent open to a lethal counterstrike. Hunt-Pride also allows you to control the traffic flow of the red zone while not interacting poorly with [Ancient Ziggurat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Ziggurat)—at least in terms of being able to play it in the first place—and has synergies with [Wall of Reverence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence) and [Spearbreaker Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spearbreaker+Behemoth). 

I cannot fully express how game-altering the Wall was on the day—even when it was the only creature on the board. It is made even better by the fact that it triggers at the end of your turn and not during your upkeep. You almost always reap some benefit from it even if your opponent untaps and kills it. I did notice that a couple of players had a chance to do something to a creature—be it bounce it or removal—in response to [Wall of Reverence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence) targeting that creature. A well-timed [Unsummon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unsummon) or black-permanent-accompanied [Dark Temper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Temper) can keep the Wall from netting any life life that turn. 

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130614215336im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/twtw/twtw24_opponents.jpg)  
I ended going 3-1 in the first flight losing to a jubilant Brett McGarry and his terrific Bant deck in the fourth round. My deck turned out to have some very exciting trumps against many of the bombs in my tournament, although I had no real answer for Brett's Elspeth. My first two opponents both had [Banefire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire)—and I lost at least one game to the brand new ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20160829100442im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/x.gif) spell—but [Wall of Reverence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence) kept me comfortably out of firing range in the bulk of those games. There was a [Martial Coup](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup) hovering near the top tables, and I had my [Spearbreaker Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spearbreaker+Behemoth) earmarked as the trump against that card. [Meglonoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meglonoth) proved to be a game-breaking trump against [Titanic Ultimatum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Titanic+Ultimatum) Joe Garcia in Round 2, with Joe hovering at 5 life and [Meglonoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meglonoth) prowling the red zone. Because "blample" triggers when [Meglonoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meglonoth) blocks, it deals damage to your opponent before anything else. Joe would have not had a chance for any of the good things that [Titanic Ultimatum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Titanic+Ultimatum) does for you to matter.

Tournament Packs have gone the way of the old card frame and tapped blockers not dealing damage, and coming into the tournament I was curious about the impact of that on Sealed Deck. In the past, the *Conflux* Prerelease would have featured card pools built from one Tournament Pack of *Shards* and three *Conflux* boosters. This would mean that the pool of commons, uncommons, and rares from the Tournament Pack—barring unusual errors and the occasional redundant foil—would only yield one copy of any card from *Shards*. At the *Legions* Prerelease, you might have ended up with three Timberwatch Elves, but you would have been lucky to have even one [Sparksmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sparksmith). Under the new system it would have been possible for players to get up to three [Sparksmith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sparksmith)s. 

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/twtw/twtw24_sparks.jpg)

In the first pool I had pairs of [Cathartic Adept](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cathartic+Adept), [Marble Chalice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Marble+Chalice), and the one-of-these-things-is-not-like-the-others [Rhox Charger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rhox+Charger). Friday Night Foils: An Affinity for Foil
[Myr Enforcer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Myr+Enforcer) has been a staple of Affinity lists since *Mirrodin* first spawned the archetype several years back. The deck continues to be dominant in today's Extended format, and this month you can begin the process of tricking out your Affinity deck in fine foil fashion with this new take on the colorless fatty by going to [Friday Night **Magic**](/Magic/TCG/events.aspx?x=events/magic/fnm).
