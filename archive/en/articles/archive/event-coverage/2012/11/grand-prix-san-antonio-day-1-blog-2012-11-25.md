Saturday, 12:37 p.m. - The Standards of Standard
------------------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*
Last week, there were a couple of big new additions to our Standard Compendium, displaying some incredible innovation.




![Unburial Rites](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Unburial+Rites)![Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Craterhoof+Behemoth)


Hoof, There It Is: According to Jacob Van Lunen, Hoof is the "newest evolution" in the Standard metagame. At its core, Hoof is a Reanimator deck that uses [Unburial Rites](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) to bring a [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) back into play. Combined with an army of mana critters, the Behemoth is virtually always lethal the turn he comes into play. In addition to reanimating the Behemoth, the presence of [Somberwald Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Somberwald+Sage) allows the Behemoth to be cast in the traditional manner, deftly avoiding cards like [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) and [Rakdos Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Charm) .

Peddler: One of the most innovative decks to come out of Grand Prix Bochum was the Peddler brew run by Pro Tour Kobe 2006 winner Jan-Moritz Merkel and Max Pritsch. The creature-heavy deck revolves around the *Avacyn Restored* common [Nightshade Peddler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nightshade+Peddler) . When paired with [Olivia Voldaren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Olivia+Voldaren) or [Izzet Staticaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Izzet+Staticaster) , the latter can be used to completely decimate opposing creatures. Considering the prevalence of one-toughness creatures, including the powerful [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) , these cards are very impressive on their own, let alone being able to kill [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) , [Restoration Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel) , and [Sublime Archangel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sublime+Archangel) in one shot. The deck also runs numerous other creature-based interactions, such as [Zealous Conscripts](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Conscripts) / [Falkenrath Aristocrat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Falkenrath+Aristocrat) s and [Evil Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evil+Twin) as a method of dealing with problematic creatures. [Tracker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tracker) 's Instinct makes the deck incredibly more consistent, allowing it to find the numerous two-card combos, as well as filling the graveyard with lands for their [Deathrite Shaman](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) s.

Game Concepts


**Burn:** Since most of the spells that are capable of directly dealing damage to a player or creature use fire or lightning imagery, they are collectively known as burn spells, even if they don't actually use fire. Examples of burn common to Standard include [Searing Spear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Searing+Spear) , [Pillar of Flame](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillar+of+Flame) , and [Brimstone Volley](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley) .




![Barbed Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Barbed+Lightning)![Beacon of Destruction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Beacon+of+Destruction)

*For every target,there is a burn spell, and direct damage for every purpose under **Magic**.* 

***Card Advantage:** The concept of card advantage has received more discussion over the history of **Magic** than any other topic. In short, the concept of card advantage relates to the equivalences of exchanges in **Magic**. Basically, if one card allows you to draw two cards or destroy two of your opponent's permanents, you are gaining card advantage.*





*![Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Brainstorm)![Gleeful Sabotage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Gleeful+Sabotage)*

**Blue gives direct card advantage in the form of extra cards, but card advantage also comes from any use of one of your cards to negate *more* than one of your opponent's cards.** 

***ETB:** A shorthand acronym for "*enters the battlefield*". Creatures with ETB effects, such as [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) , have abilities that trigger upon entering the battlefield, giving a spell in the graveyard flashback in the case of [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) . Other textbook examples with cards in Standard with ETB effects are [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) and [Restoration Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel) .*





*![Inferno Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Inferno+Titan)![Angel of Despair ](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Angel+of+Despair+)*

**ETB effects were used with alacrity during the [Hypergenesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis) era, but that's another story...** 

***Fetch:** Fetch is simply a catchall term used to describe the action of retrieving a card from the library. [Farseek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek) is an example of a card that allows players to "fetch" a land from their deck.*





*![Demonic Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Demonic+Tutor)![Mystical Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mystical+Tutor)*

**Two classic fetch cards** 

***Metagame:** The term metagame refers to the state of the current Constructed environment, most frequently speaking of the types of decks that are prominent and popular, as well as individual card choices within those decks. For example, if I told you that the three most popular decks in Standard right now were UW Flash, BR Zombies, and GW, you would have a pretty good idea of the Standard metagame. Since each tournament gives players a chance to react to what they experienced in the previous one, the metagame is constantly changing. Staying on top of and correctly predicting the metagame is one of the most challenging aspects of the professional level of **Magic**.*



***Mill:** A verb derived from the card [Millstone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) , an ancient card from the *Antiquities* set which has been periodically recuited into the core sets ever since. The act of "milling" a player is to put cards from a player's library into their graveyard. Since players lose the game when they can't draw a card, milling an opponent's entire library is one of the most frequently used alternate win conditions. In Standard, cards such as [Nephalia Drownyard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard) and [Jace, Memory Adept](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Memory+Adept) , are the most common instances of mill cards.*





*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/millstone10th.JPG)*
*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/millstoneAntiquities.JPG)*

**You'd go crazy, too, if you kept losing because you couldn't draw.** 
***"#"-Drop:** This terminology is used to describe a permanent of a given converted mana cost. For example, [Knight of Infamy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) , which costs ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) , is a two-drop. [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) is an eight-drop. This terminology applies most often to permanents, such as creatures and artifacts, but it can be used to also describe the cost of spells.*



***Red Zone:** The red zone is an allusion to the older play mats used for Feature Matches, which had a large red area between the players. Players would use this area to indicate the spells they were casting and the creatures that were attacking. The newest client for **Magic Online** includes a literal red zone feature where attacking creature cards are placed. Nowadays, the phrase "sends them into the red zone" is synonymous for attacking, whether or not there is one present.*



***Swing/Smash/Battle/Bash:** All of these words have at some point in **Magic** history been the preferred method of saying "to attack". Now, they are all interchangeable and frequently used as slang.*



***The Stack:** The stack is the order of spells that have been played during a given priority step. For example, when you play a spell in your main phase, it is said to go on the stack. After that, any spells that are played in response to the first one are said to go on the stack above them. Spells on the stack resolve from the top to the bottom.*



***Silver Bullet:** A reference to the very specific weakness of werewolves, the phrase "silver bullet" in **Magic** refers to a card that exists in a deck, usually only one or two copies, that serves the purpose of providing an advantage against a very specific deck or effect. A good example of a silver bullet is the card [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) , which is good against graveyard decks, and [Thundermaw Hellkite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite) , which is especially effective against [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) .*

***Mirror Match**: A match between two decks of the same archetype. For example, two GW decks playing against each other is called the GW mirror match.*

**by Nate Price**

*With the advent of **Magic Online**, players have near-immediate access to the latest information about the state of a given format. By looking through the last couple days worth of Standard events, players can track the rise and fall of cards and archetypes, making sure that when the time comes for them to select a deck for a Grand Prix, they have the one best suited to deal with the field.*



*Standard has changed quite a bit since the beginning of the season. From its aggressive origins to the glacial metagame present in Bochum and Charleston last weekend, players monitored the ever-slowing format and tried to come up with new and inventive ways to go over the top of their opponents, playing the newest unbeatable finisher. As of the end of last weekend's Grand Prix, that finisher appears to be [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) thanks to its ability to end the game the turn it is cast.*



*But the shifting seas of Standard never stay idle for long. A new tide has been swirling as players sail into Grand Prix San Antonio. As the decks have reached the apex of "slow," space at the bottom has freed up, allowing the "fast" decks to once again achieve dominance. Thanks to [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) , these aggressive decks have a potent weapon against [Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) and [Syncopate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Syncopate) , the control decks' greatest weapons against the early onslaught offered by decks like GW Humans and BR Zombies. As such, players wishing to play control decks have had to adapt, adding more creatures to take the place of their counterspells.*



*Carried along with the ever-shifting tides of change are the fates of certain individual cards in the format, cards like [Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) , [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) , and [Thundermaw Hellkite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite) . The fluctuations in the metagame have a very strong impact on the strength of cards like these, and they rapidly oscillate between overpowered and unplayable given the current picture of Standard.*



*Here are some of the biggest movers and shakers in Standard right now, and the conditions that led to their rise or demise:*




*![Pillar of Flame](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Pillar+of+Flame)*


*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/upArrowGreen.png)[Pillar of Flame](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillar+of+Flame) - With the rise of [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) last week, aggressive decks are on the rise. As such, spot removal spells have become very important at combating the early rush of creatures. As David Ochoa put it, "With the number of aggressive decks increasing, cards like [Pillar of Flame](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillar+of+Flame) have become very important to stop them. It kills [Thalia, Guardian of Thraben](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thalia%2C+Guardian+of+Thraben) , and the early creatures from UW, as well as doing double duty to remove [Gravecrawler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gravecrawler) and [Geralf's Messenger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geralf%27s+Messenger) in Zombies."*




*![Riders of Gavony](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Riders+of+Gavony)*


*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/upArrowGreen.png)[Riders of Gavony](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Riders+of+Gavony) - Renowned deckbuilder Sam Black was the one to point this gem out. Looking at the decklists from Grand Prix Charleston and Bochum, it was clear that there were a large number of creature-based decks filling the top levels of the tournament. Since most of them have a strong tribal base (think GW Humans and BR Zombies), [Riders of Gavony](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Riders+of+Gavony) is the ideal way for the GW decks to ensure that opposing creatures don't get in the way of their winning the race.*




*![Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Essence+Scatter)*


*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/downArrowRed.png)[Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) - Nearly every player I talked to immediately keyed in on [Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) as being the single biggest loser in Standard following last week's GPs. With three or four [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) becoming standard in every deck that relies on creatures, it is easy to see why. Ochoa explained, "Having the ability to [Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) on turn two and then flash it back with a [Snap](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snap) caster on turn four was very good at slowing down early aggression. With [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) so prevalent, it is effectively a blank card right now." Many others echoed this sentiment, including Christian Calcano, who noted, "I was less surprised by the number of [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) being played in Charleston than I was with the fact that Pat Cox was able to Top 8 with two [Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) s in his deck."*



*Ochoa was quick to point out that it's only really [Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) that took a hit. "Other counterspells are still fine. You still have to counter cards like planeswalkers and Sphinx's Revalation. Being able to do so cheaply is why cards like [Dispel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dispel) and [Negate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) are still so good."*




*![Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Supreme+Verdict)*


*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/upArrowGreen.png)[Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) - In direct response to the rise of aggressive decks, and control decks' inability to prevent creatures from reaching play, mass removal spells have returned to the main deck of many control decks. Many players in Charleston last week admitted that their lack of [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) s was a mistake. After again explaining about the rise of aggro in Standard, Calcano laughed, telling me, "Shaheen Soorani told me I should be playing four in my deck, and I think I might agree with him."*




*![Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Rest+in+Peace)*


*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/downArrowRed.png)[Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) - Owen Turtenwald and Reid Duke pretty much simultaneously spat this out as a card with significantly decreased value in the wake of Charleston and Bochum. "UW Flash isn't as popular this weekend because of [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) , so [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) just isn't as good," Turtenwald told me. Since Hoof decks are able to simply play their [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) s with [Somberwald Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Somberwald+Sage) , graveyard hate is considerably less effective against them. Since [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) rides as UW does, this week, it lays low.*




*![Alchemist's Refuge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Alchemist%27s+Refuge)*


*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/upArrowGreen.png)[Alchemist's Refuge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alchemist%27s+Refuge) - Now this was an interesting suggestion by Owen Turtenwald and Reid Duke. In contrast to worrying about [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) , Turtenwald and Duke were interested in dealing with Standard's new darling: Hoof. [Alchemist's Refuge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alchemist%27s+Refuge) provides a perfect way for Bant Control to deal with a hasty, trampling horde. "If they play Hoof, you can just flash in a [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) ," Duke explained. "It's also very good against the UW Flash decks."*

**by Blake Rasmussen**

*The breakout deck of last weekend's pair of trans-Atlantic Grands Prix was, far and away, Brad Nelson's [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) deck. Catapulting Nelson himself to a high finish and clearing the way for Martin Juza to take down Bochum, Reanicrator/Hoof, there it is!/insert hoof pun here was clearly a strong choice for last weekend.*



*But what about this weekend?*





*![Somberwald Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Somberwald+Sage)![Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Craterhoof+Behemoth)*

**Will we be seeing more of these two formerly underplayed *Avacyn Restored* cards in the weeks to come?** 

*Surprise decks are often good when unknown, as Hoof was, but become less so when they become a known entity. So as soon as Juza hoisted the trophy, the question quickly became: Does the deck have staying power, or is it a one-week-wonder?*



*The consensus among those on the floor seems to be that the deck is here to stay. Owen Turtenwald called it the "best version of Reanimator" since it was far more explosive and had a strong backup plan.*



*But, it has lost that new-car smell, and players aren't going to be surprised anymore when someone plays a [Somberwald Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Somberwald+Sage) on Turn two. That takes away some of the advantage it enjoyed last week.*



*"Oh, it's just another form of Reanimator. Probably the most popular form, though it's significantly worse against spot removal," said Patrick Chapin, citing an uptick in [Pillar of Flame](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillar+of+Flame) s and [Searing Spear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Searing+Spear) s.*



*Players have adapted in other subtle and not-so-subtle ways as well. [Alchemist's Refuge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alchemist%27s+Refuge) has become been added to a number of Bant decks this weekend in part because it allows those players to [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) at instant speed. Sure, it costs a total of seven mana to do so, but it's better than being dead.*



*Other options include [Izzet Staticaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Izzet+Staticaster) , which is excellent not only against the decks' plethora of mana creatures (other than multi-format All-Star [Deathrite Shaman](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) ), but also against [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) . Well, as long as [Gavony Township](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township) isn't active, that is.*





*![Izzet Staticaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Izzet+Staticaster)![Curse of Death's Hold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold)*

**There are plenty of ways to fight a bunch of 1/1s. Will players play enough of them to hold back [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Craterhoof%2BBehemoth)and friends?** 

*Beyond that, [Curse of Death's Hold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold) seems like a strong answer, though Black isn't the most popular color this weekend. It could certainly serve as a mirror breaker, though, coming down as turn three in that deck should the format move that way. And if things get really nutty, [Druid's Deliverance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Druid%27s+Deliverance) turns a bunch of 7/7s into a bunch of 1/1s and a 5/5 for the low, low cost of two mana.*



*Add to that the presence of [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) and [Ground Seal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ground+Seal) in sideboards and main decks around the floor, and there are plenty of answers should players want to attack the Hoof.*

*Saturday, 5:35 p.m. - The Need for Speed*

**by Nate Price**

*Last week's Grand Prix marked an upheaval in the standard order of, well, Standard. Entering the event, the speed of the format was utterly glacial. Beginning with [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) , decks had been finding more and more ways to add turns to the game, stealing the initiative from the aggressive decks in the format. *Unfortunately for the aggressive decks in the format, the slower decks in the format represented a two-pronged attack that would be hard to deal with. The UW decks attacked their early development with [Essence Scatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Scatter) and [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) . The combination of the two would allow UW Flash players to deal with three creatures over the early portion of the game, extending the game by more than enough for them to stabilize. On the other side of things, [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) presented them the problem of lifegain and a larger creatures, effectively going over the top of their creatures much as a midrange deck could. Combining the two into one deck, resulting in the predecessor of the current Bant control deck, resulted in an impossibly solid one-two punch against aggressive decks, pushing them into near-obscurity.*




*![Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Thragtusk)*


*Last week, aggressive decks proved that they were capable of competing on both fronts and reclaiming a place at the head of the class. The much-discussed [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) was the perfect answer to the early countermagic that prevented it from getting its army assembled. Even with the army in place, [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) still represented an incredibly large problem. To deal with the beastly threat, aggressive decks began to stock themselves with threats capable of reraising the [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) decks. Cards like [Hellrider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hellrider) , which can simply punch through the lifegain for a lethal blow; [Falkenrath Aristocrat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Falkenrath+Aristocrat) , which can literally go over the top of the [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) ; and [Sublime Archangel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sublime+Archangel) , which combines the best attributes of both of the previous cards. The combination of these two types of cards gave the aggressive decks exactly what they needed to get the job done, enabling their continued existence in Standard.*





*![Hellrider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hellrider)*



*![Falkenrath Aristocrat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Falkenrath+Aristocrat)![Sublime Archangel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sublime+Archangel)*



*With these cards in Standard, aggressive decks have been able to take back the turns that the slower control decks had taken from them. They are, in essence, making the format faster. With options of slowing the format down more exhausted for the meantime, many of the control decks have opted to speed themselves up. Secondly, [Farseek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek) lets the Bant player fix their mana, essential in a deck that wants to be able to cast cards like [Azorius Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azorius+Charm) , [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) , and [Jace, Architect of Thought](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought) , freely. One of the weaknesses of adding additional colors has always been ensuring the quality of the mana base, and [Farseek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek) attends to that weakness nicely.*




*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/featureNFS.JPG)

This was especially important with the prevalence of control decks in Standard at the time. Even if you established control, a single [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) could completely turn things around. As such, ending the game in two swings with a [Runechanter's Pike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Runechanter%27s+Pike) or a single hit with a [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) became a way of ensuring that opponents couldn't draw out of their situations.*



*In Bant, the linear nature of those strategies is not a possibility. Your deck is designed to interact with opponents at every phase of the game, and establish true control of the game. As such, the deck doesn't have that same two-punch feel of the previous control decks. "The deck definitely takes longer to win than a deck like UW Flash does," Martell explained. "Sure, your creatures can end things pretty quickly on an empty board, but you've usually got to start whittling them down with [Restoration Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel) s for a few turns before you can actually kill people." It's less like a firework burning and like a zombie apocalypse: slow and inevitable. Sure, there are large fliers, and [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) is a five-powered creature, but the deck still takes many more turns to kill opponents than we are used to seeing from Standard control decks. Ironically, it is much closer to what we traditionally expect from control, much more so than the quick denouement of past weeks.*



*The speed of Standard today is no doubt considerably faster than in previous weeks. With the environment becoming much more conducive to aggressive decks, control decks have had to respond by becoming faster themselves. Traditionally, control decks have been able to use their permission to affect the early turns against aggro before going over the top with larger creatures. With [Cavern of Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+of+Souls) removing one of those possibilities, the control decks have been forced to respond by getting over the top even faster than they could before with cards like [Centaur Healer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Healer) . The combined effect of a greater aggressive presence and faster control has led to a format that is markedly different from the one played just a week ago. Considering the fluidity and ingenuity of players nowadays, and their unrivaled access to information, I see no reason why this would be the final iteration of this Standard format.*



*"We've got cards like [Azorius Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azorius+Charm) and [Detention Sphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detention+Sphere) to do double duty against their aggressive creatures and their big threats. We've got [Farseek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek) to get ahead in mana. We've got [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) in case things get out of hand. We can be just as fast as they can," as Martell put it.*



*That's good, because right now, speed rules.*



*You either get fast, or you die.*

*[Silverblade Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silverblade+Paladin) - To call [Silverblade Paladin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silverblade+Paladin) a new player is a bit unfair to him. He has been an integral part of GW decks since the beginning of the season. Up through last week, the Paladin's stock has been steadily rising, as less players dedicated slots in their decks for creature removal. In an earlier interview, Josh Utter-Leyton explained that the reason people have stopped playing creature removal is the presence of incredibly resilient creatures like [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) and [Geralf's Messenger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geralf%27s+Messenger) . As such, creatures that would normally be vulnerable to this spot removal but possess a large amount of raw power have become incredibly powerful. The Paladin has the ability to not only act as one of these "Baneslayer" creatures itself, but it turns another into one as well. With the rise of aggressive decks through last week and into this one, it will be interesting to see if the Paladin and his ilk can survive the reintroduction of spot removal that seems to be on the Standard Horizon.*




*![Knight of Infamy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Knight+of+Infamy)*


*[Knight of Infamy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) - " [Knight of Infamy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) is so unfair. [Knight of Infamy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) is a card that is perfectly positioned to have a lot of lasting power in Standard. A great introduction to the BR Zombies decks that rose to prominence last week, the Knight's main initial draw was his ability to ignore [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) tokens. This allowed the BR deck to deal with one of the best defensive tactics that control decks used to stem the tide of aggression. It wasn't lost on players that the Knight's protection from white is also an excellent way of combating the GW decks in the field as well. Coming into this week, GW Humans is even more popular than before, keeping the Knight extremely relevant, even in the face of change.*

*[Somberwald Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Somberwald+Sage) - Thanks to the recognized weakness of spot removal in the current format, another card that has been able to benefit is [Somberwald Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Somberwald+Sage) and his master [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) . Normally, the Sage would be utterly unplayable. No one wants to pay three mana for a creature that dies to absolutely every removal spell in the entire format. It's simply too vulnerable to justify the cost. With that simply not the case coming into the weekend, the Sage has allowed Hoof players to successfully navigate the maze of hate directed at their graveyards by simply casting the Behemoth from their hands. Three mana from one card is a whole lot of mana, and it can allow players to do ridiculous things. One of those ridiculous things is playing a [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) on turn five or six and attacking for lethal damage. With [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) keeping spot removal on the outside looking in, it will be interesting to see if people find other uses for this impressive mana engine.*

Just like the Aristocrat, Hellkite is capable of flying high above [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) . While it certainly more fragile than the Vampire, the ability to kill [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) tokens or prevent a [Restoration Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel) from blocking is very potent, giving the card a feeling all its own.*

*Saturday, 7:27 p.m. - Looking at Standard: A Deckbuilder's Perspective*
------------------------------------------------------------------------



**by Nate Price**

*The ease of access to information in the current age of **Magic** provides both boons and banes. More information means more informed decisions, but it also means that things are constantly changing, and at a greater rate than ever before.*



*With things constantly in flux, making deckbuilding decisions can become incredibly difficult, even with the massive amount of available information. As one variable changes it completely changes the power level of every other card in the format. As such, the faster things change, the harder it actually becomes to adapt.*



*I had the opportunity to sit down with Brian Kibler, Gerry Thompson, and Conley Woods to discuss how Standard and the shifting tides is viewed through the eyes of an expert deckbuilder, someone who has to deal with it on a daily basis.*



*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/brian_kibler.jpg) *Brian Kibler** 

*"Last week, I had much less information," Kibler explained. "This week, I had a much better idea of how to tune my GW deck for the field."*



*After the weekend worth of Standard, Kibler realized that players were going to have to start playing cards like [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) to deal with the abundance of creature-based decks.*



*"Once you decide that you want to play [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) , you are more inclined to play [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) . With both of those cards in your deck, you have no reason not to play [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) . Since the control decks were going to be doing that, I needed to find a card that would be good against all of those threats to my deck," Kibler elaborated.*



*Gerry Thompson, a noted deck-tuning master, echoed Kibler's thought process.*



*"When you're preparing for a tournament, you have to acknowledge that there are all sorts of levels that players can approach the tournament with. Some show up with the best deck from the week before, some show up with the deck designed to beat that deck, and some decide that since players are going to try and beat the best deck, they'll bring the deck that beats the deck that beats the best deck. It can get incredibly confusing sometimes," he admitted.*



*Woods elaborated on Thompson's point.*



*"For example, some players will look at the Hoof deck from Charleston, decide it's sweet, and run it here. Others will realize that since the Hoof deck is sweet, a lot of players might be playing it and choose a deck that will beat it. Still others will try to go a level above that. What makes this so hard is that there is no such thing as a perfect 75 cards. You will never, especially in this day and age, have a deck that is unbeatable against the entire field. What you want to try to do, is figure out as many levels as you can and then play the deck that does the best against the largest number of possibilities. You are going to be wrong for most of the events you play in. When you're right, though, you get to breeze through the tournament. Even when you're wrong, you aren't so wrong that it kills your chances of succeeding in the tournament."*



*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/gerry_thompson.jpg) *Gerry Thompson** 

*The crux of this approach to deck selection is in properly identifying how things are currently and how they might change in the future. Much of this relies on understanding what the decks in the field are trying to do and why they are successful at it. For example, [Bant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) is a successful control deck because it plays large creatures, potentially gains a bunch of life, and can sweep the board. This combination of cards is very good against most of the aggressive decks that popped up in Charleston and Bochum, and it's why many control players have switched to [Bant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) Control over other options. Recognizing this trend, Kibler chose to take his GW deck from Charleston, which was already reasonably good against the other aggressive decks in the field, and tune it to deal with the expected shift to [Bant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) Control, and the addition of [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) especially.*




*![Wolfir Avenger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Wolfir+Avenger)*


*" [Wolfir Avenger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wolfir+Avenger) may be the best card in my deck for this field," Kibler admitted. "The regeneration is incredibly powerful against [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) and [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) both, allowing the Avenger to remain on the table as a persistent threat. In addition to that, the flash is incredibly relevant. To beat the control decks in Standard right now, you have to be able to build up early pressure while having a way to make sure you don't simply fold to removal. You want to play as many reasonable threats as possible. That's why a card like [Gavony Township](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township) is so impressive right now. With Township in play, all of my threats are legitimate threats to end the game, especially the Avenger. With decks needing to run more copies of [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) now, [Thalia, Guardian of Thraben](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thalia%2C+Guardian+of+Thraben) , has become even more powerful than she already was. [Delay](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delay) ing a [Farseek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek) , [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) , or [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) can be absolutely brutal for the [Bant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) player. Thalia can really make their decks unable to win."*



*Another card Kibler singled out as being incredibly impressive right now was [Slaughter Games](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) , a card that Christian Calcano was excited to play in Grand Prix Charleston. While the format in Charleston was certainly conducive to [Slaughter Games](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) , the rush of people to [Bant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) makes the environment here in San Antonio even better.*



*"If you resolve a [Slaughter Games](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) against [Bant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) and hit their [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) , they are going to have a really hard time beating your aggressive deck," Kibler told me. "In fact, I think that right now, an effect like [Slaughter Games](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) is probably the best that it has ever been, even considering the [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) format where resolving an [Extirpate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate) meant that they legitimately couldn't win. It's that good right now."*



*Thompson and Woods are in agreement, but it's only because this is an explored format.*



*" [Slaughter Games](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) is great, but it's absolutely abysmal in new formats, no matter how well you think you understand what people are going to do," Woods said.*



*"Yeah," Thompson continued. "It's pretty embarrassing when you Games them, name a card you expect everyone to be playing, and find out that they don't have one. Your opponent just smiles and thanks you for wasting a turn and discarding a card."*



*The most important thing that I learned from discussing deckbuilding in Standard with this trio was to focus my aggression wisely. There is a great deal to be lost if a player is trying to be too clever.*



*"It's hard to be a brewer, with that mindset, and not potentially end up getting greedy," Woods admitted. "Now, with information traveling so fast, all of the good strategies have been found a few weeks into the tournament. Players with the 'break the format' mindset at this point often end up spiraling around until they find some really obscure card combination that no one else is playing and think they're on to something when they should realize that there's probably a reason that no one is playing it."*



*![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsan12/conley_woods.jpg) *Conley Woods** 

*Words of strong advice coming from a brewmaster of his caliber.*



*"I've learned that no matter what benefit you think you may be giving your deck while tuning, you have to be careful not to take away from what your deck is supposed to be doing and what makes it good," Woods continued.*



*To this, Thompson added, "I'm playing red removal in the same slot I was playing [Unsummon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unsummon) because I thought I needed to. After playing more with it, I realized that while I gained the ability to kill creatures I might need to, I lost the ability to protect my own against their removal. I haven't really been incredibly hurt by it so far, but I could definitely see that possibly being an issue."*



*Despite a desire to stay close to what people know works, don't be afraid to look a little outside the box if it can help you without hurting you. Take one of Kibler's sideboard gems, for example.*



*"I'm playing Rootbound Defense in my sideboard, and it's just an incredible card in Standard right now. My deck is very fast, can outfight a [Thragtusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) , and kill players before they can get off a good [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) . They absolutely have to play [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) against me. Rootbound Defense is just backbreaking."*



*So remember:*



* *Try to predict the future, not just recognize the present.*
* *Prepare for as many decks as you can, but don't be upset if you can't beat them all.*
* *Don't be clever for clever's sake. Sometimes the reason something isn't being played is because it isn't that good.*
* *Always keep your deck's needs in mind.*
