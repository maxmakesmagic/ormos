Sapphire Division Top 4 Preview
===============================



 Posted in **Competitive Gaming**
 on September 5, 2019 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_richhagon.jpg)
By Rich Hagon




Rich Hagon combines a deep knowledge of the players of the Pro Tour with a passionate love of the game. He's a regular commentator for Pro Tour and Grand Prix live video coverage, and is the official Pro Tour Statistician. He has been covering Magic events since 2006. 






The MPL is back in business this week, and there's a ton to get through. Congratulations go to Matt Nass (5-2), Lee Shi Tian (5-2), Ken Yukuhiro (4-3), and Brian Braun-Duin (4-3), who join us for the Saturday Showdown at noon PT/3 p.m. ET/7 p.m. UTC on [twitch.tv/magic](https://www.twitch.tv/magic)! Javier Dominguez just missed out on tiebreaks at 4-3, while Shahar Shenhar and John Rolf had to settle for 3-4 records, leaving Janne Mikkonen (Savjz) adrift at 0-7. Here's how the Top 4 made it:


**Matt Nass – USA (5-2)**


Nass had a tough Spark Split, finishing 1-6, then started his Core Split in an 0-2 hole with losses to Shenhar's Mono-Blue Tempo and the Kethis Combo mirror against Dominguez. But he turned things around winning against Braun-Duin, then playing a brilliant set against Yukuhiro. It's definitely worth your time to watch that one from start to finish.


<https://www.youtube.com/embed/HuLIJmbuY9I>


 


The wins kept coming as he defeated Lee (Kethis Combo) and Savjz (Orzhov Vampires). At 4-2 even a loss might have sufficed, but Nass left nothing to chance by completing the five-match run with victory over Rolf, securing the No. 1 seed.


**Lee Shi Tian – Hong Kong (5-2)**


Like everyone in the Top 4, Lee played Kethis Combo, but it looked a poor choice after his 1-2 start. He turned things around in his fourth match against Shenhar. With the games tied at 1-1, Lee was under pressure facing lethal attacks over and over, each time staying alive for one more turn. Lee kept going long enough to get there, and that narrow win stabilized him at 2-2. His following three wins against Rolf, Savjz, and Dominguez secured his No. 2 seed for the weekend.


<https://www.youtube.com/embed/UqBHVnJn5lk>


 


**Ken Yukuhiro – Japan (4-3)**


It was a topsy-turvy week for Yukuhiro. The tough part of his schedule came first as he faced Braun-Duin, Nass, and Lee in his first three matches, emerging from that gauntlet at 2-1. But there was no momentum as Dominguez pulled him back to 2-2, then Yukuhiro went to 3-3 with a victory over Savjz's Vampires and Rolf's Esper Control. That made Shenhar with Mono-Blue Tempo a must-win for Yukuhiro. [Siren Stormtamer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siren+Stormtamer) with [Curious Obsession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curious+Obsession) were quite the threat, but Yukuhiro squeaked out a win to become the No. 3 seed.


**Brian Braun-Duin – USA (4-3)**


Starting out 0-3, Braun-Duin must have wondered whether he'd made the wrong call by playing Kethis Combo. Every remaining match win was needed, and by the time he'd defeated Dominguez (Kethis Combo), Shenhar (Mono-Blue Tempo), and Mikkonen (Vampires) he was back in the hunt. John Rolf with Esper Control, something that Braun-Duin knows a thing or two about, was the clinching victory as [The Elderspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Elderspell) secured his No. 4 seat for Saturday.


FOWYDRN
=======


When Zac Hill made the Top 8 of [Pro Tour Honolulu in 2009](https://magic.wizards.com/en/eventcoverage/pthon09), he had the letters "FOWYDRN" written across his knuckles. The message? *Focus on what you're doing right now*. Hill was in his final *Magic* tournament ahead of joining Wizards of the Coast, and used that acronym to, well, focus. That's what we saw in action across the MPL this week.


Matt Nass – 1-6 previous split, 0-2 start this time. *FOWYDRN*: 5-2, and top seed.


Lee Shi Tian – 1-2 start. *FOWYDRN*: Four wins in a row, and No. 2 seed.


Ken Yukuhiro – Frustration with never winning two in a row, constantly restarting his run. *FWYDRN*: Defeat Rolf for the 4-3 and No. 3 seed.


Brian Braun-Duin – The worst possible start at 0-3. *FOWYDRN*: Four straight wins to go 4-3, a tiebreak edge over Javier Dominguez, and through to Top 4 as No. 4 seed.


Want to be a better *Magic* player? *FOWYDRN*.


The Race for Worlds
===================


Braun-Duin may look back to this as the week he secured a place in the 2019 World Championship. His 8 points take him to 93 for the season, and 1st place overall. Shahar Shenhar's 6 points take him to 71 points overall, well within range of Márcio Carvalho and Seth Manfield who are tied for 4th on 78. Ken Yukuhiro enters the Top 10, with his 4-3 record taking him to 68 points on the season.


Metagame: Then and (Still) Now
==============================


The metagame for round-robin play was all about the emergent deck from the Mythic Championship Qualifier weekend for Mythic Champion V: Kethis Combo.


* 5 Kethis Combo (Matt Nass, Ken Yukuhiro, Brian Braun-Duin, Javier Dominguez, Lee Shi Tian)
* 1 Mono-Blue Tempo (Shahar Shenhar)
* 1 Orzhov Vampires (Janne Mikkonen)
* 1 Esper Control (John Rolf)

To my absolute non-astonishment, Braun-Duin (a man who lost to all three Top 4 opponents, all in Kethis Combo mirror matches, and a man who is a master of all things Esper) switched things up for the weekend and moved to Esper Hero. The other three stayed true to their combo successes through the round-robin matches.

There's a little more going in here as players look to metagame the metagame, as Paul Cheon explains:


* Lee has 2 two copies of [Yawgmoth's Vile Offering](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Vile+Offering) along with a [Teshar, Ancestor's Apostle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teshar%2C+Ancestor%27s+Apostle) in his main deck. He did trim two copies of [Ashiok, Dream Render](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashiok%2C+Dream+Render), which I believe to be the best card in Kethis Combo mirrors.
* Yukuhiro is playing a full *four* copies of the Ashiok in his main deck alongside a copy of [Jace, Wielder of Mysteries](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Wielder+of+Mysteries). His list is specifically tuned to beat the Kethis Combo mirror. He even cut all the removal from his deck, including the three to four copies of [Oath of Kaya](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oath+of+Kaya). He has just four copies of [Assassin's Trophy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Assassin%27s+Trophy) in the sideboard as a versatile removal spell that kills [Kethis, the Hidden Hand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kethis%2C+the+Hidden+Hand) and planeswalkers. I think he will have the slight edge against the other Kethis decks—maybe 3–5%.
* Braun-Duin decided to just bring something different with his swap to Esper Hero. He's very comfortable playing the deck as he won a previous split playing it. He did recognize that [Ashiok, Dream Render](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashiok%2C+Dream+Render) is the absolute best card against Kethis Combo and is playing four copies in the main deck, as it does stone nothing in most other matchups. After going 0-3 in Kethis Combo mirrors, bringing a deck that he's great at piloting and tuning it to beat Kethis is his plan.
* Nass is playing the most stock version of the deck. He does have the fourth copy of [Ashiok, Dream Render](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashiok%2C+Dream+Render) in the sideboard as it's the best card in the matchup, but he also has three copies of [Unmoored Ego](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unmoored+Ego). [Unmoored Ego](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unmoored+Ego) naming Kethis is powerful, making it difficult for the opposing Kethis Combo deck to win. He also added two copies of [Vraska, Golgari Queen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vraska%2C+Golgari+Queen), which removes any and all threats you would expect. Nass also has a basic Swamp in the sideboard, a concession that his opponents may be packing [Assassin's Trophy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Assassin%27s+Trophy) or [Field of Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Field+of+Ruin) in their decks. (Yukuhiro has four copies of [Assassin's Trophy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Assassin%27s+Trophy) in his sideboard, and went one step further than Nass with a basic Swamp in his main deck.)

A Combo Crisis?
===============


What is it about a new combo deck that pulls our collective chain? Few things set our pulse racing more than a combo deck coming from nowhere to dominate a metagame, and seeing all four players qualify for the playoff with the same Kethis Combo deck may have hearts racing. But why?


The nature of combo decks is that they are all *different*. Aggro decks throughout history look similar to each other. A [Jackal Pup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup) from 1997 and a [Skymarcher Aspirant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skymarcher+Aspirant) from 2019 are a parallel we can grasp. The same is true with control [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) to [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) to [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate): their gameplay is the same.


But combo challenges us every time. [Nexus of Fate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nexus+of+Fate) looks nothing like [Kethis, the Hidden Hand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kethis%2C+the+Hidden+Hand). [Diligent Excavator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diligent+Excavator) has nothing in common with [Cryptolith Rite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptolith+Rite). [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) and [Jace, Wielder of Mysteries](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Wielder+of+Mysteries) are entirely different ways to win. The thrill of each new combo deck is that we don't see it coming, and the pieces aren't part of our everyday *Magic* experience.


99% of matches are determined by life totals. This weekend, multiple games won't be. Scary? Maybe. Awesome? Definitely.
