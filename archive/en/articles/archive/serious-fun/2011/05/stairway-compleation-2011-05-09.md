Stairway to Compleation
=======================



 Posted in **Serious Fun**
 on May 10, 2011 










Prerelease Events, like those that happened worldwide this past weekend for *New Phyrexia*, are one of the most exciting times for me to being playing **Magic**. While we've had the luxury of both the [Card Image Gallery](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/tcg/article.aspx?x=mtg/tcg/newphyrexia/spoiler) and [Gatherer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?action=advancedamp;set=+%5B%22New%20Phyrexia%22%5D) being updated with the entirety of the set for almost two weeks, it wasn't until this past weekend that we had our first chance to see the latest cards live. I had an excellent time getting my hands even dirtier with the latest wares from the Phyrexians, and I was pleasantly surprised by the ferocity of the Mirran Resistance.

Something tells me the story isn't really over.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/sf/sf142_marrow.jpg)  
Regardless, Phyrexians make all the good guys go bad eventually, so I'm not concerned. If you don't believe me (or *do*, for that matter) then try out a [Launch Party](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/tcg/events.aspx?x=mtgcom/events/release-facts) near you this weekend; I guarantee that you'll see some amazing things!

This week, however, is a celebration of Phyrexian glory. While we may find some endearing features on the losing side, it's the winners of wars that get to write the history books. And if there are history books being written on Mirro—er, *New Phyrexia* now, the writers would be the Praetors: chief among Phyrexians, closest to the Father of Machines, and gifted with true, genuine sentience.

While I spoke about Vorinclex and a few other Praetors in [last week's article](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/sf/140) (as well as about Type 5, hint hint), this week is a chance to revisit them and share something beyond another deck that makes other players cry.

The problem I faced, however, was bringing together a surly bunch of selfish, insular, plane-dominating *jerks* into one deck.

Small Steps
I'll get back to Praetors in a moment. First, let me share a way to play that some of you are already familiar with and may still even enjoy regularly today: Rainbow Stairwell.

**Rainbow Stairwell****In Brief:** Rainbow Stairwell is a Singleton (sometimes called "Highlander") deck construction format with additional requirements on mana costs and colors, and geared towards multiplayer mayhem.

**Rules Rundown:** There are a lot of different twists and tweaks on Rainbow Stairwell, and you or your local buddies may be playing slightly differently from the rules below. However, in the interest of making things clear, the following rules should be considered a base case for the format; as with any other form of **Magic**, feel free to iterate and adapt as desired!

Your deck must be *exactly* 60 cards, six cards from each of the five colors as well as six artifacts or colorless cards. Additionally, you must have one each, for each color, of cards with converted mana cost one through six. That is, you much have a staircase of cards starting at one mana and moving up to six, for each color. Multicolored cards are not allowed.

After that, it's just normal multiplayer **Magic**!

**Pros:** Rainbow Stairwell is a wonderful confluence of restricted deck building and creative solutions to constrained decks. With a streak of hilarious randomness, particularly thanks to the theme of including *every* color as well as the rigid nature of putting a deck together, Rainbow Stairwell is a fantastic way to have a lot of fun without needing 40 more cards (as in Commander).

**Cons:** Without fudging some of the rules or otherwise adapting the premise to your own ideas, Rainbow Stairwell is a very confined way to put decks together. And while some randomness and hilariously odd board states can be entertaining, not everyone finds five-color decks of the multiplayer variety to be their cup of tea.

  
With five Praetors, five colors, five flavors of Phyrexian fatty all in one deck, Rainbow Stairwell is an excellent way to—

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/sf/sf142_anatomist.jpg)  
*This format is inadequate. Send it to the splicers for innovation.*

Uh, well, *that*. Rainbow Stairwell an excellent format for five-color fun but there's just a tiny problem: most of the Praetors costs *more* than six mana. The usual route doesn't work as we're capped at going up to six mana. But the answer is to simply follow Phyrexian footsteps and splice some *improvements* onto our stairwell: what if the colors themselves were stairs too?

**Phyrexian Stairwell****In Brief:** Phyrexian Stairwell is a variant on Rainbow Stairwell where cards have a larger total range in converted mana costs.

**Rules Rundown:** The general base for Rainbow Stairwell is in effect here, with a small tweak on mana costs. Instead of every color starting at one and moving up through six, the first color of your choice would start at one and move to six, with subsequent colors starting and ending one higher each time.

That is, your second color would have cards with converted mana cost two through seven, your third color would have cards of three through eight, and so on. Artifacts and colorless cards can be picked as one of the steps along the way, anywhere in the color order.

**Pros:** With some careful consideration, a Stairwell deck that includes *every* Praetor can be made! Additionally, since different colors can be set at the different levels of converted mana cost steps, there is a lot more flexibility and variety in constructing decks.

**Cons:** Rules baggage is rules baggage. Rainbow Stairwell is already a little tricky to explain and have other players grok; adding an additional layer of sweetness (variety) will make some players just glaze right over.

Additionally, with the increased average cost of spells in the deck, games will take longer and feel slower until the upper mana thresholds are reached. While this mimics the actual Phyrexian invasion of Mirrodin, flavor doesn't always translate well into actual gaming.

  
Now we're cooking with glistening oil! The truth is that Rainbow Stairwell is as complex or simple as you'd like it to me, at least as long as you're following the function and principle of the format. I'll share another localization of Rainbow Stairwell at the end but, for now, let's walk through a quick and *compleated* build of a Phyrexian Stairwell deck:

BONUS: Alara Shard Stairwell
Not long after *Alara Reborn* was released, the players around my local game store decided to get down Stairwell style but using a multicolor-permitting approach. Like everyone else, we also adapted Rainbow Stairwell to our desired purposes.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/sf/sf142_alara.jpg)  
**Alara Shard Stairwell****In Brief:** Alara Shard Stairwell is a variant on Rainbow Stairwell where colors available are restricted, but multicolor cards are available.

**Rules Rundown:** The general base for Rainbow Stairwell is in effect here with the following changes:


> * Choose a shard as featured in Shards of Alara.
> * Instead of using cards with converted mana cost one through six, cards of one through seven are eligible.
> * Cards should be chosen for each color of the shard, as well as for artifact/colorless cards and multicolor cards, separately.
> 

This means that you have a stairway built that excludes two colors, compensated by increasing the top of the stairs by one and allowing another set of steps. It also means that there are 25 land slots, rather than 24, to work with. (Or, alternatively, 24 lands and one remaining slot for something that costs eight mana, a flavor we liked. Do whatever you want!)

**Pros:** While the shards of Alara may be old news today, anyone who wants to mix Rainbow Stairwell with a multicolor fix can start here. Just like Phyrexian Stairwell, the flavor of a deck that lives exactly within a shard is something I know that I, and many of you, can get behind!

**Cons:** Multicolored cards can be tricky, especially when looking at the converted mana cost 1 slot. Also, thanks in part to multicolor feel of several sets back-to-back, there can be a little lingering "multicolor fatigue" among fellow players. Rebooting into a format like this may not be something everyone would be into.
