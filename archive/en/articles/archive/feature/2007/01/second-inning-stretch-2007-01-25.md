There was only one North American PTQ this past weekend (Week Three) due to the, um, massively pervasive and successful [Planar Chaos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Planar+Chaos) Prerelease tournaments (and Las Vegas didn't report to the Tournament Center), so we don't have any Week Three data yet. 

### How to Read These Numbers:

![Hallowed Fountain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hallowed+Fountain)Frank Karsten over at [Online Tech](/en/articles/archive/data-mining-extended-tournaments-statistics-2007-01-24)  has some kind of crazy "popularity" system using a massive amount of **Magic** Online Adept labor and one thousand supercomputers (rumor has it he and Richard Garfield went back to the University of Pennsylvania and burgled ENIAC itself) to tabulate his boxes… but that's okay. He's *Online* Tech. Here at Swimming With Sharks, we are old school. One blue box for every PTQ winner, and one white box for every PTQ Top 8. We don't have complete tournament data, and unlike on **Magic** Online, can't watch a million matches to break down the exact metagame. However, you can just look right to left and count boxes to figure out what the most popular decks are. You should make sure that you prepare for these decks the most, because with incomplete data, the most popular decks are the ones you are most likely to have to beat in the Swiss rounds. I tend to respect minority decks that show up in smaller numbers and finish first more than decks that "merely" graduate to Top 8 pins, and group them, graphically, accordingly.

There are many decks in this format. It might actually be the most diverse format we have ever seen… It is varied like Standard, but with *five more years* of blocks tacked on, Invasion and forward, to intersect and combine with *Ravnica*'s unbelievable mana options. Some of the decks are well known transplants from other formats, the Boros Deck Wins and Affinity ports from Standard and even Block formats past and present, and there are the decks unique to the format, created by the combination of specific cards from, say, *Invasion* and *Mirrodin*, *Onslaught* and *Ravnica*.

There are, in fact, far too many decks to analyze all of them exhaustively in a single article. So this time, I am going to just go over the absolute elite or most popular decks in the format, this time all the PTQ winners and the many variants on powerhouse [Hallowed Fountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Fountain) decks.

### TEPS

"TEPS" stands for "The Extended Perfect Storm." You may know the style of deck by another name; most Pros refer to it merely as "[Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ritual) Desire," because that is just pretty descriptive…. TEPS plays a bunch of "[Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ritual)s" ([Rite of Flame](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rite+of+Flame), [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song), and so forth), creating a critical mass of mana and spells, then rides the storm count to play and break [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire). [Chromatic Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Star) and its like play a twofold role in TEPS. First of all, they are high-velocity spells that essentially [Opt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opt) or cycle through the deck while simultaneously fixing the deck's mana. Red is easy. Black is pretty easy. ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif) for the [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire)? Not a cake walk. TEPS can borrow a little from [Channel the Suns](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Channel+the+Suns) and a little from [Chromatic Sphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Sphere) or [Darkwater Egg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkwater+Egg) to pay the necessary costs to get the big Storm going.

TEPS is an extremely simple deck to play in the abstract. You should definitely test it, or at least against it, because some version is *also* almost certainly the best deck in the format. The Hall of Fame's Raphael Levy called his version the best deck he had ever played after an awesome performance at the World Championships. The glory of TEPS is that it wins on the fourth turn essentially every game. My personal testing says that Levy's version will win on the second turn as many as two games in ten. It is extremely good against beatdown decks, and can overpower certain control decks, even the popular U/W 'Tron / big mana school.

This version is very much in the Levy camp. With four copies of [Burning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish), TEPS can play "seven" copies of [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire), even with only three physical copies of the key card in the main. Dean's deck is fast and consistent. The main kill is [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony), but there is an alternate kill with Goblin tokens thanks to [Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens) should the opponent be playing [Ivory Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ivory+Mask), [True Believer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True+Believer), or something similar.

I actually *like* that Dean removed Raph's [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)es entirely from the sideboard (Frank Karsten moved two copies to the main over [Plunge into Darkness](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plunge+into+Darkness)). [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) is actually kind of bad in this deck. I've played games where the opponent had a double [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) hand to "disrupt my disruption" in the early turns, but then didn't have enough storm to win, even when he hit third- or fourth-turn [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire). [Plunge into Darkness](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plunge+into+Darkness) is actually pretty absurd in this deck. The opponent taps out, you pay eighteen life at the end of his turn, untap, and win.

I actually built this deck to beat the top decks from Worlds and Online Extended (at least they were tops at the time): Boros Deck Wins and TEPS. G/W aggressive decks are hell on Boros Deck to begin with…. The tricky part was beating TEPS. In my version, I played four copies of [Gilded Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilded+Light); Trevor removed these for an extra [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte), a third [Worship](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Worship), and two [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant)s. [Gilded Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilded+Light) will usually beat TEPS, but with enough mana, the opponent can theoretically fall back on his [Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens) plan… In that case, you usually need [Worship](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Worship), though probably not [Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic). [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant) is better to play *while* the opponent is going off. I liked [Gilded Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilded+Light) because it counters [Gilded Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilded+Light) and kept saving me from the third consecutive [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) in testing.

![Worship](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Worship)[Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic) plus [Worship](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Worship), if not a hard lock in Game 1, will usually buy a deck this aggressive deck sufficient time to race another creature deck.   
 Most of the known decks in the format are actually byes for G/W Haterator. Flow Rock and Flow Deck Wins are easy to beat because the G/W has so many basic lands…. The Flow decks don't have any big button they can push to create massive card advantage. Affinity is favorable with or without Troll + [Worship](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Worship), but without [Disciple of the Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault), it can't really break that combo. Trinket Angels, one of the strongest "anti-" decks in the format, is just not lined up to properly beat G/W. It has no seven for [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance), and one of the four [Eternal Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Dragon)s will eventually eat all the [Exalted Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel)s. The problem for G/W is that its few bad matchups happen to be among the statistically strongest *or* relatively weak, yet *popular*, wings of the metagame. G/W can beat either of the two schools of [Hallowed Fountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Fountain) decks, but can't really be tuned to hate out both consistently during the same tournament. I played G/W in a PTQ and only lost to NO Stick decks, won when I drew [Krosan Grip](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Grip), and lost every Game 1. My conclusion was to cut [Plow Under](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under) to increase artifact hate (I actually hit all four [Plow Under](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under)s one game and still lost, believe it or not). However, Trevor needed [Plow Under](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under) to win his PTQ: His finals opponent was a U/W big mana deck. Unlike the U/W decks, which are difficult to beat, but *can* be beaten, the next PTQ-winning wonder deck is essentially impossible for G/W to beat:

This deck is based on the 5-1 deck from Day Three of the 2006 World Championships. Though we call it Aggro Loam, it isn't really that aggressive, just more aggressive than The CAL.

Operationally, Aggro Loam has one big plan: [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam). It doesn't really matter what else is going on when you are playing [Ancestral](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral) Recall every turn. [Forgotten Cave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forgotten+Cave), [Tranquil Thicket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tranquil+Thicket), [Wooded Foothills](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wooded+Foothills), and [Bloodstained Mire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodstained+Mire) all team up to give Scott three more cards in hand. All the [Dredge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dredge) makes [Werebear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Werebear) big, and sets up [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) as an extra "card in hand" from the graveyard (as if Aggro Loam needed any more card advantage).

![Devastating Dreams](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Devastating+Dreams)The deck has two different endgames, both of them superb. One is the "A" from The CAL, (Seismic) [Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Assault). With a ton of lands in hand, Scott could, you know, discard for two damage per. It doesn't take very many [Werebear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Werebear) hits and dropped lands to go lethal. The super-exciting endgame is based on [Devastating Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Dreams). [Devastating Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Dreams) requires you to discard several cards… No problem if you have been abusing [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam). Aggro Loam will usually play Dreams for six or more, for [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) + Armageddon. One creature will generally be left over, [Terravore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terravore)… who is now huge thanks to all the lands in graveyards (from Loam, from hand, from both boards). This end game is very 2002 [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)-like, a functional [Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval), though more synergistic given [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) as a potential post-Dreams backup plan.

Aggro Loam is another one of the decks that makes [Burning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) look good. Three copies of [Devastating Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Dreams) and three copies of [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) main are virtual sevens. One modification I would make to Scott's sideboard would be the inclusion of three or even four copies of [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge), specifically for the next and last of our PTQ-winning archetypes:

This deck is the great grandson of Nick West's Top 8 effort from Pro Tour Columbus some years past. It is a control deck that can play like a combo deck. The combination is [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) + [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant), a soft lock that can also void the combat phase. The addition of [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Mage+of+Zhalfir) from *Time Spiral* allows NO Stick to transition into a full hard lock. With Teferi in play, the opponent can't play anything on his upkeep, or on the NO Stick player's turn. That means that there will be no response to [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant) played during the opponent's upkeep… Not even [Krosan Grip](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Grip) can get out of this unless the NO Stick player screws up… and quite badly.

![Isochron Scepter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Isochron+Scepter)[Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction) is a virtual six here (fourth copy in the side) and teams up with [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) and [Thirst for Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge) for some serious card draw. The generally inferior [Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire) // [Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ice) + [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) can also make for a personal [Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine) while helping to control the board. One innovation I quite liked out of this version was the inclusion of Descendent of Kiyomaro. Most beatdown decks will try to max out on artifact removal to break up or pre-empt the lock, at the expense of any way to fight the Descendent.

NO Stick is anything but a settled archetype. Alesi-Mullen went with [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) as a sideboard bullet for his [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish), but it is not uncommon to fight decks with access to all four Helixes. [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) is actually the best card to imprint on [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) after [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant) (and sometimes just better than [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant)) in a variety of matchups. There are versions with more Angels, no main deck [Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath)s, and different counter mixes, including [Force Spike](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+Spike) and even [Absorb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Absorb)!

All that said, the most popular big slice of the metagame pie to play against in this format seems to be U/W big mana, the aggregate of U/W UrzaTron and U/W [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) variants. Like most archetypes in the wide-open Extended, there are no default lists here. Almost all of the ten recorded U/W 'Tron and Post decks will have innovations and peculiarities that you will not see in another deck in even the same Top 8!

Just for argument's sake, and because I like hybrid decks, I am going to use Steve Locke's second-place deck from the Minneapolis Top 8 for discussion.

This deck has a lot of interesting things going for it. The sore thumb is clearly those three [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage)s. The U/W big mana decks can afford their six and seven mana finishers… What's with the Grey Ogres? In this deck, Locke can double up with [Thirst for Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge) by playing a small toolbox of [Engineered Explosives](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Explosives), [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt), and [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) (I'm actually surprised there is no [Pithing Needle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle) main). [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) is actually a monster in this format. Try playing Chalice for one against [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) or for two against Aggro Loam and see how much time you can buy.

Steve's deck is a [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost), rather than UrzaTron, deck. There are arguments in favor of both schools of thought. I like UrzaTron because the lands all come into play untapped. The [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) decks, on the other hand, have several strikes against. What happens when you draw [Vesuva](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva) before [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)? Doesn't it suck that your lands come into play tapped? The biggest incentive, though, and the reason there is debate here, is that for a two color deck, [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) just leaves more open slots for mana… particularly important when another *colorless* land in [Academy Ruins](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Academy+Ruins) comes essentially spoken for.

![Mindslaver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mindslaver)The U/W big mana decks are all about power. Most or all will have an end game based on [Mindslaver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindslaver). In Extended, *one* [Mindslaver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindslaver) will usually be game, but in these decks, U/W can actually just win. [Academy Ruins](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Academy+Ruins)*puts [Mindslaver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindslaver) on top*, so U/W can't ever deck. Meanswhile, recurring the same threat time and again will make it impossible for an opponent to attack. 

The other threats in these decks vary wildly. [Sundering Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sundering+Titan) seems as good as anything, but you will see many kinds of kill cards, including [Exalted Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel)s, [Eternal Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Dragon)s, and even [Meloku the Clouded Mirror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meloku+the+Clouded+Mirror).

We've just touched upon the decks that have either already actually won tournaments, or are the most popular opponents that you are almost certain to face, and we are already past the high point in, say, Kamigawa Block. Extended is big, and that makes it hard to predict. Can your favorite deck compete against the monsters included in today's article? If not, may I suggest you revisit a little something my old buddy Worth Wollpert once told me, well before he started at R&D, on the occasion of my first (Constructed) PTQ win: *If you can't beat 'em, join 'em.*
