Standard: Now and Then
======================



 Posted in **Latest Developments**
 on May 26, 2006 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_aaronforsythe.jpg)
By Aaron Forsythe











Whenever a big Standard tournament like Regionals happens, I can't wait to see all the new decklists, as are most fans of the format. Of course, most people are trying to get an accurate read of what the metagame will look like going forward, but I'm interested in how R&D did with their own internal metagame a year ago.

Below I'm going to compare seven decks that finished highly (second or better) in Regionals this year with their vague analogues from the R&D labs—the “Future Future League”—of a year ago, when Guildpact and Dissension were in development.

A reminder about what we try to do as developers: The FFL wants to understand future metagames in a general sense. We want to know what colors are powerful and which are weak, how many cards each set looks to be contributing to the environment, and what we'll need to push in future sets to shake things up or balance them out. To that end, we build and play tons and tons of decks, of which the seven I chose are but a small sampling.

We generally come close to what ends up really happening a year later once the cards hit the streets. For example, we knew that Zoo and Gruul beats would be defining decks, but we never made a great “[Steam Vents](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steam+Vents)” deck at all—we couldn't tweak the numbers quite right, and didn't try the Urzatron in two-color builds. We had “Ghost Husk” pegged as a major player for the Orzhov, but couldn't get our [Early Harvest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Early+Harvest) combo decks to be consistent enough. In every case, *the decks that you took to Regionals were better than the decks we had internally*, so I wouldn't look to this column for much in the way of “tech” - instead look at it as a glimpse into the world of development. We walk a fine line—know too little, and the environment could be a train wreck; know too much and it feels hyper-engineered and “pre-built.”

I'll present the “real” deck first, and then follow it up with R&D's decklist pasted exactly as it appeared in our email folder where we collect competitive deck ideas during development. Our decklists are full of playtest names and shorthand, but they'll give you an accurate view of how we communicate with one another. I'll follow up each R&D deck with some notes explaining our card choices and how our decks influenced how cards were printed.

What's going on with that decklist:



|  |
| --- |
| *Some call her... Trent.* |

1. “Wilt-Free Zone” refers to the fact that this is an Orzhov deck without [Mortify](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortify). The playtest names of [Putrefy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrefy) and [Mortify](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortify) were “Wither” and “Wilt” respectively, and this deck oddly didn't pack the latter. I believe my earlier attempts at this deck had Wilts instead of Jittes, as I figured I could handle opposing Jittes with all my sacrifice outlets. I ended up relenting and playing Jittes anyway. It's interesting to note that the real-world version of Ghost Husk played Mortifies but no Jittes.
2. All the *Guildpact* Rusalkas had playtest names taken from the kids cartoon Xiaolin Showdown; Jack Spicer is [Plagued Rusalka](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plagued+Rusalka).
3. Everyone should know that “Bob” is [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant), named after Bob Maher, Jr. Bob's mana cost was changed from ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) to ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) so that decks like this one would be enabled.
4. “Trent” is [Teysa, Orzhov Scion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teysa%2C+Orzhov+Scion). Her design name was “Trent, Goth Executioner.” I had no idea at the time that I was designing the card that was meant to be the *Guildpact* novel protagonist, which is why her abilities aren't a great match for her character.
5. [Orzhov Pontiff](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orzhov+Pontiff) was an interesting card in development. It took us a long time to come up with an ability we liked for it (his art was commissioned based on a previous ability that wouldn't fit on a card). We eventually came up with a ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) 2/1 that had haunt and did *both* of the Pontiff's current abilities instead of being modal. He got taken down a couple notches and still ended up being a powerful card.
6. The Ghost Council used to cost zero mana to activate. He was really dumb. The funny thing is that we left him that way for a long time.

Zoo, popularized by Craig Jones at PT--Honolulu, seems to have outlasted “Heezy Street” (Gruul) as the beatdown deck of choice. With a fabulous suite of burn spells and a horde of super-efficient creatures, the deck can win blazingly fast.

Internally, lead developer Brian Schneider first put forth the idea that RGW beatdown had to be insane with *Guildpact*, even if the deck's mana did 14 damage to its controller. Paul Sottosanti built the “touchstone” version that we all used to bounce our other decks off of. If a deck couldn't hang with RGW, it probably wasn't a “real” deck.

What's going on with that decklist:

1. “RG 3/3” and “WG 3/3” are [Scab-Clan Mauler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scab-Clan+Mauler) and [Watchwolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Watchwolf), obv.
2. “Baloth” is [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch), also obv. Note that most of these cards had real names by this point, but we generally take the path of least effort when writing decklists.
3. “Kids” is, again, [Promise of Bunrei](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Promise+of+Bunrei) (I told you we liked it), and “[Stone Rain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stone+Rain)” is, well, [Stone Rain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stone+Rain). Paul's version wasn't as burn-heavy as the successful real-world Zoo builds; instead, it was more of a fast, disruptive, three-color beatdown deck. (“Three-Deuce” was the name of a popular RGW Extended deck that packed efficient creatures and disruptive elements.)
4. “Shambling City” was going to be the name of [Rumbling Slum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rumbling+Slum) until someone reminded Matt Cavotta that there was already a card named [Shambling Shell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shambling+Shell) in the block. The Slums only lasted a week or so in the deck before they were replaced with the faster “Christmas Ants,” now known as [Giant Solifuge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Giant+Solifuge).
5. Why no [Burning-Tree Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning-Tree+Shaman)? Simple… it didn't exist yet. We created the Shaman relatively late in *Guildpact* development as a foil to some of *Kamigawa's* annoying hallmarks—[Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top), [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte), and Meloku. Later versions of this deck contained the Shaman.
6. In the sideboard you'll see “Position” and “Pink Bolt.” The former is [Glare of Subdual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glare+of+Subdual)—it has the name “Position” because it wasn't quite “[Opposition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opposition).” The latter is [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix), given this cute moniker because, well, red and white make pink.
7. The [Terrarion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terrarion)s in the board are part of the “I'm scared of [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon)” package that Paul devised. The [Creeping Mold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Mold)s can up the number of LD spells to eight; we loved playing Karoos in the FFL and Paul frequently made us pay for it.

Some people call this deck “Critical Mass,” but because the (Gnarled) Mass has been removed for the new hotness—[Plaxcaster Frogling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plaxcaster+Frogling)—I'll stick to the simpler moniker “Graft.”

Internally, we had doubts as to whether or not Simic creature decks would be competitive in *Dissension* Standard, as we figured [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) was a great answer to every damage source the deck could muster. Of course, with ten counterspells to keep Wrath at bay, the deck turned out to be pretty darned good. Out versions were a little lighter on the counters… unless you're talking +1/+1 counters…

What's going on with that decklist:

1. “Wood Snake” was the design name for [Coiling Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Coiling+Oracle). It was a wanna-be [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) (which was “Rampant Snake” here in development) with the size and stats of [Wood Sage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wood+Sage), a little-known card from *Tempest*.
2. There are twelve graft creatures here—four [Plaxcaster Frogling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plaxcaster+Frogling), four [Cytoplast Root-Kin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cytoplast+Root-Kin) (which was “[Cytospawn Shambler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cytospawn+Shambler)”), and four [Sporeback Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sporeback+Troll). Say what? Ah, the Troll. The Troll originally cost just ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif), and a that cost, Nate was willing to put him in the “best two-mana creatures of all time” category. Combined with the Frogling, you could set up a board position where your creatures couldn't die in combat, and they couldn't be targeted outside of combat. (Frogling had an activation of 1 instead of 2 initially, which was incredibly frustrating to face.) We came to our senses eventually and swapped the costs on the Troll and [Aquastrand Spider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aquastrand+Spider).
3. Other playtest names: “Acorn Couatl” was [Patagia Viper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Patagia+Viper), “Double Trouble” was [Doubling Season](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doubling+Season), and “Instant 2/2 Kicker-untargetable-man” was [Plaxmanta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plaxmanta). “Tropical” is short for [Tropical Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tropical+Island), which is what we called [Breeding Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool).
4. [Cytoshape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cytoshape), at various points in its life, was (a) two mana, and (b) uncommon. Its power made us change the former, and Mark Gottlieb made us change the latter, as the card presented too many rules problems to be considered simple, regardless of how easy to read the text was. In Nate's deck it plays a lot like [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate)—when you 'Shape an opponent's guy into a graft creature, it becomes a 0/0 and dies.
5. Wait a second… Where are the Jittes? The Melokus? Heck, the [Yavimaya Coast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yavimaya+Coast)s? This is a block deck, built to test graft in the most un-diluted environment possible. This gives us a good sense of how powerful the deck and mechanic are going into the next block.

![Dovescape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dovescape)I love Nick's deck. Nick had a keen eye for Constructed back when we'd play against each other in tournaments around Pittsburgh (*Tempest* / *Urza's Saga* days), but in recent years he's shifted his attention almost totally to Limited play. So I don't know if he's the inventor of this wacky masterpiece, but it wouldn't surprise me if he was.[Grand Arbiter Augustin IV](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grand+Arbiter+Augustin+IV) and [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) are two cards that most people had a hard time nailing down. Was there a deck for them to go into? Do they spawn their own decks? Even here in R&D we could never quite find good homes for these cards, although we knew they were both very powerful. I'm glad that Nick found some place to feature both of them that looks like it works very well.

The following deck doesn't have all that much to do with Nick's deck, but it is an interesting concoction that was built to put [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) through the wringer. It actually did fairly well for me.

**Spells Alive! – FFL – Aaron Forsythe**


> 
> 4 Spells Alive!  
>  4 GW Guildmage  
>  4 Baloth  
>  4 Pontiff  
>  4 BoP  
>  4 Chord of Calling  
>  1 Tolsimir  
>  4 Mortify  
>  2 Trophy Hunter  
>  1 Nullmage Shepherd  
>  4 Scatter  
>  Land
> 
> 
> 

What's going on with this decklist:

1. This is another block deck.
2. “Spells Alive!”, a.k.a. [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape), was five mana for a period of time. Many decks just scooped to the card, and those types of effects should be expensive.
3. The goal here was to break the symmetry of [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) in as many ways as possible. Blue didn't offer many ways to do that, so I went with green and black instead. Tolsimir and [Selesnya Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Selesnya+Guildmage) can make my doves bigger, and Pontiff and [Trophy Hunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trophy+Hunter) can kill my opponents'.
4. [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) is ridiculous with [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) in play… you can pay X mana+creatures to get X+3 more doves.
5. The “Land” included multiple copies of Vitu-Ghazi.

When I first saw “Owl” posted on a message board somewhere prior to Honolulu, I showed it to my coworkers and our reactions were generally the same: “This is really a deck?”

Internally, the closest we came was a goofy [Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine) Burn deck tossed together by Nate Heiss. It had the capability to catch other decks off guard, but I don't think we ever expected something like it to actually burst onto an unsuspecting metagame. I'm tempted to say we missed this deck internally, but to Nate's credit, I'll only call it a half-miss.

What's going on with this deck:

1. “Howling Butt” refers to [Kami of the Crescent Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kami+of+the+Crescent+Moon), affectionately known as “The Butt.”
2. This deck is clearly more focused on burn as opposed to full-hand tricks. After all, it doesn't even have [Ebony Owl Netsuke](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ebony+Owl+Netsuke), a card we didn't put much stock in for constructed play.
3. “10 at 10” is [Hidetsugu's Second Rite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hidetsugu%27s+Second+Rite). That Nate is full of wacky surprises.
4. “Volcanic Island” is a nickname for [Steam Vents](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steam+Vents), and “Geddon” in the sideboard is [Thoughts of Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughts+of+Ruin).
5. This deck was built to test [Cerebral Vortex](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cerebral+Vortex) during *Guildpact* development. The Vortex was a late add to the set when we had to kill a [Wheel of Fortune](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wheel+of+Fortune) variant.

[Wildfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire) decks were a tough nut for us to crack. Historically, its power level has fluctuated between World Championship winning when it appeared in *Urza's Saga* to complete dud when it returned in *Seventh Edition*. When it came back in *Ninth Edition*, several decks that used it evolved, from Adrian Sullivan's Eminent Domain to Anton Jonsson's early [Magnivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magnivore) versions, to recent Urzaton versions like Tommy's here.

In R&D, we messed around with Red/Green [Wildfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire) for a long, long, time before Devin Low figured out how key [Annex](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Annex) was to the deck.

What's going on with this deck:

1. [Ghostly Prison](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghostly+Prison) was a card we played a ton, yet never caught on all that much in the real world. It works amazingly well in combination with [Wildfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire) and [Annex](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Annex), but perhaps that's overkill.
2. [Copy Enchantment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copy+Enchantment) works really powerfully with [Dream Leash](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dream+Leash); it lets you steal untapped permanents.
3. Most of our [Wildfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire) decks were Red/Green with [Rumbling Slum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rumbling+Slum)s and Arashi in addition to Green acceleration. We certainly never had the Urzatron in a [Wildfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire) deck. We did build a version of [Magnivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magnivore)-[Wildfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire), which [you can see here](/en/articles/archive/ninth-edition-theme-deck-4-2005-03-30).

And there you have it. Not bad guesses on our part…granted, I'm only highlighting the decks that we came close on. There are plenty of decks I could show you that never caught on, and there are plenty of Regionals decks that we didn't predict. But that's all part of the fun!
