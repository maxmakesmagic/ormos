Uncovering Extended
===================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on December 9, 2010 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











"It's like the old days," Patrick told me. "Nobody has good decks!"

And by golly he is right.

At my first Pro Tour, I played a deck with three copies of [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence), one [Demonic Consultation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Consultation) (which I used to get the last [Drain Life](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drain+Life), which I used to beat Aaron Muranaka, coming off a Pro Tour Top 8—*take that Pikula*). Chris made Top 4 with essentially the same deck (we were then-connected by future **Magic Online** boss man Worth Wollpert, Top 16), but with four [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence), and no [Demonic Consultation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Consultation) (Chris tried to convince me it was terrible in our deck). Modern thinkers will tell you that Chris and I both had four copies of [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) / [Demonic Consultation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Consultation) too few, just that our missing fours don't match. At the same Pro Tour, Brian Hacker led the Masters Swiss with two copies of [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) (but three [Erg Raiders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Erg+Raiders)!), and Patrick himself finished third in the Juniors with *one* copy of [Ball Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning).

These were among the best decks in the room.

I still don't recall if Finkel ended up cutting his two main deck [Mountain Yeti](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain+Yeti)s or not. I am pretty sure the reigning U.S. National Champion was gaming with [Nettletooth Djinn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettletooth+Djinn).

*Really.*

It was different in the old days.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Necropotence)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence)  
The Hacker and Pikula [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) decks really were some of the best performers. And Patrick was a mile ahead of the rest of the Pro Tour with his Red Deck. These things have to be evaluated contextually. No, none of our decks were optimal. But we had very different resources. You can make fun of my gaping hole in the fourth [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) slot all you want, but there were people playing on that Pro Tour with [Elvish Archers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archers) and [Yavimaya Ants](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yavimaya+Ants)—basically an unplayable strategy in the world of [Land Tax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Land+Tax), [Balance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Balance), [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence), and [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) (I know, because one of them beat me). Many of us lived in terror of a [Whirling Dervish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whirling+Dervish).

Have you tried to find some decent Extended decks for ideas, or to play against, recently? Standard? There are tons of **Magic Online** Daily events, even more deck lists, and big Open tournaments across the country (psst ... [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) is a good card). If you want to learn about Legacy, [StarCityGames.com](http://www.starcitygames.com) has been breathing new life into the format, Open-in and Open-out.

But Extended?

There are tumbleweeds blowing across the dusty Internet where information about this format is involved.

It's, beautifully, kinda-like the old days.

Pre-"hive mind."

Almost pre-Netdecking.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td120_calltomind.jpg)  
A few months from now, we will be drowning in **Magic Online** and Pro Tour Qualifier results ... But it isn't a few months from now ... not yet. 

In a sense it is an amazing time to be a deck designer. With relatively little public **Magic Online** deck-list support, only the players who have put in the work will have access to the most-tuned lists, and identification of the most powerful strategies. In another sense, Extended is a dark and scary tunnel to be exploring.

... You never know what is going to happen next!

*Just like the old days.*

So why do we care?

Isn't there a reason that there isn't much Extended information? Doesn't that mean that there haven't been important tournaments (at least since [Pro Tour–Amsterdam](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptams10/welcome))? Why are we having this conversation?

Extended is the Day Three format of this year's [World Championship,](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/worlds10/welcome) currently underway in Chiba, Japan. It might not be the ever-important PTQ format *yet*, but to determine the 2010 World Champion, mastering—or at least *managing*—Extended is going to be an important step.

So how did we get here? Wasn't there just a Pro Tour using this format?



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

*Yes* ... But this point requires some explanation. Extended has become a more dynamic format of late, involving a much smaller world than in years past. The format at Pro Tour–Amsterdam included *Time Spiral* block cards. This explains the ubiquitous [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)s in aggressive decks, and the numerous methodical cards—[Coalition Relic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Coalition+Relic), [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation), and [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings)—in Michael Jacob's control deck. 

In return, we get *Scars of Mirrodin*.

Combo
This is Hunter Burton's pre-*Scars of Mirrodin* Elves deck from Pro Tour–Amsterdam. These little green men have lost quite a bit since Luis Scott-Vargas won Pro Tour–Berlin with the strategy (no [Glimpse of Nature](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature) to draw your deck; no [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) for a better Plan B than many decks' A-games), but, because of the generally smaller format, the deck remains a contender.

The main combo of the Elves deck is [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) + [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid). [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid) allows you to produce ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) with three Elves, even if they should be suffering "summoning sickness!" You can use that mana to play more Elves, which will untap [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) (which doesn't usually have a mana-producing ability at all). Without [Glimpse of Nature](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature), Elves can no longer easily burn through its entire deck, but producing a relatively modest seven mana will let you start going big with [Regal Force](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Regal+Force).

Why did we start on Elves?

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td120_ezuri.jpg)  
Elves may be one of the greatest beneficiaries of the *Scars of Mirrodin* shot in the arm. [Ezuri, Renegade Leader](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ezuri%2C+Renegade+Leader) gives you something to do with your mana immediately. Even one active [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) can go lethal thanks to the ability on Ezuri.

Put more and more mana into it, and you can get more and more trample damage out. 

The addition of Ezuri actually makes other pre-existing Elves even better; for example [Devoted Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devoted+Druid). For five mana you can get three incremental mana activations out of [Devoted Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devoted+Druid), which is not a break even, but given the numerous sources of mana the deck already has, [Devoted Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devoted+Druid) can help put you over the top with just one or two non-sick potential attackers. With just one [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) ready to attack, you can often produce enough mana to give it 20+ trampling power.

Along with Elves in the combo wing of Extended, we have two different common combinations ... both conveniently found in the above deck.

As we lost *Future Sight*, please just forget that [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) is present in Pro Tour Champ Simon Goertzen's deck and concentrate only on the [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) half of the main. And when considering that half, remember that format stalwart [Grove of the Burnwillows](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grove+of+the+Burnwillows) is leaving at the same time (so [Punishing Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Punishing+Fire) will lose value).

Yet, [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) will remain effective in modern Extended in no small part because of [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose). On the second turn, [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose) is kind of like a terrible [See Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=See+Beyond) that lets you play [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) (or another cantrip or two) the same turn! And later, just think about how awesome doubling a [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose) is! With [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) powered up, there is nothing more exciting ... mana and card drawing all in one!

The second combo is featured in Goertzen's sideboard: [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) + [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin).

You put the [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) on the [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite). The [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) taps to make a [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), which, via vigorous butterfly wing-flaps and the mysteries of fairy dust, untaps the original (so it can tap again). You can do this as many times as you like to make more and more token [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite)s (all of which have haste), to set up lethal damage.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Relic+of+Progenitus)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+of+Progenitus)  
[Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) + [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) is an easy combination to assemble. It is also very fast (turn two play [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) at the end of your turn, often locking you out of mana; untap, [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin)). Don't be surprised to see this combo as a main deck strategy! But ... why does an Ascension deck want it? Having an additional, unique, combo out of the sideboard helps Ascension dodge specific hate: [Relic of Progenitus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+of+Progenitus), for example, is extremely easy for any deck to play, and can keep counters off a [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension). [Bojuka Bog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bojuka+Bog) does much the same thing and can be accessed at will by any [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) or [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) deck. Even second tier countermeasures like a [Leyline of Sanctity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+Sanctity) have to be considered; sure you "should" "always win" against "that" ... but what if you don't have an [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil)? Having the second combo, which is in-colors, but so different (infinite damage versus critical mass generation, attack versus burn spells, Red Zone versus graveyard), makes sideboarding against [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) challenging for some colors—especially so, when both combos are in the deck at the same time.

Players might want to consider crossover sideboard cards like [Celestial Purge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Purge) or even [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil) that can reasonably interact with—if not crush—either side.

So ... No [Grove of the Burnwillows](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grove+of+the+Burnwillows)?

There is no greater beneficiary of this particular rotation than Faeries. Previously the [Punishing Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Punishing+Fire) combination could contain almost the entire Faeries deck. But now? *It's on.*

In particular [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation) jumps up in value by a gigantic number of zeroes.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Look for this one-time Standard boogeyman to be one of the most significant decks in the upcoming format.

As with all the decks in this article, the above Faeries deck is full of cards that aren't actually legal any more (for example [Ancestral Visions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Visions)). 

The rotation of [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) will affect the details of how the Five-Color Control (or however many) decks are constructed. So you won't see singleton copies of [Pact of Negation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) (I mean other than the fact that [Pact of Negation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) isn't actually legal any more), or main deck [Celestial Purge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Purge), like Guillaume Wafo-Tapa played it in Amsterdam.

The spirit of this deck will be either Esper- or Grixis-based (or both), most likely topping up on [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) and [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan). Consider Michael Jacob's version, which—well before the hubbub and hype around the card in Standard—played four copies of [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain)!
