Breakouts
=========



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on October 13, 2011 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











As I might have mentioned last week, I got to spend this past weekend in Nashville, TN; manning the commentary booth of the StarCityGames.com Opens there with Joey Pasco of Yo! MTG Taps fame. It was a great time and an honor from my perspective, and it seems like viewers, for the most part, appreciated our efforts.

Additionally and in other news, I got to see, first-hand, some of the breakout decks of the upcoming Standard. Here is how the Top 8 bore out (and more on that in a moment):

Brian Sondag's Wolf Run Green deck was the winner of the Open, and probably the actual best deck of the tournament to boot (not actually the same thing, and not always connected when the dust clears). For dozens of reasons I believe Wolf Run Green will *also* be the breakout deck of the 2011s this weekend, and that many of you at home who are undecided (or don't want to play some kind of [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) / [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) deck, because you despise countermagic or something) should take particular notice.

*How does this deck work?*

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td164_kessig.jpg)  
Wolf Run Green looks to be the true inheritor to [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut). And as these things often go, the son is in many ways an improved version of the father. No, Wolf Run Green does not actually have the overpowered land [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle); as such, it does not have the automatic wins facilitated *just* by possessing access to that card.

It is honestly poised to be better in every other way.

The first and most obvious improvement is in the structure of Wolf Run Green's mana. With [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut), you basically had a mono-green (or green splashing red) deck... that played double-digit *basic [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s* on top of four copies of [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) (a card that both enters the battlefield tapped and does not produce green mana), *and* some mix of generally more than four copies of [Evolving Wilds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evolving+Wilds) and [Terramorphic Expanse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terramorphic+Expanse).

Am I the only one who was really bothered by a green mana ramp deck with access to [Explore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Explore) / [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) / [Khalni Heart Expedition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Khalni+Heart+Expedition) / [Cultivate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cultivate) / "whatever it wanted" that was stuck playing such a land base (and in particular the [Evolving Wilds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evolving+Wilds) part)? Yes, you could get the dumbest wins, but good players would often be saddled with not-good limitations and inconsistencies in the early game, not to mention the uncomfortable prospect of *actually having* the critical sixth land—but its being a [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut) or [Terramorphic Expanse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terramorphic+Expanse)—on the (ideally) [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) turn.

Wolf Run Green does not have any of these limitations. It is very much a green ramp deck, and its mana base is constructed accordingly: lots of [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s; lots of lands that enter the battlefield nice and straight; great access to M12 and *Scars of Mirrodin*![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)/![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif) dual lands; and its specialty cards (all of which enter the battlefield untapped). Wolf Run Green—unlike its predecessor—is a soccer team where all the players are running in the same direction. Maybe they don't have the star finisher, but at least they are all aligned.

So what does it do?



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

There is the "regular" stuff. Yes, yes—this is a green acceleration / big mana deck. [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) and [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) (for [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)) help jump the natural progression of one-land-per-one-turn. At the high end, you hit [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine) and [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) (and the numbers there are carefully tuned). Creator Brian Sondag told me he started with four [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)s but pulled back to include the extra [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine) in respect for aggressive decks. 

"Anyway," Brian reminded us, "with [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) you have a virtual seven [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)s."



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

So what *about* that aggro matchup?

Sondag keyed in on the same something Hall of Famer Brian Kibler and many other green mages keyed in on: [Viridian Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Emissary). [Viridian Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Emissary) seems like a perfect card for a format that has been—to date—defined by fast beatdown decks like [Tempered Steel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) and Red Deck Wins versus slower Esper / Solar Flare variants. [Viridian Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Emissary) isn't a Human, so it can block [Stromkirk Noble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromkirk+Noble). It is very annoying to trade with, and painful to destroy with a burn spell. [Viridian Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Emissary) can be even worse for [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) decks. Make the opponent sacrifice a creature? You aren't going to get [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan); not with this little one in play.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Viridian+Emissary)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Emissary)  
Rounding out Wolf Run Green's capabilities, we have the versatile [Beast Within](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beast+Within). There have been many decks that have *tried* to play [Beast Within](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beast+Within) in the past, but few have been quite as optimized to take advantage of it as this one. Almost everything in Wolf Run Green is bigger than the resulting Beast, and [Slagstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slagstorm) lets you mop up whatever 3/3 side effects happen to be lying around post-[Beast Within](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beast+Within).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Beast+Within)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beast+Within)  
So what about that name? [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) is a mere two-of in this deck. The goal of this deck is to use [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) to search up at lest one [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) and (over time) all the copies of [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus). You already have a [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan), and coming back from being attacked by one of those isn't exactly easy. However, with the [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) pumping more and more lands onto the battlefield, you can easily threaten a lethal [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) with just one or two attacks.

One thing to consider when playing *against* Wolf Run Green: If it attacks with [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan), very likely you will be facing all four copies of [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus). Many decks (e.g., Solar Flare) have fewer than four relevant point removal cards. Therefore you have to plan very carefully or you *will* eventually lose to ten (or more) poison counters.

One of the most impressive comebacks I have ever seen was showcased by Christian Valenti, who managed to draw all three of his [Doom Blade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade)s, plus the [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) necessary to re-buy one of them, ultimately allowing him to handle all four infectious creature-lands. As tempting as it may be at the time to "gain tempo" (or something), consider very carefully before you throw a removal spell (that can take out an [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) later) into the graveyard.

Blue-Black Control
While Sondag's deck is unambiguously innovative and powerful, Caleb Durward's deck looks like one thing but is in fact something very different. While it looked like Brian's deck was tops, I can see the argument for Caleb's deck; it's so different and so efficient at what it does. At worst, Durward's darlings-killing Blue-Black Control deck was the second-best build of the Open.

*How does this deck work?*

It looks like many other Blue-Black Control decks... until you realize it doesn't play the same cards at all. Caleb completely eschewed [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) and [Forbidden Alchemy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy)—standouts and even four-ofs in so many other blue-black-based decks (Solar Flare variants and otherwise). Caleb sees those cards as having too little impact on the battlefield; he built his deck to crush aggro.

This deck features all different kinds of one-for-one removal spells—[Dismember](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismember), [Doom Blade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade), [Go for the Throat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Go+for+the+Throat)... even [Victim of Night](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Victim+of+Night), but not too many of any one kind—the main theme between them being instant-speed access and synergy with [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td164_snapAndFriends.jpg)  
Because he doesn't have [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) and [Forbidden Alchemy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy), Caleb has to lean on [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) more for card advantage (as you can see in other decks, Solar Flare might only play two copies). That said, blue decks still need to draw cards, and Caleb's can do so without [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) and [Forbidden Alchemy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy).

**Build Your Own Mulldrifter**

How does the returning [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination) work in this deck?

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td164_divination.jpg)  
Essentially [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination) is a two-for-one for only three mana; [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) is a two-for-one but costs five total mana. There are lots of arguments you can make for one draw spell or the other (they are both Constructed playable), but for the purposes of this deck it is more useful to focus on what is great about this card in this deck.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td164_vs1.jpg)  
Because [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination) draws two cards for just one more mana, you can theoretically make up the functionality of all four copies of [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) with only two [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination)s. On top of that, you can later give [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination) flashback with [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) in order to "build your own [Mulldrifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter)." Giving [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) or [Forbidden Alchemy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy) Flashback... um... *they already have flashback.* Sure, you can gain some mana efficiency... but when you apply a [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) to a [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination), you actually net some real extra card advantage.

As awesome as it is—or perhaps part of what helps to drive its awesomeness—Caleb's deck relies on some gimmicks to deal with other control decks. It looks like a Blue-Black Control deck, but really only has a couple of [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s and the one [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination) main. Caleb made Top 8 in part due to the tentative play of opponents trying to outwit [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s (where there weren't many). Additionally, [Nihil Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihil+Spellbomb) is a surprisingly strategic cantrip here. It is important in the main to fight [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites), opposing [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage)s, and other flashback cards, and it also plays baby [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice), helping to cycle through the deck while doing something useful.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Nihil+Spellbomb)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihil+Spellbomb)  
Still? Quite spicy.

Today's Solar Flare variants are pretty consistent with each other; however different builds have different unique functionality.

**[Phantasmal Image](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image) (1+)**  
[Phantasmal Image](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image) is a great combination with [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan). It only costs two mana and is therefore an eligible target for [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan), at which point it typically becomes yet another [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan). Remember, [Phantasmal Image](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image) is a heck of an answer to [Thrun, the Last Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrun%2C+the+Last+Troll); hexproof can't fool the "legend rule."

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Phantasmal+Image)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image)  
**[Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites), [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil)**  
 The black spells appear in some Solar Flare decks, but not in others. The two work well together ([Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) both of us; set up a fat man + [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites); etc.). [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) is typically better in attrition matches, and can speed you up by a turn or two (e.g., turn three [Forbidden Alchemy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy) burying [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan), [Phantasmal Image](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image), and [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites); turn four 12+ power on two active Titans.... must / is).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Unburial+Rites)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites)  
Liliana can be a dual-edged sword. She is good creature defense (but not against Red Deck Wins or [Viridian Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Emissary)); she is a primo control killer... except when the opponent is discarding dead creature removal or flashback spells. Often a game-breaker... but I have also seen mages pass without activating any of her abilities.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Liliana+of+the+Veil)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil)  
**[Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)**  
 Believe it or not, the Top 8 featured Solar Flare decks with fewer than four [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s. In fact, Jonathan Medina, for instance, had not a one!

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mana+Leak)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)  
Solar Flare is powerful (as is, admittedly, getting a [Phantasmal Image](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image) and explaining to the opponent that, yes, we actually have both [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan)s in play), but it can falter when it is not actually doing the best stuff in the game. For example, Titans are its top end but it can be out-Titaned by, say, Wolf Run Green's ability to set up the Nexus kill.

You can take away a deck's [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic), rotate its [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk), and deprive it of the best planeswalker in the history of cardboard... but apparently you can't keep a good Sword down.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td164_feastSword.jpg)  
Described by some as the detritus of the last round of extirpations and expirations, the White-Blue Blade deck still adheres to the core principles of Caw-Blade (just now minus the "Caw" part). [Blade Splicer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blade+Splicer) is a legitimate thematic inheritor to the tradition of [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) and [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk), being a white creature that goes and gets another artifact and/or creature (and in this case, both, actually) in a single-card package. We have seen many of the other cards in play in previous X-Blade variations throughout the last couple of months; for the most part they are still good, and for the better part—especially in this sea of Solar Flare black point removal and/or [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) big green guys—sticking a [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine) is still *game.*



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

If the early weeks of the Open Series are any indication, we will see more white-blue mages erring on the side of big Solar Flare haymakers than sliding in value creatures, but I for one was cheering for Brain Braun-Duin to make Top 8, to help punctuate the shape of the format and to showcase the concept of good cards still being good despite fluctuations in popularity elsewhere. Blade still has impressive effects on the planeswalker and finisher fronts, good answers to creature beatdown and burn, a good clock thanks to Swords... and often more than one of these at the same time.

Again, my guess is that we will see more [Seachrome Coast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seachrome+Coast)s supporting Solar Flare than [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine), but the old fave is still alive and kicking. *Ish*.

RDW did not follow up on [last week's](/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/163) impressive all-red finals, but Veccie still finished strong with what should continue to be a pillar of the format. We talked extensively about various sub-archetypes last time, so instead of rehashing all that, I'll end this were with a respectful word of caution.

If you feel, at this week's 2011s, that you are stabilizing against RDW, and are about to tap out for a big six to take over, look at your life total, ask your opponent how many cards he or she has, refer to your own mana and possible countermagic access... and think (at least) twice before doing anything rash. Because I saw Nick drop the solo [Traitorous Blood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Traitorous+Blood) squarely into the center of the table against a tapped-out [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan) in the feature match area, and it was absolutely filthy. If memory serves, his opponent had attacked the previous turn with [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura). Respeck.
