The Players Tour Pioneer Metagame Primer
========================================



 Posted in **Competitive Gaming**
 on January 29, 2020 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_FrankKarsten.jpg)
By Frank Karsten











For the upcoming Players Tour events in [Nagoya](https://magic.gg/events/players-tour-asia-pacific-series-1), [Brussels](https://magic.gg/events/players-tour-europe-series-1), and [Phoenix](https://magic.gg/events/players-tour-americas-series-1), skilled competitors will bring their best Pioneer decks. [Pioneer is a new, nonrotating format featuring cards from*Return to Ravnica*and forward](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/news/announcing-pioneer-format-2019-10-21), and the Players Tour will be the first premier event after the [initial wave of bans](https://magic.wizards.com/en/game-info/gameplay/formats/pioneer) and the release of *Theros Beyond Death*. Ahead of the next two weekends of events, let's take a broad look at what you can expect from the Pioneer metagame at the Players Tour.


Overall, I expect a lot of diversity. In [published Magic Online decklists](https://magic.wizards.com/en/content/deck-lists-magic-online-products-game-info), Pioneer Leagues regularly showcase over 50 different 5-0 decks, suggesting a healthy metagame with many viable strategies. What's more, in Preliminary and Challenge events, the top finishers generally play a wide variety of decks.


Nevertheless, some decks appear more often than others. For our purposes I selected ten of the most popular archetypes based on published decklists and what I have faced in Leagues, with a representative decklist for each. I grouped the decks into five categories (Aggro decks, Midrange decks, Control decks, Engine decks, and Combo decks) with two decks each. Join me on a journey through the Pioneer landscape!


Aggro Decks
===========


I consider a deck an aggro deck when it has a low mana curve and multiple one-drops that are best at attacking. Aggro decks may contain disruptive elements, and this is indeed the case for both Mono-Black Aggro and Azorius Spirits.

Mono-Black Aggro exploits the most efficient interactive spells in the format—[Fatal Push](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fatal+Push) and [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize)—in a shell that can put a lot of pressure on the opponent. [Bloodsoaked Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodsoaked+Champion) and [Scrapheap Scrounger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scrapheap+Scrounger) aren't made for blocking, after all. If the board stalls out, then impactful fliers like [Rankle, Master of Pranks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rankle%2C+Master+of+Pranks) can still swoop in for the kill.


The main reason for staying mono-color is that this allows you to fit 4 [Mutavault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault) and 4 [Castle Locthwain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Castle+Locthwain) into the mana base. These lands provide an excellent way to mitigate mana flood and offer some mid-game staying power. I expect that Mono-Black Aggro will be one of the more popular decks at the Players Tour.

Azorius Spirits is fairly similar to the Modern version. The plan is to curve out with fliers, to use [Supreme Phantom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Phantom) and [Empyrean Eagle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empyrean+Eagle) (the so-called Spirit "lords") to boost their power, and to race the opponent with evasive creatures. The best card in the deck overall is arguably [Spell Queller](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Queller), as it provides interaction, pressure, and the right creature type all in a single package.


There are Bant versions that splash for [Collected Company](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Collected+Company), but the straight-up Azorius version has better mana, can run a few [Mutavault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault)s (which is also a Spirit), and is more popular overall. But both Bant and Azorius Spirits can play a good flash game on their opponent's turns. Opponents will have to commit without knowing whether you are holding [Spell Queller](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Queller), [Rattlechains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rattlechains), or [Nebelgast Herald](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nebelgast+Herald), and they could easily play around the wrong cards. After sideboard, when opponents also have to be mindful of [Settle the Wreckage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Settle+the+Wreckage), it is even more of a nightmare to play against Spirit decks.


Midrange Decks
==============


Midrange decks can switch into an aggro or control role depending on the matchup. Their mana curves are generally higher than aggro decks, with fewer early drops and more four or five mana creatures.

Sometimes called "Chonky Red" this deck aims to burn opposing creatures early on, cement a board advantage with something like [Goblin Rabblemaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Rabblemaster) when the time is right, and overpower the opponent with [Glorybringer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glorybringer). Although the deck is capable of aggressive draws, the first red creature often won't appear until turn three. (In about two-thirds of the games there may be an early [Soul-Scar Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul-Scar+Mage) or [Kari Zev, Skyship Raider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kari+Zev%2C+Skyship+Raider), but that's hardly reliable.) So don't mistake this for a fast burn deck that is weak to life gain spells. Instead, I view it as more of a midrange deck.


I expect that Big Red will be one of the more popular decks at the Players Tour. It will surely be one of the decks that everyone will be ready for and will have tested against.

The namesake cards are [Niv-Mizzet Reborn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Niv-Mizzet+Reborn) and [Bring to Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light), both fueled by a five-color mana base. With access to the best cards in every color pair, the dream is to use [Sylvan Caryatid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Caryatid) to ramp into a turn-four [Niv-Mizzet Reborn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Niv-Mizzet+Reborn) and to grab, say, [Teferi, Time Raveler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Time+Raveler); [Bring to Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light); [Dreadbore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dreadbore); and [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay) from your top ten cards. That should be enough to take over the game.


Niv to Light kind of straddles in between midrange and control, as the above list has only a single [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) in the main deck. However, I labeled the archetype as a control deck because the card [Bring to Light](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) can reliably find the sweeper against aggro decks, because I've seen builds with other sweepers, and because the card [Niv-Mizzet Reborn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Niv-Mizzet+Reborn) goes over the top of midrange decks like Big Red.


I expect that Niv to Light will be one of the more popular decks at the Players Tour. However, players are discovering effective ways to combat the deck. Attacking their mana base is one of them. For example, [Self-Inflicted Wound](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Self-Inflicted+Wound)s from the sideboard are rising in popularity because without [Sylvan Caryatid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Caryatid), Niv to Light players may be unable to cast their spells.

Azorius Control in Pioneer does basically the same as in every other format. It plans to say "no" to spells with [Sinister Sabotage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sinister+Sabotage), "no" to creatures with [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict), and eventually locks up the game with a planeswalker. Once the deck gets to cast [Dig Through Time](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time) or [Sphinx's Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) on a stable board and/or stick [Teferi, Hero of Dominaria](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Hero+of+Dominaria) for multiple turns, victory ultimately becomes inevitable.


A new addition from *Theros Beyond Death* is [Dream Trawler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dream+Trawler) in the sideboard. As a mix between [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) and [Prognostic Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prognostic+Sphinx), it is the perfect win condition that control players have been dreaming of.

The plan of Golgari Soulflayer is to use [Gather the Pack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gather+the+Pack) or [Grisly Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grisly+Salvage) to enable a two-mana [Soulflayer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soulflayer) with a ridiculous set of abilities. By delving [Zetalpa, Primal Dawn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zetalpa%2C+Primal+Dawn), [Gifted Aetherborn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifted+Aetherborn), [Sylvan Caryatid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Caryatid), and [Questing Beast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Questing+Beast), you produce a 4/4 with flying, double strike, vigilance, trample, indestructible, deathtouch, haste, hexproof, and lifelink. Such a creature can come down as early as turn three. Good luck beating that.


The deck is weak to cards like [Leyline of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+the+Void) in post-sideboarded games, but if opponents don't have an answer to the graveyard and don't have a way to counter or discard [Soulflayer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soulflayer), then usually they will get flayed.

The plan of Mono-Green Ramp is to use a variety of mana ramp creatures, mana ramp spells, and mana ramp lands to help cast heavy-hitting spells far earlier than intended. This deck can consistently cast [Ugin, the Spirit Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ugin%2C+the+Spirit+Dragon) on turn five and/or [Ulamog, the Ceaseless Hunger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ulamog%2C+the+Ceaseless+Hunger) on turn six.


Since the release of *Theros Beyond Death*, there are new versions popping up that splash blue for [Uro, Titan of Nature's Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Uro%2C+Titan+of+Nature%27s+Wrath). It's basically an [Arboreal Grazer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arboreal+Grazer) with upside, which is exactly what the deck is looking for. In any case, Mono-Green and Simic can be a little slow out of the gates, but their late-game is one of the most powerful in the format.

[Heliod, Sun-Crowned](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heliod%2C+Sun-Crowned) plus [Walking Ballista](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Walking+Ballista) is a two-card combo: If you give a 2/2 Ballista lifelink and ping your opponent, then Heliod will put back the +1/+1 counter. This way, you can keep pinging as often as you like.


The combo is worse than [Saheeli Rai](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saheeli+Rai) + [Felidar Guardian](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Felidar+Guardian) (which is banned in Pioneer) because normally you don't have the mana to both build a 2/2 Ballista *and* to give it lifelink on turn four. But even as a turn-five combo, it is powerful.


The big question is where it fits. Personally, I believe a [Hardened Scales](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hardened+Scales) shell is one of the best homes. [Hardened Scales](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hardened+Scales) is already a competitive strategy in Pioneer, and the enchantment synergizes nicely with both [Walking Ballista](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Walking+Ballista) and Heliod. This way, [Walking Ballista](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Walking+Ballista) and Heliod become reasonable Magic cards by themselves when you don't draw the other combo piece, and I believe that will be a key aspect to making this combo work.

Lotus Storm decks, which aim to copy [Lotus Field](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Field) with [Thespian's Stage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thespian%27s+Stage), have been around in competitive Pioneer for a while. With [Lotus Field](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Field) on the table, [Hidden Strings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hidden+Strings) and [Pore Over the Pages](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pore+Over+the+Pages) turn into rituals, enabling a powerful combo turn. But the new addition of [Underworld Breach](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Breach)—effectively a two-mana [Yawgmoth's Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will) that allows you to keep recasting the same spell multiple times—may put [Hidden Strings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hidden+Strings) decks over the top.


The key combo is with [Chronic Flooding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chronic+Flooding). If you control [Underworld Breach](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Breach) and [Chronic Flooding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chronic+Flooding), then you can repeatedly cast [Hidden Strings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hidden+Strings) to mill your entire deck. After all, [Hidden Strings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hidden+Strings) untaps lands to pay for its own mana cost, and the trigger on [Chronic Flooding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chronic+Flooding) pays for escape. This combo doesn't even require [Lotus Field](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Field). After milling your entire deck, the game is won by casting [Thassa's Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thassa%27s+Oracle) from the graveyard.


Given the potential power of this combo, I expect that the best deck brewers will be trying to break the format by finding the best build. For example, should there be [Lotus Field](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Field)s? Does the deck need more self-mill? What is the best sideboard plan to beat [Damping Sphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damping+Sphere) and [Rest in Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) in Games 2 and 3? Whoever can find the right answers to these questions might just crush the Players Tour.


Conclusion
==========


This article provided a broad look at the Pioneer metagame by going over the most popular archetypes from recent *Magic Online* events, but it merely represents the tip of the iceberg. There are many other competitively viable decks in Pioneer: for example, Mono-Black Vampires, Izzet Ensoul, Izzet Phoenix, Gruul Aggro, Jeskai Ascendancy, and many more. I have faced a huge variety of decks on *Magic Online* myself, and I expect that this diversity will shine through at the Players Tour. I can't wait to see what will end up on top of the standings, as the format is still very fresh.
