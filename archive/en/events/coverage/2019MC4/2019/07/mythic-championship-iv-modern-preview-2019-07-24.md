Mythic Championship IV Modern Preview
=====================================



 Posted in **Event Coverage**
 on July 24, 2019 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_FrankKarsten.jpg)
By Frank Karsten











Mythic Championship IV starts on Friday, July 26, in Barcelona, Spain. Friday and Saturday will both begin with three mechanically rich rounds of *Modern Horizons* Booster Draft, but I'm particularly excited about the five rounds of Modern that will follow each afternoon.


The goal of this article is to set the stage for fans who watched [Mythic Championship II in London](https://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/2019MC2/mythic-championship-ii-day-1-metagame-breakdown-2019-04-26) but haven't kept up with the Modern metagame developments since then. After reading this article, you should be well equipped as a viewer going into the Mythic Championship IV broadcast.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2019/events/2019MC4/MC2-Trophy-Shot-850x500.jpg)  
*Eli Loveman, champion of Mythic Championship II. He emerged victorious with Humans as his Modern deck.*


At Mythic Championship II in London, where [the new mulligan rule](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/news/london-mulligan-2019-06-03) was favorably tested, [the most popular decks](https://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/2019MC2/mythic-championship-ii-day-1-metagame-breakdown-2019-04-26) were Tron, Izzet Phoenix, Humans, Blue-White Control, and [Dredge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dredge), in that order. At Mythic Championship IV in Barcelona, these five decks will surely form a large part of the metagame again—but they will be using newly printed cards, and the rest of the metagame has changed considerably.


Three New Sets Were Added; One Card Was Banned
==============================================


*War of the Spark* and *Modern Horizons* rank among the two most impactful set releases in the entire history of the format, and *Core Set 2020* has added several relevant cards as well. As none of these three sets were available for MC II, it's clear that Modern at MC IV will look vastly different.


What's more, the exact Modern format for Barcelona is relatively uncharted because two weeks ago, [Bridge from Below](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) was [banned](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/news/july-8-2019-banned-and-restricted-announcement-2019-07-08).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hogaak%2C+Arisen+Necropolis)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hogaak%2C+Arisen+Necropolis)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Vengevine)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Bridge+from+Below)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below)
This ban was warranted because since the release of *Modern Horizons*, the format had been warped by Hogaak BridgeVine—a deck that would use self-mill or discard effects to exploit Hogaak, [Vengevine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine), and [Bridge from Below](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below). Using these cards, the deck could easily get 10 power in play on turn two and then use [Altar Of Dementia](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Altar+Of+Dementia) to mill out the opponent on turn three. Every deck was forced to load up on anti-graveyard cards to compete, which was problematic for the health of the metagame, and R&D took action.


I won't analyze the differences between Modern before the ban and Modern after the ban—my focus will be on the differences between MC II to MC IV—but what you should know is that the Modern format for Barcelona is very fresh. It began in earnest only two weeks ago.


What Modern Archetypes Have Found Success in This Format?
=========================================================


To provide an overview of successful Modern decklists in the two weeks since the ban of [Bridge from Below](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below), I collected the decklists from [the July 12 MTGO league](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-standings/modern-league-2019-07-12), [the Warsaw MCQ](https://www.channelfireball.com/top-8-decklists-of-the-modern-mcq-at-magicfest-krakow/), [several StarCityGames.com (SCG) Invitational Qualifiers](http://www.starcitygames.com/decks/results/end_date/2019-07-22/start_date/2019-06-22/format/28/), [the Modern Challenge](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-standings/modern-challenge-2019-07-14), [the SCG Classic in Worcester](https://www.starcitygames.com/events/140718_worcester.html), [the July 16 MTGO league](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-standings/modern-league-2019-07-16), [the July 19 MTGO league](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-standings/modern-league-2019-07-19), [the Modern MOCS](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-standings/modern-mocs-2019-07-17-0), and [the SCG Team Open in Philadelphia](http://www.starcitygames.com/events/200719_philadelphia.html).


I then used a point system to rank decks based on popularity and performance. Specifically, I gave each deck a number of points equal to its number of match wins minus its number of match losses. The resulting numbers don't translate directly to a deck's score-weighted metagame percentage because *Magic Online* leagues publish all 5-0 decks that are at least 20 cards different from each other. But they do adequately show how often decks appear in published data, weighted by performance.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2019/events/2019MC4/MCIV_Metagame_Decks.jpg)


Which Major Decks Got Better?
=============================


As the above graphic shows, almost every deck got relevant upgrades in recent months. But from the established, top-tier Modern decks, I would pick four winners in particular.

**Izzet Phoenix**: Earlier this year, Izzet Phoenix was already one of the decks to beat. Now, it picked up [Aria of Flame](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aria+of+Flame) as an alternative win condition that circumvents both creature removal and anti-graveyard cards. [Finale of Promise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finale+of+Promise) is also a strong addition because it returns [Arclight Phoenix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arclight+Phoenix) by itself. The rest of the new cards are mostly one-ofs or sideboard options, but thanks to [Faithless Looting](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faithless+Looting), [Manamorphose](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose), [Thought Scour](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thought+Scour), and [Serum Visions](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serum+Visions), they'll be easy to find.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Narset%2C+Parter+of+Veils)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narset%2C+Parter+of+Veils)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Force+of+Negation)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Negation)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Teferi%2C+Time+Raveler)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Time+Raveler)
**Blue-White Control**: Control players could already say ["no" to spells](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) and ["no" to creatures](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict). *War of the Spark* extends this range. [Narset, Parter of Veils](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narset%2C+Parter+of+Veils) says "no" to drawing extra cards, which is huge when [Faithless Looting](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faithless+Looting) and [Serum Visions](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serum+Visions) are heavily played, and [Teferi, Time Raveler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Time+Raveler) says "no" to casting spells while things are resolving, which nullifies [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light), [Finale of Promise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finale+of+Promise), [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf)'s cascade, and [Rift Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt)'s suspend. Since Blue-White Control also benefits from [Force of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Negation) and from the open decklist policy at Mythic Championships, I expect it to do well.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Karn%2C+the+Great+Creator)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn%2C+the+Great+Creator)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ugin%2C+the+Ineffable)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ugin%2C+the+Ineffable)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Blast+Zone)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blast+Zone)
**Eldrazi Tron**: [Karn, the Great Creator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn%2C+the+Great+Creator) is arguably *War of the Spark*'s most important addition to Modern. Karn can fetch such cards as [Mycosynth Lattice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mycosynth+Lattice) for an instant win on an empty board, [Ensnaring Bridge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ensnaring+Bridge) to lock down attackers, or [Grafdigger's Cage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grafdigger%27s+Cage) to stop graveyard recursion. While Karn has found a home in a variety of decks, it is at its best when you can ramp into [Mycosynth Lattice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mycosynth+Lattice) with [Urza's Mine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Mine), [Urza's Power Plant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Power+Plant), and [Urza's Tower](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Tower), and protect Karn with blockers like [Matter Reshaper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Matter+Reshaper) and [Thought-Knot Seer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thought-Knot+Seer). This is exactly why we've seen a resurgence of Eldrazi Tron, which also picked up [Ugin, the Ineffable](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ugin%2C+the+Ineffable) and [Blast Zone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blast+Zone) as minor additions.

* [![Weather the Storm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Weather+the+Storm)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Weather+the+Storm)

**Jund**: [Wrenn and Six](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrenn+and+Six) does it all. It answers [Noble Hierarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch) and [Champion of the Parish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish). It returns [Bloodstained Mire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodstained+Mire) to help hit your land drops or to break the discard symmetry on [Liliana of the Veil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil). It returns [Nurturing Peatland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nurturing+Peatland) to help you grind in a long game. And the threat of its ultimate demands an answer from control players. All in all, [Wrenn and Six](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrenn+and+Six) is a great reason to leave behind The Rock and to pick up Jund. In addition, the deck benefits from effective new creatures and powerful new sideboard options.


Which Major Decks Got Less Viable?
==================================


At Mythic Championship II in London, the sixth and seventh [most popular decks](https://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/2019MC2/day-2-metagame-breakdown-2019-04-27) were [Hardened Scales](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hardened+Scales) and Grixis Shadow. Yet neither of them were present in the table pictured above. So, what happened?


It pains me to say this, given that I've been an [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) aficionado for years, but [Hardened Scales](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hardened+Scales) and Affinity have both gotten less viable in recent months. They gained nothing of relevance while the rest of the format improved considerably. To make matters even worse, Modern now features an abundance of [Karn, Scion of Urza](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karn%2C+Scion+of+Urza) in main decks along with [Collector Ouphe](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Collector+Ouphe) or [Force of Vigor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Vigor) in sideboards. As long as these new cards remain popular, it's hard to succeed with [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager).


Grixis Shadow, which aims to stay below 13 life, was hurt a little bit by the adoption of [Aria of Flame](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aria+of+Flame) in Izzet Phoenix. But the prime reason for its fall in popularity was the introduction of [Ranger-Captain of Eos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger-Captain+of+Eos). The new card fetches [Death's Shadow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death%27s+Shadow) and cripples [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), so it encouraged players to move toward white versions (usually Esper or Mardu) instead.

Even without [Bridge from Below](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below), [Hogaak, Arisen Necropolis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hogaak%2C+Arisen+Necropolis) is a very powerful card. In the right deck, it's a zero-mana 8/8 trampler, so you can be sure that Mythic Championship competitors will try to break it. There is no consensus yet on the best configuration, and debates will rage over the relative merits of [Satyr Wayfinder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Satyr+Wayfinder), [Hedron Crab](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hedron+Crab), and [Stinkweed Imp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stinkweed+Imp).


In any case, most lists are capable of playing [Bloodstained Mire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodstained+Mire) and [Carrion Feeder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Carrion+Feeder) on turn one, followed by [Stitcher's Supplier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stitcher%27s+Supplier) and [Faithless Looting](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faithless+Looting) on turn two. You could then cast Hogaak from the graveyard, trigger [Vengevine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine), and pass with 14 power on the battlefield and a potentially lethal attack on turn three. Even [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) might come too late against that start.

When you have Urza, High Lord Artificer and [Thopter Foundry](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thopter+Foundry) on the battlefield, you can repeatedly tap [Sword of the Meek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+the+Meek) for mana and sacrifice it to create a Thopter, returning the Sword. This produces a loop that generates infinite Thopters, infinite life, infinite mana, and infinite Urza activations. With [Goblin Engineer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Engineer), [Whir of Invention](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whir+of+Invention), and cheap artifact cantrips to assemble the key pieces, it's a powerful and popular strategy.


As a note: this archetype has sometimes been referred to as Grixis Urza, but I opted for a different name to encompass the extremely similar versions that splash white for [Teferi, Time Raveler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Time+Raveler).

To my recollection, no other deck in Modern has been able to kill on turn one or two with the rate that Neobrand can, especially with the London mulligan rule. A five-card opening hand of [Chancellor of the Tangle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chancellor+of+the+Tangle), [Allosaurus Rider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Allosaurus+Rider), [Allosaurus Rider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Allosaurus+Rider), [Neoform](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Neoform), and [Botanical Sanctum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Botanical+Sanctum) will get [Griselbrand](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Griselbrand) onto the battlefield on turn one. Then, you could chain multiple [Nourishing Shoal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nourishing+Shoal)s to draw your entire deck and eventually win with [Laboratory Maniac](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Laboratory+Maniac). Although the deck is vulnerable to disruption like [Grafdigger's Cage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grafdigger%27s+Cage) or [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation), it is scarily fast.


This concludes my overview of the most prominent new decks, but the possibilities in Modern are nearly endless. How about [Mystic Forge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Forge) in Dice Factory? [Spectral Sailor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Sailor) in Blue-White Spirits? [Hexdrinker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hexdrinker) in The Rock? [Smiting Helix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smiting+Helix) in Mardu Pyromancer? The list goes on, and I can't wait to see what the Mythic Championship IV competitors will bring to the table.
