Blinding Sunrise, *Dark Ascension*
==================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on February 9, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






The brand spanking new set? *Dark Ascension*.


The locale-to-be? Paradise (aka "Hawaii").


The event? Pro Tour *Dark Ascension*.


I'd sort of like to say that Pro Tour *Dark Ascension* will be the Constructed debut of *Dark Ascension*, but the folks at Star City Games just had a Standard Open event that might give us an idea of what new cards will be high impact at this weekend's exciting Pro Tour.


Let's look at some of *Dark Ascension*'s hits and where they can contribute in Standard decks both established and new.

The printing of [Strangleroot Geist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strangleroot+Geist) has helped to spawn a whole new (well new to the current Standard, anyway) archetype of mono-green aggro. Basically, we have a deck that can start fast on [Llanowar Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) or [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) and ramp into turn-two [Dungrove Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)... or, if it misses the turn-one accelerator, "just" smash in with one of the most exciting green two-drops in years (the aforementioned [Strangleroot Geist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strangleroot+Geist)).


![Strangleroot Geist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Strangleroot+Geist)  
You'll note the deck's lands are all [Forest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s (for [Dungrove Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)), and it doesn't compromise for even one [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus), [Kessig Wolf Run](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run), or red source. So no [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) engine. Instead, we have more offense, more "get there"-ness, in a [Bellowing Tanglewurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bellowing+Tanglewurm).


This green packs the full four copies of [Sword of War and Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+War+and+Peace) in the main deck, a clear nod to the color composition of the expected enemy, and an unambiguous eye toward racing.



> 
> Aside: *How do I cast [Glissa, the Traitor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glissa%2C+the+Traitor)?*
> 
> 
> Todd's SCG Open Top 8 deck packs 24 [Forest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s and no [Swamp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swamp)s...
> 
> 
> But it also plays a copy of [Glissa, the Traitor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glissa%2C+the+Traitor). In fact, it can theoretically "go off" by combining [Ratchet Bomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ratchet+Bomb) and [Glissa, the Traitor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glissa%2C+the+Traitor).
> 
> 
> ... *But with no [Swamp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swamp)s, how do I cast the darn thing?*
> 
> 
> Todd's deck has not only four copies of [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) (which can tap for black mana) but [Green Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) (which can go and get not only [Glissa, the Traitor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glissa%2C+the+Traitor), but other singleton bullets like [Acidic Slime](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Acidic+Slime)). And that's how you cast that [Glissa, the Traitor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glissa%2C+the+Traitor)!

For that matter, what about just two *mana* but for *zero* more cards?

Yeah.


Pretty cool new card.

[Havengul Lich](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Havengul+Lich), here, combines with [Heartless Summoning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartless+Summoning) to build an engine. One of them increases your access, the other makes your extra cards more affordable.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Havengul Lich | Heartless Summoning |

Or, you can get your board wiped but then cast all your creatures *and your opponent's creatures* with this guy. Pretty cool.


[Havengul Lich](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Havengul+Lich)—new functionality or no—is a two-of in Ali's deck; his new-card four-of of [Tragic Slip](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tragic+Slip) is something else (and will probably prove one of the most commonly played cards in the set, across many decks). [Tragic Slip](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tragic+Slip) is a great new removal card that happens to be absurd (or just highly relevant) in the format. It gives you a way to fight first-turn [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) or [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets), but is also a great card that can deal with huge threats later in the game, given a little bit of setup. Probably won't come up very often, but I like how you can make a seemingly indestructible [Blightsteel Colossus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightsteel+Colossus) fall to its doom on a banana peel right into a cemetery plot.

White-Blue Humans can explode out the gates with a first-turn [Champion of the Parish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish) and commands some really powerful threats in [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure) and [Hero of Bladehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold) (which, incidentally, combines nicely with both [Champion of the Parish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish) and [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure)). The strategy is consistent and redundant. [Doomed Traveler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomed+Traveler) isn't as offensively dominating as [Champion of the Parish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish) on turn one, but it is a huge problem for control decks. [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader) on turn three is awfully awful following a turn-two [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure), pulling it out of [Shock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock) range and challenging to attack for a million.

But the new *Dark Ascension* card in question is [Thalia, Guardian of Thraben](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thalia%2C+Guardian+of+Thraben).


![Thalia, Guardian of Thraben](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Thalia%2C+Guardian+of+Thraben)  
In Standard, a turn-two Thalia is extremely annoying for some decks. Kelso's White-Blue Humans itself is not bothered particularly much. Its curve is low and the vast majority of Kelso's cards are creatures anyway.


But other guys?


Imagine being on the draw, with no one-mana removal spell, and facing down a turn-two Thalia. No, it isn't "game over," but if you were planning to go [Think Twice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) or [Desperate Ravings](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desperate+Ravings) on the second turn... well, you have to pick something else to do. Thalia makes it annoying to play even relatively cheap library manipulation. And what if your answer is a [Doom Blade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade)? Two-mana point removal is now three-mana point removal.


Thalia is just super solid, inexpensive (both in terms of mana cost and in terms of fitting a fairly efficient two-drop into your beatdown deck), and will probably be all over this weekend's coverage.

So this is the umpteenth control deck by Shaheen Soorani featured in Top Decks over the years. Soorani is typically a white-blue man, but dips into a little black for Sorin in this build. Sorin and removal, that is.


![Curse of Death's Hold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold)  
Interesting thing you might not have noticed: One [Curse of Death's Hold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold) not only contains lots of the cards we have talked about today—from turn-one attack drops or accelerators to powerful new utility threats like [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls)—*but also someone else's Sorin.* [Curse of Death's Hold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold) stops someone else's 1/1 tokens. You can keep the opponent from profiting with the –6 via Karn, your own Sorin, or [Ratchet Bomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ratchet+Bomb).


Speaking of [Ratchet Bomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ratchet+Bomb), it will be one of the most important answer cards in *Dark Ascension* Standard. Not only is it just a good defensive card, what with everything from solid one-drops to tons of tokens ([Ratchet Bomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ratchet+Bomb) for zero being fast as well as efficient) to [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) (itself both a one and a zero), but [Ratchet Bomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ratchet+Bomb) can gobble up the all-important [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure) (or other awesome equivalent). Not only is a [Crusade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crusade) effect potent when you are talking about having a ton of small guys in play, but one [Honor of the Pure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure) fully counteracts one [Curse of Death's Hold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold). And it's obvious Shaheen's deck is more than willing to pay five mana (that is, "much more") for something to that effect.


![Ratchet Bomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ratchet+Bomb)  
How about Sorin himself?


There is some very solid synergy in the planeswalkers department of this deck. It is almost like Jace, Elspeth, and Ajani got the band back together! Any planeswalkers can do productive things for no mana, so when you have more than one in play at any time, over time you can do lots of things while using your actual mana for other productive things. Meanwhile, your opponent is attacking them (or trying to) and you are—in addition to doing sometimes flashy and sometimes simply useful things with your planeswalkers—leveling them up for some eventual fireworks.


And sometimes they hold hands all nice-like and really work together well.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td181_elso.jpg)  
A great example is the *Dark Ascension* poster child (literally!) in question and [Elspeth Tirel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth+Tirel). [Sorin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sorin%2C+Lord+of+Innistrad) makes 1/1 creatures that pre-load the board for Elspeth. Tokens and emblems (two of Sorin's three) work nicely with Elspeth's –5, while both of them together can create a smokescreen of tokens to defend any and all planeswalkers while the opponent has to commit more and more resources into an eventual [Day of Judgment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) or other shenanigans.

And really, hiding behind a ton of tiny guys while you get ready to ultimate Karn is about the most fun you can have in Standard!


As for *Dark Ascension* cards to be watched for this weekend—there are lots.


And archetypes? There are plenty of different and engaging decks. We have aggro in White-Blue Humans or Mono-Green [Strangleroot Geist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strangleroot+Geist), Aggro-Control in the Delver decks, and full-on control like Shaheen's deck. The format has plenty of mid-range and board-control cards. And while it might not fit a strict model, [Heartless Summoning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartless+Summoning) feels kinda "combo" to me (certainly you start to key on that "dirty feeling" come a quick [Heartless Summoning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heartless+Summoning)).
