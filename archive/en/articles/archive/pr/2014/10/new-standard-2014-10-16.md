### PERILOUS RESEARCH


A New Standard
==============



![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)

Jacob Van Lunen

Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published


October 16, 2014
 










Standard has rapidly changed in the wake of [Pro Tour *Khans of Tarkir*](http://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/ptktk). The Green Devotion decks that many assumed would be dominating the new Standard environment have been put on the back burner in the face of [Mantis Rider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mantis+Rider)s that they can never race. Today, we'll be taking a look at the Standard decklists from last weekend's *Magic Online* PTQ and discussing the most successful Standard strategies from Pro Tour *Khans of Tarkir*.


The new Standard is still anyone's game. The Pro Tour showcased one of the most diverse and exciting Standard formats in recent memory. There's a lot of new information to be digested from last week's events. Let's take a look at the decklists that I feel do the best job of illustrating what we can expect to see in upcoming Standard events:


Control
=======


Control decks weren't expected to be a huge part of the field at the Pro Tour, but the few control decks that showed up did exceptionally well. Let's take a look!

Ivan Floch followed up [his Pro Tour victory](http://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/ptm15) with another Pro Tour Top 8. Floch's decklist is, without a doubt, the leanest control strategy we'll be looking at today. The deck aims to control the board with removal and countermagic, and it's only truly able to pull itself ahead with [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time). [Prognostic Sphinx](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prognostic+Sphinx) proves itself to be the win condition of choice for control decks in the new format.

The Mad Genius, Adrian Sullivan, also brought a control deck to the table last weekend. But his version is much less interested in winning the game, and much more interested in controlling the board. In fact, there isn't a single card in Sullivan's deck that's actually capable of winning the game on its own. Instead, he's relying on stealing his opponent's creatures with [Ashiok, Nightmare Weaver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashiok%2C+Nightmare+Weaver) or the ultimate on [Liliana Vess](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+Vess). Games can go long with a deck like this. And there are a few copies of [Prognostic Sphinx](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prognostic+Sphinx) in the board for matchups involving [Mantis Rider](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mantis+Rider), or Game Two or Three when the clock is running lower than expected.

Midrange
========


Midrange strategies were expected to be the biggest players at Pro Tour *Khans of Tarkir* and there were plenty of them in the field. The midrange decks had mixed results, though. Abzan strategies generally did very well while devotion decks seemed to be hated out of the format. As it stands, most players' decks are playing fewer cards that actually do something than cards that answer something or ramp into things. As a result, we need big cards that do more than trade one-for-one. Cards like [Wingmate Roc](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wingmate+Roc); [Elspeth, Sun's Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion); [Ajani, Mentor of Heroes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani%2C+Mentor+of+Heroes); [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino); and [Doomwake Giant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomwake+Giant) seem to be the best top-end choices for successful midrange decks in the new Standard. Let's take a look at some midrange strategies!

Aggro
=====


Aggressive red strategies picked up some nice additions with *Khans of Tarkir*. Sure, [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino) may be a rough card for the aggressive decks, but there's enough power and overwhelming potential from the token mechanics and heroic that the aggressive decks will remain ready and willing to destroy people who try to go over the top of the midrange decks with too much zeal. Let's take a look at the aggro strategies of the new Standard!

Let's find our combo aggressively and end games fast! The [ChannelFireball](http://www.ChannelFireball.com/) version of the [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy) combo seems more consistent and less resilient than the other versions from the Pro Tour. After sideboarding, the deck can drop uncounterable threats like [Surrak Dragonclaw](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Surrak+Dragonclaw) and it might catch opponents without a card that kills it in a post-boarded game. The amount of hate and bad matchups for this deck will oscillate in the coming months and there will certainly be a few weeks where the deck is extremely well positioned. Stay vigilant!
