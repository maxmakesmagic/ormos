Two Cards To Bring Them All (And In Fairness Bind Them)
=======================================================



 Posted in **Serious Fun**
 on January 31, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_adamstyborski.jpg)
By Adam Styborski




 Stybs has played Magic the world over, writing and drafting as part of the event coverage team and slinging Commander everywhere his decks will fit. 







Prerelease! Hurrah!


Launch Party! Woo!


*Awesome things have happened, are happening, and will happen soon. You should go. I did, I will, and I promise it's super sweet.*


*Dark Ascension* is about to be released everywhere, dropping a rallying cry for wannabe slayers and all-too-monstrous monsters to battle. Getting mauled at the Prerelease—which happens at *every* Prerelease for me—was a total blast. I'm sure you have your own stories to share too.


This week isn't about the glamorous occurrences of Sealed combat, though (although the glorious paces of such battle, if so desired, is something you'll see hit upon in [Steve Sadin's article today](/en/articles/archive/limited-information/sealed-dark-2012-01-31)). In fact, if you were here last week you'll probably chime in about déjà vu.


I'm going to talk about Commander again, but not the Commander you're thinking of.


What I mean is this: consider the [already-well-discussed-everywhere](/en/articles/archive/latest-developments/cage-match-2012-01-27) [Grafdigger's Cage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grafdigger%27s+Cage) from *Dark Ascension*. It does a few things:



> 
> * Stops cards like [Tooth and Nail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail), [Green Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith), [Zombify](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zombify), and [Living Death](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Death)
> * Stops cards like [Yawgmoth's Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will), those with flashback, and (most unfortunately) [Panglacial Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Panglacial+Wurm)
> 



![Grafdigger's Cage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Grafdigger%27s+Cage)

 

Why are these important? Aren't the restrictions only relevant for Standard, Modern, and other tournament formats?


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf180_grafdigger.jpg)[Grafdigger's Cage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grafdigger%27s+Cage) | Art by [Daniel Ljunggren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22Daniel+Ljunggren%22%5D)


They aren't, yet I'm not hinting at just slamming the Cage into your next Commander deck. Let's back up a little further. One of the new cards created for ***Magic: The Gathering** Commander* was this mean guy:



![Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Stranglehold)

 

[Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold+) is similar to [Grafdigger's Cage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grafdigger%27s+Cage) in that it turns off several types of cards (albeit only for your opponents):



> 
> * Stops cards like [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan), [Enlightened Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enlightened+Tutor), [Demonic Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Tutor), [Trinket Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage), and [Gamble](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamble)
> * Stops cards like [Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp), [Time Stretch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Stretch), and [Time Sieve](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Sieve)
>

[Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) is *brutal*. When many of us grizzled, scarred veterans begin thinking about Commander, our thoughts wander to ["Prime Time"](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) and an array of powerful ["Fetch me this!"](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?text=+%5B%22search%20your%20library%22%5D) effects. In a related way, the desire to take extra turns only grows stronger in a multiplayer game—why *wouldn't* I want to have another turn? Everyone else takes too long, and I just want to do my thing!


Let's get messy and talk about "fairness."


Fair is a relative term in **Magic**. There isn't any good definition as, well, fair is itself relative. [Day of Judgment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) feels fair when your back is against the wall, but that view probably isn't shared by the horde-directing aggressor across from you. I could drill through dozens of examples but you get the point: *fairness* is a dicey proposition to solicit.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf180_strangle.jpg)[Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) | Art by [John Stanko](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22John+Stanko%22%5D)


More often than not, fairness isn't decided by what *is* so much as what *isn't*. Commander carries a ton of things that my local play group decided was a little unfair, but solved it quite elegantly.


 

*Boom. I'll let the ramifications sink in for a minute. When you're ready to move on, scroll past the epic art for [Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold).*


There're many things in motion when we begin considering [Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) Commander, and the shortest summary possible was how I listed it under the pros and cons above. (So it's a little easier to, you know, explain the general weal of it to your friends. You're welcome.)


### Tutor Begone!


The "tutor" shorthand refers more specifically to the cards descended from [Demonic Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Tutor), hence the nickname. [Diabolic Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Tutor), [Enlightened Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enlightened+Tutor), [Gamble](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamble), [Green Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith), and the newest of the bunch (*Dark Ascension*'s Increasing Ambition) are probably what come to mind first. These cards are ever-present, and generally desirable, in Commander, thanks to their functional ability to bypass the randomness of an oversized singleton deck. Just last week [I shared a sweet three-card combination](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/commanding-darkness-2012-01-24) possible through Increasing Ambition. It's fair to say I really like these cards, as I'm the biggest proponent of "being able to do the thing you want to do" style of Commander games.

But there's another angle to tutoring that everyone takes for granted. It's baked into just about every Commander deck conceived, regardless of the core colors involved. You probably rely on it without even realizing it. How often do you see a derivative of this card played in Commander?


Between "fetch" lands ([Arid Mesa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa), [Polluted Delta](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta), and friends), [Cultivate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cultivate) (and it's dozen or so previous variations), [Sakura-Tribe Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) (and *it's* family tree of cousins, like [Dawntreader Elk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dawntreader+Elk)), and artifact representatives ([Solemn Simulacrum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum), [Armillary Sphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armillary+Sphere), [Journeyer's Kite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Journeyer%27s+Kite), and many more), ripping lands out of the library is trivial. I know this as a fact since I once had a landfall-esque [Kamahl, Fist of Krosa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kamahl%2C+Fist+of+Krosa) Commander deck packed to the brim with every land grabber I had, yet ran a razor-thin count of just thirty-one lands. Usually, forty or so is the magic number; thirty-one is pretty thin for a deck desiring to play a land every turn.

Mana, I feel, is one of the most important features of a Commander deck. As it stands, it's incredibly easy to go overboard in ways to set that up.



![Time Stretch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Time+Stretch)

 

Oh yeah, *that card*. [Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) strangles it off too. While taking extra turns is usually a blue-tinted affair, being blue isn't the issue. Consider the classic example of labor and cost:



> 
> * A crew of five labors have to wait one hour for a late materials delivery.
> * Each laborer is paid hourly at a rate of $20.
> * That one hour cost the company an extra $100, at least, due to the delay in materials.
> 


Of course, the delivery guy isn't the one eating the cost for the lateness. And most material delivery contracts don't include terms down to the exact hour of delivery. After all, there is a reasonable expectation that most deliveries arrive timely *except* for circumstances beyond the control of the deliverer.


This analogy holds true for multiplayer games:



> 
> * Six players sit down to play.
> * One player decides to take multiple extra turns.
> * Five players have to wait two minutes for each extra turn taken.
> * Each extra turn taken is ten minutes of time taken from other players.
> 


While, yes, there is some waiting inherent in multiplayer games, taking an extra turn causes a cascade effect, delaying every other player's ability to engage and develop in the game. Now don't get me wrong; taking an extra turn is pretty sweet. You get do more of the things you want to do, and it's usually harder for other players to stop it when you get to do two or more turns in a row. How fair taking an extra turn is perceived is, again, very relative. Being cognizant of the actual impact of those turns, however, is a very concrete idea you should keep in mind.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf180_time.jpg)[Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp) | Art by [John Foster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22John+Foster%22%5D)


So where is this leading? The ability to grab everything on command—mana, men, and magic bullets—as well as loop all the turns together, contribute to some of the doldrums in the format. Every time I write about Commander I receive several emails that cite a number of things that are an issue for the writers:



> 
> * "I never get to do AWESOME because somebody else always finds the card that totally wrecks it!"
> * "So many decks use so many of the exact same cards! Everyone brings different decks that just all play the same!"
> * "I bought one of the new Magic: The Gathering Commander decks, but it can't keep up with the decks that my friends have."
> 


These, ladies and gentlemen, *are problems*. Sitting down to a game but finding it decidedly unfun is a surefire way to close off wanting to sit down again. The Commander banned list is comprised of the worst offenders of the highest order of unfun-makers, but it certainly isn't an all-encompassing litany of things that can be perceived as unjust.


[Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) Commander was my group's way of enforcing a more level playing field. It doesn't make the format less exciting—you can still do all sorts of absurd and powerful things—but it does force more normalized methods for making things happen. When the draw step isn't just the one card between the upkeep and main phases, we think it's a move in the right direction.


This brings us back to [Grafdigger's Cage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grafdigger%27s+Cage), the start of the journey. While [Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) Commander certainly affects the methods many of us apply to dealing with a big random deck, I don't think it goes far enough to create the types of **Magic** games I enjoy most.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf180_rise.jpg)[Rise from the Grave](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rise+from+the+Grave) | Art by [Vance Kovacs](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22Vance+Kovacs%22%5D)


When [Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) is out there is still a way to tutor things and get ahead: recursion. [Animate Dead;](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Animate+Dead%3B)[Emeria, the Sky Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria%2C+the+Sky+Ruin); [Karmic Guide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karmic+Guide); [Rise from the Grave](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rise+from+the+Grave); and many more similarly sinister reanimation effects become decidedly more powerful. Libraries might be sealed, but the graveyard is still a free-for-all grab bag of goodies. I noticed the same players who best leveraged tutor effects similarly adjusted their focus to graveyard cards when building for [Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) Commander.

Looking at a massive list of cards is daunting, but I wanted to lay it out since many of you asked to see these lists anyway. Here's a breakdown of a few key standouts from the deck.


### Staples Still Survive


[Sword of Fire and Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice), [Maze of Ith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maze+of+Ith), [Strip Mine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine), and [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) are all present and accounted for. The massive overlay of far-reaching rules doesn't remove every card we just can't help but play everywhere. [Stranglehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stranglehold) Cage Commander isn't about being on a budget or removing rarer cards from the mix, but rather removing the types and effects that cards can provide.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf180_3ith.jpg)### Cards Get Better


It's obvious that some cards get worse under these rules. [Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp), [Rampant Growth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth), and [Zombify](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zombify) all "do nothing." But some cards get *way* better. Compare [Swords to Plowshares](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares) to [Path to Exile](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile); when you can't search libraries, [Path to Exile](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) is more formidable. [Mind's Eye](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Eye) is a classic feature of multiplayer games since you have more opponents drawing more cards. It's often on the "nuke on sight" lists players often compile. [Diviner's Wand](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diviner%27s+Wand) is considerably less attractive normally, but functions as a more reliable mana dump for drawing cards while providing flying for a creature. [Ghost Quarter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) steps very close to [Strip Mine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine).

And these are just easy ones from my deck. When you throw cards like [Boldwyr Heavyweights](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boldwyr+Heavyweights) into the mix, the potential of things you've specifically avoided becomes clearer!


### Themes Are Representative


I wanted to take advantage of white's assorted "destroy all creatures" effects, so I added three cards to help me out: [Colfenor's Plans](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Colfenor%27s+Plans), [Cold Storage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cold+Storage), and [Helvault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Helvault). But "saving" my own creatures to stockpile for a future reload is sneaky. Similar to recurring them from the graveyard, I can get ahead while bludgeoning the battlefield. It takes more work and dedicated persistency to ensure it happens, though, and, unlike the looping grave grabbers seen so regularly, getting to use [Helvault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Helvault) or [Cold Storage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cold+Storage) is significantly more interesting to work with.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf180_3storage.jpg)Adding rules that completely reshape a format's experience isn't an everyday idea. In fact, I would advise that you very carefully consider exactly what rules you add and why. Most importantly, everything should be discussed with your friends and play group. They, too, want to see awesome things.
