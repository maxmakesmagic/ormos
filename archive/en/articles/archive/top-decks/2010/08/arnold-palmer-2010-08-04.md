Arnold Palmer
=============



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on August 5, 2010 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











It's half iced tea.

  
It's half lemonade.

Half Standard; half Legacy Grand Prix.

This, dear readers, is the Arnold Palmer!

**1 Part Legacy:**

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_chart1.jpg)  
This Top 8 is clearly as insane as Caleb Durward's Madness deck.

How great is seeing Tom Martell ("Hi, Mrs. Martell!") deep in the elimination rounds of a Sunday stage? Is there any more fitting champion of almost any tournament than Tomoharu Saito? Saito's continued enthusiasm for **Magic** is simply humbling. Has there ever been a player so consistently near the top for so long as Saito who—more often than not—was *also* the best deck designer in the world? (Okay, okay, Gabriel Nassif.) Saito is a layup on the Hall of Fame ballots this year—both of them, actually.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_players.jpg)

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_fish.jpg)  
It's pretty cool to see a Legacy Fish deck that plays, you know, actual *Fish.* The vocab of the Eternal formats is such that "Fish" can just as soon refer to a disruptive creature deck, featuring [Jötun Grunts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=J%C3%B6tun+Grunts) as it can a deck like the great Saito's, full of twenty different Merfolk.

So what about those Merfolk?

Saito played twenty creatures, but the cool thing is that fully twelve of them were some kind of Merfolk-boosting Merfolk Lords ([Coralhelm Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Coralhelm+Commander) in addition to the more common [Lord of Atlantis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lord+of+Atlantis) and one-time Standard standout [Merrow Reejerey](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merrow+Reejerey)). These creatures obviously all built upon one another, combining tribal synergy with baseline card efficiency. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

[Æther Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial): The thing that really makes Saito's Merfolk deck exciting is this one-mana artifact. Of course [Æther Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial) is a busted [Sol Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sol+Ring)-like artifact that turns all your creatures into uncounterable instants. The curve in the Merfolk deck is particularly attractive, with so many one- and two-mana creatures, and only one sort-of three, so [Æther Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial) will hang out at two most games.

[Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill): [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill) is a powerful card, but doubly so in combination with [Æther Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial). Basically, you can play all your creatures via [Æther Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial) without triggering the [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill). The pressure is on the opponent to break the [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill), probably filling Saito's hand with some of the deck's cheap or free counterspells.

Now we see some of the vaunted variety of the Legacy format. Ford's deck—also a [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill) deck—is quite different in implementation than Saito's.

Instead of aggro-control we have almost a true control deck, all counterspells and card drawing, heavy on the defense with tons of one-mana creature elimination and [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed)s. 

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Life+from+the+Loam)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam)  
One of the unique elements of Ford's deck is how he used [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam). Typically we see Loam decks with cards like [Tranquil Thicket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tranquil+Thicket). Instead, he focused on blue card-drawing and used [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) to recycle dead [Mishra's Factories](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factories) and overload the opponent with [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland)s.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_cunningWish.jpg)  
Ford's deck has something we haven't looked at in some months (or years?) ... a [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) engine. His sideboard is heavy on the instants, designed with specific singletons that can be gobbled up by [Cunning Wish,](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish%2C) depending on what is coming after him across the table. To wit:


> * [Diabolic Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Edict): Creature elimination ... effective even against creatures that can't be targeted.
> * [Echoing Truth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Truth): Whatever; in particular, the filthy spawn of [Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens).
> * [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate): Graveyard-based strategies such a Reanimator (perhaps in response to an [Animate Dead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Animate+Dead)?). Also very clever when you know what is in the opponent's hand.
> * [Forbid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbid): The lockdown with card drawing.
> * [Krosan Grip](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Grip): [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance).
> * [Mindbreak Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindbreak+Trap): Storm; also a counterspell that can pierce [Boseiju, Who Shelters All](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boseiju%2C+Who+Shelters+All) or the equivalent.
> * [Ravenous Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Trap): Graveyard strategies again; Dredge.
> * [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle): Whatever; might just mana-screw someone on a creature-land.
> * [Submerge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Submerge): Anything! [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf).
> * [Tsabo's Decree](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tsabo%27s+Decree): Goblins, Merfolk, et cetera.

Those physical copies of [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) have done nothing but crush tournaments for Tom Martell. I broke them in for him, qualifying at National Qualifiers in New York, but then Tom won a San Juan Last Chance Qualifier, and now they put him into the finals of a Grand Prix. If the trajectory holds true (National Qualifiers Top 8 to Pro Tour Qualifer win to Grand Prix finals ... ) we can probably expect Martell in the Top 8 of U.S. Nationals in a couple of weeks, followed by a Pro Tour win the week after. There's simply no arguing with it. This is math, people.

What about the rest of his deck?

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_top.jpg)  
Martell's core combination is [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) + [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance). Basically you can counterspell anything if you have a card of the correct mana cost in your top three cards; sometimes you just get mad lucky and cast [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) on turn two without mana open to use the Top (or sometimes even no Top at all) and it fires away and counters just lucky-ducky style.

| --- | --- |
|  |  |

His creatures included combo-breaker [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique) and optimal threat [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) (no big surprises there, especially on the second one).

Tom's deck is unique in its many colors. He went four-color with red for [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) and white for [Swords to Plowshares](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares). Tons of flexibility and—*chuckle, chuckle—*basic [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) and [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains) both! "[Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) is too good."

Brad Nelson's deck, care of Brian Kowal, reminds me a lot of Chris Pikula's triumphant Deadguy Ale comeback at Grand Prix–Philadelphia (another Legacy tournament) a few years back. He has the [Gerrard's Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gerrard%27s+Verdict)s next to the [Hymn to Tourach](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach)s, and [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) as the black two drop.

This time the availability of [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) and [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) make green too attractive not to include. With [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) comes some singleton tailoring:


> * [Horizon Canopy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Horizon+Canopy): Kinda like card drawing.
> * [Karakas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karakas): [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn)(or whatever).
> * [Maze of Ith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maze+of+Ith): Basically like tutoring for [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) (but largely better).
> * [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland): Not singletons, but welcome redundancy in some match-ups.

The Grand Prix coverage had a great video interview with Christopher Gosselin, which you can see below.

This deck is one of the most unique, inventive, and apparently *effective* home-brews I have seen in some years. Basically you only need to resolve one spell to win the game. That can be [Show and Tell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) (for [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn), of course), or [Doomsday](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomsday).

The deck is deadly fast (for example you can cast [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) + [Doomsday](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomsday)), but is not in a hurry to win, necessarily.

Okay, how does [Show and Tell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) work?

In the video, Gosselin explained that depending on the match-up, you might get different things. A typical scenario would be a five-card library of:


> 1. [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm)
> 2. [Shelldock Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shelldock+Isle)
> 3. [Cloud of Faeries](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloud+of+Faeries)
> 4. [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn)
> 5. [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement)
> 

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_brainShelldock.jpg)  
You cast the [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) to get the [Shelldock Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shelldock+Isle) and put the [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) under it. Then cast the [Cloud of Faeries](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloud+of+Faeries) to untap the [Shelldock Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shelldock+Isle) ... as you only have one card in your library at this point, you are well within the parameters of [Shelldock Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shelldock+Isle), so go ahead and cast that free [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul)!

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_emrakulAttacking.jpg)  
With your free turn—yes, you get one—you can pick up and cast [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement). Neither you nor your principal permanent can be targeted, so you are probably in good shape given that [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) doesn't need to attack very many times to win the game.

Really, a fine brew.

Age's deck also has several ways to get a free [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn) onto the battlefield:


> * [Show and Tell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell)
> * [Sneak Attack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sneak+Attack)
> 

Sneaking [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) onto the battlefield is simply filthy ... and not at all slow. You can easily [Lotus Petal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Petal) an active [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song) onto the battlefield on turn two, probably with [Daze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze) / [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) backup.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_emrakul.jpg)  
As good as a quick 15/15 annihilator is, Age's secondary threat may be more interesting to talk about. [Woodfall Primus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woodfall+Primus) is a "mere" 6/6, but has a wonderful [Desert Twister](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desert+Twister)-like 187 attached. When you play a creature with Sneak Attack, you are supposed to sacrifice it at the end of turn.

What happens when you sacrifice a [Woodfall Primus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woodfall+Primus)? Persist. 187 ... again.

It's kind of like a [Plow Under](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under) that hits for 11.

The disappearance of [Mystical Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Tutor) did not deter Cook from playing one of Legacy's most popular combo decks (they became an [Infernal Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor) and [Silence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silence) in his list). 

How does this deck work?

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_adArt.jpg)  
[Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) is the main card-drawing engine. With the exception of the two storm kill-cards, everything in this deck is super cheap—zeroes and ones for the most part—allowing Cook to draw, you know, *lots.*

Then it's all about [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm)ing and dropping [Moxes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moxes) and [Petals](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Petals), looking at the top card of his deck, and so on ten times.

Finale: Either [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony) or [Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens). Either one, given sufficient storm, can set up a kill within one turn.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Just as we saw a [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) sideboard in Ford's [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill) list, Cook played a [*Burning* Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) engine. His sideboard was almost all sorceries that could either serve as the kill on the spot ([Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens), [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony), or even [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot)) or get him out of jail. For example, [Shattering Spree](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shattering+Spree) could blow out Affinity or just take out a key artifact threat under pressure. Again, this kind of sideboard offers lots of options, but at the cost of consistent customization in Games 2 and 3.

Durward clearly had the most exciting deck in the Top 8. Stone Kold Blue-Green Madness that was so popular in Block and Standard, (and even competitive in Extended) grafted onto a [Survival of the Fittest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest) engine *and* the Standard world's hottest new threat? What could be more exciting?

Just imagine re-buying multiple [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s with free [Basking Rootwalla](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basking+Rootwalla)s!

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_free2.jpg)  
Hell's Bells, this deck is hot.

Another super-exciting aspect of Durward's deck is [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch)-into-[Trygon Predator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trygon+Predator) on turn two. Sorry [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) decks!

One of the cards that you will see in a variety of decks, but is probably best here, is [Faerie Macabre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Macabre). [Faerie Macabre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Macabre) is a "free" out against graveyard effects from [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll) to [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine) itself. But in this deck, [Survival of the Fittest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest) can actually go and get the powerful card when the deck is under pressure.

Despite being the most exiting of the eight, *fo sho,* I would still point out some possible areas of improvement:

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td102_fauna.jpg)  

> * [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman): Given that this deck doesn't have [Arrogant Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arrogant+Wurm) or [Circular Logic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Circular+Logic), it can probably afford the slightly slower [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman) over [Aquamoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aquamoeba) ... a little less speed and power, but so much more long-term [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine) action.
> * [Squee, Goblin Nabob](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squee%2C+Goblin+Nabob): A long-time stalwart of these kinds of decks, just a little additional card drawing with [Survival of the Fittest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest) online goes a long way, especially when the deck is meeting resistance.
