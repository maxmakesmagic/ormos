Into the Light...
=================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on June 6, 2002 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_jaymoldenhauer-salazar.jpg)
By Jay Moldenhauer-Salazar











One word describes your [Second House of Cards Deck Challenge](/en/articles/archive/judgment-upon-us-2002-05-23) submissions:

Wow.

First, you managed to send in over 400 decks (with a limit of one deck per person!) in just over a week. For the first time since I have started this column, I couldn’t respond to everyone who wrote me. Some of you received "thank you"'s from me and some of you didn’t; I assure you that my response was dictated entirely by the volume of submissions that particular day and not the quality of your deck.

Second, your submissions were fantastic. The overall creativity and diversity of decks -- as you’ll soon see -- was phenomenal. Many more decks than appear below had me scratching my chin and muttering “Okay, now *that* is a cool idea.” Nicely done. In fact, the decks were so great that it made my inability to respond even more frustrating. Moreover, for the most part the decks followed The Rules.

Finally, you inflated my ego beyond recognition. Pretty much everyone interpreted my call for submissions as *requiring* you to compliment me. Although some of you are horrifically bad at positive feedback, many of you had me blushing. Next time I won’t make any jokes about sucking up, because I don’t think my chest can survive much more puffing.

Thank you all. The [first](http://archive.wizards.com/default.asp?x=mtgcom/daily/jm7) Deck Challenge was about as fun for me as it was exhausting. This Challenge was a lot more exhausting, and exponentially more fun.

Remember that the decks below are not necessarily the best in design. Instead, they stood out from their peers in terms of creativity. Basically, I just like them.

But enough babbling! Let’s explore some wacky green and white possibilities for Type 2 with *Judgment* as seen through your eyes...

### THE PHANTOM MENACE

The single most submitted deck involved using the new Phantom creatures alongside 



|  |
| --- |
| The "Phantom Trick"
**Why it works - from the FAQ**
* Each time a Phantom would be dealt damage, prevent all of that damage. Then remove one +1/+1 counter from it. It doesn't matter how much damage would be dealt to the Phantom at any one time. You still remove only one +1/+1 counter from it.
* A Phantom's ability prevents damage even if there are no more +1/+1 counters on it. So if a Phantom's toughness is raised by some other effect (like [Elephant Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elephant+Guide)), it becomes impossible to destroy with damage unless an effect like [Flaring Pain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flaring+Pain) states that damage can't be prevented.
 |

creature-pumpers such as [Elephant Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elephant+Guide), [Armadillo Cloak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armadillo+Cloak), [Glorious Anthem](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glorious+Anthem), [Mirari's Wake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirari%27s+Wake), and [Seton's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seton%27s+Desire) (to name a few). The result, of course, is creatures that can’t die to damage at all. Many decks added to an opponent’s misery by using [Benevolent Bodyguard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Benevolent+Bodyguard) or [Sylvan Safekeeper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Safekeeper) to further protect their critters, and [Ironshell Beetle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ironshell+Beetle) or [Fleetfoot Panther](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fleetfoot+Panther) to replenish +1/+1 counters.I fully expect people to start playing decks like these after *Judgment* is Standard-legal in July. For that reason -- and because it was the most common decktype -- I couldn’t find any decks that stood out as particularly creative (recall that a similar thing happened in the results of Deck Challenge I). They all looked solid and scary, but none made my toes tingle.

### ALTERNATIVE LIFESTYLES

Next in quantity, people tried very hard to use the new alternative win conditions. [Test of Endurance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Test+of+Endurance) by far received the most submissions of the two, with pretty much every gain-life card attempted at some point or another. Many people rode [Atalya, Samite Master](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atalya%2C+Samite+Master) to victory with the most successful attempts hiding behind some combination of [Ensnaring Bridge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ensnaring+Bridge), [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant), and [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement). Kudos to people who remembered that [Reverse Damage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reverse+Damage) is in *Seventh Edition*.

The real problem with most [Test of Endurance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Test+of+Endurance) decks was that they either looked like they were as likely to win with creature damage as the Test (and thus weren’t focused), or they didn’t look to me like they would gain 50 life faster than they would lose to opposing decks.

One unique approach came from Lord\_Shiner (yes, parents are cruel), who used the model of many [Nantuko Shrine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nantuko+Shrine) decks to try something just crazy enough to work...

[Epic Struggle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Epic+Struggle) produced fewer submissions partly because, I think, people realized that with 20 creatures they were far more likely to win with damage than some silly green enchantment. A few people came up with the same notion, all of them with good decks. ### TOKEN OF APPRECIATION

I should point out that a significant number of people used [Epic Struggle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Epic+Struggle) as a secondary win condition in dedicated token decks. These decks relied heavily on [Parallel Evolution](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Parallel+Evolution) and [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) to make opponents cry. I tried not to hold these decks’ bad timing against them since MagicTheGathering.com did Token Week during the submissions window, but in the end I didn’t see any one deck that stood apart from the others.

Two kinds of Token decks became their own deck categories: Squirrels and Wurms. Magic players’ fascination with fuzzy wuzzy squirrels is alive and well judging by the number of Squirrel decks I received. I think I’ll save some of the submissions for the Squirrel Week we talk of doing some day. As for Wurms, for some reason Wurm enthusiasts usually use too little land in their decks to support their favorite token creatures.

![Symbiotic Deployment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Symbiotic+Deployment)### MY SPECIAL LITTLE PLACE

Many, many decks abused [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement). This is bad news for players like me who are annoyed by lock decks. Here is what you can expect from a meanie near you: Drop [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) on the table along with either [Symbiotic Deployment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Symbiotic+Deployment), [Verduran Enchantress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Verduran+Enchantress), or [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) in the graveyard. Lots of decks tried [Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine) too, but that doesn’t work since you’re skipping your draw step. In any case, each deck had a different way of eventually winning, but the most evil used [Spirit Cairn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spirit+Cairn) or madness creatures. Imagine [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement), [Symbiotic Deployment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Symbiotic+Deployment), and [Spirit Cairn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spirit+Cairn) at one time and you will start to see the wickedness of this strategy.

I’m not showing these decks to you. So there. Meanie.

### WRATH OF FROG

Ask a hardcore tournament player which is the best card on *Judgment* and you will often hear [Anurid Brushhopper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anurid+Brushhopper). The ability to evade death, win after a reset like [Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval) or [Obliterate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obliterate), and be a 3/4 for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) is very good. Lots of submissions decided to take the Brushhopper for a test drive and really see what it can do. Given the color restrictions of the Deck Challenge, most people used [Anurid Brushhopper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anurid+Brushhopper) in combination with madness spells like [Basking Rootwalla](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basking+Rootwalla), incarnations like [Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glory), and big sweepers like [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God).

In fact, one deck tried so hard to get the most out of its Wraths that I had to give it kudos despite what is probably too little mana. [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) may kill most creatures, but not Barry’s, dangit! I particularly like the idea of [Elephant Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elephant+Guide) on a [Penumbra Bobcat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Penumbra+Bobcat)...

### THE FAT AND THE GREEDY

Players were equally enamored with [Hunting Grounds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hunting+Grounds). Any card that can slap a [Phantom Nishoba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantom+Nishoba) directly into play is worth a look, even if it usually is an enchantment that does ABSOLUTELY NOTHING (does anyone else find this hysterical?). I lumped [Hunting Grounds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hunting+Grounds) decks with general “fattie” decks that tried other (read: [Elvish Piper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Piper)) ways of skirting large creatures’ casting costs.

![Hunting Grounds](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hunting+Grounds)I sure love big creatures. I sure love [Hunting Grounds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hunting+Grounds). I sure wish that one of these decks had stood out from the others. But alas, most decks couldn’t justify how they would reach threshold reliably. The last deck submitted to the Challenge, by Blake Contreras, did a good job using [Rites of Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rites+of+Spring) and [Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine). Actually, my favorite is an unsigned deck using [Anurid Brushhopper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anurid+Brushhopper), [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), and lots of cantrips but, well, that would crowd out Barry.

On the other hand, I absolutely fell in love with the decks that focused on producing obscene amounts of mana to actually *cast* big, enormous, and otherwise large spells. These decks earned the moniker Lot-O-Mana decks, and several of them deserve to be highlighted:

* Hans, who sent in a deck with 30 (!) [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s along with [Molimo, Maro Sorcerer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molimo%2C+Maro+Sorcerer) and [Uktabi Wildcats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Uktabi+Wildcats).
* Reed Karriker for his Druid/[Nature's Revolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Revolt) deck.
* Alex Patterson for his valiant attempt at making a deck to actually cast both [Roar of the Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm) and [Crush of Wurms](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crush+of+Wurms)... with [Mirari](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirari).
* Teleute for his weird [Nature's Revolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nature%27s+Revolt), [Mirari's Wake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirari%27s+Wake), [Life/Death](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life%2FDeath) deck.

In particular, I admit to a keen fondness for the following deck from Alex Churchill, who also did a terrific job in Deck Challenge I. Alex probably needs more win conditions, but he managed to splash red into a deck while sticking to a G/W feel, proving that House of Cards “rules” can be bent...

### WHICH CARD? DIS-CARD

Obviously the entire *Odysse*y Block places a heavy emphasis on the graveyard. In *Judgment*, the Incarnations add the final wrinkle to mechanics like flashback, threshold, and madness. Many submissions attempted to mash all of these together and create a graveyard-focused deck. [Wild Mongrel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) made an appearance in all of them, usually alongside cards like [Patrol Hound](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Patrol+Hound), [Spirit Cairn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spirit+Cairn), [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis), [Brawn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brawn), [Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glory), [Valor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valor), [Anurid Brushhopper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anurid+Brushhopper), [Basking Rootwalla](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basking+Rootwalla), and [Arrogant Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arrogant+Wurm). Interestingly, [Silver Seraph](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silver+Seraph) also made a heavy appearance.

Most of these decks, I think, missed the correct balance of cards that allow you to discard with card that are useful to discard. Many, though, were really slick and could probably make great tournament decks. Try as I might, I couldn’t decide on a single deck of the bunch to feature.
