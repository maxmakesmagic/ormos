### TOP DECKS


A Matter of Course
==================



![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_luisscott-vargas_0.jpg)

Luis Scott-Vargas 

Luis Scott-Vargas plays, writes, and makes videos about Magic. He has played on the Pro Tour for almost a decade, and between that and producing content for ChannelFireball, often has his hands full (of cards).


August 22, 2014
 







We are bogged down in the twilight (mire) of this Standard season, and it feels like it's about time to start looking at what powerful engines will survive the upcoming rotation. The first thing that stands out to me is to take a look at what dominated [Pro Tour *Journey into Nyx*](http://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/ptjou14), which was Block Constructed. Block has traditionally been a good place to look at what might be good in upcoming Standard, even if it is missing a large set's worth of pieces. Given that there was a two-card combo that showed up 28 times out of a possible 32, I'd say we are on to something.




![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sylvan Caryatid)


![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Courser of Kruphix)


The combo, of course, is not the kind that wins the game on the spot, which I'm thankful for. Not only do you get to play a long game of *Magic*, I'd much rather hit land drops, gain life, and essentially draw extra cards than just end the game. Who wants to end games where you can't be killed and are up a ton of cards? Those are the kinds of games I play *Magic*for, and I've been happy every time I have a Courser in play. I even got to [preview the card](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/283), and was excited to do so.


The reason that Sylvan Caryatid is such a big part of the "combo" is because of how well it meshes with the intrinsic game plan of Courser, not because there's some strange interaction you are missing. The key part here is that a turn-two Caryatid enables a turn-three Courser, even providing double green, and gives you a shot at playing a land off the top. It's also crucial to note that unlike [Elvish Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Mystic) or the like, Caryatid is invulnerable to removal spells, making this a perfectly acceptable mana accelerant to play in your control decks (as long as you aren't also trying to play [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict)). Caryatid also blocks 2/2s, and keeping you alive to cast sweet spells is definitely in line with what Courser is trying to accomplish. Also, just so we are clear: playing a land off the top with Course is straight-up drawing a card, and a three-mana 2/4 that draws an extra card more than 40% of the time (thanks to higher land counts and light library manipulation like scrying or shuffling) is awesome, and that's without factoring in the lifegain.


Another very important thing to consider is that [Lifebane Zombie](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lifebane+Zombie) is going to rotate, which gives you way more freedom to play green monsters along with this combination. It is awesome that you can play Caryatid + Courser with little fear of Lifebane, and if you look at most of the decks I'll be highlighting, they don't have a ton of targets past that. Given that Caryatid is rarely Lifebaned, and Courser only is on the draw, I don't think fear of Lifebane is a good reason not to play one of these decks right now (and they will only get better).


So, now that you have this perfectly crafted combination, what are you doing with it? Let's take a look at a few current Standard decks to get an idea of how Courser is being used today.

This was one of the standout decks at [Pro Tour *Magic 2015*](http://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/ptm15), and it put two (slightly different) copies into the Top 8. One of the similarities between the two decks was, you guessed, Sylvan Caryatid + Courser of Kruphix. Both decks also played [Elvish Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Mystic), which is pretty good when all you want to do is ramp out four- and five-mana Planeswalkers. Decks like this don't depend on specific cards as much as classes of cards (good accelerants, good removal, good Planeswalkers), and the most replaceable of those things is all that's rotating. Losing [shock lands](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&format=+%5b%22Return+to+Ravnica+Block%22%5d&type=+%5b%22Land%22%5d&text=+%5bpay%5d+%5b2%5d) is a big deal, don't get me wrong, but I expect *Khans of Tarkir* to have a decent amount of mana fixing, even if the wedge scheme means that BRG is not a color combination that you can easily play. Past that, losing removal spells and keeping all the Planeswalkers besides Vraska is a good place to be in, as Planeswalkers are way less common (and way more mythic rare) than removal spells.


As for right now, I think the deck is still a great choice. You have your pick of the following cards to end the game with, although I do think it is insane to play fewer than four Xenagos:


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/cards01_td20140822.jpg)
Accelerating into any of these 'walkers and playing a bunch of removal spells is a simple but awesome plan, and I don't expect it to go anywhere even after rotation, although the base colors might be totally different.


Another deck closer to the control end of the spectrum is Junk, which uses Courser much like the Planeswalkers deck does. The main difference is that it finishes the game with [Blood Baron of Vizkopa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Baron+of+Vizkopa) and [Obzedat, Ghost Council](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council), while gaining card advantage through [Underworld Connections](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Connections).

This is what I'm most excited about when it comes to Courser's future. You may think that Courser + [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) isn't a combo, and you know what? You are exactly right, and that's the current problem. So many of the good control decks right now really depend on [Supreme Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict), and I have never been happy with a Bant deck that tries to play all of them. As natural as it would be to combo Courser + [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation), it never quite worked for me. I'd always end up either cutting the Revelation/Verdict/[Detention Sphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detention+Sphere) package or just cutting the Coursers, and therefore the green. What I'm excited about is a new generation of control cards, one that hopefully does not revolve nearly as much around a board sweeper that kills Courser. [Anger of the Gods](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anger+of+the+Gods) is more what I'm looking for, and I will be looking at delving into plenty of Sultai (BUG) and Temur (RUG) builds that aim to finally get the combo of [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix) + blue cards to really go off. We even played BUG at the last Pro Tour, and imagine how much better it gets once a whole new set's worth of cards is added!

I've thus far avoided playing with RG Monsters in a tournament, but I've sure lost to it over and over. It really preys on the WU control decks, which will no longer exist once the Azorius guild rotates out, so if you like this deck, now is the time to play it. I like the addition of Nissa, which makes the deck much less vulnerable to black's one-for-one removal spells, making this deck solid against all the big decks except Mono-Blue.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/cards02_td20140822.jpg)
Speaking of Mono-Blue, it's another deck that looks like it's taking a pretty huge hit come rotation. Losing [Cloudfin Raptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudfin+Raptor), [Frostburn Weird](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frostburn+Weird), [Tidebinder Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidebinder+Mage), and [Nightveil Specter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nightveil+Specter) really hurts those devoted to the cause of Thassa (poor [Judge's Familiar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Judge%27s+Familiar) is much more replaceable). I think [Thassa, God of the Sea](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thassa%2C+God+of+the+Sea) and [Master of Waves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Waves) are powerful enough to incentivize a new blue deck of some kind, but the green-slaying blue deck of current Standard is about to hit a drought of early drops. That's great news for Forests, as the watery menace dampened the enthusiasm of green mages everywhere, and all signs point to green improving drastically. It is possible that we are going to see a whole slew of great cheap blue cards, in which case the rotation will be a wash, but I somehow doubt that (and [Mutavault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault) leaving is a huge deal, and that one is unlikely to be back anytime soon).


Soon enough, it will be quite easy being green, and I look forward to gaining lots of life off Courser of Kruphix, a Courser I cast off of [Sylvan Caryatid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Caryatid) on turn three (hitting a Temple, of course).
