Holiday Serves Eggs to GP San Diego!
====================================




March 16-17, 2013












Grand Prix San Diego 2013
-------------------------


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsd13/trophy.JPG)


Nathan Holiday is flying high with his first Grand Prix win here in San Diego! Though he has a math test tomorrow, Holiday passed his Magic math exam today with meticulous play of the methodical Eggs combo deck. A California native, Holiday will be traveling back to San Diego in two months' time to try his hand at his first Pro Tour, Pro Tour *Dragon's Maze*.


The story of the weekend is diversity. With a grand total of 36 different archetypes playing in Day 2 and eight completely distinct decks in the Top 8, Modern is showing itself to be the format where anything is possible. You want to play fair, midrange creatures? Brian Kibler's Domri Naya rolled through the field into the Top 8. Faster? Gruul Blitz and Robots both had strong showings. Slower? UWR Control or the 4c Gifts Ungiven both had great finishes with one in the final eight. And, as Holiday proved, there are unfair options as well. In addition to Eggs, Through the Breach, Scapeshift, Burn, and multiple Pod variations made it deep into Day 2 play if not the final cut.


Daylight Savings time means the sun is still shining in San Diego on Saint Patrick's Day. Which works out well metaphorically, because though the end of day is in sight, there's plenty to still look forward to before the World Magic Cup qualifying season disappears into the night. We've got more Grand Prix coming soon: Pittsburgh, Strasbourg, Portland, and Beijing. And it all culminates two weeks after the release of the last set in the *Return to Ravnica* block at Pro Tour *Dragon's Maze* right here in San Diego, California!


Have a great evening from everyone here in gorgeous California, and send Nathan Holiday good wishes on his math test tomorrow—because he'll likely be staring at his trophy all night.

Quarterfinals - Sammy Tukeman (UWR Control) vs. Bryan De La Torre (Robots)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Ben Schwarz*

UWR Control is one of the big decks of this tournament. Though it's susceptible to larger control decks, such decks seem to have trouble consistently beating the fastest aggressive decks in the format. So although UWR auto-loses to [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) , it's much more common that players like Sammy Tukeman run into aggressive deck after aggressive deck and run them into the ground.


Enter Bryan De La Torre. De La Torre's Robots list has been good enough to get him into his first Grand Prix Top 8. However, now he was facing a deck that, with a decent draw could blow his robot boat out of the water. With a couple strong draws, De La Torre can sweep games out from under these control decks ( [Etched Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Etched+Champion) is a thorn in everyone's side), but he'd have to keep the pressure coming. If his deck can give him two games of the first-turn hand drop that makes Robots so famous, and he can avoid mass removal, De La Torre just might be able to make it to the semifinals.


De La Torre won the roll. “I'm going to play.”


“Are you sure?” Tukeman inquired.


“Yeah.”


Sick mindgames, dudes!


**Game 1**


“Wow, only two cards cast on the first turn?!” Tukeman asked. [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) and an [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) were the first plays out of Bryan De La Torre's deck.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsd13/qf-de-la-torra.jpg)


“I know. I'll make up for it.” He cast a [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) , two [Springleaf Drum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Springleaf+Drum) , another [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) , and equipped and attacked with the first one. It was an impressive looking main phase, but Tukeman cast a [Lightning Helix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) and all of De La Torre's blustery hoopla was for naught.


Tukeman again just laid a land for his turn and passed back to De La Torre's artifact sortie. The Robots deck animated the [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) and equipped the Plating. An [Electrolyze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrolyze) took out both it and the second [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) . Tukeman was completely in the driver's seat.


It was his fifth turn and he had 23 life and an untapped [Celestial Colonnade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade) . The Robots deck can certainly explode, but it's hard to come back from this position against such a controlling deck. Tukeman could simply counter what he wanted to and let through what he didn't. He was playing draw-go and passing with six and seven mana available on any given turn.


After countering [Etched Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Etched+Champion) and an [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) , Tukeman started to plink away and brought De La Torre to 12. Tukeman himself was at 19. Everything had its answer—a second [Etched Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Etched+Champion) hit a [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) , another Nexus was taken out by [Electrolyze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrolyze) .


Slowly but surely, the 4/4 animating land did his job and destroyed Bryan De La Torre before he could wrench anything back in his favor.


Sammy Tukeman 1 – 0 Bryan De La Torre


**Game 2**


[Blinkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinkmoth+Nexus) , [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) , [Memnite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite) , [Ornithopter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter) . A little stronger first-turn play for sure. With the little bits facing down a mere [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) , they rumbled in and took Tukeman to 17. De La Torre declined to play another land and cast another [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) . Torre laid a [Stony Silence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stony+Silence) with his turn, but it looked like the damage might have already been done—De La Torre attacked for 7 more and laid a third [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) . Tukeman went to 10. He had all those [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) s in his opening hand, by the way.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsd13/qf-Tukeman.jpg)


The next turn Tukeman used an [Electrolyze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrolyze) to snuff out two of the Pests before they could destroy him, but he still sunk to 6. When he got the turn back he had three land, only representing one White. When he laid a [Tectonic Edge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge) , De La Torre said, “That's not Wrath mana.” He smiled.


Tukeman sighed and said, “No it isn't.” But he had a “ Wrath -for-one-less-that-cantrips” in another [Electrolyze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrolyze) . He burnt away the last [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest) and the freshly laid [Vault Skirge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vault+Skirge) , and only went down to 5— [Memnite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite) the only attacking creature.


De La Torre looked to be out of gas and right on cue, Tukeman was ready to punish him for it. Within two turns he had a [Batterskull](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull) and two [Spellskite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) s on the field. Combined with the [Stony Silence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stony+Silence) , Tukeman was playing like he wasn't afraid of anything. And why should he be? In subsequent turns he went up to 9, then 13, then completely out of reach. During this time De La Torre's blockers slowly disappeared; his life total going along with it.


De La Torre extended his hand, and in two short games, De La Torre's Top 8 life had been extinguished.


Sammy Tukeman 2 – 0 Bryan De La Torre





Quarterfinals - Eric Froehlich (Jund) vs. Matt Ferrando (GWB Junk)
------------------------------------------------------------------

Quarterfinals - David Sharfman (Scapeshift) vs. Ken Yukuhiro (Jund)
-------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Mike Rosenberg*

Ken Yukuhiro has traveled a long way to reach the Top 8 of Grand Prix San Diego. In addition to the fifteen rounds of hard-fought play, he had to travel the width of an ocean. The last time I saw Yukuhiro was in Yokohama a couple of weeks ago, where he was fighting off a terrible illness. Now recovered, he came to San Diego in search of more points to fuel his push for Gold. His first opponent in the Top 8 is David Sharfman, who is pushing for a chance to reach Gold as well, only four points behind Yukuhiro.


This matchup is one that is fairly uncommon in Modern these days, with Yukuhiro's Zoo deck falling out of favor in recent months. Still, feeling he had a strong matchup against the midrange Junk and Jund decks that filled the field, he chose to resurrect the archetype for a run at the title. Sharfman's [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) deck is one of the slower combo decks in the field, often taking until turn six or seven to finally assemble the cards needed to end the game. Despite that, it still has enough defensive capability to withstand onslaughts like those Yukuhiro's deck is capable of producing.


Sharfman began with his deck's main threat: [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) . Yukuhiro saved him the trouble of one trigger by sacrificing a [Scalding Tarn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scalding+Tarn) to put a [Stomping Ground](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stomping+Ground) into play untapped, allowing him to cast a Deathrite Shaman. Sharfman played a mana accelerate of his own, using [Farseek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek) to give him his third land.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpsd13/qf-sharfman.jpg)

### 5.) [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls)



![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lingering Souls&options=)

Throughout the Swiss portion of the tournament, there was no deck that could touch the GWB deck known as Junk for popularity. It was absolutely everywhere, ending up with more decks in Day 2 of the Grand Prix than any other deck. One of the biggest reasons that so many players opted to play Junk in this post-banning Modern is [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) . But Junk wasn't the only deck to feature the powerful sorcery. Team Channelfireball's A-Jund-i deck dipped into white for both [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) and [Ajani Vengeant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) . BW Tokens and Junk Tokens use it as a primary win condition. If a deck could afford both black and white mana, it played [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) .


The format was perfect for [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) to be reasonably good. As a virtual roadblock against the aggressive decks in the field, [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) buys decks enough time to enact their strategies. Against the control and combo decks, [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) provides a potent offense and a fast clock. And best of all, it works incredibly well with [Liliana of the Veil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) , giving a “free” discard to protect the other cards in hand.


### 4.) [Ajani Vengeant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant)



![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ajani Vengeant&options=)

Where the heck did [Ajani Vengeant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) come from? While the big, angry kitty had seen some play in Standard after his release, Modern was certainly not where I expected to see a four-drop that doesn't immediately win the game making an impact. But with the rise of UWR decks and the recent banning of [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) , Ajani has found himself a home in Modern.


UWR decks of all flavors are running Ajani as a perfect way to permanently impact the board. While they have to tap mana on their own turns to arrange this, the ability to lock down one permanent every turn is very potent. In addition, the ability to pay four mana, kill a creature, gain some life, and likely soak up some damage is still an incredibly efficient use of the card.


One of the most interesting new homes for Ajani is in Jund. Many of the members of team ChannelFireball, including semifinals competitor Eric Froehlich, brought a variant of Jund that LSV was referring to as A-Jund-i. Dipping into white for [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) , [Ajani Vengeant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) was an ideal card to fill the now-vacant four-drop slot. While the card isn't a flashy or swingy as [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) , it does do a wonderful job of teaming up with Liliana to punish combo, control, and midrange players alike. That, and it's, well, legal.


Still, there were many differing opinions about the card amongst the players in the tournament. Finalist Sammy Tukeman named it as the best card this weekend, while Quarterfinalist Bryan De La Torre named it the weirdest. Still, it was clearly a very important part of both Froehlich's semifinals deck and Tukeman's finals deck, earning it a spot in the Top 5 cards of the weekend.


### 3.) [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)



![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lightning Bolt&options=)

At the beginning of the weekend, we set out to get an idea of what people felt was powerful in this current iteration of Modern. What we found was an overflowing love for a little one-mana instant from *Alpha*. Numerous players we talked to listed it as one of the best cards in the format, putting it alongside cards like [Deathrite Shaman](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) and [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) . Despite the prevalence of obviously powerful cards like many of the others on this list, [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) seemed a bit out of place. It is a simple effect that lacks the ridiculous game-changing power of [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) , the crushing advantage of [Lingering Souls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) , the explosive potential of [Reshape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) , and the ability to lock a game down like [Ajani Vengeant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) . It seems like it should be *under*powered if anything.


Yet, in a format filled with aggressive and powerful one-drops, [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) has found an integral home on both sides of the fence. It is the weapon of choice to fend off robots, animals, and ghosts for most of the decks in Modern. It's also a powerful way to give aggressive decks enough extra reach to finish games off a turn or two ahead of time. Five different archetypes played the card in the Top 8, and the only decks that didn't either aren't playing red or opted for a more situationally powerful alternative ( [Galvanic Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast) in Robots). It has done work both keeping players alive and ending games prematurely.


In the Top 8, things were no different. It helped Eric Froehlich get past his quarterfinals opponent by allowing him to efficiently use his mana, removing a [Deathrite Shaman](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) and racing with an [Olivia Voldaren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Olivia+Voldaren) in his final game. David Sharfman used it to kill an [Aven Mindcensor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aven+Mindcensor) that flashed into play in response to his [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) , allowing him to win his match.


### 2) [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift)



![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Scapeshift&options=)

There was talk, not too long ago, about the death of the deck that takes in namesake from this card. But it takes much more than preparation and knowledge of the opponent's deck to keep a one-card combo out of a format. [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) , since its Modern unbanning, has shown that it is about exactly as good as its supposed to be—powerful, but not unbeatable. There were [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) decks at or around the top tables all weekend, and even put David Sharfman into the Semifinals. The deck that's job is to survive until seven land has proven rather resilient and adaptable. The [Clutch of the Undercity](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clutch+of+the+Undercity) version swaps stable mana for some versatility, and Ben Stark and Baby Huey's version traded stable mana for an incredible sideboard and a [Sphinx's Revelation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) .


Though the format has added tricks and pitfalls— [Aven Mindcensor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aven+Mindcensor) , [Leyline of Sanctity](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+Sanctity) , maindeck [Ghost Quarter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) and [Tectonic Edge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge) —Sharfman and the other green sorcery lovers have shown that if you're a smart, adaptable player, an “Oops, I win” card can still help you to oops, you win.


### 1) [Reshape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape)



![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Reshape&options=)

“...because two mana [Tinker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker) for [Black Lotus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Lotus) is a legal play.” - GP Champion Nathan Holiday on why he played Eggs.


There's very little to that quote that requires expounding. Of all the over-the-top antics the Eggs deck can muster, none of it makes the deck hum, no, *exist*, the way [Reshape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) into [Lotus Bloom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) does. The Darksteel dud—originally designed as a purposefully underpowered [Tinker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker) —is pulling another [Lion's Eye Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) , another nerfed card that found a degenerate home. With the printing of the no-cost [Lotus Bloom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) , the interaction between the Bloom and [Reshape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) has proven itself worthy as a Grand Prix– and Pro Tour–winning deck centerpiece. Not to mention, if you're lucky enough to have a [Chromatic Star](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Star) on the field when you cast it, you can even draw an extra card for your troubles.


Sure, suspending [Lotus Bloom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) like you're supposed to can be good, but often the deck needs to combo out on turn three, and there's no way to get the mana necessary to do that without Reshaping into a Bloom or two. Nathan Holiday won both games in the Grand Prix finals on turn three doing just that and ended the consistent undervaluing of this extremely powerful, if a little tedious, Modern deck.


Though the [Reshape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) / [Lotus Bloom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) interaction is yet to make a splash outside this archetype, it's overwhelming power and a second tick in the win column is more than enough to make this overlooked sorcery the number one card of the weekend.
