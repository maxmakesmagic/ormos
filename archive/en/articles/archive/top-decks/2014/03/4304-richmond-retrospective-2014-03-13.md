4304, A Richmond Retrospective
==============================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on March 13, 2014 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 







Welcome back to Perilous Research! Last weekend, more than 4,300 players showed up in Richmond, Virginia, with dreams of winning [the largest Constructed Grand Prix thus far](/en/events/coverage/liu-shatters-richmond-record). Perhaps more impressively, we weren't playing Block Constructed or Standard, we were playing Modern. [The most recent Pro Tour](/en/events/coverage/mclaren-weaves-victory-valencia) had the community electrified with excitement over the format. Today, we'll be taking a look at what happened in Richmond. It's hard to digest the sheer volume of information available from an event that large, but we're not going to find a more complete snapshot of Modern as a format. 


 We knew that Grand Prix Richmond would be diverse, but the scale of variety is quite daunting when we're dealing with over 4,000 players. We'll start by taking a big-picture look at the Modern format as it panned out in Richmond. Looking back on the Grand Prix, the first thing we notice about the Top 8 is the ever-comforting "skill game" revelation. With over 4,300 players in the mix, Luis Scott-Vargas, Ben Friedman, Jamie Parke, and Josh McClain managed to break into the Top 8 of the event. It's easy to get discouraged with competitive **Magic** when we're subject to bad draws or unlucky matchups, but, in our heart of hearts, we can rest comfortably with the knowledge that the better players will enjoy better results in the long run. 


 The biggest story from Richmond for deck builders is the success of [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod). When the dust settled after fifteen rounds, five [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) decks found themselves in the Top 8 of the event. 


![Birthing Pod](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Birthing+Pod)
  
 There were four copies of Melira Pod and one copy of Kiki Pod. Let's take a look at the [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) decks from Grand Prix Richmond.

 Three of the five [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) strategies in the Top 8 of Grand Prix Richmond were a lot like this. The deck has a very effective midrange game plan with an explosive finish. The combination of [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast) alongside an outlet to sacrifice creatures and a persist creature creates an infinite loop. When looping [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), the Melira Pod player gains infinite life. When looping [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap), the Melira Pod player deals infinite damage. The deck has a lot of angles for difficult matchups. Against Affinity or Infect, a Melira Pod player can [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) for [Orzhov Pontiff](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orzhov+Pontiff) to eat the opponent's entire board. Against midrange creature strategies, [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw) can be a haymaker. [Thrun, the Last Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrun%2C+the+Last+Troll), is nearly impossible for Red-White-Blue Control strategies to deal with. The traditional Melira Pod strategy offers players a good deck with a lot of room to play their way out of the most dire situations. It makes sense that so many of the greatest Modern players chose to pilot this deck last weekend.

 One of the greatest players to ever play the game, Luis Scott-Vargas, chose to play Melira Pod as well. Luis's version of the deck has a unique twist, though. In addition to the [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast) combo, Luis also had another infinite combo in the form of [Archangel of Thune](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Thune) and [Spike Feeder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spike+Feeder). Removing a +1/+1 counter from [Spike Feeder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spike+Feeder) allows the pilot to gain 2 life, which in turn gives all creatures another +1/+1 counter. This combo allows a player to generate infinite life while putting infinite +1/+1 counters on all non-[Spike Feeder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spike+Feeder) creatures. [Archangel of Thune](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Thune) also interacts nicely with [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), allowing it to persist back indefinitely just like [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast). It's also nice to have a gigantic lifelinking flier to close the game with. Going forward, I would be surprised if we didn't see more versions of the deck including this combo in addition to the normal [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast) plan.

 Brian Liu chose to go a different route entirely with his [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) deck. Kiki Pod uses [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) to assemble [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) and [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch). [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker) taps to put a copy of the [Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) into play, which untaps Kiki-Jiki, which makes another [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Deceiver%2BExarch), etc. The deck plays similarly to Melira Pod, but it sacrifices a bit of consistency for the sake of a higher mean in card power level.

 Affinity is the new top-dog aggressive strategy in Modern. The deck unloads its hand brutally fast and closes the game with a big card like [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating), [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager), or [Steel Overseer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+Overseer). Affinity's success last weekend will likely lead to a lot of [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge), [Creeping Corrosion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Corrosion), [Hurkyl's Recall](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurkyl%27s+Recall), and [Vandalblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vandalblast) in the coming weeks. We can expect this deck to do well in the future, but we'll probably need to wait for the hate to wane.

[Grand Prix Miami Top 8 Competitor](/en/events/coverage/duke-draws-blood-miami) Sammy T managed to Top 16 the largest Constructed **Magic** event in history last weekend with this interesting version of [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin). [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) helps Sammy find his combo while making him resistant to aggressive starts from his opponent. Additionally, [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) can pick up a [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) to let him draw two cards a turn or get copies by Kiki-Jiki for the same effect. [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel), [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite), and the rest of Sammy's creatures let him apply pressure, often forcing his opponent to tap out and get comboed. [Swan Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swan+Song) plays a strong role here as a hard counterspell that can protect his combo for a single mana.

 Todd Anderson and Brad Nelson played this version of [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) to a combined 2532 record at Grand Prix Richmond. I'm not a math scientist, but 2532 is very impressive, and I wouldn't be surprised if this deck and others like it became the deck to beat in the Modern format. The deck has a strong midrange game plan with cards like [Scavenging Ooze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scavenging+Ooze), [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), and [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) alongside good disruption and spot removal, but its ability to end the game with [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) out of nowhere makes it a very dangerous entity.

 A lot of people wrote off Faeries after a lackluster Pro Tour performance, but the deck has a lot of strengths and it definitely deserves a second look. The deck applies pressure on the opponent's end step while controlling the pace of the game with countermagic and removal. Alex Sittner managed a 132 record with the Fae Menace and I expect the deck to increase in popularity as decks like Zoo and Burn become less popular. [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) is very good on the play against Affinity and the deck does tremendously well against combo and control strategies. Sittner didn't include [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast) in his sideboard, but I expect the one-mana instant to be an integral part of the deck's plan against Affinity going forward.
