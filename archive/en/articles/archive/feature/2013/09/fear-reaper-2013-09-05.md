Fear the Reaper
===============



 Posted in **Feature**
 on September 5, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_2016_LSV.jpg)
By Luis Scott-Vargas 




Luis Scott-Vargas plays, writes, and makes videos about Magic. He has played on the Pro Tour for almost a decade, and between that and producing content for ChannelFireball, often has his hands full (of cards). 







I haven't featured a five-color Commander deck yet, mainly because I really like focused decks, and the five-color decks often end up as a mishmash of powerful cards and mana fixing. There's nothing wrong with that by any means, but I like building Commander decks around cool interactions and strong themes, so most decks with five-color commanders don't appeal to me quite as much. However, the deck I found for this week ([built by hatriarch](http://magic.tcgplayer.com/db/deck.asp?deck_id=1148483)) might make me eat crow. 


![Reaper King](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Reaper+King)
 This deck uses [Reaper King](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reaper+King) as the commander and isn't scared to include as many ways to trigger him as possible. The first and foremost is the legion of Scarecrows, from the actual [One-Eyed Scarecrow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=One-Eyed+Scarecrow) to all sorts of Changelings. 


 Besides the Scarecrow army, the [Reaper King](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reaper+King) has many other methods of triggering. Once you have a single Scarecrow in play, cards like [Deadeye Navigator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deadeye+Navigator), [Flickerwisp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flickerwisp), [Esperzoa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esperzoa), and [Clone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clone) all [Vindicate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate) your decision to play the deck. Even if all your Scarecrows die, there is no shortage of ways to bring them back from the dead, some of which are reusable every turn (I have a soft spot for [Skeleton Shard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skeleton+Shard) in particular, as I drafted my deck around it in the first PTQ I won). 


 It does sound like this costs a ton of mana, but [Reaper King](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reaper+King) has your back. Between [Grand Architect](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grand+Architect), [Gilded Lotus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilded+Lotus), [Sol Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sol+Ring), and a ton of fixers, you should be able to deploy your Scarecrows in a timely fashion. The lands are quite colorful, and if you wanted to put more resources into the deck, Revised dual lands or Ravnica shocklands certainly wouldn't hurt (except for the shocklands, those literally can hurt you). 


 You could also easily customize this by adding more Scarecrow synergies, either with graveyard recursion effects ([Unburial Rites](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) springs to mind) or ways to tutor up specific creatures. I also wouldn't mind a few more card-drawing spells, although the creator of this deck opted for the [Howling Mine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine)/[Temple Bell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temple+Bell) route, which may play better in multiplayer.
