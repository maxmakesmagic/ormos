Saturday, 10:15 a.m. – Photo essay: Stuffed with Steak
------------------------------------------------------


*by Brian David-Marshall*

There are many reasons why assignments in Japan are so coveted by coverage team but none are more compelling than the food. Whether you like sushi, pork, steak, or are simply a junk food addict the food in Japan is unlike any other place on earth. I have had several of my most memorable meals in Kobe including a teppanyaki staff dinner in 2004 that featured the most delicious beef I had ever encountered – until last night.


Wizards staff members who have been coming to Kobe since the first Grand Prix in this town back in 2001 have long raved about the food at [Sakurai](http://www.steak-sakurai.co.jp), a nearby steak joint that has been turning out absurdly good food since 1987. Friday was the first chance I have had to sample the food first hand and I was not disappointed. The inside of the small restaurant – with its walls covered in hand-written messages from raving fans -- featured only 16 seats around a teppanyake bar.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/Sakurai.jpg)


Along with Scott Larabee, Rob Alexander, and our host Ron [Foster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Foster) we eagerly awaited a multi-course celebration of the cow. The message over our heads promised happy results.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/stuffed-with-steak.jpg)


I will not bore you with pictures of the soup – which was delicious – and skip right to the single most delicious thing I have ever consumed in my life. I am sorry fattiest tuna, you have been replaced in my heart by Kobe Beef nigiri. It was the most succulent bite of food I have ever encountered. I ate everything on this plate except for the flowers and the big leaf. The citrus on the plate is apparently something called sudachi which tasted like a mild lime.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/Kobe-Beef-Nigiri.jpg)


Even the salad had steak on it... You can see in the picture that the restaurant was a rare non-smoking venue in Japan – essential in such closed quarters with this quality of food.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/steak-salad.jpg)


We did a little bit of surf and turf next with some Cthulu Nuggets...


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/Cthulu-Nuggets.jpg)


A steak and mushroom dish rounded out the parade of cow flesh. Lucky for me Scott Larabee does not like the mushrooms and I got to double up on the fungus! This dish had a heaping mound of fresh garlic chips on the side which Scott threatened to protect with lethal force if need be.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/garlic-chips-and-steak.jpg)


We were the last patrons left and our jaws dropped as head chef and owner Masatake Sakurai personally prepared a steak dinner for his staff. If you ever have a chance to visit Kobe you should make a point of getting yourself stuffed with delicious steak!


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/Happy-Customers.jpg)


 

Saturday, 11:35 a.m. – Last Chance for Innovation: The winning decklists from Friday's Grand Prix Trials
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Coverage Staff*

With this being the last weekend of the PTQ season you might not think there is much room for innovation in Extended but Kenji Tsumura says you would be wrong. Kenji is MIA this weekend but his presence was felt in the very first of nine GP Trials that were held on Friday when a Tezzerator variant he built took down the three byes. The deck, dubbed The Stretcherator, uses the Cloudpost/Vesuva mana engine to power out Time Stretch, with those extra turns it is easy to get the loyalty counters just right on Tezzeret to overrun your opponent with an army of 5/5 artifacts.


Most of the other winning lists were decks you have seen before; Ranger Zoo, Rock, and next Level Blue. Apparently the hottest cards at the dealer's tables yesterday and this morning were Ethersworn Canonist, Vedalken Shackles, and [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte), so we there should be more various and sundry Level Blue decks this weekend. The other deck that was interesting among the nine also relied on Cloudpost and Vesuva and bore a striking resemblance to Gabriel Nassif's deck from Worlds in Paris.


Tomohiro Tajima took down the fourth Trial with MatryrPost. Can he and Tanaka continue their winning ways in this Pro laden field? We'll begin to find out once the fourth round begins and the action begins in earnest. Enjoy these decklists in the meanwhile.

Feature Match Coverage: Hisaya Tanaka (Stretcherator) vs. Takahiro Katayama (Elves)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Brian David-Marshall*

You will have to permit me a round of indulgence. As we pushed into Round Four – the first round in which all the players in the tournament are playing -- I could easily be covering matches featuring former Player of the year Shota Yasooka, former Rookie winner Yuuya Watanabe, or multiple Pro Tour Top 8 competitor Kazuya Mitamura. This round it is a Grand Prix Trial winner that intrigued me more than all those other title holders.


Which is not to say that Hisaya Tanaka – who won the first GP Trial on Friday with an exciting Tezzeret variant utilizing [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)s and [Time Stretch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Stretch) – is by any means a slouch with a second place finish at Grand Prix Kyoto during the ‘02-03 season. His opponent was Takahiro Katayama, who won Grand Prix Hamamatsu as a member of the team Tanii Monogatari. (Interesting piece of useless trivia: Akira Asahara was involved in the finals of both of these players' GP finishes.)


### Game 1


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/R4Tanaka.jpg)I was unsure if Tanaka was still playing the deck he used on Friday to sweep through the first Trial as his deck looked like a blue-white control deck with nary a [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) or [Vesuva](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva) in his hand. It was quickly apparent what his opponent was playing as he led off with [Nettle Sentinel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel). Tanaka used [Spell Snare](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare) to catch an [Elvish Visionary](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Visionary) but Katayama just shrugged and dropped Birchlore Ranger. Tanaka [Repeal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Repeal)ed [Nettle Sentinel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) to end the turn.


[Nettle Sentinel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) wanted to make a reappearance and Tanaka allowed it. He did not allow [Wirewood Symbiote](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Symbiote) using [Cryptic Command](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) to counter it and draw a card. Katayama had a Hivemaster and another Symbiote and his board began to get out of hand. [Solemn Simulacrum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum) tried to stave off the team for Tanaka but Katayama just sent everyone into battle. The Simulacrum blocked the Symbiote but Pendlehaven saved it -- after damage was on the stack. Katayama did not want Tanaka to gain any card advantage. Visionary joined the team.


[Triskelion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Triskelion) came down for Tanaka and promptly shot the Symbiote and Hivemaster but it did not look like it would be enough as Katayama had [Glimpse of Nature](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature) for his next turn and a handful of cards. [Heritage Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid), Llanowar Elf, [Heritage Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid). Visionary and [Essence Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Warden) were parlayed into [Chord of Calling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) for [Ranger of Eos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos), which in turn found a pair of [Wirewood Symbiote](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Symbiote)s. Katayama bounced [Heritage Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid) and continued to dig through his deck.


Tanaka could only rest his head on his hand and hope to untap. He did manage to snipe a Symbiote with the remaining [Triskelion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Triskelion) counter when Katayama tapped his [Pendelhaven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pendelhaven) for the mana to keep his turn moving forward. It did not matter. Katayama had found his [Brain Freeze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze). They began to count up spells but Tanaka waved him off.


### Game 2


As he reached for his sideboard I craned my neck to see if he was even playing the same deck he had used in the Trial the previous day. As I watched Tanaka contemplate his Game 2 options it became apparent that he was running Stretcherator. He did relegate half of his Stretches as well as his [Cryptic Command](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)s to the sideboard in favor of cards good in the matchup like [Ethersworn Canonist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethersworn+Canonist) and Crovax Ascendant.


Tanaka, who could not lose with the deck yesterday, sighed as he shipped his opening hand back for six new ones. He led off with a [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost). Katayama had [Nettle Sentinel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) to kick things off.


Tanaka played a pair of [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)s and did nothing while Katayama added Symbiote and Llanowar Elf to his side of the table. Tanaka had [Thirst for Knowledge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge) and discarded [Solemn Simulacrum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum). He pondered the board across from him before deciding to lay a [Plains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains) and passing the turn. Katayama made a play with [Chord of Calling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) for one and it resolved as [Heritage Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid). Tanaka decided to play [Path to Exile](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) on the Symbiote before handing the turn over to his opponent.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/R4Katayama.jpg)Katayama played [Choke](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Choke) and Tanaka gruntingly allowed it. Another [Nettle Sentinel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) came down for Katayama but he was down to one card. Tanaka had to tap out – locking down three [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)s – to play [Triskelion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Triskelion) and shoot the Druid and Birchchlore Ranger. Katayama played [Chord of Calling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) for Ranger again and fetched [Wirewood Symbiote](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Symbiote) and [Heritage Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid). ------------------------------------------------------


*by Brian David-Marshall*


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/events/gpkob09/KJ.jpg)The last time there was an Extended Grand Prix in Japan it was in 2005. The event was Kitakyushu and the winner was Tomoharu Kaji – a season before he would be a member of the Pro Tour Charleston winning team. There were only 272 players at that event – one of the smallest Grand Prix in this part of the world. The Top 8 from that event was billed as possibly “The Best Top 8 Ever?” by the coverage team. It has only gotten better with hindsight with Kaji winning Charleston and Mihara becoming a juggernaut at events he able to attend.


While he is in attendance this weekend I can assure you he will definitely not become a back-to-back Extended champion this weekend. That is impossible since Kaji is here as a coverage reporter for the Japanese side of the coverage. I asked him a couple of questions about what it was like being perpendicular to the red zone, covering the action as opposed to participating.


“It is really hard. The work is really difficult,” said Kaji, who has retired from playing to continue his education. “Normally, these days, I would be reading the coverage but now I have to write the words – and that is very hard. Even as a player I always thought it would be interesting to do some coverage work. It is very interesting to be on this side of it.”


As someone who has successfully navigated his way through Day One of an event and then managed to win it all on Day Two, I asked Kaji to describe what was necessary for a deck – and player – to succeed at an event like this.


“Sideboarding is very important,” he answered immediately. “There are strong cards in this format that can be shut down by other cards that come in from people's sideboards. You need to anticipate that and sideboard against people's sideboards. Mind's Desire, for example, gets shut down by [Rule of Law](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rule+of+Law). If you assume people are going to bring in [Rule of Law](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rule+of+Law) against your deck, what do you need to bring in against [Rule of Law](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rule+of+Law)?”


“It is a very fast format – it is a very complicated format. There are five or six decks that are really strong. Everyone's decks are strong – that's a given. The difference comes in playing. If you make a lot of little mistakes or overlook things, it will cost you. The person who practices the most, the person who understands how their deck works, and can play correctly can be the ‘better player'”.


Based on the amount of practicing and playtesting that is taking place in the Tokyo community around Tomoharu Saito it would not be surprising to see someone from that community do well this weekend – although Kaji would obviously prefer if that person was Saito himself. The two were teammates on the winning team for Pro Tour Charleston and remain close friends.


As for what deck, Kaji would have sleeved up for this event if he was playing?


“Mind's Desire combo,” said Kaji wistfully.
