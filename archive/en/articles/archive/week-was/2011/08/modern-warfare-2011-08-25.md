Modern Warfare
==============



 Posted in **The Week That Was**
 on August 26, 2011 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Brian David-Marshall











Things have been moving forward quickly for the newly minted Modern format, which reaches back for tournament legal cards all the way to the original *Mirrodin* block and *Eighth Edition*. Players in the [**Magic Online** Community Cup](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/other/04262011c) got the first crack at playing it—albeit with a different banned list and the Unified deck construction rules in effect—and community interest in the format quickly grew after that first look.

During the San Diego Comic Con **Magic** panel, Aaron Forsythe casually commented that Wizards was going to be moving forward with the Modern format in the near future, which set the Twitterverse ablaze. The "near future" will arrive, sooner than many expected, next weekend, when several hundred of the world's greatest **Magic** minds will come together at Pro Tour Philadelphia. With the announcement came a fairly extensive and preemptive banned list that meant that Caw-Blade, [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut), traditional Affinity, Fairies, [Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis), [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths), and a handful of other known archetypes would not be easy layups for PT participants.

Reactions were, as always, mixed, with many players very excited about the change—Frank Karsten among them. In fact, the Hall of Famer was not planning on attending the Pro Tour at all due to disinterest in the well-explored Extended format. He announced on his Facebook page:


> ![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/twtw/twtw157_fb.jpg)I wasn't originally planning on attending Pro Tour Philadelphia, but the new format exites me. Aiming to break the format, I just booked a flight! Philly, here I come!
> 
> 

  
I have been in the Wizards offices all week doing Hall of Fame research, and I stumbled across an old picture of Frank that I posted on his Facebook wall.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/twtw/twtw157_frank.jpg)  
I also asked Frank how playtesting for the new format was coming. To give you an idea of some of the crazy interactions that are possible with this card pool, I present to you his response—but keep in mind that Frank is also going to be playing the "War of Attrition" event deck at Nationals this weekend, so you might not want to try this at home:


> ![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/twtw/twtw157_fb.jpg)  
> Bubbles used to be our lucky team mascot :)
> 
> As for Philly, I've been discarding [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) to [Lightning Axe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Axe), followed by [Goryo's Vengeance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goryo%27s+Vengeance) in response to [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul)'s shuffle trigger ... this format surely is exiting!

I actually had an opportunity two weeks ago, just a few days after the announcement was made, to play in [an unsanctioned Modern event](http://magic.tcgplayer.com/db/deck_search_result.asp?Location=TCGplayer+75k+Championship+-+Modern+2k+-+8%2F14%2F11) at the TCGPlayer.com Championship in Chicago. You may have seen the Top 16 deck lists posted, which included my mash-up of Red Deck Wins and Zoo sitting in 16th place. I have had a number of people approach me in person and through social media to ask about the deck list and the tournament metagame. The card choices I made were dictated by a last-minute scramble to find cards for a deck—any deck—and while I was able to cobble together something I like to call Red Deck Goes to the Zoo, it did not have the [Punishing Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Punishing+Fire) / [Grove of the Burnwillows](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grove+of+the+Burnwillows) combo or even the right assortment of fetch lands and *Ravnica* dual lands to run optimally.

If you are looking for something a little more relevant to get a sense of the format, you need look no further than **Magic Online**. When the virtual tournament center emerged from its weekly downtime on Wednesday, it did so fully Modernized and promptly fired off an 85 person Daily Event that churned out some of the first meaningful—and battle-tested— decks of the new format's life, with just over a week to go before Round 1 of Pro Tour Philadelphia.

The tournament produced six undefeated players sporting five different archetypes. There were even more deck possibilities lurking in the 3-1 bracket but from that scrum of players one deck emerged as the early front-runner: TwelvePost.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/twtw/twtw157_cloud.jpg)  
The origins of TwelvePost go back to [Pro Tour Kobe](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Events.aspx?x=mtgevent/ptkob04/welcome) and Pro Tour Hall of Famer Gabriel Nassif. The format for the event was *Mirrodin* Block Constructed and Nassif played a [Tooth and Nail](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) deck that ramped into and entwined the game-breaking card with [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)—and eight more virtual [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)s in the form of [Sylvan Scrying](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Scrying) and [Reap and Sow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reap+and+Sow) to fetch additional copies.

The deck carried him all the way to the finals, and the name has stuck with the archetype, which, conveniently, can conceivably have twelve posts on the battlefield at once thanks to the recent printing of [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost) and the continued presence of [Vesuva](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Vesuva)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva)  
Two different versions of the deck went undefeated in that first Daily Event. When the format and banned list were first announced there was some concern that Zoo decks would run rampant, but when a deck capable of playing a turn-three [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)—[Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) on turn one, [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) and [Simic Signet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Simic+Signet) on turn two, play a green source on turn three, and you can make a Titan—can also gain you 8 life off of two copies of [Glimmerpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost) and another 10 life when you attack, things can go really far south, really fast for a deck trying to scratch together 20 damage with a limited supply of creatures and burn.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/twtw/twtw157_gTitan.jpg)  
If you happen to find yourself not under any pressure to gain life, you can also turn that turn-three Titan into a turn-four [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn). Follow the same formula as before, but when you play [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) get two more copies of [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) / [Vesuva](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva), which will give you at least eighteen mana to work with on turn four—and that is assuming you don't play a land or attack with [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) first. If you haven't drawn your [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) over the first couple turns of the game you can also slow things down a bit and get [Eye of Ugin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eye+of+Ugin) with your [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) on turn three to search for your [Emrakul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) with your embarrassing wealth of mana on turn four.

The two undefeated versions of the deck went about their business in very different ways. The one piloted by Ume\_Nao not only had [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) to cheat additional Loci into play but went with [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift)—a card that we have seen do some unfair things with [Valakut](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut) and that is likely to get paired with landfall creatures in some versions of Modern Zoo—as well. Paukan did not play [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) but did go old school with [Reap and Sow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reap+and+Sow), a card that seems like it is going to be a key card in the mirror for this archetype. Keep in mind that the various Posts all look at all Posts on the battlefield, not just under your control, so keeping your opponent's lands at bay seems like it could be a "big deal."

Take a look at the two decks side by side and you can see where they overlap and some of the significant differences:

There was definitely a question as to how well the Affinity archetype could hold up with the banning of all its crucial colored-mana-producing artifact lands, but this version has very few colored-mana spells in its main deck—just a set of [Galvanic Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast)s, [Fling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fling)s, and [Atog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog), which it is able to run off of four [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s, [Mox Opal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Opal), and [Springleaf Drum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Springleaf+Drum). The deck gets exciting after sideboarding, when it brings in six [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) effects to slow down archetypes ranging from [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven)-based four-color decks to the aforementioned TwelvePosts.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/twtw/twtw157_3Cards2.jpg)  
One of the popular archetypes lurking down in the 3-1 bracket was [Living End](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End), and the All-in-Affinity deck was able to dance around that deck's eponymous spell by sacrificing all of its artifact creatures to [Atog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog) and [Fling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fling)ing it in response. When the dust settled, the [Living End](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End) deck had its creatures out of the graveyard, but the Affinity deck was right back where it started.
