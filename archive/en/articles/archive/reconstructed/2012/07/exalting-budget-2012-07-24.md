Exalting on a Budget
====================



 Posted in **Reconstructed**
 on July 24, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorPic_GavinVerhey.jpg)
By Gavin Verhey




 When Gavin Verhey was eleven, he dreamt of a job making Magic cards—and now as a Magic designer, he's living his dream! Gavin has been writing about Magic since 2005. 







Welcome to ReConstructing on a Budget!


With [Building on a Budget](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Archive.aspx?tag=Building%20on%20a%20Budget&description=Building%20on%20a%20Budget) retired, I've seen many of you clamoring for something to fill that hole. Two weeks ago, I put out the call for budget decks to see if this could be a popular recurring feature. All I can say is wow—the response was incredible! I'll definitely be taking a look at budget decks again in the future.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc205_infamy.jpg)[Knight of Infamy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) | Art by [Peter Mohrbacher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Peter+Mohrbacher%22%5D)


Before getting into the thick of reconstructing, I'd like to lay out a few basic tenets I have for budget deck building. These can be bent in some cases, but I follow these for the most part. They're going to be useful in helping you to follow my decision making.


### 1. Include No New Rares or Mythic rares


Defining budget is trickier than herding a group of velociraptors. Everyone's collection and card accessibility situations are different. Some players have every rare they could possibly need and only some powerful mythic rares are off limits, while others feel budget is nothing but commons. It's hard to strike the appropriate balance when the sides are so disparate.


My general rule is to make the deck as budget as possible, then allow people to add in cards they own.


If the core of a deck is all cards that are easy to acquire, then it's easy for people to tweak that to fit their situation. If the core of the deck starts off further along the rare axis and asks people to remove cards, then many budget players are right back where they started with, by finding substitutions for cards they can't acquire. As a result, I won't add any rares or mythic rares into the decklist that weren't already in the original submission.


With that said, at the end of the article I'll note some rares to consider and other directions you could take the deck if you had access to everything. That way you have some direction with knowing what cards to look out for.


### 2. ...Except For Mana Fixing


The one exception to the above are mana-fixing lands. Often, these are rare cards. I refuse to compromise on mana, and neither should you. I might choose which decks to cover based on the mana feasibility—a five-color Modern deck that needs twelve *Ravnica* dual lands is out of the question—but for Standard purposes I'm going to be adding in lands as necessary.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc205_isolated.jpg)[Isolated Chapel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isolated+Chapel) | Art by [Cliff Childs](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Cliff+Childs%22%5D)


Fortunately, the upside is that these are mostly cards you should try to own anyway since they can be used in so many decks. I know many of you go to great lengths to acquire dual lands each time a new block comes out, and that effort definitely isn't in vain: you'll end up playing with them a *lot*.


### 3. No Substitutions


The number one mistake players make when trying to build on a budget is to make substitutions. You might look at a Pro Tour winning decklist, identify twelve cards you're going to need to replace, and begin looking for substitutions.


The problem is that this means you're just building a much worse deck. Budget should not mean bad (more on this in a moment)—it should just mean budget. Most cards don't have substitutions. If you have to change the arbitrary win condition in your control deck, that's one thing, but there are no good substitutions for cards like [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) and [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft).


Instead of trying to retrofit a top-end spaceship with go-kart engines, you're better off just setting it aside and focusing on finding a sturdy ship that you can more reasonably build a good model of.


### 4. Budget Does not Equal Bad


Many competitive players seem to equate the word "budget" with "bad deck." That simply isn't true. Playing on a budget is about working with limited resources, and there are still plenty of good decks you can make with those resources. You can still build an awesome spaceship, you just might not be able to fit dinosaurs on it.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc205_oblivion.jpg)[Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) | Art by [Franz Vohwinkel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Franz+Vohwinkel%22%5D)


Most formats have at least one good mostly commons and uncommon deck, whether a red, green, and/or white beatdown deck; a combo deck using a ton of common mana production and card filtering; or even some control decks. Look for the best decks on a budget and you can play competitively without needing a huge collection.


With that said, let's get down to business for today.

### The Battle Plan


Solly's deck takes an aggressive midrange stance. A handful of removal can clear out any blockers while the creatures push into combat over and over, even larger than usual thanks to exalted. It is fairly straightforward: play creatures, attack, and use your removal to get any resistance out of the way.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc205_nefarox.jpg)[Nefarox, Overlord of Grixis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nefarox%2C+Overlord+of+Grixis) | Art by [Aleksi Briclot](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Aleksi+Briclot%22%5D)


The key to reconstructing this deck is going to be refining the theme to be the best it can inside of our restrictions. Right now it falls a little into the midrange camp. You can't up the mana curve to put it more on the controlling midrange spectrum since many of the control finishers I'd want are rares or mythic rare—but moving it to be more aggressive is definitely an option.


Additionally, this deck might just need some more texture. Right now it's just a combination of exalted cards and removal. It really isn't bendy at all; it's very predictive and straightforward. I'd like to give it some other levels of interaction so that you can mess with your opponent's game plan.


### Deck Breakdown


Solly has a good exalted start here. Is every piece necessary? Let's look through the cards in detail!


**Exalted Creatures**


I'll start right off with the suite of exalted creatures Solly provided. All twenty-two creatures in this deck have exalted, no doubt with the idea that having each creature pump your creatures up would work well in multiples.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc205_aven3.jpg)It can be a little misleading though. Let's say you have three [Knight of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Glory)s on the battlefield. Which deals more damage: attacking with one Knight or attacking with all three?


The answer is attacking with all three. Of course, there are reasons to attack with just one—a 5/4 has a better chance of connecting with your opponent's skull than a 2/1. However, there are also reasons not to attack with just one. You don't want to time walk your attack step over and over by getting chump blocked. There will certainly be times when I want the ability—it is strong—but at the same time it's important to not overvalue it.


The way I view exalted is it's a nice bonus for combat and useful for dealing some extra damage—but I only want to play cards that are at least on the edge of Constructed playability without the exalted ability. A deck doesn't need to have a ton of exalted for the ability to have a worthwhile effect.


For example, Servant of Neferox is a 3/1 with exalted for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161225105850im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless2.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif). Would I play a 3/1 for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161225105850im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless2.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif)? No. Exalted doesn't add enough to change that. On the other hand, a 2/1 with protection for two is certainly on the edge of Constructed playability. Toss in exalted, and you have a mighty fine card.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc205_knight3.jpg)Using this criteria, the cards I am interested in keeping around are the [two Knights](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&name=+%5Bknight%5D+%5Bof%5D&set=+%5B%22Magic+2013%22%5D) and Nefarox. Neither [Duty-Bound Dead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duty-Bound+Dead) or the Servant are cards I would normally want to play enough without exalted, and the exalted doesn't push them over the top.


[Aven Squire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aven+Squire) is a little closer. Its evasion is especially noteworthy because evasion alongside exalted is very powerful. I could see playing it in this deck, but I ultimately found a few other cards with evasion I preferred.


**[Angelic Benediction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angelic+Benediction)**


[Angelic Benediction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angelic+Benediction) helps with the exalted theme a lot by pushing a creature out of the way. However, at four mana it is a little more costly than I would prefer. Most of the time, I would just rather have a cheap removal spell that can permanently clear a blocker away.


**[Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring)**


[Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) is a great fit for this deck. It's flexible removal that can deal with everything from Equipment to Planeswalkers. You don't want your hand to be overloaded with them since it costs three and is sorcery speed, but I definitely want to play three.

### Tweaking on a Budget


After all of those changes, we have quite a bit of room to work with. How much room is there really to be added with just commons and uncommon? You might be surprised...


**[Vampire Nighthawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Nighthawk)**


Nighthawk is a powerhouse in any kind of creature matchup—and he's even better when he's being boosted by exalted! While not great against control, his strength against beatdown is solid enough that I'm willing to start with four copies. This might need to drop down to three, but it entirely depends on how creature-focused your local environment is.

**[Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls)**


While tokens work poorly with exalted, [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) is an immensely powerful card. **[Ravenous Rats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Rats)**


Now this might not have been one you were expecting! However, some discard helps give me the kind of bendiness I was looking for earlier. While it might seem like nothing but an annoyance, it's so much more. The tiny incremental advantage that [Ravenous Rats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Rats) provides adds up, giving you a body while also nibbling a card out of your opponent's hand. In combination with a couple [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)es (which I am also adding) it gives you some much-needed control over the flow of the game.

**[Doomed Traveler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomed+Traveler)**


The versatility of [Doomed Traveler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomed+Traveler) is nice. It can block early to buy time to find Nighthawk against quick decks, or swing in for 2 on turn two if you have an exalted creature. It gives you an aerial token, which works well with exalted. It gives you another turn-one play, which works well with this deck's two-and-three-heavy curve. It's also a Soldier. Why does that matter? Well...


**[War Falcon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=War+Falcon)**


Now this guy might seem like a little odd at first, but let me run through his many uses.


First of all, even if he can't attack, the falcon blocks well. Against beatdown decks you're going to take the control role, trading off creatures early and surviving to stabilize with Nighthawk or [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls). [War Falcon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=War+Falcon) trades with [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets), [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft), [Huntmaster of the Fells](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Huntmaster+of+the+Fells), and more.


Second, it's pretty easy for him to attack in this deck. With eleven Knights and Soldiers combined, he's not too difficult to push into combat. Your exalted Knights can pump him, and so in many cases you can be attacking for 3 in the air on turn two! You don't want more than a couple because an all-[War Falcon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=War+Falcon) draw is pretty poor, but a couple means you'll see one every now and again in your opener and be able to build around it.

### Unbudgetifications


If I was looking to unbudgetify this deck and start adding in rares, what might I do? Well, there are a few different ways I could see taking it.


One way would be to go a much more aggressive route. The Knights are humans, so a [Champion of the Parish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish)-powered deck with a full set of [Doomed Traveler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomed+Traveler)s, Champions, Knights, and [Gather the Townsfolk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gather+the+Townsfolk) would be a place to try. I'd top out with [Sublime Archangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sublime+Archangel), although you could also play one or two [Angelic Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angelic+Destiny) depending on how popular [Vapor Snag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vapor+Snag) is in your metagame.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc205_champion.jpg)[Champion of the Parish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish) | Art by [Svetlin Velinov](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Svetlin+Velinov%22%5D)


You could also continue to push the midrange/control avenue. Cutting some of your cheap aggressive cards for [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) and [Sorin, Lord of Innistrad](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sorin%2C+Lord+of+Innistrad) changes the deck pretty radically, but allows it to grind down your opponent in the long game.


[Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel) could also be a route to explore since it's good alongside [Ravenous Rats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Rats). At that point you could also try some other creatures that are strong with flickering, like [Disciple of Bolas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+Bolas) and [Skinrender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skinrender). [Disciple of Bolas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+Bolas) is especially awesome alongside exalted: Get a power bump on one of your creatures, and then sacrifice it after combat while the power is still huge!
