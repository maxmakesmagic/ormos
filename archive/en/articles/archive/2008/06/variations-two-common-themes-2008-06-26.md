Variations on Two Common Themes
===============================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on June 26, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







This week’s Swimming with Sharks has only one PTQ Top 8 proper to discuss, but it is a doozy.


As always, the blue box is the PTQ win; the white boxes are Top 8 near misses. Congratulations to Bryan Eleyet, who took down the Portland, Oregon PTQ.

Cool new tech for this week seems to be more permission out of the sideboard. [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate), the un-[Remove Soul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remove+Soul), seems like a good answer to all-in breakers like [Mind Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring) out of the mirror; [Counterbore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbore) may be even better. The latter seems like it would punish decks leaning on recursion or key facilitators, such as [Mulldrifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter), though it seems very expensive and situational.

Four [Vivid Creek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vivid+Creek)s.


Four [Vivid Marsh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vivid+Marsh)es.


Staple them together, and you get two lands that look like they produce black and blue but secretly pump out red or green mana in service to [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout).



![Vivid Creek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Vivid+Creek)

![Vivid Marsh](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Vivid+Marsh)

We’ve established in the last couple of months that largely due to the printing of [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), Faeries has shifted slightly from aggro-control to more or less “true” control. It is a sit-there [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) deck that has the space to play threats during the opponent’s turn (or alternately with “something unpleasant on the stack,” as Jon Sonne used to say when [Aether Burst](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Aether%2BBurst)ing his [Mystic Snake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Snake) or [Flametongue Kavu](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flametongue+Kavu)) in order to generate card advantage or simply raise the hairs on the back of the other guy’s neck. What do sit-there [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) decks love? That’s right, a [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God)  [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=+Damnation). In Block, black and blue don’t have a proper [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation), but they can splash [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) with Vivid lands in order to get much of the effect.


Red hits the Merfolk or Kithkin on the ground, and not playing green allows the Faeries deck to maintain its own board position freely, which is not an option with a legitimate [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation). On balance, were Goldman-Kirst behind in a mirror, he could apply the green side to take out the opposing Faeries, potentially as a surprise play.


The eight Vivid lands also let him splash a trio of [Wispmare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wispmare)s out of the sideboard, [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) chompers extraordinaire and, conveniently, 1/3 bodies left back to block any remaining 1/1 Faerie Rogues.

The most exciting new deck to come out of this PTQ is the Doran Shamans build that Christopher Pauly took to second place. It is reminiscent of some other Shamans decks that we have seen with [Rage Forger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rage+Forger), but plays just much more awesome Shamans to go along with it.

The most awesome of all is obviously the one with the trickiest mana cost, [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower). Doran is, yes, a Shaman. He is awesome at battle in and of himself, and works just fine with [Rage Forger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rage+Forger). The little played—but still awesome—[Masked Admirers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Masked+Admirers)... also a Shaman. You can pretty much just read [Masked Admirers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Masked+Admirers) and see that this card comes down on the third turn following [Bosk Banneret](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bosk+Banneret) and clogs the ground while advancing Pauly’s board position.


[Leaf-Crowned Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leaf-Crowned+Elder)... Alpha Shaman among Shamans. That’s the one that flips up the [Fulminator Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fulminator+Mage)s and [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus)es (convenient) does all the disgusting card drawing. The Doran Shaman deck draws lots of cards with its four-drop Shamans, blows up land with its three drop Shamans, kills almost everything with its two mana black spells, and can kick all kinds of butt with Doran and [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus).


And yes, it can seamlessly play ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20170708024743im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black-red.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20170708024743im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black-red.gif) three-drops next to [Teneb, the Harvester](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teneb%2C+the+Harvester)’s colors (also on three), in a base green deck that needs green on the second turn, and as much as ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) to run properly. How can this be accomplished? It’s Vivid lands and [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool) again!


Pauly ran eight Vivid lands next to [Murmuring Bosk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murmuring+Bosk) (which can admittedly pay whatever is needed on the normally difficult Doran), [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool) (of course)... *and [Primal Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Beyond).* [Primal Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Beyond) is not an obvious choice in a deck like this with relatively few Elementals, but in addition to giving a lift on [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher) or [Fulminator Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fulminator+Mage), [Primal Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Beyond) is an absolutely perfect pair to [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool). The [Primal Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Beyond) might not be able to tap for everything (when it’s not an Elemental being played), but with [Primal Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Beyond) in play, [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool) has no such reservations.

Now *obviously* we saved the best for last. My favorite deck from the week is the "Little Kid" Green-White deck that Camran Ahmad ran, presumably cribbed from Brian David-Marshall’s article [Expected and Unexpected](/en/articles/archive/week-was/expected-and-unexpected-2008-06-20), last week’s [The Week That Was](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/columnarchive&column=TheWeekThatWas).


This is a deck that I have been working on with Asher Hecht (ManningBot) and Julian Levin (a.k.a. Mother Superior IV), present and past Number One Apprentices. The deck actually came to me while I was tabulating Regionals Top 8 listings, and I saw some Standard versions of the green-white strategy. While the deck doesn’t seem *obviously* such, it is actually almost as linear as the tribally themed decks we see elsewhere in this summer’s Block Constructed (Kithkin, Faeries, Merfolk, etc.). The green-white hybrid mana cards are super easy to play.... You can almost play only green sources of mana but for a particular All-Star that requires nine [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains).




![Gaddock Teeg](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Gaddock+Teeg)


Patrick Chapin recently challenged me to break Block, and while I don’t think I quite accomplished that, the "Little Kid" Green-White deck provides some nice resistance to about 80% of the commonly available matchups, largely due to [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg). If you stick Teeg (optimally on turn three in most matchups), he turns off most of the breakers in the format. Faeries loses the ability to race because it can no longer generate tempo with [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command), and in fact all eight commonly played permission spells go offline (the other typical four-of slot, [Broken Ambitions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broken+Ambitions) has an “X” in the mana cost).


Why is turn three the so often the best turn to play [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg)? If you play him on turn two, he might get killed by a [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion); likewise, if you play him on turn two on the draw, [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout). BDM called the deck a “BarkshellBlowout” and [Barkshell Blessing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barkshell+Blessing) is one of the key cards in the deck. This innocuous-seeming [Giant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Giant+Growth) “counters” [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) and [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout), two of the few commonly played removal cards that get around Teeg in Block.


I have always approached deck design from a position of aggressive reaction—that is, allowing the rest of the metagame to shape choices and then attacking the expected—but as the "Little Kid" Green-White deck is a take on a linear group of cards that was in fact inspired pretty closely by recent deck lists elsewhere, it has some synergistically built-in offense. If you’re on the attack, two-drop into [Shield of the Oversoul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shield+of+the+Oversoul) is a nice way to go, though my favorite has been [Wilt-Leaf Cavaliers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wilt-Leaf+Cavaliers) into Shield, which is pretty hard to break main deck.


The biggest incentive to this deck (and the leading deck when I first started working on the green-white deck, though now, as predicted, much less popular) is Kithkin / Mirror Master—the matchup, that is. Basically, "Little Kid" Green-White has a better drop at every mana point, can attack without reservation, and counters all the straight white breakers with [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) (no [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession), which is a six only masquerading as a three; nor [Mirrorweave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirrorweave), obviously a four). Kithkin is a super easy matchup... You basically never get attacked after the first few turns, they basically never block because [Barkshell Blessing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barkshell+Blessing) will kill all their blockers, and even if you don’t draw Teeg and they have the nuts, you can turn it around with a surprise [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher).



![Oversoul of Dusk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Oversoul+of+Dusk)

While there are many ways to break up the incentives to this deck, not very many are played in Block, so it is in a sense a metagame deck (you are gambling that you don’t get out-Rogued, or out-mid-ranged)... But there is a pretty big strategic hole in Elementals, and a potentially bigger one with the new Doran Shaman deck (talk about being “out-midranged!”). [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw) is a big problem for this deck, and [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) is a super blocker, even if you have something along the lines of an [Oversoul of Dusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oversoul+of+Dusk) out of the sideboard (which I would play, personally).

I like "Little Kid" Green-White a great deal just because I worked on it; the limiting factors are the popularity of Elementals in your area (and how good the Elementals pilots are) and whether there are still a lot of Kithkin in the local metagame. Up Elementals (or Doran Shaman) + down Kithkin (as seems to be the case this week) is not a promising combination for the "Little Kid".

**2. What decks did you beat in the Top 8?**


In the Top 8 I beat a Faeries Mirror (he had [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder)), Merfolk (he played turn-two Sygg on me all three games... I played turn-two [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) all three), and White Weenie. The Merfolk guy had [Leech Bonder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leech+Bonder) out of the board, and *destroyed* me with turn-three and -four [Leech Bonder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leech+Bonder)s Game 2.


**3. What is the major incentive to playing Faeries?**


The major incentive to playing Faeries is that it is the best deck. It is versatile, adapting well to being the control or the aggro in any matchup, and that its cards are just very powerful. Most of your plays are at instant speed, be they threats or answers, and that means you can make decisions exactly when you want to and no sooner. No other deck in the format has that option outside of the occasional [Makeshift Mannequin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makeshift+Mannequin).


**4. What was your toughest matchup in testing?**


I’ve heard that the Elemental deck is tough, but I never tested it. I tested the three-deck metagame quite a bit, and although an explosive draw from White Weenie was scary, Faeries had the edge against both Five-Color Control and White Weenie if both players knew what was going on. Frankly, I was more worried about the mirror, which is why I went to 3 [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) in the main.


**5. What deck, if any, did you lose to during the day?**


The only round I lost was Round 5. I kept an [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island), [Mutavault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault), spells hand on the draw and never saw another land. My opponent played out some [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) and Vivid lands and killed me quickly. I sideboarded for Five-Color Control only to discover that I was actually playing against a Green-Black-Blue Elves deck. Sans [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion), I was cold to a turn-two [Vanquish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vanquish)er.



![Mistbind Clique](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mistbind+Clique)

**6. What card was your MVP?**
I drew a lot of [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom)s on the day, but the MVP was definitely [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique). When you cast a Clique on the upkeep following your fourth turn, you just have this sense that you’re going to win the game, and it’s usually true if you play smart. If Wizards wanted to neuter Faeries, this would be the card to target. [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) is great, but it’s vulnerable to the same hate as the rest of the deck. Mistbind is a [Time Walk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Walk) and a really big body, the only one of its kind in the deck.


**7. Why did you select the Fae?**


I tested with Asher Hecht, Steve Sadin, and Julian Levin primarily. After the PTQ in Boston where I played White Weenie, Asher assured me that Faeries had to be the best deck—he had missed Top 8 but been very happy with the Fae, losing only the mirror I think. I was reluctant to run it because everyone said the mirror was really random and I hated [Broken Ambitions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broken+Ambitions). I found a way to solve both issues at once—I made room for maindeck [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) by cutting back on the [Broken Ambitions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broken+Ambitions). It improved the mirror, and the deck.


**8. I assume if you had it to do all over again, you would play the same deck... but what changes, if any, would you make if you had to run it back?**


I think the list is pretty clean, and I’m a big fan of the spell diversity offered by all the two-ofs in the main. Two [Broken Ambitions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broken+Ambitions) was perfect. They didn’t clog my hand but they showed up often enough to make my opponent think about them. [Mind Shatter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Shatter) was underwhelming, but I didn’t play against Five-Color Control at all so that may be why. I think [Consign to Dream](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consign+to+Dream) is probably better than [Eyeblight’s Ending](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eyeblight%E2%80%99s+Ending), so I’d switch those unless I expected a lot of Kithkin. [Peppersmoke](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Peppersmoke) was also really good for me, so squeezing in a third (perhaps going to 1 [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique)) might be good, especially if you expect a lot of mirror matches. [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique) is pretty terrible in the mirror, as the red zone is unkind to 1-toughness men.
