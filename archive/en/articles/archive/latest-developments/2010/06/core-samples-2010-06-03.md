Core Samples
============



 Posted in **Latest Developments**
 on June 4, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/tom.jpg)
By Tom LaPille




Tom LaPille makes things. Some of the things he makes are card sets, like Dark Ascension and Born of the Gods. Sometimes he makes stories, too. Sometimes he makes unexpected things, like 16th-century Japanese clothing. He's probably making something right now. 






It's getting close to the time when we start telling you in earnest about ***Magic** 2011*. From **Magic** Ramp;D's perspective, however, doing that is impossible without talking about the legacy of ***Magic** 2010*, the first core set we made under our new model. To some degree, it was an attempt to reboot **Magic**, and to do what we now wish had been done from the beginning. We got many of those things right, but while making ***Magic** 2011*, we learned that some of the things we did in ***Magic** 2010* couldn't be done again, and we also discovered a small number of things that we still did a little wrong. Today I'll talk about a few of each of these things.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_m11.jpg)Fireball
![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_jhgb6raftghsdion.jpg)Some cards with simple, appealing names and concepts don't actually have simple text boxes. [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) is one of them. [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball)'s history goes all the way back to Beta, a set that had many beautifully simple cards but also many cards that used complication to get top-down flavor across. As far as I can tell, [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) was an attempt to put the **Dungeons amp; Dragons** spell *Fireball* on a **Magic** card. One of the original target audiences for **Magic** was roleplaying gamers, so **Magic**'s [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) has the splitting clause on it because *Fireball* could also hit multiple enemies. The actual mechanics behind *Fireball*'s splitting changed from edition to edition, but [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) is clearly recognizable as a translation. 

The resulting **Magic** text box is, frankly, pretty weird. It talks about rounding, any number of targets, and paying extra mana. However, that text lets you do a bunch of cool things, and I would say that you haven't lived until you've paid three mana to fireball three [Illusionary Servant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Illusionary+Servant)s for zero each. Also, the legacy of *Fireball* lived on in games other than **Magic**, where game designers with similar backgrounds to the first **Magic** designers made similar fireball spells that could also hit multiple targets. The **Dungeons amp; Dragons** *Fireball* created a common language of area-of-effect fire spells, and we are happy to continue to speak it in ***Magic** 2011*.

Lightning Bolt
![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_uyds354dfghabn.jpg)[Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) has a fantastic name, a simple and appealing effect, and is famously powerful. Its return was something that ***Magic** 2010* lead designer Aaron Forsythe requested, and it was considered carefully. Some members of Ramp;D initially thought that it would be too powerful; playtesting showed that their concerns were valid, but that Standard could handle it just fine. When [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)'s presence in ***Magic** 2010* was revealed to the world, many players had similar concerns. Happily, our playtesting proved correct, and Standard is happily chugging away. [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) is one of the format's defining cards, but the format is very popular and everything now appears to be balanced rather well. 

[Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) is a strong card, and we understand that. Don't expect it to be around forever; part of what makes **Magic** fun to play is that it changes over time, and there's only so much red Standard decks can change when the best burn spell is so obvious. We are, however, keeping it around for one more year.

Dragonskull Summit
![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_uyfd35dfghbs.jpg)As a developer, one of the pieces of ***Magic** 2010* that I am happiest about are its rare lands. The pain lands from *Ninth Edition* and *Tenth Edition* were tournament staples for a long time, but it was difficult to explain to new players why they should play with [Yavimaya Coast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yavimaya+Coast) in their green-blue deck when they may end up taking damage as a punishment for trying to cast their spells. The pain lands are also fiddly to play with, since using them for colored mana requires either adjusting a twenty sided die or writing down a new number on a sheet of paper.

[Dragonskull Summit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonskull+Summit) and its friends were created by ***Magic** 2010* lead designer Aaron Forsythe, and they solve tons of problems that the painlands had. They don't require any life adjustment, often come into play untapped, and are powerful enough to be tournament cards. When ***Magic** 2010* released, there was some unhappiness from players about these lands' inability to cast spells on turn one. Having played with them for nine months before the rest of the world, I was confident that they were good enough anyway, and I'm happy to see that everyone else agreed. We like how these lands work, and we're going to keep them around.

Baneslayer Angel
One of the most famous and iconic early **Magic** cards was [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel). For years and years, that card was popular. There are several reasons why this was the case. It is an angel, which is a trope that most of us recognize. It had art that many **Magic** players found appealing. It has flying and vigilance, so attacking with it was fairly safe and didn't leave you without the ability to block. It was one of very few white cards in early **Magic** that was actually big enough to put a game away. All of this combined to make [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel) one of the iconic cards that players thought of when they thought of **Magic**. 

[Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel) served as the face of **Magic** until *Fifth Edition*, from which it is mysteriously absent, ostensibly for being too powerful. This was a curious move, and to my modern eye one of the odder things that early developers did. [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel) was far from a powerful tournament card at the time, and I can't recall it being played in any tournament decks at all. Removing a card that was so loved and didn't have onerous effects on the Standard environment strikes me as a rather foolish move.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_ytds3d4fdfghjsbn.jpg)One of the design decisions that Aaron Forsythe made while he was designing ***Magic** 2010* was to include [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel) as an uncommon, which is the rarity at which it was first printed. No doubt, he was thinking about [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel)'s status as one of the marquee cards of early **Magic**. This inspired ***Magic** 2010* lead developer Erik Lauer to wonder if he could capture the same lightning that made [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel) great with another angel, one that would be legitimately powerful in tournaments and serve as a new face of **Magic** for a new era. The result of that was [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel). 

[Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) has done an admirable job of picking up the torch that [Serra Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel) carried for so long. It is an angel, it has appealing art, and it's powerful enough to play in tournaments with a straight face. We are happy to have it be one of the faces that represents **Magic** to the world, and it will do that job for another year.

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20121127091547im_/http://www.wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_m11X.jpg)  
Although there are plenty of cards from ***Magic** 2010* making a return, there are also a number of ***Magic** 2010* cards that will not be coming back in ***Magic** 2011*. I'll chat briefly about a few of them. 

Open the Vaults
![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_uyf3s4dfghjdfns.jpg)One of the special things about **Magic**'s core sets is that they are not beholden to any particular setting or flavor. Instead, they are intended to represent a baseline from which **Magic** can vary in other sets. Under our new model, however, during their time in Standard they are inextricably connected with the block that comes immediately before it. For ***Magic** 2010*, that was the *Shards of Alara* block. 

 One of the five shards in *Shards of Alara* is Esper, which had an artifact theme. Although [Open the Vaults](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Open+the+Vaults) is not creatively connected to Esper, we made the card with the intention that it would interact powerfully with Esper's artifacts. In that we succeeded, as [Open the Vaults](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Open+the+Vaults) was powerful in Standard with [Time Sieve](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Sieve), and Niels Viaene made the Top 8 of [Pro Tourâ€“San Diego](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptsd10/welcome) with a [Time Sieve](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Sieve)-less version of [Open the Vaults](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Open+the+Vaults). However, those potential interactions will be gone when *Shards of Alara* rotates out of Standard. That would have made [Open the Vaults](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Open+the+Vaults) a much less interesting card to have around, so it won't be coming back. 

Mold Adder
![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_ytef45fgsnet.jpg)The story of [Open the Vaults](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Open+the+Vaults) is overall a happy one. It came out, did the job we intended it to do, and will be exiting stage left at the same time as all of the other cards it needed to shine. [Mold Adder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mold+Adder) is the opposite case. [Flashfreeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flashfreeze), [Deathmark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathmark), and [Celestial Purge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Purge) are cards that have been largely adopted by the **Magic**-playing public as excellent sideboard cards, and every once in a while they show up in maindecks as well, when things get a little out of balance.

[Karplusan Strider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karplusan+Strider) was the first card that was made to be a member of a color-hating uncommon cycle. No one played it, so [Mold Adder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mold+Adder) was created to replace it in ***Magic** 2010*. However, no one played [Mold Adder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mold+Adder) either. We think that cards like [Celestial Purge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Purge), [Deathmark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathmark), and [Flashfreeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flashfreeze) are good for **Magic**, so in ***Magic** 2011* we once again tried to find a green card that could have similar effects on Constructed **Magic**. I will be slightly sad to see [Mold Adder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mold+Adder) go, as I'm the one who named it, but I'd be much more excited to have a similar card that people were actually happy to play.

I got permission to tell you about a few other cards that aren't in ***Magic** 2011*. They are ...

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/ld/ld93_hgsx2c4e5r6t23b5.jpg)  
For these cards, though, I can't yet talk about why they won't be returning. I suppose for now you'll just have to guess.
