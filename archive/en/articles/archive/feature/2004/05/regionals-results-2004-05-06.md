Regionals Results
=================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on May 6, 2004 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_alexshvartsman.jpg)
By Alex Shvartsman











The results are in, and Affinity is a clear winner at this year's U.S. Regionals. As of my writing this Ravager Affinity decks earned more than 50% of all U.S. Nationals invites that were up for grabs this Saturday – an unprecedented number! 

Goblin decks were the only other heavily represented archetype in the top 8s across the country, most versions showing up with some Swamps for [Patriarch's Bidding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Patriarch%27s+Bidding). Tooth and Nail and White Control decks made a notable appearance, while every other deck to earn an invitation showed up only a couple of times. All told, there were eleven archetypes that took players to the invitation slots:

Considering many of the Regional tournaments drew over 500 players, these numbers are an excellent indication as to the state of Standard at the moment.

| *Was there less anti-affinity this time around?* |

So what is it about Ravager Affinity that makes it so successful? Obviously the deck is very resilient or it would not enjoy such success. However, that alone is not enough. After all, Regionals in other countries saw Affinity perform well, but not this well. Looking at the deck lists from various American Regionals, I believe the answer is the lack of anti-Affinity cards in the main decks. Players in Japan and elsewhere went as far as to run 4 [Shatter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shatter) and 4 [Echoing Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Ruin)s main in their Goblin decks. Now I am not saying that doing so is the right metagame call – after all, your deck becomes far weaker against anyone not playing artifacts – but that kind of hate may have prevented Affinity strategies from dominating other metagames the way they have dominated in the United States.

In [last week's column](/en/articles/archive/regionals-2004-04-29) I was able to correctly identify the top 3 archetypes of this Regionals for you. This week we can look at some of the interesting rogue decks that were able to succeed in this field.

| *Bad news for opposing Affinity decks…* |

Not only did Pierce qualify for Nationals using this highly unorthodox strategy, he also finished in first place overall, winning his Regional. Considering that six of the top 8 decks in his region were Affinity, [Disciple of the Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault) must have been an MVP in this deck. Affinity decks often rely on Disciple to win the game and literally cannot win without many artifacts being sent to the graveyard. Pierce's deck turns the table on them with four Disciples of his own. There are even [Misery Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Misery+Charm)s in the deck to bring the Disciple back should an opposing Affinity player manage to remove it via [Electrostatic Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrostatic+Bolt) or [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast).

Worship might be something most players overlooked in this metagame, but it is a powerful answer to decks like Goblins and Tooth and Nail. Affinity is able to get around it by using the [Disciple of the Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault) effect to finish off an opponent, but after sideboarding it is apparently negated by [Ivory Mask](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ivory+Mask)s and [Leonin Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Elder)s. Another very interesting factor in this deck is the choice to run as Green-White control in the first game, but with the ability to morph into a Tooth and Nail deck should the match-up warrant it.

This deck is particularly nasty against Affinity, since [March of the Machines](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=March+of+the+Machines) (which you can often cast on turn 3) effectively [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon)s then, not to mention prevents them from using [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp). Once Lattice comes into play, it's [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) for everyone! At that point lands just won't stay in play, and you are left with a 6/6 artifact creature to beat up on your opponent with. 

Although [Thoughtcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtcast) is never going to be as good in this deck as it is in Affinity, paying three mana to draw two cards is good enough – and [Thirst For Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+For+Knowledge) is excellent with all the Talismans, extra Lattices and Citadels. The deck wastes no slots on victory conditions as setting up its combo is a perfectly good way to win, so it is able to play a high mana ratio and a lot of card drawing in order to set it up quickly. In metagames where beatdown decks such as Goblin Bidding are more prevalent, you might want to replace [Thoughtcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtcast)s with [Sanctimony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanctimony), COP: Red or another quick way to stave off damage long enough to draw Wrath or Vengeance.

 Play Of The Week *Courtesy of Dale K.*

“I was playing a Sliver deck, and my friend was playing his death-by-token deck which featured lots of elf mana, [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon), [Snake Basket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snake+Basket), and many ways to abuse hordes of green snake tokens. 

I played first and dropped a land every turn. His first two turns included [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise), [Gaea's Cradle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Cradle), and [Snake Basket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snake+Basket). 

On his third turn, he played [Channel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Channel) and paid 15 life to activate the [Snake Basket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snake+Basket) for a boatload of snakes. He then played [Juniper Order Advocate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Juniper+Order+Advocate) to make all of them 2/2s. I was saving my mana for an end-of-turn [Krosan Tusker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker) cycle, but the opportunity was just too good... I cycled [Slice and Dice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slice+and+Dice) in response and killed all the tokens, leaving him at 5 life with just the Advocate in play. 

The really sad thing for him was he had [Fireball](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) and [Last-Ditch Effort](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Last-Ditch+Effort) in hand. It was a 3 player game, and he could have killed both of us on turn 4, but he couldn't even use the [Last-Ditch Effort](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Last-Ditch+Effort) since he tapped out for the Advocate”
