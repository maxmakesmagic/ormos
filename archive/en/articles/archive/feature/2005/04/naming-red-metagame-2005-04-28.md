Naming the Red Metagame
=======================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on April 28, 2005 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







> 
> *“All Paul Sligh did was to qualify for the Pro Tour with the deck and post it on the ‘Net. It bothers me, it boggles my mind why this guy is so famous… I think people having their names on the deck is a bit silly. It isn't a big deal that I don't get any credit for the deck, as long as Sligh doesn't either.”*  
>  -Dave Price
> 
> 
> 


One of the most influential decks of all time also carries with it one of the most controversial names: Sligh. It has become synonymous with any sort of Red Deck. Red beatdown? Sligh. Red mid-range? Sligh. As we will see below, even big fatty Bogardan Phoenix decks that blow up every permanent in play are also Sligh (well, sort of).

“Sligh” gets its name from Paul Sligh, a player who won a Pro Tour Qualifier with the above Monored deck close to 10 years ago. Though it bears his name, Sligh himself didn't design the deck, which is generally attributed to Jay Schneider.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/global/images/mtgcom_daily_mf18_pic1_en.jpg)The Sligh deck was very special for exactly one reason. Sure, it added flava to competitive **Magic** by breaking down the formerly newspaper-like metagame of black and white decks, but that isn't the reason Sligh became so memorable. Basically, the game of tournament **Magic** as defined by the first Pro Tour started on turn four. The defining threat was a 4/5 for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130050im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless3.gif), hopefully followed up by a good old ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130050im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless3.gif) ([Erhnam Djinn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Erhnam+Djinn) and [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon)), and the defensive card of choice was, and still is, a ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161225105850im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless2.gif) Sorcery. Sure, there were some cards that saw pre-four mana play, primarily out of the Necropotence deck, but even the [Knights of Stromgald](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knights+of+Stromgald)-packing black mage defined his game with a four-mana artifact in those days.


Sligh, on the other hand, started playing **Magic** on turn one. It had essentially the same game plan as a modern Red Deck… only instead of [Jackal Pup](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup) it had [Goblins of the Flarg](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblins+of+the+Flarg) (yes, yes, on the same team as half-dozen Dwarves, I know). Starting the game so early allowed [Ironclaw Orcs](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ironclaw+Orcs) and company to start chipping away at the opponent's life total to set up a lead, even if the opponent had a better mid-game. This meant that once the opponent's slow four-mana game came online, Incinerate and company could end it all while the other guy was still tapping for cards such as [Serra Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Angel) or [Ihsan's Shade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ihsan%27s+Shade) to lock out the [Dwarven Trader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Trader) beatdown.


The Sligh deck in essentially the above form was adopted by Pro Tour greats Pat Chapin and Dave Price. Pat added a lone [Ball Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning) and made third place at the 1996 PT Dallas Juniors (this was before the advent of the modern JSS). Dave began his transformation of the deck from its [Orcish Cannoneers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orcish+Cannoneers) board-control focus to beatdown by going all out with the damage sources. Price's version had four [Ball Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning)s and ignored any kind of mid-game card advantage plan. Dave started off his Red Deck legend by winning a Paris PTQ and then followed up with a perfect 7-0 at U.S. Nationals 1997:

This deck was perfectly suited to fighting the U.S. Nationals 1997 metagame. Price accurately predicted that the pros of U.S. Nationals would avoid cards such as [Freewind Falcon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Freewind+Falcon) and instead go for power cards such as [Necropotence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) and the returning [Swords to Plowshares](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares). Necropotence decks had a general disadvantage against Red Decks, and [Lava Hounds](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Hounds) lost its disadvantage (but none of its virtues) against slow Thawing Glaciers decks with [Swords to Plowshares](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares). Though originally conceived of as a pure metagame choice, the 1997 version of Deadguy Red was appropriated en masse, and inspired young designers around the world.


Now about a year later, legendary Internet writer Jamie Wakefield unleashed his own brand of Sligh on the realms of Dominia. While Price's innovations led to a Pro Tour win in L.A. and a follow-up Top 8 at U.S. Nationals 1998, there is actually a pretty good reason to look at Jamie's Regionals deck.

There are two kooky and influential things about this deck. First of all, it is 62 cards with 26 lands. Second of all, it only has three [Jackal Pup](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup)s . . . but makes room for a dedicated late game with [Bogardan Phoenix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bogardan+Phoenix) and [Jokulhaups](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jokulhaups) (super combo). The highly influential aspect of Jamie's deck was that it inspired Wisconsin cheesehead Brian Kowal to make a Rath Cycle PTQ deck that would ruin Red Deck naming forever.


Kowal's Ponza Rotta Red, or simply “Ponza” as it would later be called, was born on the Wakefield principles of 26/62. Probably you are wondering what a Ponza is, and why someone would want to name a deck after it. The answer to the first question is that it's kind of like a calzone, but deep fried; the answer to the second is that it is split up into different classes of cards -- crust (land), meat (creatures), and of course cheese -- i.e. “no one will ever know and we have to live with it.”

![Aftershock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Aftershock)Jake Welch won Wisconsin States with this descendent of Wakefield Sligh. Notice how he plays clunky giants and big sweepers just like Jamie, never forgetting for a moment to run that 26/62 mana configuration. Despite selecting cards such as [Aftershock](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aftershock) in a format where he was perfectly allowed to cast Intuition, Welch was very confident and only needed 14 sideboard cards to take the Wisconsin title (hence the [Grizzly Bears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Bears)).
 Welch's win occurred when [Tolarian Academy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tolarian+Academy) was the best deck in the **Magic** universe. His victory came over the powerhouse squad of [Stroke of Genius](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stroke+of+Genius), [Windfall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windfall), and [Time Spiral](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Spiral) . . . piloted by none other than Bob Maher. Really, what is [Voltaic Key](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Voltaic+Key) going to do against the echoing strength of [Lightning Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Dragon)?


I suppose the [Stone Rain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stone+Rain) element of the deck caught on with whoever is in charge of calling decks what, because subsequent to this period of time in Wisconsin, every red deck with any kind of land destruction, dedicated or light, has acquired the same moniker. As such, either of the following decks can be, or more properly, have been, called Ponza.

Mark beat Dan Paskins in the red-on-red mirror to win the title. Even after all these years, I still remember what Paskins wrote was the defining characteristic of Red Deck Wins over other decks: *“It has more land than any other Red deck because in the mirror matchup you need to lay land and Scrolls…”*


Anyone who reads my work knows I am a big Paskins fan, but I don't know how accurate the assessment was. Certainly it didn't really differentiate Red Deck Wins as a deck of a particular name, versus Deadguy Red or one of its other contemporaries.


![Genju of the Spires](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Genju+of+the+Spires)Today's Magicians seem to throw the name “Red Deck Wins” the same way players from the previous generation said “Sligh.” Any deck with [Genju of the Spires](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genju+of+the+Spires), for example, seems to be Red Deck Wins. Standard red decks since the *Mirrodin* Block differ on many counts. Some have tons of creatures and some have just [Solemn Simulacrum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum) and [Arc-Slogger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger). Some have [Pulse of the Forge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pulse+of+the+Forge) *and* [Shrapnel Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) whereas others get by on just a few [Magma Jet](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Jet)s. No matter how different they are, the fact that most of these decks run [Molten Rain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molten+Rain) to fight [Tooth and Nail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) makes them – you guessed it – Ponza. The problem with all this, in case you missed it, is that a lot of these names end up losing the differentiation that made them useful to begin with. Rather than thinking about red decks in terms of names, I find it more useful to think about them in terms of capabilities.
 *Sligh*  
 The first of the great names, the deck that gave us the mana curve is almost synonymous with “red.”


*Deadguy Red* or *Deadguy Sligh*  
 Greater emphasis on beatdown and damage, less emphasis on selective card advantage or specific mana curve.


![Lightning Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lightning+Dragon)*Ponza*  
 Deviating from its origins as a deck laden with crust (lots of lands, like 26), meat ([Lightning Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Dragon)s or even [Flowstone Giant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flowstone+Giant)s), and cheese ([Lightning Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Blast) and the like), Ponza today is indicative of mana control. Sometimes the mana control is overwhelming and sometimes it is mildly disruptive, but with Ponza . . . it's always there.
 *Red Deck Wins* versus *Goblins*  
 The most important differentiation of the recent Extended format, I think that these decks split down a line where one is a little slower and the other is bad against [Engineered Plague](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague). It is greater folly to confuse these decks than almost any others, because, as contemporaries, it's often safe to go to three against Goblins, whereas the other will almost always run eight mana-control lands.


The most important elements to track are haste ([Slith Firewalker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slith+Firewalker), [Goblin Warchief](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Warchief), or even [Lava Hounds](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Hounds)), mana denial, and reach. Goblins might have the most card advantage, but you would be shocked at how many games [Chrome Mox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) into [Slith Firewalker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slith+Firewalker) followed by a [Molten Rain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molten+Rain) – with nary another spell cast – wins. In a strange way, it's like Paul Sligh's deck getting the jump on everyone all over again.
