Dragons of Tarkir: Multiplayer Edition
======================================



 Posted in [Serious Fun](/en/articles/columns/serious-fun-archive)
 on March 23, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_brucerichard.jpg)
By Bruce Richard




 Bruce's games invariably involve several friends, crazy plays, and many laughs. Bruce believes that if anyone at your table isn't having fun, then you are doing it wrong. 





With each new set, I like to take a look at the cards to see how they function in multiplayer games. I'm looking for that card that gets an extra benefit in games with multiple players. [Sarkhan Unbroken](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sarkhan+Unbroken) is a great card, but it doesn't get better with extra opponents. I want cards that go from good to great, or great to awesome, simply because there are more opponents.



![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/images/daily/cardart_SunscorchRegent.jpg)


[Sunscorch Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscorch+Regent) | Art by [Matt Stewart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5b%22Matt%20Stewart%22%5d)

Mirror Mockery
==============



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mirror+Mockery)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Mockery)



[Mirror Mockery](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Mockery) has two uses:



**1. Target an opponent's creature to limit its usefulness on attack since you'll have a copy to block with every time they attack you.**



Barring an ability like "protection from creatures," or the creature being a 6/1 trampler, making a copy of a card is an effective way to block that creature from damaging you. Most opponents will prefer to give you a copy of the creature, particularly when you only get it for such a short period of time, and attack elsewhere. [Mirror Mockery](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Mockery) means you aren't using the opponent's creature against the opponent directly, just as a way to encourage him or her to attack elsewhere. I like this as an option, particularly when the card only costs two mana, but realistically, I'll want more.




**2. Target a creature to get the "enters the battlefield" trigger on the card.**



Most of the time, you're playing [Mirror Mockery](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Mockery) because you'll want the "enters the battlefield" effect on some of your creatures to happen again and again, and this is a cheap way to make that happen. Planning on using it this way means that the card is rarely ever dead in your hand. With multiplayer games, you'll often be able to find an opponent who can't deal with even a weaker creature attacking, so you'll get repeated benefit without too much risk to your enchanted creature.



Some of the time, you'll be able to use it on an opponent's creature and get the benefit. With multiplayer games, you'll have more opponents and more creatures around to target. The risk to targeting an opponent's creature is certainly greater than targeting your own. The opponent could simply stop attacking, or choose to make a lethal attack. However, for two mana, even getting the benefit only once or twice may be worthwhile.



If [Mirror Mockery](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Mockery) will work in your deck, you may want to consider including a way to sacrifice the token for extra benefit. It seems a shame to have a token that only survives in the combat phase, and not get a little something more.

Hedonist's Trove
================



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hedonist%27s+Trove)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hedonist%27s+Trove)



I'm including [Hedonist's Trove](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hedonist%27s+Trove) in this list, but I'm not really sure if it will work out. With extra opponents, you'll find someone with a graveyard you want to exile. I'm just not sure if paying seven mana will make the ability ineffective. The only benefit you get immediately is an opponent's exiled graveyard. While that is great, I can get that ability on a [Bojuka Bog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bojuka+Bog) as it enters the battlefield. If you choose an opponent who is running cards a different color from your own, you'll likely have to wait a turn or two until you can get the exiled lands onto the battlefield so you can cast the spells. I'm just not sure if it will be worth it to play.

Descent of the Dragons
======================



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Descent+of+the+Dragons)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Descent+of+the+Dragons)



I've heard plenty of discussion around [Descent of the Dragons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Descent+of+the+Dragons) so I wanted to mention it here. There are a variety of ways to use this card, but only one of them is recommended.



**1. Use [Descent of the Dragons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Descent+of+the+Dragons) as a removal spell.**



If there is a creature or group of creatures you can't handle, cast [Descent of the Dragons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Descent+of+the+Dragons) and destroy them. My concern with this lies in what is left behind. I understand there are things worse than a 4/4 flying Dragon, but not much. Give an opponent a couple of Dragons and suddenly your life will become quite miserable, not to mention your dwindling life total. Perhaps you are also running [Katabatic Winds](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Katabatic+Winds) and have no fear of flying creatures? If you are using [Descent of the Dragons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Descent+of+the+Dragons) as a removal spell, I'm confident you can find something better.




**2. Use [Descent of the Dragons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Descent+of+the+Dragons) as a political tool.**



You can give your opponents a 4/4 Dragon after extracting a promise not to attack you with it. This can work out well since you can give them Dragons on your turn, and they can use it on their turns, possibly before the other opponent has a turn to prepare.



There are a variety of political options, and I'm typically a fan of political plays. I love the idea of having your opponents attack each other and a 4/4 Dragon can be some strong incentive. My issue is that it may be too good an incentive. At some point, everyone who plays the political game feels the knife in their back. Giving a few Dragons to an opponent will likely leave a fatal blow to your back. I prefer my political incentives to be promises of friendship, gifts of an extra card draw or two, or perhaps some 0/1 Hippo tokens from a [Phelddagrif](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phelddagrif). Dragons are a bit beyond my largesse. However, if you're playing in a game with me, I'll be happy to accept as many Dragons as you'll give me. I promise to use them responsibly.



Really.



I swear.




**3. Change my creatures from weak little critters to 4/4 flying Dragons.**



This is probably the best option, but even here, there are issues. Part of the problem with this plan lies in [Descent of the Dragons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Descent+of+the+Dragons) being a sorcery. The creatures are destroyed and replaced, but the new Dragons don't have haste. Everyone gets a full round to try and deal with all of these creatures. The other limitation is that we're talking about token creatures here. Bounce or flicker a regular creature, and it can return to the battlefield. Do the same to a token creature, even a Dragon token creature, and it is just gone. Your army of flyers is just a little more susceptible to removal than they were before.



In spite of the limitations, you do have a horde of 4/4 flying Dragons! I assume you weren't going to do this unless you had plenty of creatures on the battlefield, so this will likely mean serious damage or a game-ending attack if the Dragons are still there the next turn. There are also a variety of ways to give all your creatures haste. You tend to be more willing to deal with a lot of limitations when the end result involves you winning the game.

Dragonlord Dromoka
==================



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dragonlord+Dromoka)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonlord+Dromoka)



So let me see if I have this straight. My opponents can't counter my creature spells? My opponents can't surprise me with combat tricks when I attack? I get all of this and a 5/7 flying Dragon with lifelink?! All for six mana? What are we waiting for? Where do I sign up!?



With multiple opponents, the likelihood of people wanting to do things on your turn goes way up. Having a way to stop all of that gives your permanents a level of protection that is not foolproof, but certainly offers more than it appears. Opponents are then forced to use their cards on other players' turns ignoring you a greater percentage of the time.

Dragonlord Silumgar
===================



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dragonlord+Silumgar)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonlord+Silumgar)



When a card lets you steal a creature or Planeswalker on the battlefield, you should be hoping you're in a multiplayer game. The options are bound to be better with more opponents. Multiple opponents mean more cards drawn and more land on the battlefield. This will let you pick and choose which creature or Planeswalker you really want, as opposed to taking whatever happens to be available. In one-on-one games, half the benefit of taking the permanent is so you won't have to deal with it while using your other threats to try and win. In multiplayer games, you want that stolen permanent to do work for you, so you'll want something that actually makes sense to take.



Silumgar's ability to steal a creature or Planeswalker will often mean that you can remove the only threat to Silumgar currently on the battlefield. With flying and deathtouch, Silumgar will make quick work of anything else that may be in his way, while driving your opponents' life totals steadily down. All of this and only six mana makes Silumgar a true threat.

Deathbringer Regent
===================



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Deathbringer+Regent)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathbringer+Regent)



Multiplayer *Magic* regularly involves more complex board states, so cards that remove all the creatures from the battlefield are commonly used and highly valued. While one-on-one games use "wrath" effects, multiplayer games practically guarantee amazing value for your mass removal. [Deathbringer Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathbringer+Regent) gives you the mass removal spell, and an evasive creature ready to attack the next turn.



The limitations on [Deathbringer Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathbringer+Regent) are not all that oppressive in a multiplayer game. Most games will include five creatures on the battlefield after only a few turns. Seven mana for the [Deathbringer Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathbringer+Regent) is a lot, but within the regular realm of multiplayer games. Being forced to cast it from your hand is a restriction, since it prevents getting the wrath through graveyard recursion, flickering, or [Kaalia of the Vast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kaalia+of+the+Vast) shenanigans. Even then, if you need to wrath again, you can return the card to your hand (through a variety of means) and recast it.



Adding a 5/6 flying creature that survives the wrath puts this card over the top. Add in a way to give the Regent haste and this card will be truly miserable for your opponents.

Sunscorch Regent
================



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sunscorch+Regent)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscorch+Regent)



As a Dragon, the Regent gets all the benefits of being a Dragon offered in this set. So many cards get better if you have a Dragon on the battlefield, or get some benefit because they are Dragons, and [Sunscorch Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscorch+Regent) gets all of that. Even as just a 4/3 flier for five mana, you are doing all right. A 4/4 flyer seems to be the standard, but when you consider the Regent's ability, losing 1 toughness is practically nothing.



The [Sunscorch Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscorch+Regent)'s ability is fine. You'll gain a few life points and likely see your Regent become a 7/6. It is a solid card that should be fun to play. When you are playing multiplayer *Magic* though, the Regent is the best card in *Dragons of Tarkir*. In a four-player game, by the time you'll be able to cast [Sunscorch Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscorch+Regent), your opponents will likely be playing about three spells per round. This means when your next turn comes around, you'll have gained 3 life and the Regent will be a 7/6 flyer.



Looking quickly at just the lifegain, consider how valuable [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden) can be. You can regularly gain 10 or more life from a [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden), and that is just from creatures. The Regent gives you that benefit for every single spell. Slow lifegain like that seems to be able to slip under the radar, and I expect the Regent will earn you 10 life points without breaking a sweat.



As far as the +1/+1 counters, I've watched [Forgotten Ancient](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forgotten+Ancient) and [Taurean Mauler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Taurean+Mauler) accumulate ten or fifteen counters in a couple of rounds. And the Ancient can spread the wealth around, meaning it can put the counters on a creature that is far more dangerous. The Regent is already dangerous. With built-in evasion, [Sunscorch Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscorch+Regent) will be very difficult to stop.



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Soul+Warden)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Forgotten+Ancient)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forgotten+Ancient) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Taurean+Mauler)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Taurean+Mauler)




The big downside to the Regent is that it dies to practically everything. Even bounce spells can limit how dangerous the Dragon will be. If you are relying on the Regent to give you the win, you'll need to pack ways to protect it because, trust me, once people realize just how good this creature is, it will not be long for the battlefield.



After talking to a handful of folks on Twitter, I came up with this deck option to make the most of [Sunscorch Regent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscorch+Regent):
