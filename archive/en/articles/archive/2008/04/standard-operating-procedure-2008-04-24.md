### Part I: The City Champs Top 8 Transplant


*How does this chart work?*


At the far left, we name the deck (for example Faeries or Merfolk); the boxes in aggregate show how many times decks fitting a particular description appeared in the [City Championships Top 8s](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/city-champs-2008-top-8-decklistsdecklists-2008-04-16) based on our information.


Blue boxes indicate City Championships wins; white boxes indicate "mere" Top 8 appearances. Blue boxes, similar to their purpose in describing PTQ Top 8s, show us where an invitation occurs, as City Champions get to play in their respective U.S. or Canadian National Championships; Top 8 players get byes at Regionals later this spring.

### Part II: All About the Decks!


Partly because we haven't covered Standard in some time and partly because there are so many different sorts of decks to cover, we're going to jump right in.


Where to start but the unconditional king of the current Standard, Blue-Black Faeries?


**Faeries**


Faeries didn't *quite* claim more City Championships wins than every other archetype put together, but the little black and blue guys and gals did their best to get there, picking up nearly half the available blue boxes, not to mention logging more total Top 8 appearances than any other sort of deck. How does this deck work?


Faeries is a classic CounterSliver deck. Rather than trying to control the game with its permission spells and winning on card advantage, CounterSliver (or aggro-control) decks clock like beatdown decks, but protect their tempo, life, and positional leads with permission.



![Bitterblossom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Bitterblossom)

Historically the aggro-control decks have been great against control and combo, generally failing against other beatdown due to their creatures being out-classed on the ground; modern Faeries has many advantages...



1. [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) – We will see over many decks that [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) may have overtaken [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) as the most ubiquitous and maybe overall best two drop in Standard. Of all the decks that can play [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), Faeries arguably plays it best because [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) produces Faerie creature tokens, which work perfectly with [Scion of Oona](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scion+of+Oona) and [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique)
2. Flash – Many Faerie creatures have Flash, so Faeries can approximate draw-go play like a control deck, choosing to play threats on the opponent's turn, during combat, when value can be extricated, when targeted spells are on the stack, or when it looks like [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) might kill its master. What in the name of [Oona, Queen of the Fae](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Oona%252C%2BQueen%2Bof%2Bthe%2BFae) am I talking about? Imagine someone is planning to nuke the [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation) you have in play to get back their [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)... Can you imagine a better play than [Scion of Oona](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scion+of+Oona) in response? What if you are dipping precariously below 10 life and the Red Deck across the table is about to hit that critical three-mana position after stalling on land drops for a turn or two? Might [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique) championing [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) itself (check that type line) be a nice response given that Red is historically lousy against 4/4s (especially when mana is tight for the other guy)? What about any troublesome spell (cheap ones work best) at any time? Welcome to [Spellstutter Sprite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellstutter+Sprite)-ville; population, you.
3. [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation) – Many decks play this more tempo-oriented [Control Magic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Control+Magic), but none so well as Faeries (for the same reasons they make a best [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom)).
4. Racing – [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique) and [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) can keep the opponent from taking the one big turn or making that one crucial attack necessary to win a race; otherwise, Faeries all fly and can largely be played at will. Don't blink, or the Wachowski brothers might make a film about [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique)!

**Merfolk**


Right behind Faeries—with less than half that deck's City Championships wins, truth be told—are the *other* little blue tribe, Merfolk. Merfolk represent a very similar strategy to Faeries, that of tempo-oriented aggro-control, but with a different set of incentives.


Yes, Merfolk *also* top up on [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command), that rascal of a [Dismiss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismiss) that can also help race another fast strategy, but instead of the raw power of [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique) and the neverending flyers of [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) (and everyone, actually), Merfolk are built for two things: slender speed and *islandwalk.*

Merfolk are card-for-card the faster tribe. They monopolize the critical two-spot, and with cards like [Silvergill Adept](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silvergill+Adept), cash in on their card advantage more rapidly. With both [Lord of Atlantis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lord+of+Atlantis) and Merrow Reegery, Merfolk can apply offensive pressure with a near-White Weenie clip. So what is the deal with islandwalk? Obviously it's a solid ability against control, but the real macro value is *against Faeries.* Faeries is a blue deck that plans to race. They might be slower to start, but they produce a never-ending string of chump blockers. What happens when they can't block? Exactly!

These green-red decks typically run [Search for Tomorrow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Search+for+Tomorrow) and [Into the North](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+North) as well as [Wall of Roots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Roots) to accelerate; [Into the North](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+North) gives them a natural "in" to the snow theme, so [Mouth of Ronom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mouth+of+Ronom) and [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) become almost automatic tools to help in creature suppression.


Santoro's splash is black for main deck and sideboard [Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Void), plus some [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate)s (presumably for [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), but probably lots of stuff), though you will also see blue splashes for [Aeon Chronicler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aeon+Chronicler), combinations of both, or straight green-red with access to neither.


The green-red decks have numerous powerful cards, expensive spells that they can justify due to the large investment in mana. [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) of course plays a big game for not much cost, but [Siege-Gang Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander) and [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher) actually need the mana lift help (Faeries needs help against the latter). The only thing between [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher) and a nearly automatic victory over Faeries is Bitterblossm.

The Standard Doran deck mingles many compelling elements. While Doran doesn't have quite the mana base it was able to command in Extended (certainly no [Shizo, Death's Storehouse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shizo%2C+Death%27s+Storehouse)), it nevertheless has one of the most elegant mana bases in the *Standard* environment ([Murmuring Bosk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murmuring+Bosk) is a [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)!). Speaking of mana, the Doran decks with [Ohran Viper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ohran+Viper) (or, I suppose, different versions with, say [Hypnotic Specter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypnotic+Specter)) have many [Fires of Yavimaya](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fires+of+Yavimaya)-like openings where [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) or [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) is followed up on the second turn with a great three-mana threat... In fact, those three-mana threats are friends *with each other!*
Doran plus [Ohran Viper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ohran+Viper)? A "3/3" [Ophidian](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ophidian) if ever there was one... And [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)? Better than without Doran in play, certainly. Both creatures benefit from [Loxodon Warhammer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Warhammer) in play, particularly [Ohran Viper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ohran+Viper), who can use it to draw cards (almost like one of the blue decks that finished nearer the top of the page), not just smite the opponent like a typical green mage.


Plus, Doran is a fair [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) deck!

The burn deck borrows greatly from a similar Extended strategy ([Shard Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shard+Volley) and [Keldon Marauders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Keldon+Marauders) mostly); its goal is to reduce the opponent from 20 to 0 life without undue effort in The Red Zone, mostly via a number of direct damage spells.


Note, creature removal is quite poor against this deck. Most of the deck's creatures are either completely disposable, or have a built-in shelf life (like [Keldon Marauders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Keldon+Marauders)). One of the advantages—especially in a world where many of the top decks are starting [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation) even off-tribe—is that Sower is pretty weak against the burn deck, even though it does a fair amount of damage via creatures.

This Green-Red Snow beatdown deck packs a little special something. Almost all of its creatures have the same job, Warrior. ![Imperious Perfect](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Imperious+Perfect)![Obsidian Battle-Axe](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Obsidian+Battle-Axe)



![Chameleon Colossus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Chameleon+Colossus)


[Boreal Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boreal+Druid) and [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) are the exceptions that prove the rule, but they are important for getting the Battle-Axe in play on turn two in the first place. From there, it is an orgy of speedy slaughter. Down comes [Imperious Perfect](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperious+Perfect); haste. Open [Snow-Covered Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snow-Covered+Forest)? The token gets haste too. [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus)? Changeling means haste! Think about doubling this guy's power, facing Faeries and their annoying 1/1 black creatures, when the Colossus is carrying a Battle-Axe. Rah!


**Gauntlet of Power**



![Gauntlet of Power](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Gauntlet+of+Power)

Capable of presenting an overwhelming amount of power turn after turn after turn, the [Gauntlet of Power](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gauntlet+of+Power) deck is in an offensive league of its own. [Dauntless Dourbark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dauntless+Dourbark) is even bigger than you might think; another Treefolk in play will give it not only a size boost, but trample. [Gauntlet of Power](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gauntlet+of+Power) and Verdeloth at the same time is just too much power; the Saprolings are anything but saps. [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher) and [Hail Storm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hail+Storm) are invaluable tools for racing [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), and [Brooding Saurian](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brooding+Saurian) helps to turn off [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation), one of the format's most compelling threats.

And while you are rifling through your deck with [Into the North](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+North) or [Summoner's Pact](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Summoner%27s+Pact)? Why not run out the free [Panglacial Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Panglacial+Wurm)? It's probably bigger than anything not on your side of the table.

We might not *expect* green to be a great compliment to [Makeshift Mannequin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makeshift+Mannequin), but the green cards actually have a lot in common with their black and blue counterparts... They do cool things when they come into play! As such, [Civic Wayfinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Civic+Wayfinder) is quite similar to [Mulldrifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter), and [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher) is like a bigger and hairier [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw). This version doesn't *have* to be *focused* on the Mannequin strategy... [Makeshift Mannequin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makeshift+Mannequin) is just a good spell in the mix. It can be a [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror), a [Lay of the Land](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lay+of+the+Land), an [Inspiration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inspiration), or a mini [Hurricane](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurricane), always putting a creature in play to mess up the opponent's attack (or just his face).





![Civic Wayfinder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Civic+Wayfinder)![Makeshift Mannequin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Makeshift+Mannequin)



![Cloudthresher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cloudthresher)


Of course sometimes Mannequin is "just" a [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf).


This deck is very solid on the fundamental metrics. While not exactly the fastest option in the format, it is chock full of two-for-ones, and with a strange eight pack of [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) and [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation) in the sideboard, has a tremendous amount of lasting power long game. On the way there, the black-green Mannequin is nothing but card advantage and, in [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command), card advantage looking for more card advantage.

Green isn't the only color that gets to play [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) (blue actually has some of the best incentives to playing snow); Tim Sussino's deck has got all sorts of snow interactions hybridized with a blue control deck...

[Phyrexian Ironfoot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Ironfoot) and [Rimefeather Owl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rimefeather+Owl) are the obvious ones—these creatures thrive on snow, are really only good in snow decks...


But what about [Stuffy Doll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stuffy+Doll)? [Stuffy Doll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stuffy+Doll) at instant speed, dropped from on high by [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Mage+of+Zhalfir) in front of an unsuspecting [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)? Ouch. Untap and ping for a point? Little boo boo. Drop a land and [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) the Doll? Next game!


Okay. Here's the crazy thing: There are probably more than thirty viable decks in pre-*Shadowmoor* Standard... but the lists just get better with the new set. Mono-Red, for example, gets [Flame Javelin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame+Javelin), and Green-Red Big Mana might consider [Deus of Calamity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deus+of+Calamity) (though thankfully, Faeries—already a world-class deck—doesn't get another [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom)-class upgrade... in fact everybody else got [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout)). So what does that mean?


More decks.


More options.


More matchups.


And maybe, just maybe, more opportunity.
