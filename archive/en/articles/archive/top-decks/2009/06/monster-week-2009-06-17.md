Monster of the Week
===================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on June 18, 2009 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











There is a deep tradition in some wings of episodic television to introduce one-use antagonists—typically called "monsters of the week"—to be discovered and dealt with by our intrepid heroes ... only to be replaced by some other monster the next week. For example, in the third season of *Ben 10*, young Ben Tennyson thought he was bit by a werewolf in one episode, then had to deal with variations on a the mummy and Frankenstein's monster in successive adventures before going head-to-head with a ghost-like alien as the "big bad" of the season. Very old school.

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130614111410im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/td/td43_tvCross.jpg)  
Or for those of you who don't watch nonstop Cartoon Network with your five-year-old daughters, the series of cursed items from *Friday the 13th: The Series*, minor cadres of vamps / demons / lawyers from *Buffy the Vampire Slayer* or *Angel,* meteor freaks (and supervillains) from *Smallville ...* or, possibly the most recognizable, that parade of aliens, alien conspirators, and occult baddies that bedeviled Mulder and Scully week-to-week on *The X-Files* would all be examples of the monster-of-the-week tradition.

Which brings us to the current Standard, a monstrous example of monster of the week chaos.

Case in point, [the week before Regionals](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/38)—and largely [*through* Regionals](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/39)—we were frightened of evil Spirits (well ... maybe not "evil").

Then for some reason there was all kinds of ducking our heads in the sand due to [Swans](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/40) ... *Swans?* Like some swimmy kinds of birds? Ridiculous, I agree. 

Then in a kind of return to normalcy, [Faeries were the problem](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/41) (again). Faeries are the kind of people who will steal your soul for eating the wrong kind of food. Very sneaky-like.

Then [Elves](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/42)! Elves are not what you would typically think of as monster-of-the-week material on account of being largely beautiful and often allies to us humans. But I can't imagine the Swans—yes, Swans—that they chased out of the tournament area felt they were very friendly.

And now, another week gone by, and we find ourselves at odds with yet another sort of monster: Kithkin.

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20151105155316im_/https://www.wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/td/td43_goldmeadowSplash.jpg)  
I was very happy to hear that several of my friends had weathered their ways to the Top 4 of the enormous PTQ in Philadelphia, PA this past weekend. A truly gigantic event for the area, it was nine rounds that culminated in a reappearance of Black-Green Elves in the finals, two copies of Green-White Tokens in the Top 4 (Luis Neiman, a.k.a. Luis Not Vargas, and Phil Napoli, a.k.a. Pnaps, playing essentially the same deck) with the big winner being Matt Boccio with Mono-White Kithkin:

Matt got his deck from Cedric Phillips, who you might recall cracked [his first Pro Tour Top 8](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptkyo09/welcome) earlier this year with a Red-White version of Kithkin; according to Boccio, Kithkin is favored to beat Elves, Five-Color Blood, and green-white decks ... his prediction for the makeup of the PTQ field.

"Originally I liked the black-white version of Kithkin with [Zealous Persecution](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Persecution), but Cedric Phillips gave me this list the day before the tournament and I decided to go with it. [Zealous Persecution](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Persecution) is great against Tokens and the mirror but against every other deck [Glorious Anthem](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glorious+Anthem) is much better. Also the mana in the mono-white deck is much better because you don't have to run [Fetid Heath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fetid+Heath).\* The deck really has game against all of the creature decks in the format ... The only matchup which is godawful is White-Blue [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark).

"I wanted to play [Stillmoon Cavalier](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stillmoon+Cavalier)s but couldn't get my hands on them before the tournament; I would probably take out the [Purges](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Purges) for them."

 \* In a deck full of one- and two-drops, filter lands like [Fetid Heath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fetid+Heath) can be a liability because they don't tap for colored mana in and of themselves; that is, you can't play a [Goldmeadow Stalwart](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goldmeadow+Stalwart) on a first turn [Fetid Heath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fetid+Heath)... or anything for that matter.  


Matt never got to blow out an opponent with [Mirrorweave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirrorweave), but he feels the card has a lot of play, especially when the opponent doesn't see it coming. Same goes for [Militia's Pride](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Militia%27s+Pride); [Militia's Pride](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Militia%27s+Pride) being aimed at White-Blue and Faeries.

The main differences between these mono-white and black-white decks is the presence of [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos). Our tournament champion Alves ran three copies of [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) starting, which allowed him to play with [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender) in the main as an incremental [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) "target." Once upon a time [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender) was primarily a foil to *actual* red decks, and secondarily as the white deck's primary line of defense against [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) out of Five-Color Control.



|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
|  |  |  |

Today, no one games with [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) any more, with even the Five-Color Control decks going with [Volcanic Fallout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Fallout) to help keep Faeries off of them, and red decks, while not completely out of the format, are fewer and farther between. However, [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender) still gets work out of [Boggart Ram-Gang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Ram-Gang), but these days the Gang is usually spat off of the top of a deck via [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf). [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender) holds off either. While not complete proof against Cascade Swans or [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) decks in general, these creatures still pull up the emergency brake for anyone driving a cascade car.

Santini made a number of unusual choices for this archetype. 

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Paladin+en-Vec)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paladin+en-Vec) is a card we don't normally see [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) players investing space in, not with the potential blockers in this strategy. Another surprising addition is [Wall of Reverence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence). This one is kind of awesome. It is not a card you typically get *back* with [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) ... but it is stupendous in its efficacy in a number of matchups. For example, Faeries has a lot more difficulty with [Wall of Reverence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Reverence) than the usually-more-dramatic [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) at the same mana point.But the *really* unusual addition is [Clone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clone)!

[Clone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clone) is quite interesting .... It can be *anything!* At 0/0 it is quite eligible for [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) recursion, too. The question is whether we will see more [Clone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clone)s cropping up before the card (potentially) rotates with the coming of ***Magic** 2010*.

Let's look at his creature configuration to begin with. This is a much different deck than we have seen from any of the [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf)-based decks in terms of creature selection. There is not a [Boggart Ram-Gang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Ram-Gang) in the list. It actually climbs *up* the curve to [Enlisted Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enlisted+Wurm). Is that a singleton [Caldera Hellion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Caldera+Hellion)?

It is obvious this is much more of a control deck ... In addition to [Caldera Hellion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Caldera+Hellion) there are more sweepers main, including [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) and [Volcanic Fallout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Fallout).

The easy analysis is on the fours: [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) and [Captured Sunlight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Captured+Sunlight). Main-deck these cards are always hitting either [Volcanic Fallout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Fallout) or [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks). Crucias's deck is kind of like Cascade Swans that way; you know what you are getting with more regularity than most other cascade decks.

As for the spells in this deck, the fives are quite interesting: eight Commands, and none of them  [Cryptic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=+Cryptic) (poor [Austere Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Austere+Command) .... With [Incendiary Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incendiary+Command) playing a key role in this deck, Austere is the ugly duckling for sure)! In this list the [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command)s and [Incendiary Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incendiary+Command)s team up to manage the opponent's board. [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command) is like half a [Plow Under](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under) with some additional bonus, and [Incendiary Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incendiary+Command) is like an expensive [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) that packs an additional punch. But expensive as these cards are, they are largely *just good enough* to contain decks laying lands that come into play tapped every turn.

The sideboard is semi-transformational. Crucias can bring in [Fulminator Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fulminator+Mage) and [Rain of Tears](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rain+of+Tears) to give this deck more of a rudder when it comes to three-mana spells to hit with cascade. While there can *technically* be a greater variety of three mana spells in his deck, Crucias can lay them out so that he can consistently cash in a [Captured Sunlight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Captured+Sunlight) for a [Fulminator Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fulminator+Mage), [Rain of Tears](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rain+of+Tears), or conditionally [Maelstrom Pulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Pulse) to control the opponent's mana. 

So consider this potential sequencing ...

[Fulminator Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fulminator+Mage) + block.

[Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) into [Rain of Tears](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rain+of+Tears) (leaving back the [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) to block).

[Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command) an opponent's comes into play tapped land and fetch [Enlisted Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enlisted+Wurm).

[Enlisted Wurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enlisted+Wurm) into [Incendiary Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incendiary+Command).

This set of turns three through six seems absolutely *brutal* when it comes to the sometimes-chaotic mana bases that we see in Standard. Players are often relying on a single [Vivid Creek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vivid+Creek) to power up their [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool)s, and if you can surgically eliminate a single font of colored mana, the opponent can be reduced to nothing but [Twilight Mire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twilight+Mire)s, say.

Many times land destruction decks cripple the opponent's mana but themselves fall behind against non-mana-producing permanents. In the scenario we described above, the deck is actually *advancing* its board position as it annoys all holy eff out of the opponent. The opponent is reduced to two lands *but you also have a 5/5?* I can imagine a whole lot of worse places to be against a mana-screwed opponent.

We don't really talk about "Tier Two" metagaming any more, and maybe the term is not even appropriate today, in this world of [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) and [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), but certainly you can see the great—and constantly emerging—variety of decks that are available in Standard. Will you battle for the Blue Envelope swinging at kneecaps and Achilles tendons with the miniscule monster of the week? Or perhaps show the opponent a cascade of crippling card advantage with an eight-pack of Commands? Or help the world decide that *this* is going to be another Faeries week? 

Good luck, whichever deck you choose. (And you certainly have a lot to choose from!)
