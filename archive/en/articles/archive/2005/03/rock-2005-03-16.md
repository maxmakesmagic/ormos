Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic."

The Pro Tour was this past weekend. For those of you who didn't follow the coverage, you can check out the smiling faces of victorious Nova [here](/en/events/coverage/nova-burns-brightest-atlanta). Anyone who has ever wanted to hear the dulcet tones of yours truly's voice rather than merely imagining them narrate such classics as "Finding the Tinker Deck", "The Old Switcheroo", or "Mental Magic: Proactive Flashback" rather than simply imagining the said can do so at the above link as well; like at PT Columbus, I did the Sunday webcast coverage with Randy Buehler. The Top 4 was an exciting one where Gabriel Nassif, **Magic**'s reigning Player of the Year, graduated from "best player never to win a Pro Tour" to... well... Let's just say that the now-bareheaded Yellow Hat just lost one of his many nicknames.

The side effect of a Pro Tour weekend is that, because many players on the team that brings you Swimming with Sharks spent said weekend grinding out event coverage, we have a smaller pool of deck lists to examine for this week. The weekend of March 12 gives us data on only three Top 8s so far (though I'm guessing that many more will be updated on the [decklists page](/en/events/coverage/pro-tour%E2%80%93philadelphia-qualifying-season-extended-top-8-decklists) by the time this article goes to print). From that limited information, I can only say "Rock On":

Over those three Top 8s, The Rock leads all decks in Top 8 appearances and claims two of the three PT slots.


The last time this column focused on Bryn Kenney, it was the weekend of [Pro Tour Columbus](/en/events/coverage/canali-crushes-columbus). Kenney battled to the Top 4 of the Last Chance Qualifier there and took home (or back to his hotel room, I guess) a slot to the Extended Pro Tour. He followed up his first professional event with a standout finish in the Columbus Amateur Challenge the same weekend. At PT Columbus, Bryn ran mono-green Tooth and Nail; this time around, he added black for the beatdown Macey Rock to take Harrisburg:

Kenney's deck is essentially a more streamlined version of Grant Struck's GP Top 8 deck, which we looked at [last week](/en/articles/archive/revenge-urza%E2%80%99s-block-2005-03-09). He goes for better resistance to combo and control (by playing a fourth [Mesmeric Fiend](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mesmeric+Fiend)) and is less dependent on inconsistent synergies. With only one [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed), Kenney has to rely on his [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)s to get that particular answer, but has a more consistent overall game plan with triple [Diabolic Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Edict) and a second [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) to punch through.

![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Treetop+Village)


















With the maximum number of copies of both [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) and [Wall of Blossoms](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Blossoms) in the early game, Pehlke's deck is very much a control deck. He makes a change that all of us have thought of -- but most were afraid to pull the trigger on, I'm sure -- and removed [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) from his [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed) deck. There's nothing like ramping up the mana for [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed) with [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)... just to blow them right up. Pehlke makes up for the missing staples with two sets of two drops that remove a little bit of [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village)'s "comes into play tapped" sting and help stabilize the paradise free mana base... With nothing that costs ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) on turn one, there is no downside to playing the [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village) first.


The unique element of Pehlke's build is its focus on a creatures (and lands) sideboard. With three [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) main, John plays essentially the same game all three duels. He can access almost every card in his sideboard from game one, and can use [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) as a hoser in certain matchups.


 



![Urborg Shambler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Urborg+Shambler)

Though John has access to 1+2 regular old [Engineered Plague](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague)s (likely a boon against the surging Goblins), [Urborg Shambler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urborg+Shambler) is a look at the same strategy with a lot of cool upsides. It can come out in game one against Aluren (hosing [Cavern Harpy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy)) via [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish); it is attached to a 4/3 body and can double as a beatdown threat; it is an incidental answer to a deck like Suicide Black that, while viable, wouldn't normally demand specific answers in a format that includes more than 20 *other* viable decks.

In addition, [Kamahl, Fist of Krosa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kamahl%2C+Fist+of+Krosa) is a little seen card that has powerful synergy with [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed). With this card, The Rock can turn the [Nevinyrral's Disk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nevinyrral%27s+Disk)-like [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed) into half an [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon), too. Land is just about the only thing that can live through a Deed, and by turning some number of lands into creatures prior to popping the Deed, Kamahl takes out even those permanents. With sufficient mana, [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) can allow a Rock player to dodge the opponent's hand destruction and set up a turn to take almost absolute control of a game.

I had to read Nick Little's deck list three times before I realized what was going on with his [Minamo, School at Water's Edge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Minamo%2C+School+at+Water%27s+Edge). With no legendary creatures, I figured it was just worse than an Island in an environment that includes both [Dust Bowl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dust+Bowl) and [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland). However, Minamo can untap legendary PERMANENTS... including other Legendary Lands.

Minamo works just fine as a "second [Gaea's Cradle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Cradle)" to break the Legend Rule by, ironically, playing a DIFFERENT Legendary Land to take advantage of the redundancy.


 



![Opposition](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Opposition)

Though U/G Opposition decks have shined in many formats, from GP Memphis in the hands of Dr. Mike Pustilnik to the US National Championships in the hands of Eugene Harvey, this version, with its [Sword of Fire and Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Sword%2Bof%2BFire%2Band%2BIce), is similar to Satoshi Nakamura's deck from the recent PT Columbus.

The goal of U/G Opposition is to generate an imbalance on the board, usually via cards like [Deranged Hermit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deranged+Hermit) that generate multiple creatures via a single spell, and then use Opposition to lock down the opponent's permanents. Given enough [Wall of Blossoms](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Wall%2Bof%2BBlossoms) and Squirrel tokens, Opposition can stall any number of threat creatures and, eventually, every land the opponent controls too. Without [Static Orb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Static+Orb), this is only a "soft" lock, but it's one that most decks have problems escaping.


One of the decks that WOULDN'T have many problems with Opposition is Red Deck Wins. With no [Static Orb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Static+Orb)s preventing an untap, that deck can float mana on upkeep to use [Cursed Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll) and instant speed burn to win regardless of being slowed down on the table. Because of that, [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) fills many holes. This card speeds up the clock, turning even [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) into four damage a turn that can't be stopped by all-too-red burn cards. Like we saw in Trinity Green last week, [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) out of Little's sideboard is a powerful answer against both red and blue opponents. On one counter it holds off [Cursed Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll), [Lava Dart](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart), and [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer) shenanigans; on two, the Chalice puts the kibosh on [Sapphire Medallion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sapphire+Medallion), [Sunscape Familiar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscape+Familiar), [Snap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snap), and [Brain Freeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze).
