Comboing Off in Standard
========================



 Posted in [Reconstructed](/en/articles/columns/reconstructed-archive)
 on October 5, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorPic_GavinVerhey.jpg)
By Gavin Verhey




 When Gavin Verhey was eleven, he dreamt of a job making Magic cards—and now as a Magic designer, he's living his dream! Gavin has been writing about Magic since 2005. 





Welcome to Land Week!


Zendikar is all about lands. Lands, lands, lands!


So what better way to kill your opponent than by attacking for potentially hundreds of damage with lands using a cool new combo deck?


Let me explain.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/images/daily/cardart_BFZ_Noyan-Dar-Roil-Shaper.jpg)


[Noyan Dar, Roil Shaper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noyan+Dar%2C+Roil+Shaper) | Art by [Karl Kopinksi](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+[%22Karl+Kopinski%22])


Back in *Khans of Tarkir*, we released a card into the wild that caused *quite* a stir. None other than [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy)!


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jeskai+Ascendancy)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy)
This combo-riffic card briefly popped up, with a version of the deck making the Top 8 of Pro Tour *Khans of Tarkir*. But then it slowly faded into the distance.


Well, get ready to dust them off.


You see, untapping your creatures is pretty good. But the real sauce? Untapping your lands by turning them into creatures to produce more mana every time you cast a spell!


However, that's pretty much been untenable up to this point—I mean, that would require turning your lands into creatures! That'd be *far* too hard to do.


Enter: *Battle for Zendikar*.


Turning your lands into creatures is easier than ever. A new combo deck is coming over the horizon. Prepare to be on the cutting edge of Standard.


Let's take a look!

The Battle Plan
===============


There's a lot going on here, so let's dive right into what is really at play.


When everything is working smoothly, the key card is the powerful [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy). First you need to make sure you have one of those.


The next step is animating your lands. There are a few different options for this:


* You can animate a creature land, like [Lumbering Falls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lumbering+Falls).
* You can use awaken to turn a land into a creature.
* You can use [Noyan Dar, Roil Shaper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noyan+Dar%2C+Roil+Shaper) to pseudo-awaken a land.

If you have to start with [Lumbering Falls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lumbering+Falls) or awaken, then things take some finesse. What do you do?


Well, then you want to cast a flurry of cheap spells that also untap your lands! With at least two lands activated, one-mana spells will generate mana for you. All the while, each spell you cast is untapping your creatures—and making them larger!


You can win the hard way by just casting a bunch of spells and eking out enough damage, but eventually you'll reach a Noyan Dar, and that will make things even easier (and more reliable). With all the mana you've generated, you can cast him. Then, with each instant or sorcery spell you cast, Noyan will make another land a creature and give you 3 more power. From there on out, it should be pretty easy to get to lethal damage.


. . . Of course, if you started this combo with Noyan Dar and [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy), then you're basically on easy mode! Noyan turns every noncreature spell you cast into an animated land, meaning that before you know it, all of your lands will be untapping and growing with every instant or sorcery spell you cast—and winning from there should be fairly trivial.


How do you put this two-card combo together? Well, [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) (quite literally) brings it all together! [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) for Noyan Dar makes it pretty easy to combo out the next turn if you have an Ascendancy, while also finding ways to help you stay alive like Planar Outburst. Plenty of card filtering will get the job done, too.


And, to top it all off, this deck can certainly just win without its combo. Featuring cheap instants and sorceries as well as some control elements, if your opponent can't remove Noyan then they might just die to him a turn later.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/images/daily/cardart_BFZ_Rush-of-Ice.jpg)


[Rush of Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rush+of+Ice) | Art by [Deruchenko Alexander](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+[%22Deruchenko+Alexander%22])


So, what is there to work on refining? Well, making sure everything in this deck is serving an appropriate role is crucial. Some cards aren't helping with the combo, the backup plan or staying alive—and some of those can go. The cards to add in are definitely ones that make the combo more robust while also ideally contributing to the deck in other ways.


What exactly did I have in mind? Well, let's dive right on into it!


Deck Breakdown
==============


Which cards can stay, and which ones don't quite land in this deck? Let's go through the deck card-by-card and find out!


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Noyan+Dar%2C+Roil+Shaper)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noyan+Dar%2C+Roil+Shaper)
Noyan is one of the key cards of this strategy. If you untap with him and have a [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy) in your hand or on the battlefield, there's a pretty good chance that's just game over for your opponent. You can animate a land and start going off with the Ascendancy, all the while creating an army that will overtake your opponents.


Normally, you have to be careful about how many copies of a legendary creature you play. However, considering that Noyan is essentially a "kill-me-or-lose" legendary creature, I'm okay playing the full four. Either your opponent removes it and you deploy another, or they don't and you set up for victory. Sounds good to me!


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hangarback+Walker)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hangarback+Walker)
Hangarback is certainly cute with [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy). Every time you cast an instant or sorcery Hangarback untaps, meaning you can grow it even further with counters!


However, when you look at the core of what this deck is trying to do, Hangarback doesn't really help it function. Although it's a very strong card in its own right, it doesn't really help your combo or your control plans dramatically. In fact, it even takes away from the deck's density of instants and sorceries. It's always important to consider why you're playing each card, even powerful ones such as this—and Hangarback just doesn't fit here. Farewell, thopters!


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Archangel+of+Tithes)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Tithes)
This deck features [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light), which makes it tempting to play all kinds of one-of tutor targets . . . which tends to be one of the largest traps you can easily fall into with tutors!


How often are you really going to search for this card over anything else? The times are so low that it isn't really worth having here. It doesn't really help your combo at all, and it's unwieldy to cast in a deck such as this. Archangel can go.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dig+Through+Time)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time)
A combo deck like this one wants to look for very specific cards, which [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time) does with finesse. Considering all of the instants and sorceries this deck casts along with the plethora of fetch lands, casting it for cheap is going to be no problem at all.


There certainly is a question of whether [Treasure Cruise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise) should feature in this deck at all. Dig is more efficient at finding particular cards, but Cruise is better when comboing out since it costs only a single mana. And even with all of the cards entering your graveyard, you can only delve so much.


In the end, I'd rather have the card that helps me put my combo together in Dig. Cruise is close, and a perfectly fine substitute if you don't have Digs, but I would rather be Digging here.


In addition to Dig, I would also like to have some additional cheap instants and sorceries that help find combo pieces. There are two great ones I'd like to add in.


The first is [Anticipate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anticipate). For two mana, it triggers Ascendancy and Noyan when you're going off and helps find the card you need early on. It's a perfect fit.


Second is something perhaps less expected: [Magmatic Insight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magmatic+Insight). However, when you're comboing off, you're no doubt going to end up with excess lands in your hand. Additionally, it's a one-mana spell you can cast when you only have two creature lands to help generate mana. It helps you search for what you're looking for throughout the game. While I don't want to draw a hand full of them, I'm happy to add three copies to this deck.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jeskai+Ascendancy)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy)
This is the card that absolutely makes the deck tick. You want to draw it every game and if you have one (or more!) active, you are going to quickly move toward winning. Multiples are totally fine, and can even allow you to go off with just a single animated land. This is a card that the deck absolutely needs four copies of.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Wild+Slash)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Slash)
In any kind of combo deck, it's always important that you have the tools to stay alive. It's even better if those tools double as combo pieces.


[Wild Slash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Slash) does that admirably. Not only does it kill problematic aggressive creatures for just one mana, but you can also use it as a spell when going off to trigger [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy) and Noyan Dar. If you get one of your lands big enough, it even means some rogue Turbofog opponent can't ruin your day with a timely [Revealing Wind](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Revealing+Wind). I'd like to play the full four of these.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Stasis+Snare)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stasis+Snare)
Although it is a catch-all efficient piece of removal, a three-mana spot removal card that doesn't trigger Noyan Dar isn't so much what I'm looking for here. I'd rather play other pieces of removal. Keeping things efficient and constantly pushing on the same axis is important for not running out of steam when it actually is time to combo out.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Bring+to+Light)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light)
At an extraordinarily base level, to make a combo deck work you need to find all of the pieces of your combo. Simple enough. As a result, a key part is cards that enable you to find your combo pieces. [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) does just that!


If you can't find a Noyan Dar, [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) functions as extra copies. It can also search up sweepers such as [Planar Outburst](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Planar+Outburst) in a pinch, and is very flexible in general. And while it is a tad expensive at five mana, and it does require you to play enough ways to converge it for five, the reward is worth it. I'll move up to four copies.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Planar+Outburst)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Planar+Outburst)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Radiant+Flames)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Radiant+Flames)
*Battle for Zendikar* introduces a couple new sweepers that fit right into what's going on here: [Planar Outburst](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Planar+Outburst) and [Radiant Flames](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Radiant+Flames). They each have their advantages and disadvantages.


Flames comes out for less mana, and can prevent you from getting crushed by a swarm of aggressive creatures. On the other hand, it doesn't work with [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light). Outburst, on the other hand, is excellent with your game plan—it keeps your lands alive—and you can [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) for it, but it's a bit more on the costly side. And, to boot, there's only so much room for sweepers in this strategy.


My conclusion is to actually split them. You need at least two [Planar Outburst](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Planar+Outburst) to be able to [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) twice if necessary for them, but otherwise most of the time I'm going to want [Radiant Flames](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Radiant+Flames) to soak up the early game. I'm going to split them two and two, and I would definitely sideboard the rest of the copies of [Radiant Flames](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Radiant+Flames) for aggressive decks.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Rush+of+Ice)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rush+of+Ice)
Rush serves two important roles here. Role number one, in its base state, is that it is a one-mana spell that helps you combo out. Role number two is that it can animate one of your lands to get your engine rolling in the first place. And then, of course, it can always buy you some time early on too.


Well, first of all, I believe these should be [Clutch of Currents](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Clutch+of+Currents) and not [Rush of Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rush+of+Ice). Bouncing by sending a creature under the lake is going to be a lot better than freezing most of the time, since it takes the same amount of turns for that creature to attack you again and forces them to spend mana to recast it.


Next, you really can't afford to draw too many of these. If they don't have creatures because they're playing control or something, then it's difficult to cast, and it only fits your game plan in specific (but important) situations. I'd like to move to two copies.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Encircling+Fissure)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Encircling+Fissure)
The Fissure has something rather uncommon going for it (well, besides its rarity): It is an instant awaken spell. That means you can cast it at the end of your opponent's turn to animate a land and set up the combo on your next turn. Additionally, it's far from useless on its own: you can use it to fog your opponent for a turn early on and set up for a board sweeper such as [Planar Outburst](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Planar+Outburst). (Which, perfectly, leaves your land alive!)


Two is just the number I want here. This is not the kind of card I really want to draw a fistful of, but finding one to help craft my game plan around with something like end step awaken, untap, activate [Lumbering Falls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lumbering+Falls) and start going off, is just what I'm looking for.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jeskai+Charm)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Charm)
When it comes to one-of [Bring to Light](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bring+to+Light) targets, [Jeskai Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Charm) is cute—it does enable you to do things like give all of your lands lifelink. However, it's not really helping that much on the whole toward any of the deck's main game plans. It can go.

When you looked over the card list for *Battle for Zendikar*, this is probably not quite what you expected to see—but many combo decks take some time to discover, and this is one hidden just beneath the surface.


Setting up the combo is surprisingly easy, between all the card searching and filtering this deck has. You can be susceptible to removal, though a lot of it is sorcery speed. And eventually, they should run out of answers as you keep sifting through your deck and deploying must-kill threats. [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy) alone helps you loot over and over again, getting you to exactly the cards you need.


One additional thing I could see trying is [Alhammarret's Archive](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alhammarret%27s+Archive). When the Archive gets rolling with [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy) you're pretty hard pressed to lose with all of the cards you'll be seeing.


As what boils down to a two-card combo engine, this deck has a lot of competitive potential. Have fun with this one!

**Modern Battling**


It's time for the part of this week's article where I challenge you to send in something for two weeks from now. I'll pick one of these decks and tweak it throughout that article, much like I've done here today!


Since the Pro Tour is next weekend—and when you're making decks and I'm writing, it won't have happened yet—let's stay out of the Standard space and look at Modern!


And ReConstructed readers, I have quite the doozy of a challenge for you. Normally, I don't make the deck building challenges too hard—but the one this week requires some finesse and ingenious deck building. It takes something that's only really been a big focus in *Battle for Zendikar* and pulls it back into a Modern context.


I believe in you all. Ready to see what it is?


**Format:** Modern


**Restrictions:** Your deck must be centered around devoid/colorlessness, and focus on at least one card from *Battle for Zendikar*.


**Deadline:** Monday, October 12, at 6 p.m. Pacific Time.


**Submit all decklists by emailing me at** [**reconstructeddecks@gmail.com**](mailto:reconstructeddecks@gmail.com)**.**


Decklists should be submitted with YOURNAME's DECKNAME at the top. Underneath should be one card per line, with just a leading number. For example:


12 [Mountain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)


4 [Makindi Sliderunner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makindi+Sliderunner)


3 [Valakut Predator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut+Predator)


4 [Wild Slash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Slash)


. . . and so on. Please don't use anything but a space to separate the card numbers and names—don't write "4x [Wild Slash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Slash)," for example. Well-formatted decklists have a much better chance of being read and making it into the column. Poorly formatted decklists are more likely to be ignored. If I can't read your decklist, I certainly can't talk about it!


This one is tricky! And no, don't just send me your Affinity deck with one copy of [Transgress the Mind](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Transgress+the+Mind) somewhere—find a place where you can actually make colorlessness or devoid matter and build a deck around it. This is an advanced deck building challenge, but I already have a few cool ideas. Hop to it!


In the meantime, if you have any feedback on this article or deck, I'd love to see it! Feel free to send me [a tweet](http://www.twitter.com/gavinverhey) or ask me a question on [my Tumblr](http://www.gavinverhey.tumblr.com) and I'll take a look at whatever you might have to say.


I'll be back next week with a look at both Zendikar and Eldrazi. Steel yourselves for battle, and I'll talk with you again then!
