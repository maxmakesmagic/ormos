Information Overload
====================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on August 7, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







Here at **magicthegathering.com** we billed the weekend past as "A Weekend of Champions" ... and with good reason. For Standard afficionados, four different National Championships were decided. For those slugging it out in the Block Constructed PTQs and unsure what to run this weekend... a Japanese Grand Prix tells us the answer (yet again).

Michael's deck is highly reminiscent of the recent Japanese attack deck played by Nakamura and Saito. Michael "left in" the defining creature in this deck—[Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger)—one that was chosen for its resilience in the metagame, despite experimentations (elsewhere) with [Boggart Ram-Gang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Ram-Gang) and other options at that drop. Jacob echoed Saito's original explanation of [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger), that it withstands most of the commonly played removal (bigger than a [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion), [Lash Out](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lash+Out), or [Incinerate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incinerate); can't be targeted by [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror) or [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw)) and additionally pointed out that even when Faeries steals it with a [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation), [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger) won't block for *them*, either. Now everything nice that can be said about [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger) applies (defensively) to the quite *offensive* [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge), which shares its 4 toughness and "being black" special abilities both.


Unlike some red decks with slightly more burn—just have a look at U.S. National Team alternate Marsh Usary's deck, below—Jacob played with a relatively light burn package, only the eight top flight burn spells... But *unlike* Usary, Jacob put his [Snow-Covered Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snow-Covered+Mountain)s to work, employing the mighty [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) as point creature removal.

Superficially Black's Elves deck looks to be built on the same model as the one Charles Gindy used to win [Pro Tour–Hollywood](/en/events/coverage/gindy-puts-name-lights) earlier this year, but there are some key differences to the main deck; specifically, Black's deck list is down a [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), a [Civic Wayfinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Civic+Wayfinder), and all its planeswalkers.

In exchange, this deck is much more defensive than most; rather than debating three [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror)s versus four [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion)s or three versus four [Profane Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Profane+Command)s, the Elves deck Black played had *seven* pieces of main-deck point removal *as well as* the full load of [Profane Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Profane+Command)s. While this positioning may have served Sam earlier in the tournament, the no [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) / heavy black point removal setup put him at a disadvantage against eventual champion Michael Jacob; lots of [Slaughter Pact](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Pact)s and [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion)s is *exactly* what Michael wanted to play against with his [Ashenmoor Gouger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ashenmoor+Gouger)s and Demigods, and Jacob even commented that [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) is one of the more effective Elves cards against his version (he can't punish it explicitly due to playing [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) rather than more burn that can hit one of the opposing planeswalkers).

![Shadowmage Infiltrator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Shadowmage+Infiltrator)

Lone Faeries proponent Paul Cheon very nearly repeated as the U.S. National Champion, a title he earned just two years ago. While Faeries is generally considered the most talked-about deck in both Standard *and* Block, Paul's version has some twists, keeping it interesting *as well as* effective for the tournament.

Specifically, there are some key Faeries *missing.* We normally assume four [Spellstutter Sprite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellstutter+Sprite)s... but this deck? Only three. The really eye-opening change, though, is zero copies of [Scion of Oona](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scion+of+Oona), exchanged for four [Shadowmage Infiltrator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shadowmage+Infiltrator)s! This is a card that we haven't ever really seen in Faeries before, but its inclusion seems inspired.


One of the main differences between Standard and Block Faeries is the presence (in Standard) of [Ancestral Vision](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Vision) for card advantage. This can be important, because outside the "[Dismiss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismiss)" factor on [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command), Faeries has surprisingly little card advantage. Its plays are mostly one-for-one; [Ancestral Vision](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Vision) is a reload that gives Faeries the ammunition it needs to continue to play an essentially one-for-one game while the free [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) tokens start working up a bad attitude. [Shadowmage Infiltrator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shadowmage+Infiltrator) is potentially very good in the Faeries model because, even though Faeries is a control deck, it does not play [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation) main (instead controlling the board with fast one-for-ones). [Shadowmage Infiltrator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shadowmage+Infiltrator) therefore has no [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation) to worry about from its owner (something *Time Spiral* Block Infiltrators wouldn't have been able to claim), and instead can play a refill game, keeping the one-for-ones—both permission and removal—flowing, and with no additional mana expenditure.

I got a chance to chat with Mark about his update to this popular deck.


While Mark shaved the fourth [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) to fit a smattering of miser's one-ofs, the most exciting—and very different—of that crop has to be [Nucklavee](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nucklavee). Brainchild of Innovator Patrick Chapin, [Nucklavee](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nucklavee) (in Quick 'n Toast) is kind of like a 4/4 [Tidings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidings) that always draws [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) and [Dismiss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dismiss) (*in fact*, the only red sorcery, main or side, that [Nucklavee](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nucklavee) can return *is* [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout)). Unfortunately, Mark was never in a position to [Makeshift Mannequin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makeshift+Mannequin)[Nucklavee](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nucklavee), but I'm sure that would be been very exciting, especially mid-combat.



![Nucklavee](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Nucklavee)

What the [Pro Tour–Honolulu](/en/events/coverage/living-heezy-street) Champion thought was the real innovation to the deck was the addition of [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) in the sideboard. Mark actually thought he was a favorite to beat Jacob's red deck in sideboarded games, but did not have the draws to beat multiple copies of [Magus of the Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magus+of+the+Moon), back-to-back. [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) is an important innovation because it can deal with problem creatures like [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), or *really* deal with the gigantic problem of [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge). Historically, very few control decks have been able to contain Demigod, especially in multiples, but [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn), rather than destroying the Demigod, puts it at the bottom of the other guy's library, where it can't as easily hook up with friends.

This deck is two lands off of Tsuyoshi Fujita's legendary 20/20/20 model... but I suppose you need a couple more lands when your mischievous burn spells are chomping up lands along with their mana.


This deck has two unique elements worth mentioning, one main, one side... Main deck, Carl played [Grove of the Burnwillows](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grove+of+the+Burnwillows) and [Kavu Predator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kavu+Predator), a combination rarely seen outside the red-green-white Predator.dec. [Kavu Predator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kavu+Predator)'s trample is valuable in the format simply due to the presence of cards like [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom); it is a nice proactive way to "deal with" the strength of the life gain from some elements ([Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks)) without being reactive. A big surprise for many of Carl's opponents out of the side was [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout)! [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) is the kind of card you usually see *opponents* defending themselves *against* decks like Carl's... So when Carl matched up with other aggressive decks... they often walked right into his surprise removal, in fact.

This five-color version of [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) has many significant differences when compared with the base white-blue versions you may have seen before. First of all... it's five colors! Like many decks, [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) has gotten on the [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) bandwagon. Green isn't a *requirement* on this spell (or on [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks)), but it can help the otherwise red sorcery knock Faeries out of the sky. Black is present for a fair amount of discard out of the sideboard.



![Firespout](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Firespout)

The simplest [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) combo for this build is [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon), [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double), [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), and [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap). With [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon) suspended, and [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) and [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap) in the graveyard, play [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark). Sacrifice [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) to [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon)... but don't quite take that suspend counter off! There is a [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) trigger first. Return [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) (copying [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), now in the graveyard) and [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap). Send 2 points to the opponent's forehead. Still with that Suspend counter on the stack, sacrifice [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap) sufficient times to get him into the graveyard; respond by sacrificing [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double)... which will trigger and return itself (as [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark)) and [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap), ready for the next of potentially infinite iterations. Rinse, repeat, kill.

The main differences in Peyronnel's deck are five comes-into-play tapped lands over Jacob's two, and the fact that Christophe played ordinary [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s instead of [Snow-Covered Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snow-Covered+Mountain)s (this facilitates [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock) rather than [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) as the one-mana burn spell). Essentially the only other difference is [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) over [Magus of the Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magus+of+the+Scroll) as the second one-drop (both champions agreed on [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny)).



![Figure of Destiny](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Figure+of+Destiny)

The sideboards of the two champions' decks were quite different, though, with Peyronnel playing [Faerie Macabre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Macabre) against [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) and [Manabarbs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manabarbs) against the middle turns. Christophe had a fair number of point removal spells ([Puncture Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Puncture+Blast) being a semi-solution to [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks)), whereas Jacob had a sweeper in [Sulfurous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sulfurous+Blast) and no analogue to cards like either the Macabre or [Manabarbs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manabarbs).

It looks like Oliver Ruel had a very similar idea to Mark Herberholz regarding [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn) in Quick 'n Toast. That addition makes weathering [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) much less difficult.


The interesting thing about this deck is that 61st card: [Platinum Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Platinum+Angel). This is an archetype—and a deck in particular—rife with miser's singletons, but if there were ever a card that *really* could dig you out of an insurmountable hole, [Platinum Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Platinum+Angel) is it.
