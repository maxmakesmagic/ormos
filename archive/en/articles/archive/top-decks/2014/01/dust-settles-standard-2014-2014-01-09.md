The Dust Settles (Standard in 2014)
===================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on January 9, 2014

By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 







Welcome back to Perilous Research! It's a new year, but Standard has continued to evolve since we last visited the format at [Grand Prix Dallas](/en/events/coverage/gutierrez-seizes-victory-fort-worth). Today, we'll be taking a look at the new face of Standard. Black and Blue Devotion strategies continue to enjoy success, but the other big decks of the format seem to change with the wind. We will examine and discuss the major players of the Standard format. 

### Black Devotion

 The first deck we need to be looking at is Black Devotion. Black Devotion strategies have been a dominant force in the new Standard since Brian Braun-Duin managed to pilot the archetype to victory at [Grand Prix Louisville](/en/events/coverage/braun-duin-leader-pack-louisville) last October. 

 The deck plays a huge suite of spot removal that punishes opponents trying to cast big creatures like [Polukranos, World Eater](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polukranos%2C+World+Eater) or [Stormbreath Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormbreath+Dragon). The spot removal suite also allows the deck to survive the early turns of the game against the most aggressive decks, especially when the Black Devotion deck gets to be on the play. The major change to the removal package recently is the increasing popularity of main-deck [Pharika's Cure](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pharika%27s+Cure). A lot of players intend on running over the clunky Black Devotion deck with cheap and efficient creatures, [Pharika's Cure](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pharika%27s+Cure) gives the deck more of a fighting chance in these matchups. 


[Pack Rat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat) allows the deck to pick up a lot of free wins, especially when it's used in conjunction with [Mutavault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault), conveniently also a Rat. There are a good number of games where a post-[Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize)d opponent reveales a slow draw without an answer to [Pack Rat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat). It doesn't take many turns for [Pack Rat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat) to take over a game. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Pharika's Cure | Pack Rat |

 The rest of the creatures are deadly efficient for a single-colored deck. [Desecration Demon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desecration+Demon) is all kinds of unfair, [Nightveil Specter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nightveil+Specter) grinds out card advantage while providing a huge boost to [Gray Merchant of Asphodel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gray+Merchant+of+Asphodel), and Gray Merchant turns races around like no other card in the format. 


[Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) is still one of the most important cards in this deck. Disruption in combination with removal will make the game very difficult for a lot of people and prevent opponents from building game plans around big turns.

 Let's take a look at Owen Turtenwald's Black Devotion list from last weekend. Turtenwald already won [Grand Prix Albuquerque](/en/events/coverage/grand-prix-albuquerque-2013) (he also won a Legacy Grand Prix [the weekend before](/en/events/coverage/turtenwald-finally-triumphant-dc)!) with a Black Devotion deck, and it looks like he has no intention of relenting after his recent victory in Indianapolis.

### Selesnya Aggro

Selesnya Aggro is a metagame deck that's designed to punish blue decks. The deck can have trouble with Black Devotion, which is certainly a problem, but the sheer aggression of the deck can let it pick up some wins there whenever the opponent stumbles a bit. The deck is absolutely fantastic against blue decks of all shapes and sizes.

### Gruul

 Gruul Monsters has been around since [Pro Tour *Theros*](/en/events/coverage/dezani-proclaims-allez-les-bleus-pro-tour) where my roommate for the event, Brad Nelson, piloted the deck to 30th place. (Just one spot behind the most handsome wizard in the world.) The deck made it all the way to the finals of [Grand Prix Louisville](/en/events/coverage/braun-duin-leader-pack-louisville) the following weekend in the hands of Jon Stern. 

 Gruul decks have access to a lot of powerful cards. [Xenagos, the Reveler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xenagos%2C+the+Reveler) matches up very well against Black Devotion and the control decks, while [Garruk, Caller of Beasts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk%2C+Caller+of+Beasts) gives the deck longevity that will prove to be unbeatable if left unchecked for a few turns. [Domri Rade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Domri+Rade) acts as a removal spell that threatens to win the game if left unchecked to look at the top card over and over. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Xenagos, the Reveler | Garruk, Caller of Beasts |

 The deck plays a lot of mana creatures with the intention of sticking an early [Polukranos, World Eater](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polukranos%2C+World+Eater) or [Stormbreath Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormbreath+Dragon) to establish board control. 

 The most impressive finish available to this strategy is [Flesh & Blood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flesh+%2F%2F+Blood). This card hasn't been utilized much in competitive Constructed, but it packs one hell of a wallop when combined with [Ghor-Clan Rampager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghor-Clan+Rampager). Here's some food for thought. A Gruul player has six lands and a 2/2 [Scavenging Ooze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scavenging+Ooze) in play with three cards in hand and no creatures in either graveyard. The Gruul player attacks and the tapped-out opponent waves the Ooze through. "[Ghor-Clan Rampager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghor-Clan+Rampager), [Ghor-Clan Rampager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghor-Clan+Rampager), [Flesh & Blood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flesh+%2F%2F+Blood)." That's 20 points of damage completely out of nowhere.

### Blue Devotion

 Blue Devotion decks dominated [Pro Tour *Theros*](/en/events/coverage/dezani-proclaims-allez-les-bleus-pro-tour) in Dublin and continue to post high-profile results. 

 The Blue Devotion deck focuses mostly on the power of [Thassa, God of the Sea](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thassa%2C+God+of+the+Sea) and [Master of Waves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Waves). The rest of the cards in the deck are there to maximize the value of these two cards. Sure, it may seem a little bold to play a deck that can be that all-in on two cards, but the consistency and inexpensive nature of the deck allow it to win a lot of games where the opponent stumbles, even when it can't find a [Master of Waves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Waves) or [Thassa, God of the Sea](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thassa%2C+God+of+the+Sea).

 There has been some debate as to whether [Bident of Thassa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bident+of+Thassa) or [Jace, Architect of Thought](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought) is the card-advantage, four-mana card of choice. Over time, it seems that a split of the two cards is probably correct. [Bident of Thassa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bident+of+Thassa) is huge against the decks that Blue Devotion can fly over, while [Jace, Architect of Thought](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Architect+of+Thought) applies card pressure even when we're not attacking and significantly reduces the offensive potential of other Blue Devotion strategies and Human decks.

### Azorius Control

Esper Control had a bit of success in the early stages of the format, but the deck seems to stumble too much and its lack of success in recent events has led most control savants to switch to the more wholesome Azorius Control.

 Azorius Control is the most reactive deck in the current Standard format. The deck only plays one win condition in [Elspeth, Sun's Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion). If [Elspeth, Sun's Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion) is dealt with, the Azorius Control player can simply shuffle her back into the deck with [Elixir of Immortality](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elixir+of+Immortality). If [Elspeth, Sun's Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion) gets exiled, then the Azorius Control deck can still win by decking its opponent by repeatedly looping [Elixir of Immortality](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elixir+of+Immortality) while firing off huge [Sphinx's Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation)s and [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict)s every turn.

### Esper Midrange

Esper Human strategies have been quietly building a reputation over the last three weeks. These decks are tremendously well positioned in the current Standard format.


[Xathrid Necromancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xathrid+Necromancer) does a lot of work in a deck with this many Humans. [Xathrid Necromancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xathrid+Necromancer) is incredible against the Black Devotion strategies that are packing no less than four copies of [Devour Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devour+Flesh). It makes [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) decks need a [Detention Sphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detention+Sphere) if they don't want to get run over. Against creature-based strategies, the Esper midrange deck can win trades even against creatures that outclass it by double- or triple-blocking and picking up an army of Zombies. 

![Xathrid Necromancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Xathrid+Necromancer)
[Obzedat, Ghost Council](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council) may not be seeing a ton of play right now, but that card is completely insane. In fact, if I had to pick the best five-drop in Standard, I could easily and decisively pick out [Obzedat, Ghost Council](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council). It races everything and becomes a completely unstoppable force when the Esper Midrange player is able to find [Whip of Erebos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whip+of+Erebos). 


[Detention Sphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detention+Sphere) is why the deck is splashing blue. The three-mana enchantment gives the deck an out to a [Pack Rat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pack+Rat) that's capable of getting out of hand and opposing Planeswalkers, and it often picks up a two-for-one against the heavy-creature format that we're currently experiencing.

 The deck is, however, quite weak to [Blood Baron of Vizkopa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Baron+of+Vizkopa), only packing a few [Far // Away](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Far%2B%252F%252F%2BAway) to deal with the board-dominating finisher. 

Let's take a gander at Brandon Nelson's Esper Midrange list. This is the version of the deck that seems to have the most traction right now. You should play a few games against this deck if you're planning on playing in a PTQ this weekend.
