The Many Faces of Malka
=======================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on November 17, 2004 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






The Rock came into the recent [Pro Tour Columbus](/en/events/coverage/canali-crushes-columbus) as the unconditional leader in terms of popularity. With over 60 representatives, The Rock made up more than 20% of the field... yet not a one made Top 8 in a tournament that boasted *eight different archetypes*.

Now for players interested in statistical performance, this should tell you that the “[noblest deck](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/live-coverage-2005-pro-tour-columbus-2004-10-30-0)” fared pretty badly, but I am pretty sure that regardless of the numbers, the Pro Tour aspirants out there will reclaim their love for The Rock once the Extended PTQ season hits. Over the past three years, The Rock has gone from a 100% rogue choice to a deck that every team has to prepare for. 

Now unlike previous years, the PT Columbus crowd showed us several legitimately different looks at Sol Malka's baby. The most basic is probably exemplified by the version played by Joe "Mouth" Kambourakis:

Mouth's deck shows off a combination of new innovations without ever deviating from the classic Rock plan. He replaces [Wall of Blossoms](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Blossoms) with [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder), which makes up a lot for the missing [Yavimaya Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yavimaya+Elder) (whose spot was stolen by the superb [Eternal Witness](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness)). This deck is pretty straightforward, with the same 23 lands we have seen for several years, 4 [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village)s, all of it. In addition to the Snake Shaman, *Champions of Kamigawa* adds [Kokusho, the Evening Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kokusho%2C+the+Evening+Star) and *two* copies of [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction) to the main deck.

Jeroen is one of the most decorated Malka players in recent years; he did not disappoint in Columbus, playing straight B/G once again to a money finish.

Even though his deck is straight B/G, Remie's deck is a far cry from what we would expect from a classic Rock. His deck brings back [Spiritmonger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spiritmonger) -- albeit only two copies -- as the big finisher, but adds a ton of beats along the way.

Turn 1: Forest + Bird or Elf  
 Turn 2: Swamp + Negator or Troll

Turn 1: [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village)  
 Turn 2: Swamp + [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) + Bird or Elf  
 Turn 3: [Ravenous Baloth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Baloth)

Remie's deck adds [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) to [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise), allowing his deck to get serious threats like [Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic) and [Phyrexian Negator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Negator) down in short order. Contrast the baseline philosophy of this B/G deck with the previous one. While both Jeroen and Mouth play two colors with many cards in common, Jeroen almost doesn't need a card like [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction) to fight combo. He has four [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) and four [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) starting, and shortens the number of turns a Mind's Desire or Aluren deck has to win after even light disruption *just because he has [Phyrexian Negator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Negator) in his deck*!

Jeroen can wipe away blockers with [Diabolic Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Edict), and has a good long game with his [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed)s because he plays with actual regenerating creatures. In order to play these cards, his version gives up the gradual card advantage from [Wall of Blossoms](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Blossoms), [Yavimaya Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yavimaya+Elder), and [Dust Bowl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dust+Bowl) that have been The Rock's hallmarks.

For a complete departure from classic The Rock design, check out the version played by the current best player on the planet:

This is the example that breaks the rule. If The Rock is meant to be the noblest deck in Extended, steer any judges away from Nassif's version... it's full of broken cards! [Intuition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intuition) seems a natural addition to the deck, setting up [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis), [Recurring Nightmare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Recurring+Nightmare), and other threats. Again, we see Nassif playing [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction), setting that card up with [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor). Though he doesn't start [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) is better in Nassif's deck than in either of the previous versions because of his Walls.

One mistake too many of The Rock's adherents made in Columbus was to underrate Affinity. They assumed that with four [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed)s and a couple of [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)s, they would have the Affinity matchup in the bag. Nassif is arguably worse against an Affinity deck because he only plays two Deeds... but his sideboard finishes off the set with [Oxidize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize) and, more importantly, [Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux). With [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor) setting up [Energy Flux](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Energy+Flux) and ready access to his Island, Nassif's version seems the best to me at fighting Canali's deck over the course of three games.

That said, we saved the best for last. Torben Twiefel made Top 16 with Red Rock.

![Flametongue Kavu](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Flametongue+Kavu)The fun thing about typing in innumerable deck lists is that you can really get into a player's head when you are reading his list for an open field event. It's almost like different players are playing in different tournaments. Twiefel makes a concession to combination decks with his 7 [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)es and main-deck [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction)... but Torben plays with main-deck [Flametongue Kavu](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flametongue+Kavu) as well! While most of his contemporaries had [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder), Red Rock stacks its four-drops with [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum). It's almost like Twiefel is prepared for *a different tournament*.But Torben obviously had something up his sleeve, because he was the only player out of over 20% of the field to crack the Top 16 with The Rock. What does it mean when the anti-creature version, splashing red and playing more clunky four mana creatures, is the best performer? Does it mean that the lone [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction) silver bullet is enough to ward off combo decks? Does it mean that the format isn't as fast as we thought?

I am guessing that any Malka players out there who want to go the way of Mr. Twiefel will want to add at least one [Meltdown](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meltdown) to the sideboard. You've got 3 [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)s, after all.

For yet another completely different look at Red Rock, here is a deck that Kai Budde used to finish in the money at GP Reims two years ago. Kai's deck is obviously several sets removed, but what I think is interesting is his choice to splash red for [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate) main and nothing else; while [Flametongue Kavu](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flametongue+Kavu) is good at fighting creatures, [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate) is a lot better at killing enemy [Spiritmonger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spiritmonger)s.

Obviously, there are several ways to build The Rock. Many players -- some of them great -- have gone several different ways. Not that long ago, **Magic's** original Juggernaut, Gab Tsang, finished in the Top 32 of PT Houston with a version touching white for [Vindicate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate). He didn't have access to [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) (or Torben's [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum)s), but supported those flexible white cards with the help of four [Krosan Tusker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker)s! 

Whether straight B/G or splashing one of the other three colors of **Magic** is the right build remains to be seen. Despite its disappointing finish in Columbus, I would be shocked if the noblest deck in Extended did not buy many a Top 8 finish and PT slot in the upcoming qualifier season.
