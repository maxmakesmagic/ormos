Grand Slam
==========



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 28, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Let's begin with a Regionals wrap-up. [Last week](/en/articles/archive/top-decks/deck-anti-deck-2009-05-21) (as some of you guessed in the forums) I wrote my article without the benefit of the Top 8 deck lists from Regionals, and so focused on a couple of decks played by my own friends to Top 8 at my own Regionals. Now we have, if not a full reporting, a fine number of Top 8s, and plenty to talk about.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td40_chart1.jpg)  
As we predicted a couple of weeks ago, the top decks at U.S. Regionals were Black-White and Green-White Tokens decks. So how about my bet with Jon Becker? It looks like your old pal michaelj lost this one. I stipulated that *more than half* of qualifying deck lists would be based on [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession), [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights), and their assorted good buddies (most notably [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger), but with a tip of the hat to [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), [Marsh Flitter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Marsh+Flitter), and in the case of the red-white cousin, [Siege-Gang Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander)). It looks like just *under* half of the qualifying decks meet my description, so ... Looks like I owe the Godfather a(nother) Katz's pastrami sandwich!

Traditionally I would spend the next couple of paragraphs talking about how super important it will be, as we continue through the present PTQ and Grand Prix format, to either shuffle up or prepare for the two major tokens decks and their close relatives (Bant, B/W Kithkin, regular old Kithkin, and so on)... After all, it wasn't long ago that we drew a line in the virtual sand and said that [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) is the top card in the format.

*But...*

I figure at this point you get how that strategy works.

Instead I will talk about what can only be considered the most important new strategy to come out of U.S. Regionals.

But first, I would like to talk a little bit about social networking.

I mean live coverage from the Tournament Center now includes [a Twitter feed](http://twitter.com/magicprotour), but what is more interesting and funny given the current state of events came from a site that most of you probably already visit every day. To wit, **Magic: The Gathering** bossman Aaron Forsythe (himself a onetime Regionals deck designer of some gigantic excitement):

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td40_facebook.jpg)  

> **Aaron Forsythe** is hoping some interesting new things come out of Regionals.

For those of you who didn't know, right before he signed on with the Renton, WA illuminati, Forsythe produced a hellacious deck that ran all over summer Standard, which debuted at the Ohio Valley Regionals that year. Basically, he took a perfectly good mono-green land destruction deck and added cards like [Avalanche Riders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avalanche+Riders) and [Ancient Hydra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Hydra), resisting suggestions from the Mad Genius of **Magic** Erik Lauer to include such spells as [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock).

Aaron's only response? A cryptic call-back from Boat Brewer Brian Kowal (Kowal Trivia: As far as the "Brew" in Boat Brew goes, Brian hails from Milwaukee, Wisconsin, though he is now a resident of Madison).


> a 40 land deck q'd at ours. Is that interesting enough?

Before gaining notoriety with his red-white [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) / [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights) deck, Kowal was best known for taking a perfectly good color (red), eschewing the popular one-mana spells, and replacing them with what Randy Buehler once called "terrible, uncastable spells" ... So you can see that these guys see eye-to-eye when it comes to some things Standard. 

The deck that Brian was talking about was Cascade Swans; the Chicago-area deck, for the record, featured forty-two lands.

Those of you who regularly read Brian David-Marshall's column here on the mother ship probably already know all about this powerful new strategy.

![Swans of Bryn Argoll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll)  
Top Decks has already spent several columns talking about Cascade, as a mechanic, in terms of the incremental advantages available; that is, what most players probably thought of as the "obvious" utilization of the mechanic. The Cascade Swans deck takes a route more like our original discussion of a Limited [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate); when [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate) is the *only* two mana spell in your forty-card deck, than any three-mana cascade spell is going to double as a [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate). The Cascade Swans deck takes this principle and applies the same to a single *three*-mana spell: [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault). Therefore when you play a [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), you have exactly one kind of card that will appear thanks to cascade: [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault); that is, half of your combination.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td40_elfIntoAssault.jpg)  
The other half of the combo is of course [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll).

With both of these cards in play (and assuming the opponent doesn't have something along the lines of a [Pithing Needle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle) set to [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) already in play), you can take any of the many lands that are inevitably in your hand and you can start throwing them at the Swans; each land would deal 2 points of damage, but will instead allow you to draw two cards. Your deck is over two-thirds lands, so you are favored to *at least* "break even" on land draws until you can assemble the ten or so lands you need in hand to start throwing them at the opponent.

Now look at the spell slots in Parth Modi's deck. [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) is there as a low-danger card drawing option. Basically, the deck is over two-thirds lands, so you can draw a fair amount of lands without investing much life.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Ad Nauseam | Bituminous Blast |

The more important and last spell is [Bituminous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bituminous+Blast), [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf)'s cousin in Cascade. This card can *only* flip up [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll), [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), or [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) as [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) and the other copies of [Bituminous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bituminous+Blast) are five mana spells. Because [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) can itself only produce a [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) via Cascade, that means that [Bituminous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bituminous+Blast) will yeild a [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll) about one third of the time and a [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) about two thirds of the time; in either case, it *will* be producing a combo piece (you just don't usually get to pick which). 

One of the cool side effects of playing so many lands is that this deck is essentially never manascrewed (flooded is another issue entirely, but it is *never* manascrewed!). But far from being flooded on a consistent basis, this version of the Cascade Swans strategy packs a ton of functional lands. There is a basic [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) for [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile), and a host of man-lands to initiate attack against decks that produce a lot of Blue mana: [Mutavault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutavault), [Ghitu Encampment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghitu+Encampment), *and* [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village)!



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Mutavault | Treetop Village |

But on top of all those man-lands—and in the spirit of all these [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights)—the deck plays the red hideaway land, [Spinerock Knoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spinerock+Knoll).

![Spinerock Knoll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Spinerock+Knoll)  
In this deck the card you really want to get under [Spinerock Knoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spinerock+Knoll) is [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll). The deck can easily hard-cast [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) with [Spinerock Knoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spinerock+Knoll) and two other sources of red mana (which are obviously plentiful in this deck, including [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village) when combined with [Fire-Lit Thicket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire-Lit+Thicket)). Then on the fourth turn you can untap and initiate thusly:

1. Toss four lands at the opponent.
2. Having dealt 8 damage (that is, one more than 7), you get to activate [Spinerock Knoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spinerock+Knoll).
3. Reveal ye olde [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll) ...
4. And that's the combo! Start hammering the Swans with your remaining lands in hand, then finish the game.

Since Regionals, [Spinerock Knoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spinerock+Knoll) has fallen a bit in popularity due to the very realistic possibility of just revealing a bunch of lands (after all, there aren't very many spells *in* the deck, and pretty much the only one you want is [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll)). However there is an incentive in that you get one more way to win on the fourth turn.

Hugo De Jong didn't play [Bituminous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bituminous+Blast)*at all*, and the other two decks in the Top 8 cut the Blasts to two.

The fact is, [Bituminous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bituminous+Blast) was / is probably the weakest card in the deck. As a five-mana spell, it is slow; as a creature removal spell you can only play it at all when you have something to aim it at, and as we noted above, you don't get to pick what you are going to get. Note that all three decks played [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command) on five, which is another card that can help you find [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll).

Something many players may have forgotten in their adoption of, say, the Fog deck is how [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique) works. You see, a careful Faeries player can easily wait to have a lethal alpha strike on the board and then play [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique). Not only is that a worthwhile spell to fight over on the opponent's end of turn, but the Fog deck simply doesn't have that many permission spells that it can aim at a [Mistbind Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mistbind+Clique). As such, the Clique—*some* Clique, you *do* have four—should resolve. Once that happens the opponent should be mostly or totally tapped, allowing you to get one good shot in that your foe can't successfully Fog.

Neri's deck isn't a Five-Color Control deck as we typically talk about them, or even a new-fangled Five-Color Beatdown sorta-control deck based on [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) (but that still has many of the trappings of the control side). This is more of a planeswalker deck. It invests in powerful and expensive permanents and uses them over and over again.

Case in point, two permanents that you may not have seen in serious decks, [Obelisk of Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obelisk+of+Alara) and OMG IS THAT [NICOL BOLAS, PLANESWALKER](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=NICOL+BOLAS%2C+PLANESWALKER) OMG!!!

![Nicol Bolas, Planeswalker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Nicol+Bolas%2C+Planeswalker)  
[Obelisk of Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obelisk+of+Alara) does a little bit of everything ... It can dig you out of a hole, keep creatures off your back, or win the game directly, 3 points at a time.

![Obelisk of Alara](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Obelisk+of+Alara)  
Hopefully this will not be the last time we see Nicol Bolas in a serious sixty. I actually feel that Nicol Bolas is a superb sideboard card for Five-Color Control (or straight Grixis Control, assuming there would be any) specifically against midrange control decks or something along the lines of a Jund Mana Ramp. Basically these kinds of decks can't kill you before turn eight, and if they let you untap with Nicol Bolas ... well ... there is a reason this particular planeswalker costs eight mana. 

In sum, and with a long summer PTQ season ahead of us focused on this format, I think the message is that Cascade Swans is the exciting new It Deck (for now) but that there are twenty or more viable decks, and that—just as with the conceptual leaps that glued a very mid-range Control mechanic onto a combo kill in the Swans deck—customization remains alive and well and available throughout the format. Good luck to everyone reaching for the Blue Envelope this weekend!
