*Innistrad* Limited: First Impressions
======================================



 Posted in **Limited Information**
 on September 27, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_SteveSadin.jpg)
By Steve Sadin











This past weekend I played in the *Innistrad* Prerelease at [Jim Hanley's Universe](http://www.jhuniverse.blogspot.com/) (JHU) in Manhattan, and I had an absolute blast. I'm looking forward to going back there for some Saturday afternoon drafts as soon as the set gets released.


It's interesting to go from playing ***Magic** 2012*, where players would jump through hoops to be able to block and trade with creatures early because of bloodthirst, to *Innistrad*, where players are incentivized to go out of their way to keep their opponents' creatures alive because of morbid (and because of cards like [Stitched Drake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stitched+Drake) that require you to exile creatures from your graveyard).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/li/li162_drake.jpg)[Stitched Drake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stitched+Drake) | Art by [Chris Rahn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22Chris+Rahn%22%5D)


Over time, players will certainly adjust to this new reality, but at the Prerelease a lot of players still seemed to be in ***Magic** 2012* mode, where they would take any reasonable trade that they could make (provided it would prevent them from taking any damage that turn).


Yes, it's still important to protect your life total, but you'll be able to earn quite a few extra wins if you are conscious of the fact that many of your opponents are going to actively want their creatures to die so they can summon bigger, and better, replacements.


### How Fast Is It?


While I don't think it will end up being as fast as ***Magic** 2012* Limited was, *Innistrad* Limited is faster than you probably think it is. Werewolves punish players for missing early drops. Vampires like [Bloodcrazed Neonate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodcrazed+Neonate) can grow to abnormal sizes if you don't have a way to deal with them quickly. [Darkthicket Wolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkthicket+Wolf) can take huge chunks out of your life total if you don't deal with it fast. You've got creatures like [Stitched Drake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stitched+Drake) that are absolute all-stars for their costs. And to top it all off, you've got cards like [Brimstone Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley) and [Rally the Peasants](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rally+the+Peasants) that can allow you to close out games in a hurry.

Over the next few weeks, I'm going to keep a close eye on the speed of the format. Will slow control decks have the tools to thrive? Or are the tools for aggressive decks so strong, and the penalties for not casting spells (and consequently turning on opposing Werewolves) so high, that players will need to fill their decks up with cheap creatures in order to remain competitive?

I finished the tournament 3-1 and was very happy with the deck's performance.


While my opponents were choosing to draw first and expecting that they would have plenty of time to get their three colors of mana and eventually close out the game with six- and seven-mana bombs, I was busy killing them.


My two copies of [Rakish Heir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakish+Heir) were absolute all-stars for me. There were games where I would use my removal to clear a path for my Vampires, and they would gradually grow to unmanageable sizes. Then there were other games where my opponents would trade with my [Rakish Heir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakish+Heir) at the first opportunity, and this would enable me to cast a 5/5 [Festerhide Boar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Festerhide+Boar).

| Rakish Heir | Festerhide Boar |

I don't suspect that [Rakish Heir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakish+Heir) should be that high of a draft pick, but as far as morbid enablers go it's pretty great. Your opponents need to deal with it immediately (and turn on morbid), or else watch it grow, and grow, and grow.


Without a good number of early threats like [Darkthicket Wolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkthicket+Wolf), [Ambush Viper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ambush+Viper), [Villagers of Estwald](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Villagers+of+Estwald), [Kessig Wolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf), and my two copies of [Rakish Heir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakish+Heir), I would have had a deck with a bunch of uninspiring creatures at the middle of my curve (like [Festerhide Boar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Festerhide+Boar)s that I would have a lot of trouble powering up, and [Ulvenwald Mystics](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ulvenwald+Mystics) that I would have had trouble transforming) and an insufficient amount of removal to deal with opposing bombs.


### Building Your Sealed Deck to Beat Bombs


If your Sealed Deck pool doesn't have bombs and/or multiple good ways to deal with opposing bombs, then you need to build your deck so it's aggressive enough for you to win before your opponents can take over the game with their game-breaking cards.


My white was initially appealing thanks to the fact that it had a strong removal package including [Fiend Hunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiend+Hunter), [Smite the Monstrous](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smite+the+Monstrous), [Rebuke](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rebuke), and [Divine Reckoning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divine+Reckoning), but the green-white decks that I put together all wound up being fairly unimpressive.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Fiend Hunter | Rebuke |

While the white version of the deck would have been stronger against decks that relied heavily on their bombs, it was noticeably slower than the red (2 [Rakish Heir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakish+Heir)s and a [Kessig Wolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf) drastically improved the odds that I would be able to make a 5/5 [Festerhide Boar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Festerhide+Boar) on turn four). The white version also didn't have any finishers that would allow me to take over stalemated games in the same way that [Heretic's Punishment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heretic%27s+Punishment) would.


![Heretic's Punishment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Heretic%27s+Punishment)  
I sideboarded into the white once, and I immediately regretted it. While it was better at dealing with individual bombs than the red-green version of the deck was, my green-white deck just wasn't powerful enough to compete with a barrage of good (but not necessarily great) cards.


While having a couple of all-purpose answers in your deck is certainly good, if the rest of the cards in your deck are just weaker than the cards that your opponent is going to be throwing at you, then it won't matter that you can kill any given creature that they play.


### The Importance of Being Human


When [Avacynian Priest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacynian+Priest) was previewed, I didn't even stop to think about it. I saw a good tapper, and just kept looking. However, the fact that [Avacynian Priest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacynian+Priest) can't tap Humans actually winds up being a huge limitation.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Avacynian Priest | Bonds of Faith |

While [Avacynian Priest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacynian+Priest) and [Bonds of Faith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bonds+of+Faith) are certainly good, they're nowhere near as versatile as I thought they would be before I actually played with the set. If you're playing against a base white deck, [Avacynian Priest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacynian+Priest) might not be able to tap anything.


Even if you don't have any cards that directly benefit you for having Humans, you're going to want to prioritize Humans over non-Human creatures whenever you get the chance (unless you have other tribal synergies going for you). Doing so will minimize the effectiveness of opposing [Avacynian Priest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacynian+Priest)s and [Bonds of Faith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bonds+of+Faith). This is especially relevant for cards at the top of your curve—the cards that you really want to attack with.
