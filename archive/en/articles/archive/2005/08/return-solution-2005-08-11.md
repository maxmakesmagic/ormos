Return of the Solution
======================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on August 11, 2005 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic."

*Invasion's* Block Constructed Pro Tour represented a lot of firsts for Professional **Magic**. Not counting Worlds 1999, Pro Tour Tokyo in 2001 was the first actual Pro Tour to take place in Japan; appropriately, that Pro Tour featured the first Japanese player to make the break to Top 8: today's Resident Genius (and still arguably the world's best deck designer four years later), Tsuyoshi Fujita went all the way to the finals. The *Invasion* Block Pro Tour gave us a look at pure dominance that has not been matched in a constructed format since. One team of eleven players ran essentially the same deck; four of them -- Ryan Fuller, Philip Freneau, Lucas Hager, and Chris Benafel -- made Top 8, with many others finishing in the money. More than that great team or deck accomplishment, front-man Ryan Fuller actually posted a breathtaking *14-0* record in the Swiss, the first player ever to go truly undefeated approaching the elimination rounds; Ryan's juggernaut-like set of wins finally ended in the quarterfinals, where his streak went down in a hotly contested 3-2 mirror match against teammate Chris Benafel... Fuller missed a ton of land drops and declared a lot of mulligans in losing to his cohort.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/pttok01/655.jpg) But amidst all these firsts, the first Japanese Pro Tour (something we take for granted as being on the schedule these days); the first appearance of the now-dominant Japanese in their home country; a clear crack in the format represented by a powerhouse archetype, an ascendant team, and an undefeated record... They all pale compared to the finish, the real finish that everything was all leading up to. In a format where players expected B/R/U [Yawgmoth's Agenda](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Agenda) to be the most popular deck, where G/R control-killing beatdown ended up being the most consistent and powerful deck across the field, it was a little U/W deck originally dubbed "notasbadasitlooks.dec" that took home the Pro Tour Championship.

Eventually called The Solution, the U/W deck played by Champion Zvi Mowshowitz may not have broken *Invasion* Block Constructed... but it did manage to solve the puzzle of the format. The Solution had a little something for everyone. For creature decks, it had [Exclude](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exclude) and [Repulse](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Repulse). It could do a little beatdown itself with several early drops, many of which had relevant special abilities. The Solution ran a card drawing instant at ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130050im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless3.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif) against which today's gold standard – [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) – pales in terms of utility. And to top it all off, the deck had some countermagic, too, for those times when the only reasonable response to a question is, “I think not.”


Was the Solution as powerful as some of the other decks out there? No way. It lacked the offensive punch of G/R. It could keep parity with B/U, [Fact or Fiction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction) for [Fact or Fiction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction), [Absorb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Absorb) for [Undermine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Undermine), and so on... until [Yawgmoth's Agenda](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Agenda) hit. But somehow, the deck's curious combination of threat/answer hybrid cards, from [Stormscape Apprentice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormscape+Apprentice) (a quick drop who defends), to [Meddling Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meddling+Mage) (a sort of walking pre-emptive [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)), to the clunky [Voice of All](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Voice+of+All) (a 2/2 for four mana) came together as exactly the right deck to take on the Tokyo field. The lesson of the Solution is that you don't necessarily have to have the biggest, bluntest, object to brain the opponent when you can present a sufficiently quick, efficient, and *well-aimed* answer. Along with a little card advantage, the tempo of a fast response can go a long way. The Solution, though, was ultimately a metagame deck. Knowing that a lot of the format's removal would be red (don't forget that [Flametongue Kavu](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flametongue+Kavu) was then the hip new kid on the block), the Solution's adherents made good use of the seemingly innocuous [Crimson Acolyte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crimson+Acolyte)... to the tune of all four main deck.

With a "regular" White Weenie draw, you could end up with a stray mana and one less the next turn. Scarier for a [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) deck are the games where Deck-X can play turn four [Yosei](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yosei). Consider the above draw. Without even making a fourth land drop, Fujita could tap his three lands, tap and untap his Shaku for Isasamaru and Jitte, and have six mana of Yosei. With a fourth land, Fujita would be threatening [Blessed Breath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blessed+Breath).

"I don't think the [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) deck can win even 30% of games," said Brian David-Marshall, magicthegathering.com Tournament Center reporter on-site in Taipei. "They have to kill the Yosei... and then they're tapped out for [Hokori](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hokori)."


Deck-X was not designed to purely race aggressive decks, but it can still get a nice beat going with [Konda's Banner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Konda%27s+Banner). Attaching [Konda's Banner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Konda%27s+Banner) to Meloku allows the deck to go [Glorious Anthem](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glorious+Anthem) with his team of Illusion tokens. Because every creature in Deck-X is Legendary, Fujita could attach [Konda's Banner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Konda%27s+Banner) to any of his White Weenies, setting up a [Crusade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crusade)- or even [Death Pit Offering](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Pit+Offering)- level boost for his Foxes, Spirits, and Samurai. Most people might not play [Kentaro, the Smiling Cat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kentaro%2C+the+Smiling+Cat) or [Sensei Golden-Tail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei+Golden-Tail) *at all*, but with [Konda's Banner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Konda%27s+Banner), those Samurai can be pretty good together.


Like the Solution in Tokyo, Deck-X looks a little under-powered. It doesn't have the complete lockdown capability or massive card advantage engine of [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven). It doesn't have the robust mana acceleration of Kamigawa's green decks. With no [Charge Across the Araba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Charge+Across+the+Araba), it doesn't even have the pure rush capability of a "real" White Weenie deck. What Deck-X does have is a curious combination of answers and tempo that make it a powerful metagame foil, ideal for the three bye metagame of a [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven)-dominated Grand Prix.


 Like the Solution in Tokyo, Deck-X looks a little under-powered. Deck-X might not be able to loop infinite [Fog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fog)s or [Mutilate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutilate)s, but it can hold a lead against a tapped out opponent with [Hokori](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hokori) and [Blessed Breath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blessed+Breath). Though it lacks access to the rock solid [Sakura-Tribe Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) and [Kodama's Reach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kodama%27s+Reach) suite played by every green deck, Deck-X's combination of [Honor-Worn Shaku](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor-Worn+Shaku) and Hokori expresses a classic “Prison” synergy, allowing Fujita tons of operable mana while the opponent – even one with Forests in play – is stuck untapping only one land per turn. Though it doesn't have all the efficacy of a pure rush White Weenie or Black Hand deck, Deck-X can post a Counter-Sliver offense, protecting its board with cards like [Hisoka's Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hisoka%27s+Defiance), holding small leads instead of giving them up against sweepers like [Hideous Laughter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hideous+Laughter) or [Kagemaro, First to Suffer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Kagemaro%252C%2BFirst%2Bto%2BSuffer).


Will Deck-X lose some potency now that it's out of the bag? Of course! The fact that this week was probably the first time you had heard of it is a testament to its surprise value… but that doesn't mean an unprepared Gifts Ungiven deck will be any better able to beat it.


All season we have followed the dominant decks of Kamigawa Block. First it was [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) in beatdown decks. Tech quickly evolved such that the White Weenie decks made better use of *Saviors of Kamigawa* and the Black Hand decks -- just beginning to grab at that title of best deck in the format -- began to move away from purely suicidal, if effective, drops. Then all of a sudden the champion -- the real thing as it were -- from the Pro Tour came back and said "Don't you forget about me," retook the reins of Kamigawa Block and hasn't let go. Sure, there have been some decks trying to compete. Mono-Blue, Blue/Green, and others have cropped up, challenging "the best deck." Deck-X doesn't try to do that. Deck-X knows that it isn't the best deck. It doesn't have to break the format... Gifts Ungiven already did that. But maybe, just maybe, Deck-X can be the Solution to what we have seen thus far… Osamu Fujita can't be disappointed with how it ran. Now let's see how Deck-X handles in a wider field.
