A Little Bit of This, a Little Bit of That
==========================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 6, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






So last weekend was the start of a new Standard Pro Tour Qualifier cycle with *Rise of the Eldrazi*. We also had a couple of big cash tournaments from Star City Games.com and TCGPlayer.com, basically lots of stuff going on, all different kinds of decks winning ...

... and barely any Jund.

Not zero Jund, mind you, but if week one is much indication, I don't see [Sprouting Thrinax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax) and company dumping black, green, and red paint all over the metagame as many feared.

Meet the new boogeyman:

This version of White-Blue Control is like—but not exactly like—any of the white-blue decks we looked at last week, or previously. It has some Tap-Out to it—particularly the [Everflowing Chalice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice)s into big creatures—but has eschewed the [Knight of the White Orchid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid) engine for [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) (the basis for most white defensive decks now that *Rise of the Eldrazi* is legal). You'll notice that while this isn't a "permission" deck, it does have some counterspells. One strategy is to pass on a turn two [Everflowing Chalice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice), tap four for it turn *four*, and then play a [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) turn five with counterspell backup. As with most successful blue decks in Standard, Phil's deck is built on a [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) base to help smooth draws and defend against Jund; [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) is the only blue four-of in the list.

Phil is one of the good men of the NY/NJ area, despite working for Goldman Sachs. He is the kind of guy who gets a hundred high fives on Facebook upon winning the first PTQ of the season, and each and every one is much deserved.

He is also the kind of person to share the wisdom of the win, so gather 'round:

1. Unlike some of the other decks we will look at this week, Phil didn't play [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura); the reason is that there are too many situations where [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) can either win on the spot or steal a game where Gideon will not. [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) actually puts the opponent under pressure to have an answer RIGHT NOW, and—by now most of us know this—hands players free wins.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Deprive | Celestial Colonnade |

2. Phil was very happy with [Deprive](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deprive). It does not give players as much of an edge in the mirror as [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) (see below), but it does set up an edge. Principally, [Deprive](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deprive) can rescue [Celestial Colonnade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade) from [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas). [Deprive](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deprive) is not a desirable early game play, but is flexible—even advantageous—in long games.

3. Surprisingly, Phil doesn't like [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) as much as everybody else. He only lost games due to mana issues, and as such, may be going back to a [Knight of the White Orchid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid) / [Fieldmist Borderpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fieldmist+Borderpost) engine.

4. Phil beat a wide variety of decks ... Jund (3), Bant Conscription, Vampires, Grixis Control, White-Blue Control, and [Polymorph](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polymorph); he felt that having counterspells was instrumental in beating a wide variety of decks (rather than a true Tap-Out strategy).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td89_sphinxArt.jpg)  
5. Unlike yours truly, Phil loves a [Sphinx of Jwar Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx+of+Jwar+Isle). It is a planeswalker trump that forces opponents to keep in [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) (or whatever) after sideboarding.

6. DON'T BOARD OUT [DAY OF JUDGMENT](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=DAY+OF+JUDGMENT) AGAINST JUND (just because they have [Sprouting Thrinax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax)). Every [Siege-Gang Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander) is "backbreaking," and you have to have an answer to [Malakir Bloodwitch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Malakir+Bloodwitch).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td89_dayWithCards.jpg)  
7. "[Mind Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring) is one of the best cards in the format and I could not see not playing it."

Kar's deck is basically a White-Blue Tap-Out deck that cuts the creatures—the non-[Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) creatures anyway—for a planeswalker-based offense. The deck was designed to hold most of the advantages of the default white-blue deck, but to dominate the "mirror" thanks to [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant); an unanswered Ajani will of course prove lethal for most control players. 

Some important differences between this deck and stock White-Blue cards to date:

![Scepter of Dominance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Scepter+of+Dominance)  
### Scepter of Dominance

You'll notice that this deck doesn't play [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile). [Scepter of Dominance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scepter+of+Dominance) forces creature opponents deeper and deeper into commitment to the battlefield, where Kar's White-Blue, Splashing Red can sweep up the mess with its, you know, six-odd sweepers. This card is excellent against other control decks, tapping down [Celestial Colonnade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade) after the opponent has paid down a ton of mana; plus it can guarantee that you win the [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) race. It is also the "[Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile)" that can deal with any Eldrazi giant, even ones that have protection from colored spells. Speaking of Eldrazi giants ...

![Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ulamog%2C+the+Infinite+Gyre)  
### Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre

I wanted to play some Eldrazi giant and convinced anyone who would listen that it was right to play a singleton [Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ulamog%2C+the+Infinite+Gyre) in the sideboard (Phil even had one in his!). Why is it right? Well, for one thing, the White-Blue Control mirrors will go long ... but not necessarily fifteen mana long (even with [Everflowing Chalice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice)s). That makes [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn) perhaps a little bit profligate. [Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ulamog%2C+the+Infinite+Gyre) is the next best option; a hard-cast [Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Ulamog%252C%2Bthe%2BInfinite%2BGyre) is not an auto-win the same way a hard-cast [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Emrakul%252C%2Bthe%2BAeons%2BTorn) is, but the one big thing that [Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Ulamog%252C%2Bthe%2BInfinite%2BGyre) has going for it is the ability to take out a loyalty-laden [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) even if it is countered. And if it is countered, great! Your deck is juiced and you can deck your opponent anyway. [Kozilek, Butcher of Truth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kozilek%2C+Butcher+of+Truth) is a distant third for our purposes; it is not particularly resilient and drawing four cards isn't going to save you if the opponent is about to go ultimate.

In addition to playing trump over other White-Blue Control-ish decks, the Eldrazi giants make for great long game weapons against any midrange creature or control decks, viz. mono-white [Emeria, the Sky Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria%2C+the+Sky+Ruin) types. Not so subtly, they are exactly what you want against these fancy [Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine) and/or [Archive Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archive+Trap) decks. If the opponent doesn't have a card set up to specifically deal with an Eldrazi giant's shuffling trigger, you can give any such deck depletion opponents quite a headache.

Kar was kind enough to share his thoughts on the deck, and its place in the metagame.

1. He hasn't tested extensively with [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura) yet, but points out that one of the main ways his Red-White-Blue can deal with Gideon (Game 1) is to keep him tapped with [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) ... the problem is that the Laskin deck (see below) has just as many Ajanis as Kar's version of Red-White-Blue. However, it only has one [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain), so an opportune [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) can often guarantee "Ajani advantage." That said, even if you can keep Gideon in check (at least theoretically) with [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) or [Scepter of Dominance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scepter+of+Dominance), Gideon's "plus" ability can keep [Celestial Colonnade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade) off the other planeswalkers.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td89_boo.jpg)  
2. There were essentially no Vampires decks in the PTQ Kar played in; that means no [Mind Sludge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sludge)s. With no [Mind Sludge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sludge)s, even the one [Cancel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cancel) is less relevant. On balance, [Polymorph](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polymorph) won that tournament, and [Cancel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cancel) is obviously useful in that matchup.

3. In contrast to Phil's experience, Kar found [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) to be great! It was particularly good against the multiple [Devastating Summons](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Summons) Mono-Red decks he beat. One thing he noted was that [Banefire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire) (the original third X-spell before I realized that [Everflowing Chalice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice) is also an X-spell) would be a reasonable anti-White-Blue Control kill card. Any time you can cast [Ulamog, the Infinite Gyre](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Ulamog%252C%2Bthe%2BInfinite%2BGyre), you can [Banefire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire) for 10 and win outright.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td89_banefireArt.jpg)  
4. Kar beat Mono-Red twice, Mythic three times (one Conscription), and drew twice. His Jund record was 1-1, with the loss sadly in the quarterfinals. According to Kar, the only way you can realistically lose to Mythic is if you let them play a Sovereigns of Alara into an [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription), bashing you for 10 or more. So, recognize that, plan accordingly, and don't let them execute.

Laskin beat Stark in the Top 4 of the Atlanta $5K tournament, before taking down the dominant Alex Bertoncini's Jund in the finals.

The Laskin-style Red-White-Blue Planeswalker Control is quite different from Kar's Red-White-Blue Control, despite sharing both colors and strategy. The deck eschews the [Everflowing Chalice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice) engine, and arguably the deck's strongest card—[Mind Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring)—in favor of just more planeswalkers. Despite dipping into red, it plays no [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Jace, the Mind Sculptor | Elspeth Knight-Errant |

The single biggest structural difference (besides replacing [Mind Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring) with [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination)) is the presence of [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura), a card that was deliberately not played by either Phil Napoli's straight White-Blue Control deck or Kar's look at Red-White-Blue Planeswalkers. [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura) does something in a Planeswalker-dedicated deck that you might not have noticed initially. Because [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura) can taunt opposing creatures into attacking him, he simultaneously keeps those attackers off of *all the other* planeswalkers; as such, each other planeswalker on the battlefield can continue to accumulate loyalty (or even help control the board with [Unsummon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unsummon)s, [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix)es, or blocking) while [Gideon Jura](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura)*also* protects your life total. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td89_attackGideon.jpg)  
Another way to attack the boogeyman "mirror" is to go Esper rather than Red-White-Blue. Traditionally speaking control "mirrors" are typically won by either 1) the mono-blue deck (if there is one in the fight) or 2) the control deck with the most colors. I know this seems at odds (with itself even); the mono-blue deck typically has more counterspells to answer the opponent's threats, plus instant-speed card drawing ... Otherwise the deck with more colors has more options and more angles to attack (just think about how a Quick 'n Toast deck could "splash" ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) to play an instant-speed [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher) to either resolve on the opponent's end step or tap his mana so you could main phase a [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum)).

### Liliana Vess (and Mind Rot)

An unchecked [Liliana Vess](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+Vess) is trouble for a control deck ... kind of like the [Disrupting Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disrupting+Scepter)s Brian Weissman used to dominate this game fifteen years ago, but without the ongoing mana commitment. [Mind Rot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Rot) is a discard spell without the commitment, a reasonable compliment to ...

![Esper Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Esper+Charm)  
### Esper Charm

Just about the best thing you can do with three mana in Standard, at least in these colors. Both the "two card" abilities are relevant during most games, and there will even be some times (like against a turn two [Luminarch Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Ascension)) that the "[Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant)"-like third ability keeps you safe and warm at night.

The more powerful mirror-breaking cards are in the sideboard: [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) is a classic that can help force through other spells against permission, or remove cards like [Mind Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring) or a key Planeswalker from the opponent's hand before he can play them. [Haunting Echoes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Haunting+Echoes) is just your Ultimatum; the goal is to play it and win on the spot.

Lauren "Mulldrifting" Lee couldn't make it to the New York PTQ that Phil Napoli won, so she decided to hitch a ride to the Boston PTQ the next day, in what I believe was only her second ever PTQ. 

Mulldrifting's Misers:

### Gideon Jura

Her "fifth [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel)" and an upgrade to [Sphinx of Jwar Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx+of+Jwar+Isle).

### Elspeth, Knight-Errant

Takes the place of one [Rafiq of the Many](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rafiq+of+the+Many). Less damage, maybe, but trades off with evasion. A serious threat, long term, for White-Blue Control decks and a legitimate answer to [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor). Speaking of which ...

### Jace, the Mind Sculptor

Lauren doesn't actually like this card in Mythic; she used it largely to [Unsummon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unsummon) non-[Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens) / [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) blockers. A fine answer to Eldrazi giants, in particular out of [Polymorph](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polymorph) decks.

![Dauntless Escort](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dauntless+Escort)  
### Dauntless Escort

"It's like a [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) on a 3/3 body." [Dauntless Escort](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dauntless+Escort) is of course a great answer to [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment)-type effects; she even played extra (first one took the place of a [Rhox War Monk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rhox+War+Monk) which is "hella useless" against White-Blue Control decks).

Matt made a similar change in his [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription) version, moving [Dauntless Escort](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dauntless+Escort) to the main as a four-of, cutting *all* the [Rhox War Monk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rhox+War+Monk)s. This was a concession to White-Blue Control (our new boogeyman), but also a fine move in a fair number of other matches. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td89_vs.jpg)  
So which is the superior version of Mythic, classic or Eldrazi-style? Sperling makes an interesting argument. If you lay out two Mythic decks against each other, the [Rafiq of the Many](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rafiq+of+the+Many) / [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) version can be chump-blocked. The matching big boom-booms [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara) + [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription) lead to a dead opponent. The other Mythic deck can't necessarily block very well due to the trample on [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription), and over multiple attacks, annihilator kicks in and then kicks down the door.

The dominant White-Blue Control deck was addressed with [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) and [Deprive](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deprive) (the fifth [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate)) ... because [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) is just that damn good against control.

Josh Herr's deck is a good representative, I think, of the current crop of Jund decks. This is a deck very much in the vein of "all lands, no removal." Herr played 27 lands and no [Bituminous Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bituminous+Blast)s (or other expensive removal). 

The rest of the deck is just good threats ([Putrid Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech)) or card advantage ([Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning)) or both ([Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), [Broodmate Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broodmate+Dragon), [Siege-Gang Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander), [Sprouting Thrinax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax)) ... Okay, mostly "both."

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td89_cascade.jpg)  
Jund is a deck long on card power, with one of the best offensive two drops of all time, into [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning), sometimes into a second [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) off a [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf). The main thing holding the Jund deck in check is the Blue card [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) (you'll note that most of the controlling Blue decks played four). Almost half the Jund deck's lands come into play tapped at least some of the time. That means they can be "kept down" as fake [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)s. Because the Jund mana requirements are so strict (look at [Putrid Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech) or [Sprouting Thrinax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax)), a [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) will often be worse than a [Stone Rain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stone+Rain).

I certainly enjoyed reviewing so many great, and different, decks this week. Thanks to everyone who did well, and good luck to everyone looking to do well *this* weekend!
