Big, Little, Bleed, and Extort
==============================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on March 28, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







When [Mark Rosewater](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Archive.aspx?author=Mark%20Rosewater) first introduced the Orzhov some seven years ago, he described what R&D called a "bleeder" deck—one exemplified by cards like [Pillory of the Sleepless](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillory+of+the+Sleepless) or [One Thousand Lashes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=One+Thousand+Lashes)... cards that could neutralize the opponent while generating an incremental advantage. This kind of a description of the Orzhov—what the Orzhov might be before we ever drew cardboard off our libraries—might have been more along the lines of The Rock.


*Was this good?*


*Would it be good?*


**Magic** at the time was increasingly about big, sometimes "over the top" offenses like [Keiga, the Tide Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Keiga%2C+the+Tide+Star); [Magnivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magnivore); or [Godo, Bandit Warlord](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Godo%2C+Bandit+Warlord). Standard was tied together by a thick paste of [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) on one end and [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) on the other (both the most powerful tool of the beatdown and the greatest tool against the beatdown). How good would a point here or a point there be? One extra card, 1 life point, a tiny extra edge?


It turns out...


...that packing a little extra value onto a card—into the intrinsic strategy of a color combination—did not necessarily have to be "little." Consider one of the iconic cards of the Orzhov Syndicate:



![Angel of Despair](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Angel+of+Despair)

 

[Angel of Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Despair) has proven a smashing—rather a *permanent*-smashing—card over the years, a vital threat that contributed to a number of important strategies, from BGW mana denial to [Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis), perhaps most famously to Standard Solar Flare.

Solar Flare, as you can see, was a deck that—although blue as well as black and white—really embraced the extra value system of the Orzhov Syndicate. Every [Court Hussar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Court+Hussar), every [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand), pilfered, plucked, or put away that extra card; a spiritually gray binder grinder bathed in blue.


So, Orzhov could go quite *big*.


But in its first run, it was more about going *little*.


Possibly the most "Orzhov" white-black deck ever was the influential Ghost Dad.


Ghost Dad was many things: initially exciting, later overrated, very much talked-about in its day... even responsible for inspiring [one of the greatest theory articles](http://www.starcitygames.com/magic/fundamentals/12201_Information_Cascades_in_Magic.html) of all time. But as I said, more than any one of those things, it was perhaps the most "Orzhov" Orzhov deck. Ghost Dad really was a bleeder, an incremental advantage machine that wanted to kill the opponent a tiny slice at a time, a card here, a point there... even gaining a point or three for extra value while hiding behind a 1/3.


Ghost Dad was also a study in synergy.


Most other successful Orzhov weenie decks were about individual card impact (Hand in Hand) or explosive power (Ghost Husk), but Ghost Dad wanted to grant the opponent's death via a thousand cuts... often *literally* (well, twenty cuts).

The original idea behind Ghost Dad was that Ben Goodman and team wanted to play eight Shoals, both [Shining Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shining+Shoal) and [Sickening Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sickening+Shoal). Shoals were powerful but required a player to discard a card much of the time. You can't do that too many times before the opponent bowls you over. That said, Shoals were also Arcane... which allowed them an interesting avenue for card advantage via a seldom-seen Spirit.


Enter [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp).



![Tallowisp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Tallowisp)

 

[Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) offered an engine that allowed Ghost Dad to recoup card economy on a Shoal and just set up legitimate card advantage—and even tutoring—given a particular structure in deck design.


So beyond super stock cards like [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) (how could you play your Orzhov incremental card advantage deck without that grindiest little two-drop?) the deck was built on Spirits and Arcane for [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp), and of course [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp)'s target enchantments (which in many cases were themselves also grindy little sources of incremental cards or damage).


So instead of [Mortify](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortify) (which every other Orzhov deck played), you had [Pillory of the Sleepless](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillory+of+the+Sleepless) as the three-mana creature-stopper. Left alone, this kill spell would actually kill the opponent, given twenty turns!


[Indomitable Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Indomitable+Will) was a bullet combat trick (although a creature enchantment, you could play it as an instant) and [Strands of Undeath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strands+of+Undeath) allowed you to tutor up a discard spell! *Card advantage*.


But the grindiest of the grinding Ghost Dad cards was the Ghost Dad himself: [Ghost Council of Orzhova](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Council+of+Orzhova).



![Ghost Council of Orzhova](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ghost+Council+of+Orzhova)

 

Here is a card that defies control removal spells with great flexibility and could also challenge unanimous "best creature in Standard" [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch) on the field of head-to-head combat. [Ghost Council of Orzhova](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Council+of+Orzhova), like so many cards in Ghost Dad, was a Spirit, promoting the synergy with [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) while providing tremendous individual card efficiency.

Olivier Ruel built up his Hall of Fame career with a Top 8 finish at [the first Pro Tour Honolulu](/en/events/coverage/living-heezy-street) with this WB beatdown deck. It may be most famous for being on the wrong side of the $16,000 [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) (probably the most famous topdeck in the history of **Magic**):




![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220216050541im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/img/interface/video-place-holder.png)[.](http://www.youtube.com/embed/4t0pzLnSWw0)

 

 

Ruel's deck had the same [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)s and Ghost Councils as Ghost Dad but drew its Orzhov grinding not from [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) synergy, but rather packing extra value in many different individual cards. [Ravenous Rats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Rats) and [Shrieking Grotesque](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrieking+Grotesque) offered self-contained two-for-ones, and [Plagued Rusalka](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plagued+Rusalka) allowed Ruel to gain even more value out of their otherwise pathetic X/1 bodies by trading them, oftentimes, with the opponent's creatures.


You might not be able to easily see the advantage generated by [Hand of Cruelty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hand+of+Cruelty) or [Paladin *en*-Vec](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paladin+en-Vec), but these creatures—one or the other—could usually get past the opponent's defenses to set up a ninjutsu strike by [Okiba-Gang Shinobi](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Okiba-Gang+Shinobi) (more value). Of course, in a format relatively thin for combo decks, [Paladin *en*-Vec](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paladin+en-Vec) + [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) might have been the best "combo" in the format... with both protection from black and protection from red, the spot removal that could shut down a [Paladin *en*-Vec](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paladin+en-Vec) was few and far between, but the chaos the little lawman could create with Jitte was immediate and obvious.

Ghost Husk was initially met with skepticism and incredulity, especially from pro players. It seemed like a gimmick. Many conservative players were more attracted to the simple, relatively no-frills, individual card efficiency of a straight WB a la Oliver Ruel.


Ghost Husk... Ghost Husk was a *Johnny* deck.


So how did this deck work?




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| 
Nantuko Husk

[Nantuko Husk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nantuko+Husk) and [Promise of Bunrei](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Promise+of+Bunrei) were the unique elements of this style of WB beatdown. You could sacrifice a creature to set up [Promise of Bunrei](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Promise+of+Bunrei)... which would give you four more creatures to sacrifice. Ergo, [Nantuko Husk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nantuko+Husk) could strike for an additional 8–10 damage out of nowhere, or at the very least bowl over almost any creature standing in its way.


[Promise of Bunrei](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Promise+of+Bunrei) could also give the deck some resistance to [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) or other creature elimination.


Many important theorists consider Ghost Husk the true monster of the era. It was explosive and powerful, the [Angel of Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Despair) to the rest of the Orzhov weenie family's [Ravenous Rats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Rats). It was so good, so powerful, that *it didn't even have to play [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte)*.


How did Ghost Husk get away with this supreme act of arrogant omission?


[Nantuko Husk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nantuko+Husk) could give this combat-oriented style of deck an easy way around [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte); the Husk player could block and sacrifice the blocking creature prior to damage. Because the opponent never got to damage, he or she would never get to place a counter on [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte).


So while the deck lacked Jitte's offensive power, Ghost Husk could neutralize it in the hands of the opponent; and of course, the deck had a level of offensive power with the Husk/Promise combo that few other decks could match in the red zone.


What might not be immediately obvious is that Ghost Husk was *not* an Orzhov-themed bleeder deck, [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) and [Ghost Council of Orzhova](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Council+of+Orzhova) notwithstanding (and the latter only a three-of)... those were just great *cards* in-color, regardless of theme. Ghost Husk was a deck that knew what it wanted. It started with a 2/2 on turn one! Its strategy, its offense, existed regardless of what the opponent wanted it do... it just so happened it could execute despite what the opponent wanted to do.


What about Orzhov, not in the context of *Ravnica* Block, but *Return to Ravnica* Block?


At the time of this writing, we haven't seen a dedicated Orzhov deck—that is, straight WB—as a major and unique contributor to Standard or other relevant formats. But that doesn't mean there aren't powerhouse tools afforded by the syndicate. Cards like...





![Cartel Aristocrat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cartel+Aristocrat)


 
As seen in both The Aristocrats and Human Reanimator, [Cartel Aristocrat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cartel+Aristocrat) has provided the ability to sacrifice your own creatures... with and without extra value attached. In The Aristocrats, [Cartel Aristocrat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cartel+Aristocrat) can sacrifice [Doomed Traveler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doomed+Traveler) to set up an extra (flying) 1/1 body or to enable the morbid on [Skirsdag High Priest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skirsdag+High+Priest). In Human Reanimator, it helps set up either infinite life with [Huntmaster of the Fells](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Huntmaster+of+the+Fells) or infinite mana with Burning Tree Emissary by sacrificing a [Fiend Hunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiend+Hunter) covering your own [Angel of Glory's Rise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Glory%27s+Rise) (which will of course bring back the aforementioned [Fiend Hunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiend+Hunter) once it returns to the battlefield)... and will do so again and again.







![Orzhov Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Orzhov+Charm)


 
 



As seen in The Aristocrats, [Orzhov Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orzhov+Charm) can do everything from bringing back a [Champion of the Parish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Champion+of+the+Parish) to getting you a re-buy on the mighty [Zealous Conscripts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Conscripts) to giving you a way to kill a pesky [Boros Reckoner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Reckoner). At least two of these abilities are non-trivial in their power level. One [Zealous Conscripts](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zealous+Conscripts) will often win the game... but two? [Boros Reckoner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Reckoner) is multicolored and therefore immune to [Ultimate Price](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ultimate+Price), and offing it with a [Searing Spear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Searing+Spear) is... *expensive*. A two-mana solution to the best three-drop in recent memory is nothing to shake a stick at... especially given its other abilities.





![Vizkopa Guildmage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Vizkopa+Guildmage)


 
As seen in Modern lifegain decks, [Vizkopa Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vizkopa+Guildmage) allows a player going long to turn a [Martyr of Sands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr+of+Sands) into a machine gun.






![Gift of Orzhova](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Gift+of+Orzhova)


 
As seen in Bant Hexproof, [Gift of Orzhova](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gift+of+Orzhova) is the latest in a long line of [Ethereal Armor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethereal+Armor)s, [Rancor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rancor)s, and [Spectral Flight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Flight)s that make [Invisible Stalker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invisible+Stalker) and [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) that much more lethal. [Invisible Stalker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invisible+Stalker) is hard enough to handle by its lonesome... but give it some extra toughness and a set of wings? [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft)'s one nod to fair play is that you can block and kill it with almost anything. You might take some damage in the meantime, but the 2/2 Geist itself is not so hard to contain... unless it becomes next to impossible to block, that is!






![Obzedat, Ghost Council](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council)


 
As seen everywhere from The Aristocrats to Junk Rites to Esper Control, [Obzedat, Ghost Council](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council) is simply one of the most powerful and effective large creatures in the Standard format. A 5/5 creature for five mana is already near the appropriate cost-to-power level for competitive play, but Obzedat has multiple forms of upside. Built for a long game, Obzedat will *expect* to gain more life than the average [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk)... and will do so while chunking away at the opponent's life total. How positively *Orzhov*! And because it can disappear during the opponent's turn, [Obzedat, Ghost Council](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council) is a powerful threat against control. Good luck killing it with a [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict)!



And then we have the extort cards...


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td240_extort.jpg)*[Kingpin's Pet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kingpin%27s+Pet), [Syndic of Tithes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Syndic+of+Tithes), [Treasury Thrull](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasury+Thrull), [Blind Obedience](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blind+Obedience), [Crypt Ghast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crypt+Ghast)*
 

[Blind Obedience](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blind+Obedience) and [Crypt Ghast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crypt+Ghast) have seen some play in reasonable-to-good Constructed decks... [Crypt Ghast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crypt+Ghast) in particular ensures you have enough mana to keep your extort engine moving. From my perspective, extort is a beautiful mechanic. It isn't just iconically *Orzhov* (which it most definitely is), it rewards careful players who are good at math and racing, and helps flatten out games for players who are mana flooded. Ever have two extort permanents out and running simultaneously? It can feel quite powerful as long as your opponent isn't playing "over the top" **Magic**.


I am just surprised [Syndic of Tithes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Syndic+of+Tithes) hasn't made any kind of splash yet. Perfect racer and great opportunity to give a beatdown deck reach when drawing a near-dead "bear" late in a game.


Only time will tell if this generation of Orzhov cards come together to make a straight WB deck that we will be talking about six or seven years from now. But even if they don't, we will certainly see cards like these help grind out successes for a variety of multicolor strategies.
