From the Regionals Scrapheap
============================



 Posted in **Learning Curve**
 on May 12, 2004 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/author-photo_BDM.jpg)
By Brian David-Marshall




 Brian David-Marshall is a New York–based game designer who has been involved with Magic since 1994, when he started organizing tournaments and ran a Manhattan game store. Since then, he has been a judge, a player, and one of the longest-tenured columnists on DailyMTG.com, as he enters his second decade writing for the site. He is also the Pro Tour Historian and one of the commentators for the Pro Tour. 






So I didn't play at Regionals. One of the dangers of writing about Magic is that you relate to the game more from behind a laptop than across from an opponent. I ended up covering the waning rounds of the event for the Neutral Ground website instead of playing in them. I tried to dodge losing to [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) and [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp) by playing in side drafts during the early part of the day. Inevitably, I was knocked out of one of my drafts by those very cards with a [Loxodon Warhammer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Warhammer) to boot!

I actually put in quite a bit of work for the tournament. I worked with a group of players that included Mike Flores, Seth Burn, Steve Sadin, Mike Clair, Jon Becker, and Josh Ravitz (I am certainly forgetting people and I apologize). The first deck that we built for the event was a collaboration between Steve Sadin and I that was inspired by the Anti-Affinity Affinity deck from the Hong Kong Regionals.

Actually the deck was inspired by that deck and an old *Mirage* Block Constructed deck that was among my favorite silly decks of all time. Diamonds and Dragons was a deck that featured twenty land, twenty of the mana-producing Diamonds, and twenty dragons. It was a fun five color deck that was surprisingly powerful for a fun deck. With pleasant memories of that deck in mind I started work on a Regionals deck with Steve Sadin. The first thing we cobbled together had four [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon) and four [Rorix Bladewing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rorix+Bladewing) and accelerated to them with Talismans, [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox), and [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song).

The deck was a lot of fun—especially on the rare occasions that it managed a second-turn Rorix—but it was not quite consistent enough to warrant too much attention past the initial days of testing. What it did do was set the wheels of playtesting in motion and began the discussions that would eventually shape everyone's deck choices. It also left us receptive to the idea of [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song) and [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon) in the same deck. More on that later.

Everyone settled into pet decks during testing. Mike Flores hammered away at green-white decks the entire time. He started with a green-white worship deck that included [Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic) and [Silver Knight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silver+Knight). He eventually settled in on a nearly mono-white deck that touched green for [Oxidize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize) and a transformative [Tooth and Nail](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) sideboard. 

There should never have been any doubt that Seth would play [Goblin Bidding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bidding). I am somewhat dubious about his claims of having “invented” the deck. I am pretty sure it was created by Wolfgang Eder's uncle and eventually it wound its way from Wolfgang to Tsuyoshi Fujita to Seth Burn. Possesion is nine-thenths fo the law and Seth has definitely staked his claim to the deck having played it longer and harder than anyone else. He appeared set to play Ravager Affinity but at the last minute reverted to form and played a Goblin Bidding deck with three [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp)s. After losing the first of eleven rounds Seth went on a nine round winning spree and was able to draw the last round to secure one of the eight spots—a job very well done.

In the few days that we were testing this build we actually toyed with changing the [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song)s to [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon)s. [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song) is mostly there as a mana fixer as opposed to an accelerant since the ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif) is pretty hard to reach reliably. Despite making your lands into Mountains, [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) does not stop them from also being artifact lands. [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) would not only “fix” your mana but it would not affect your affinity. It would also let you have additional cards to bring in against decks with Urza lands and [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)s. In the end we decided it was a little too cutsey and opted for the version that kept testing so consistently well in the mirror. 

Mike had tested so much Tooth and Nail that he was overly concerned with the deck going into the tournament. He had [Electrostatic Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=+Electrostatic+Bolt)s for [Platinum Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Platinum+Angel) with [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) for the Abunas where [Pyrite Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrite+Spellbomb)s should have been. That ended up being his only complaint about the deck. The Bolts—while good—affected his affinity draws. He would also have changed the [Blinkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinkmoth+Nexus) to additional Citadels for the same reason. Mike breezed through countless mirror matches thanks to his ability to “ritual” out a [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon) and devastate the other side of the table.

The deck I was testing the whole time was a black-white variant of Ravager Affinity that combated what I thought would be a heavy mono-white field with [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) and had [Leonin Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Elder) for the mirror. The deck ended up being a little clunky with seven non artifact lands and just not as powerful as the straight-up Ravager deck with the [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon) sideboard.

There were a number of different versions. Some featured [Leonin Abunas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Abunas) and others had [Smother](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother) and at least one had both. One of my favorite tricks with the deck involved holding multiple [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) in hand with at least two white sources of artifact mana in play. You could sacrifice everything to a Ravager and Sunrise them back into play effectively untapping your mana to repeat the whole process. I still have a version of this built and I may give it a try at a NAC Qualifier or Friday Night **Magic** just to actually play it once under actual battle situations before *Fifth Dawn* dramatically alters the constructed landscape.
