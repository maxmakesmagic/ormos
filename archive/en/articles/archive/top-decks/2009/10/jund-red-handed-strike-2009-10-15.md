Jund-Red Handed Strike
======================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on October 15, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






This past weekend in Philadelphia gave us our community its first look at *Zendikar* in Standard action. Here is how the Top 8, and (in this case more interestingly) the Top 16, went down:


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_table1.jpg)  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_table2.jpg)  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_table3.jpg)  
**The Jund-Red Handed Strike**


The Top 8 was quite clearly dominated by Jund Aggro decks (those crafty [Savage Lands](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Savage%2BLands) even crammed one more in the Top 16). These decks look a lot like the aggressive, hybridized, Jund Cascade decks that we have seen since the release of *Alara Reborn* ... just with different mana bases. To wit, the Jund decks always played basic lands in response to [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile), but now with [Verdant Catacombs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Verdant+Catacombs) they have a reason to play more basics, all of which mesh together quite nicely with [Dragonskull Summit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonskull+Summit) and [Rootbound Crag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rootbound+Crag).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_2Cards1.jpg)  
The Jund Aggro decks had some variation, with playable (if not universally played) potential game enders like [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) or [Resounding Thunder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Resounding+Thunder) in some versions but not others. For the most part they topped up on [Broodmate Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broodmate+Dragon) and bottomed out on [Sprouting Thrinax](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sprouting+Thrinax) and [Putrid Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech), [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate) and [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt). These decks all played a moderate cascade theme, plus big [Broodmate Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Broodmate%2BDragon), the Dragon that always "cascades" into an [Air Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Air+Elemental)!


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_cascadeInto.jpg)  
On the other end, a [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) out of these decks could spit out a variety of cards, ideally [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning), but possibly any number of removal cards ([Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), [Terminate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate), [Maelstrom Pulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Pulse), [Resounding Thunder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Resounding+Thunder)), or possibly a good-to-great creature.


The consensus number of lands for these non-ramp Jund Aggro decks seems to be 25; of the Top 8 competitors, only Jake Klomparens played a "mere" 24, but Jake was one of the Jund players who ran [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker), a potential mana booster (provided Jake hit four lands). Even without a fifth land drop, his deck could go [Garruk Wildspeaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Garruk%2BWildspeaker) into [Broodmate Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broodmate+Dragon).


![Goblin Ruinblaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Goblin+Ruinblaster)
  
The Jund Aggro decks were a bit light on the *Zendikar* additions, but one that really stood out was [Goblin Ruinblaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Ruinblaster). [Goblin Ruinblaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Ruinblaster) seems like a solid weapon in a Jund mirror, for instance. So much depends upon hitting the right amount of mana to play your own big spells; the [Goblin Ruinblaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Goblin%2BRuinblaster) can snipe a [Savage Lands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savage+Lands) or play the [Boggart Ram-Gang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Ram-Gang) role, coming off the [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Bloodbraid%2BElf) for less short-term damage, but more destructive disruption.


All the Top 8 Jund Aggro decks ran very common themes.

Christian Calcano hung up his usual [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) / counterspell combination to go with a weenie red (and white!) Deck combination for the first outing with *Zendikar*. This deck played basically one million new *Zendikar* cards, woven together with existing cards to form an exciting and fast-paced offensive weapon.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_4CardsAcross.jpg)  
Here are fourteen cards that really love a [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos).


![Ranger of Eos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ranger+of+Eos)
  
[Elite Vanguard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elite+Vanguard) is simply the [Savannah Lions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savannah+Lions) of the day, and needs no explanation. [Goblin Bushwhacker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker) is an interesting two-of that can attack as a 2/1 the turn it comes into play, and also has potential synergy with *Zendikar* neighbor [Kor Skyfisher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kor+Skyfisher). [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide) was a card that I initially dismissed, but in the context of a fast deck with both good creatures and good burn spells (headlined by [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)), it can help chop down the 20-life tree rooted across the table ... sometimes starting turn one!

The Vampires tribe came out in force in Philadelphia, putting Ted Renner into the Top 8 and positioning another two players in the Top 16.


As we talked about last week, Vampires is a nice intersection of good creatures and good spells. It can blow up the opponent's hand with [Mind Sludge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sludge) or profit from the death of opposing creatures with [Tendrils of Corruption](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Corruption). [Bloodghast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodghast) helps to make Vampires relentless ... but so does [Vampire Nighthawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Nighthawk).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_vampireCross.jpg)  
[Vampire Nighthawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Nighthawk) may be my favorite member of the tribe. It is such a good example of how far creatures have come. [Vampire Nighthawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Nighthawk) is essentially the same mana cost as a [Hurloon Minotaur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurloon+Minotaur) (**Magic**'s iconic red creature)... but with two very relevant abilities (rather than nothing) tacked on. [Vampire Nighthawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Nighthawk) can elongate a game, especially against beatdown decks.

I love these kinds of white-based control decks!


Don't try to beat this deck with creatures! As long as Lapine isn't missing a lot of land drops, a creature-based offense is going to be problematic. The deck has [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring), [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile), [Pitfall Trap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pitfall+Trap), [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment), and [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup). It can destroy roughly one million ba-zillion creatures, plus exile another nine or so from the game.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_scepterSlice.jpg)  
[Scepter of Dominance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scepter+of+Dominance) is a very functional two-of in this deck. It can slow down a the many decks whose lands come into play tapped, putting the opponent waiting for a strategic "big turn" yet another turn. More importantly, [Scepter of Dominance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scepter+of+Dominance) can contain a single creature, forcing the opponent to commit multiple creatures ... right into the waiting grip of [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) or [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup).

[Emeria, the Sky Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria%2C+the+Sky+Ruin) is one of my favorite new cards from *Zendikar*. It is basically a [Debtors' Knell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Debtors%27+Knell), but one that can't be easily stopped by blue permission spells. While it can be useful in essentially any long-game situation, consider this one: The game goes forever; the white deck over-draws to eight and discards [Iona, Shield of Emeria](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Iona%2C+Shield+of+Emeria).


... Rinse and repeat!


How are you going to contain that gal, exactly? With [Emeria, the Sky Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria%2C+the+Sky+Ruin) in play?


Like some of the other decks in this section, Michael chose to play [Luminarch Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Ascension). [Luminarch Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Ascension) can be very punishing to (other) slow, do-nothing decks. These kinds of decks accumulate card advantage, but might not do enough damage (at least early on) to contend with a [Luminarch Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Luminarch%2BAscension).


Any prospective do-nothing players out there should heavily consider playing [Esper Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Esper+Charm), just so they don't get blown out by just the [Luminarch Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Luminarch%2BAscension) ... because it will happen!

The Philadelphia Top 16 was rounded out by a pair of [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) Naya decks. 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td60_cobraSplash.jpg)  
Superficially these are "just" Naya-centered active decks, but the presence of [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) can have a significant impact on the deck's ability to act. For example, a second-turn [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) can easily produce a turn-three [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf).


The strategy was always going to get a little bit of a boost with [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa), but [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) really gives this kind of deck a head start. The [Knight of the Reliquary](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Knight%2Bof%2Bthe%2BReliquary) is a great card to begin with, but in a deck with a landfall theme?


Consider this: play a [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains), make a 4/4 with [Rampaging Baloths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampaging+Baloths); sacrifice the [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains) for an [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa), 4/4; sacrifice the [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa) for another [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains)... another 4/4. At the same time, your [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) would have left being a 4/4 itself in the dust of 5/5 and beyond.

So despite some early format dominance from the Jund Aggro wing, there seem to be lots of options in this Standard. You can play Mono-Black as a versatile attack deck, any number of [Luminarch Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Ascension) decks, or of course one of the multicolored green decks with [Savage Lands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savage+Lands) or [Jungle Shrine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jungle+Shrine)s ... and still not scratch the surface of what this card pool is capable of producing. For instance, there was not one [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) mentioned in this article!


Now, as for non-Standard Constructed excitement, starting tomorrow is this year's Extended Pro Tour. I, for one, am very excited to see what kinds of decks appear. Will we encounter fast Cascade combo decks using three-mana cascade cards like [Ardent Plea](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ardent+Plea) to search out [Restore Balance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restore+Balance) (as a game wrecker) or [Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis) (as a game winner)? How about [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths)? Is that too gimmicky, or exactly the kind of deck that can come out of nowhere to win a Pro Tour? Me? I am looking forward to reading about four [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s and four [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix)es, linking up arm in electrical arm for the first time in Professional Constructed **Magic**.
