Feature: A Modern Primer
========================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on September 1, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_davidsutcliffe.jpg)
By David Sutcliffe










Pro Tour Philadelphia is upon us, and a bright new dawn awaits as the Modern format is given its first real shakedown by the best players in the world. A new format can often be confusing, especially for people who are just tuning in and haven't spent the last two weeks hothousing the best decks and strategies. We'll start you off with a primer on just what Modern means, and what sort of decks and cards you can expect to be reading about over this *Magic* Weekend.



### What Is Modern, Anyway?



Modern contains all cards released in core sets from **Eighth Edition** forward and expansion sets from **Mirrodin** forward. The format doesn't rotate, and new sets become legal in Modern on release.


**Eighth Edition**  saw the debut of the "modern" card frame, and that card frame has been used on every expansion since, from our original visit to **Mirrodin** right through to our return for **Scars of Mirrodin**, *Magic* *2012*, and the impending trip to the dark lands of **Innistrad**. That makes the card frame a pretty good rule of thumb, but it's not perfect, so be sure to check [Gatherer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/) if you're wondering if a card is Modern-legal.


The added wrinkle to the Modern format here at Pro Tour Philadelphia is the banned list of twenty-one cards. In a format with as many different cards available as there are in Modern the risk of a few combinations dominating the field was very real. In many ways Modern is like a Greatest Hits of *Magic* format, that allows people to play pretty much any of their favourite decks from the past eight years. In among those *Magic* greats were a few decks that nobody really wanted to see come back to haunt us, and the banned list aimed to slay a few of the more obvious problem decks before they could become issues.


To read more about the banned list, and why cards were chosen for the list, check out [Tom LaPille's article about it.](http://www.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/ld/155)


So, now that you know what the Modern format is... what do you want to play?



### I Want to Play Aggro



If your idea of fun is tearing across the table as quickly as possible, taking chunks out of your opponents with creatures or frying them to crisp with direct damage, before finally celebrating over their bruised and battered corpse... then Modern may be just your sort of format. Although it's possible to play a Jund or [Bant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) deck, inspired by cards from the **Shards of Alara** block like [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) or [Knight of the Reliquary](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary), the aggro world has really split into two popular camps: Zoo and Affinity.


Zoo has been around for the longest time, and the version that you'll see in Modern will probably be very familiar to those who have seen the deck in the past. The deck list reads like something from *The Blues Brothers*, with Jake and Elwood getting the band back together. [Wild Nacatl](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl)? Check. [Kird Ape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape)? Check. [Grim Lavamancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer)? Check. [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)? Check. [Dark Confidant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)? Check. [Lightning Helix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix)? Check.

These are all cards that should be familiar to both Extended and Legacy players, and Modern Zoo doesn't have too many added wrinkles. Every card in the deck is cheap, efficient, and aggressive, and it makes Zoo one of the essential building blocks of the Modern format. Zoo is always going to be there to put a consistent clock on the opponent and keep the other decks "honest," because the punishment for being a little bit too slow, or too clever, or too tricksy, is having a [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) knocking on the front doorâ€”and it doesn't take "Hold on a minute, I'm just in the shower" as an answer.

Affinity is a name that can cause night tremors in many older players who were around for the deck's glory years, when **Mirrodin** block was first printed. The artifact lands like [Seat of the Synod](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seat+of+the+Synod) sent the affinity for artifacts mechanic over the edge, and horrendous things resulted. The monster that **Mirrodin** created was a deck that spat out a hand full of artifact creatures in the first two turns and powered up beatings through either [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) or [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating), which both threatened to convert all those artifacts into extra damage. In Modern all but one of those artifact lands are part of the banned list ([Darksteel Citadel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Citadel) being the odd one in), but the remainder of the deck is pretty much intact, and Affinity remains a serious contender, albeit on a more even playing field with its contemporaries.


The interesting thing about Modern Affinity deck is that there has been a real divergence in their strategy and there are now several distinct flavors. A white-based variant uses [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) from **Scars of Mirrodin** to convert all the little [Memnite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite)s, [Signal Pest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest)s, and [Frogmite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frogmite)s into serious threats, while a red-based version sees all those little guys as food for the ever-hungry [Atog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog), before being entirely willing to then [Fling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fling) the morbidly obese [Atog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog) across the table for a killer strike.

Potentially a surprise MVP in the Affinity decks could be the [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus). This humble land can become a serious threat when it is handed either a [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) or the +1/+1 counters from an [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager), with its infect keyword meaning that Affinity can now quite happily kill you twice as fast with poison counters should you somehow survive the assault on your life total.

### I Want to Play Combo



You want to play combo? Come on in, friend, do we have a format for YOU!


Combo comes in as many colors as the rainbow, and as many different styles as you can think of. A lot of the bannings in Modern were aimed at killing some of the fastest and most consistent combo decks in the field. But like fighting a hydra, killing one combo head only seems to spawn two more and for every "turn-three kill" combo deck that was lost, two "turn four kill" combo decks took their place... and they all have turn-three kills in their locker, should the draw go well.


We could sit here all day and discuss the various merits of [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension), [Living End](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End), Elves, and Twelvepost, but we don't have all that time. In the meantime, here's a guide to a few of the most common ones.


Twelvepost is the new way of counting to fifteen. You want to count to fifteen because when you can count to fifteen using your mana pool, you get to play [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn), and then you almost certainly win. Twelvepost gets to count to fifteen through the application of, well, twelve "posts"â€”the posts in this case being the Loci [Glimmerpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost) and [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost), with the help of "[Vesuva](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva)post" to round out the dirty dozen. Assembling your posts in one place means you can then tap your [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)s for four or five mana apiece and then counting to 15 is a matter of seconds. Cards like [Explore](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Explore) and [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) help you to gather your Loci together, and the latest buzz in the Philadelphia Convention Center has been the move to playing [Amulet of Vigor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Amulet+of+Vigor) and [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) to search out your perfect Post combination.

It seems as though a lot of the development and movement is around Twelvepost, and we've seen that duel played out at the dealer stands with cards waxing and waning in popularity. The [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) combination made the deck stronger, but players responded to that by buying up [Bribery](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bribery) in order to steal [Emrakul](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) from the opposing deck, and then the Twelvepost players hit back by picking up [Brooding Saurian](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brooding+Saurian) to reclaim their stolen Eldrazi, and [Through the Breach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Through+the+Breach) to try and sneak their big hit in even earlier.


The perfect build of Twelvepost remains to be seen, but it's a deck that is right at the top of everyone's list to either play or ensure they beat.

[Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) is a deck that Standard players will recognize, and it's here in Modern and in pretty fine shape. [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) is one of the slower combo decks available, but it packs plenty of countermagic to ensure that it survives into the later turns to win and can disrupt other combo decks along the way. [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) and [Disrupting Shoal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disrupting+Shoal) are both great "free" counterspells that protect the deck.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| 
Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker
 | 
Deceiver Exarch
 |

The biggest gain the deck makes in Modern is that it can have backup cards for both its key combo pieces. [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) has a twin in the form of [Pestermite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), and [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) can be replaced with the dreaded Akki goblin [Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker). With either Kiki-Jiki or [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), the deck can tap a creature to create a copy of another creature, and the creature that you copy ([Pestermite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) or [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)) can then untap the creature that made the copy, then fireworks ensue. You create a zillion hasty copies of [Pestermite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) or [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), and the opponent is finished.


A hidden strength of [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) in Modern is the sideboard, which can pack [Blood Moon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) to disrupt a lot of the decks that rely on nonbasic lands (like Twelvepost). But in a world of faster combo decks, is [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) up to the task?

Both those combo decks have a familiar ring to them, as [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) and [Emrakul](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) have been winning Standard events for a while now. The next combo deck is something that you probably won't have seen in Standard for a while: [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension).

The blue-red combo decks were the talk of Thursday, and they come in a few very similar flavors: [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension), [Pyromancer's Swath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath) and [Empty the Warrens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens), or a combination of any of those decks. At the heart they're all very similar: use cheap blue scrying and card-drawing effects to find your red cards, cast lots of red cards... profit. With [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) you can double the effect of all your spells, while with [Pyromancer's Swath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath) and cards like [Grapeshot](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot) you can storm your way to massive damage very quickly. Draw cards, generate mana, draw more cards, generate more mana... bury your opponent in damage.

### I Want to Play Control



You do? I think you'd better take a seat. I don't want to give you this news while you're standing up.


Control may not existâ€”at least, not the way you think of it.


The threats of the aggro and combo decks are so huge, so quick, and so varied, that it just seems like nobody has found a control deck that can answer them all. Sure, you could counter a few spells, but they will get you anyway, and the blue-red combo decks can pack up to eight "free" counterspells to protect their combo with. It's a big ask. Sitting at the top of the anti-control pile is [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn), which can't be countered and threatens to tear decks to shred. Sure, you could [Mana Leak](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) their [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift), but eventually they will find some other way to count to fifteen, and then you'll have [Emrakul](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul) squatting on your front lawn anyway.


In *Magic Online* tournaments we've seen the odd deck crop upâ€”a No-Stick deck won, an [Enduring Ideal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) deck won, and there have been a few sightings of [Mystical Teachings](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) posting good resultsâ€”but nothing is consistent, and no deck has been appearing time and time again. Talking to the Pro players yesterday, they didn't seem to think any differently and if a solid control deck exists then we haven't found out about it. Yet.

But if you want to redefine control to mean a deck that will play early disruption and then win, then you could be in luck. [Inquisition of Kozilek](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek) and [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) provide early hand disruption, and that buys you the time to crash in and end the game before the Twelvepost deck can find a second [Scapeshift](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift). Jund and [Doran, the Siege Tower](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower) decks exist in this bracket, and what you might think of as "midrange" decks are probably the closest we've got to control in Modern. The key is that you can't afford to wait until turn nine to kill your opponent, because they will have found a way out by then and will have killed you anyway. You have to disrupt, and then strike, with massive damage from cards like Doran the Siege Tower, [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), and [Blightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning).
