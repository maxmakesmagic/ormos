Catching up with Standard
=========================



 Posted in **Perilous Research**
 on February 27, 2014 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 






Welcome back to Perilous Research! Over the last few weeks, we've been focusing on Modern in preparation for [Pro Tour *Born of the Gods*](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptbng14/welcome). With the Pro Tour in the books, we can refocus on lenses on the Standard format. A lot has changed in Standard since we last visited the format. *Born of the Gods* has encouraged many strategies to shift gears or add new colors while simultaneously creating a number of new and viable strategies for the format. Today, we'll be looking at the landscape of the new Standard format by discussing decklists that have performed particularly well in recent Magic Online events.


Many of us expected Black Devotion strategies that were already a dominant force in the Standard format to snowball in terms of strength with the release of cards like [Bile Blight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bile+Blight) and [Drown in Sorrow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drown+in+Sorrow). As it turns out, this has not been the case. Black Devotion remains strong, but cards like [Courser of Kruphix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix) and hexproof creatures that punish the Black Devotion strategies for cutting [Devour Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devour+Flesh) are quite good.


Let's take a look at the new face of Standard's archetypes!

Aura strategies have been around for the last few years with scattered success, but we haven't seen many Aura strategies since *Theros* was released. This is about the most dedicated Aura strategy we've seen in some time. The deck thrives on the fact that Black Devotion strategies have "upgraded" their [Devour Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devour+Flesh) in [Bile Blight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bile+Blight). Strapping a lot of Auras onto a creature with hexproof seems like a good plan with fewer [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict)s in the face of [Ephara, God of the Polis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ephara%2C+God+of+the+Polis) and fewer [Devour Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devour+Flesh) in a world with better spot removal.

The king is dead. Long live the king! Black Devotion decks were able to upgrade their removal spells to include a play set of [Bile Blight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bile+Blight) with the release of *Born of the Gods*. However, the deck does have trouble with [Ephara, God of the Polis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ephara%2C+God+of+the+Polis)'s constant stream of card advantage and the new wave of Control and Hexproof strategies seem to be reasonably matched up here as well. That being said, nothing has a very good matchup against four [Underworld Connections](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Connections). Sure, the deck may not be as dominant as it once was, but that doesn't mean it isn't the best deck in the current Standard.

This is the end. [Maze's End](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maze%27s+End) was a very successful Block Constructed strategy that had trouble being successful in Standard because of cards like [Acidic Slime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Acidic+Slime) that we no longer need to worry about. The deck aims to use board-clearing effects like [Merciless Eviction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merciless+Eviction)/[Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) and combat-damage prevention like [Fog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fog) and [Riot Control](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Riot+Control) to prolong the game to a point where it can achieve victory by activating [Maze's End](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maze%27s+End). The new Standard has slowed down a bit and it seems like a perfect time for the [Maze's End](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maze%27s+End) decks to come in and ruin everyone's day. We shouldn't be surprised if this deck had a lot of success in the coming week.

I've played a lot of games with the Esper Humans deck and we should be confident that it's one of the most powerful Standard strategies when everything goes according to plan. [Ephara, God of the Polis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ephara%2C+God+of+the+Polis) is one of the very best cards in the new Standard and it combos very nicely with cards like [Precinct Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Precinct+Captain), [Xathrid Necromancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xathrid+Necromancer), and [Obzedat, Ghost Council](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council). We can expect this deck's success to be inversely correlated with Blue Devotion decks. Blue Devotion strategies are very strong against this deck unless it's able to curve out into an [Obzedat, Ghost Council](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obzedat%2C+Ghost+Council) and take over the game.

We can expect red strategies to become more focused on a burn plan in the coming weeks so long as Black Devotion remains popular, which should be a safe assumption.

Esper Control has changed very little with the release of *Born of the Gods*. The deck plays a lot of the most powerful cards in the current Standard format, but most of the best threats in Standard require fairly specific answers and it isn't unusual for the Esper Control deck to lose games when it has plenty of lands and a lot of cards in hand. That being said, this is still a tremendously powerful strategy that will always be capable of beating any opponent as long as it draws its cards in the correct order.

The deck transitions roles fluidly, and may be one of the more difficult decks to pilot in the current Standard.

White-Blue Control was easily the best control strategy in Standard before *Born of the Gods*. The deck aims to control the game with interactive cards before taking over with a big [Sphinx's Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) and unlimited [Elspeth, Sun's Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion)s thanks to [Elixir of Immortality](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elixir+of+Immortality). The deck has weakened now that Red-Green Monsters has picked up steam with its [Stormbreath Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormbreath+Dragon)s, but we can expect it to rise to power again when Black Devotion decks try to take over.

We should expect most Blue Devotion players to adopt [Ephara, God of the Polis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ephara%2C+God+of+the+Polis) and [Detention Sphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detention+Sphere) in the not-too-distant future.
