The 2008 Vintage Year in Review
===============================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on January 12, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/generic-avatar-150_299.png)
By Stephen Menendian











![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_time_stop_splash.jpg)  
A*nd so we return and begin again.* That's the opening line of Grant Morrison's brilliant opus *The Invisibles*, (move over, *Watchmen*!) to symbolize the cyclical nature of reality, something that is especially noticeable when the reader settles down for a second helping. It also captures my state of mind as I look back on the storylines that defined Vintage **Magic** at the end of each year.

The notion of time—of progression—is a relatively recent concept to our species. Before modern civilization, the concept of time did not exist as we understand and measure it. Human beings living as hunter-gatherers experienced life as essentially static or cyclical. Life was defined by the unchanging, repeating seasons without technological advances to differentiate the years. Many ancient cultures, from the Mayans to the Babylonians, conceived of time as circular, and represented it as such with a time wheel, without a beginning or an end.

As we celebrate the New Year and the year recently past it's hard not to be aware of the passage of time in a much more linear sense. Vintage has changed so much from year to year and over the years. It is a history that is intimately connected with the history of **Magic** itself. The early history of Vintage *is* the early history of **Magic**.

And yet, at the same time, it's also hard to escape the feeling that time is cyclical, almost repetitious. The big storylines of 2008 feel like throwbacks to earlier years. The return of The Perfect Storm to the top of the Vintage metagame and the restoration of [Time Vault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Vault) are echoes of the past, different eras converging in the present. The murder of [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) and company was a repeat execution for a repeat offender. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Time Vault | Gush |

  
Time is perfect theme for Vintage **Magic** in 2008. In spite of all of the changes wrought by 2008, perhaps none is so profound or so lasting as the final, inevitable functional restoration of [Time Vault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Vault), after well over a decade in the wilderness. In a sense, it's a return to a time before time in **Magic** terms, to the early months of 1994, when the nascent DCI restricted and then banned [Time Vault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Vault) in the space of two months. It's taken almost 15 years to get it back. What fitting card—the Vault of Time—to represent this momentous occasion. 

On account of all of this and more, incredibly, 2008 was somehow more exciting for Vintage than 2007, the year that saw *Future Sight* explode onto Vintage, the simultaneous slaying of [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Gifts%2BUngiven) and the return of [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) into the Vintage metagame via DCI edict, the errata (and restoration) of Flash, and the creative redundancies and possibilities of *Lorwyn* for Vintage deck design. 

Let's revisit the storylines that captured the imaginations of Vintage players in 2008. 

When we left off [last year](/en/articles/archive/2007-vintage-year-review-2007-12-10), *Lorwyn* triggered a metagame shift in favor of [Mishra's Workshop](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Workshop) prison decks sporting nine Sphere effects, including [Thorn of Amethyst](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thorn+of+Amethyst) (in addition to [Sphere of Resistance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+Resistance) and [Trinisphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinisphere)). The decision to unrestrict [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) had brought Grow decks back to the center of the metagame, pushing out [Dark Ritual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) based storm combo and blue based [Mana Drain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Drain) decks that had dominated in the Gifts era. These nine-Sphere Workshop decks took the very advantages which made Grow so dominant—the light mana base packed with cheap cantrips and intense disruption—and turned them into a disadvantage. So long as players were running nine Spheres, [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) decks had a difficult time winning in the traditional way.

Together, MUD and GAT were the two best performing decks in Vintage. Each deck made up about 25% of Vintage Top 8s over globe at the time, with Workshop decks having a slight edge. It was only fitting that they should place first and second, respectively. As powerful as GAT was, it simply couldn't beat MUD's nine-Sphere plan, even with [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) in the sideboard. Its light mana base, the hallmark of [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) decks, couldn't withstand the barrage of Spheres. [Chalice of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) only added insult to injury. With the advent of these MUD decks, it looked like it was going to be a long, cold, dark winter for blue mages, in the icy embrace of merciless Workshop pilots.

Since the printing of [Forbidden Orchard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Orchard) in 2004, the fastest Oath decks paired [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Akroma%252C%2BAngel%2Bof%2BWrath) and [Spirit of the Night](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spirit+of+the+Night) to deal 18 damage in the space of two turns. Although [Spirit of the Night](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spirit+of+the+Night) was superceded by [Razia, Boros Archangel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Razia%2C+Boros+Archangel), the clock remained the same. Although that may seem like a fast kill, it gave most opponents a chance to do something about it. Workshop decks packed [Duplicant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Duplicant) into sideboards and main decks, primarily to deal with [Darksteel Colossus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Colossus), but were quite happy to remove a legendary Angel from game as well. Even worse, [Duplicant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duplicant) could be reused with [Goblin Welder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Welder).

This Oath list makes those Oath lists look like Standards decks. 

Jeff and Jeremiah put together a very sophisticated combo with [Tidespout Tyrant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidespout+Tyrant). After flipping much of one's library into the graveyard and putting a [Tidespout Tyrant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidespout+Tyrant) into play, the pilot has any number of options. The bottom line is this: by the end of the turn, the opponent will have no permanents in play, and you will have infinite mana, infinite storm, and a dead opponent.

It works like this:

Flashback [Krosan Reclamation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Reclamation) to shuffle [Yawgmoth's Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will) into your library. Use the Tyrant trigger to return a [Mox Sapphire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Mox%2BSapphire) into your hand. If you have another Mox in play—or any other artifact—replay that Mox to generate infinite mana. Flashback [Flash of Insight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash+of+Insight), removing enough cards to put the [Yawgmoth's Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will) in hand and then play it to easily win the game. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Krosan Reclamation | Flash of Insight |

  
From there, all you need to do is play [Brain Freeze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze) and then [Ancestral Recall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Recall) your opponent, after dispensing with their library, for the win. Alternatively, if you've already used the Ancestral, you can spend the additional mana by bouncing and replaying Tyrants until you've emptied your mana pool. Then, pass the turn and watch your opponent die on their draw step. The original list used [Triskelavus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Triskelavus) for the same result, fueling infinite mana into infinite damage with [Triskelavus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Triskelavus), replayed by bouncing it with Tyrant.

The Tyrant combo works exceptionally well with the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-[Fastbond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fastbond) engine. There are a host of synergies, such as the combo of [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) and [Tidespout Tyrant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidespout+Tyrant) themselves: instant speed bounce for any permanent on the table. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_tidespout_splash.jpg)  
This was the first major deck that successfully ported the Gush engine honed and refined by Gro-a-Tog, but it was not to be the last. Oath, like GAT, required only a modest amount of mana, and the search and cantrip engine made perfect sense. [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) was a one-cost [Impulse](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse) to help find Oath and Orchard. The primary difference and the one true drawback to Oath is that the Oath combo consumes a lot of deck space, so Tyrant Oath isn't nearly as disruptive as GAT. However, the upside—far more important—was that it only needed to resolve a two-cost enchantment, [Oath of Druids](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oath+of+Druids), to win the game against Workshops, unlike GAT which needed not only to resolve the [Quirion Dryad](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Quirion%2BDryad) or the [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Tarmogoyf), but to have it go the distance over many turns. When you factor in the fact that a resolved Oath is very potent against GAT, you have a recipe for a top metagame deck. 

Jeff and Jeremiah took first and second place in a 50-person tournament in Salem, Massachusetts on the second weekend of the month. They helped set in motion a series of events that would play out for the next six months.

### February – The Calm before the Combo

Nearly a hundred players traveled to Stratford, Connecticut for the annual Winter "Waterbury" tournament. This tournament is one of the marquee events of Vintage calendar, with a reputation for being an exceptionally well-run and community-building event. Rich Shay, no stranger to winning Vintage tournaments, climbed through the standings and hacked his way through a Top 16 playoff to victory with Tyrant Oath, solidifying its position as the Deck to Beat.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_ichorid_splash.jpg)

Hulk Flash has been an established archetype in Vintage since the DCI removed power-level errata from [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) in May of 2007. In that time period, [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) had see its share of success, but rarely showed up in more than 10% of most Top 8s that period. According to [my 2007 year-end round-up](http://www.starcitygames.com/magic/vintage/15238_So_Many_Insane_Plays_Rounding_Out_The_Vintage_Year.html), [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash)-[Protean Hulk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Protean%2BHulk) made up exactly 6.38% of Top 8s from June to December 2007, proving that it was a viable and strong contender, but no metagame wrecker. [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) had actually been a boon for Vintage. Aside from a minimum amount of power, it used no other high-end cards. The combo itself was well supported with [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merchant+Scroll) and [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), often winning on turn one with multiple counterspell protection, including *Future Sight*'s [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation). This speed was balanced by the fragility of the combo. A single [Fire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Fire) could stop the Sliver kill (1 [Heart Sliver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heart+Sliver) + 4 [Virulent Sliver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Virulent%2BSliver)) in its tracks. Most importantly, almost all Vintage players packed their sideboards with [Leyline of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+the+Void) for both [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) and [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash). With Leyline in play, [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) became a three-card combo, as it needed a bounce spell to combo out. Meanwhile, the tactic bought time for opposing decks to establish a workable defense.

The [Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) kill gave [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) an additional speed and power boost. The previously preferred kill of [Heart Sliver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heart+Sliver) and 4 [Virulent Sliver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Virulent%2BSliver) required an attack step. This deck was capable of winning at instant speed for the same number of cards. Here's how it works:

1. Play [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash), putting [Protean Hulk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Protean+Hulk) into play and then the graveyard, triggering Hulk's "leaves play" ability.
2. Search your library for [Carrion Feeder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Carrion+Feeder) and [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double), putting them into play, copying [Protean Hulk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Protean+Hulk) with [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double).
3. Sacrifice [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) to [Carrion Feeder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Carrion+Feeder) to trigger the [Protean Hulk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Protean+Hulk) "leaves play" ability.
4. Search your library for [Mogg Fanatic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) and [Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) and put them into play.
5. Sacrifice [Mogg Fanatic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) to deal 1 damage to your opponent.
6. Sacrifice [Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) to [Carrion Feeder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Carrion+Feeder) to return [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) and [Mogg Fanatic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) to play, copying [Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) this time with [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double).
7. Sacrifice [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) to return [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double), copying [Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark). At this point, you have an infinite loop. Each time you sacrifice [Body Double](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) to return itself, bring [Mogg Fanatic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) with it.

You can relive this combo by using the goldfish function on the upper right hand corner of the deck list. After a few goldfishes, you'll have a good feel for how the combo works. It remains one of the great combos of the year! This deck shot to the top of the Vintage charts, and was the best performing deck in Vintage in the months of March and April. 

But why would being able to win at instant speed make such a big difference in the fortunes of [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) pilots? 

Being able to win at instant speed made [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) a much stronger card. It became functionally closer to a more efficient [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will). [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) can be used offensively to stop an opponent from winning or defensively to protect your own combo. Before [Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) was almost exclusively used to protect a [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) on the stack or to keep someone someone from interrupting the Sliver attack step. Because the [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) pilots could now win at instant speed in their own upkeep with the Pact trigger on the stack, [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) could be used much more often. For example, a [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) pilot could play turn one [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merchant+Scroll) for [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash), and protect [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merchant+Scroll) with [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation), even though they couldn't pay for [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) on their next upkeep. They can then [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) in response to the Pact trigger and win at instant speed. In addition, [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation) could also be used defensively, to stop an opponent from playing [Trinisphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinisphere) or [Yawgmoth's Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will), provided the Flash pilot was able to play [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) on their next upkeep. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_reveillark_splash.jpg)  
 Unlike Gro-a-Tog, with its heavy disruption, Tyrant Oath was much weaker against Hulk Flash. Tyrant Oath had a minimal amount of disruption to make room for the Oath combo parts. Hulk Flash was packed to the brim with disruption, and could easily overpower Tyrant Oath's meager defenses. And like Tyrant Oath, Hulk Flash could largely ignore a Sphere since it just needed to resolve a two-cost spell to win the game. The two previous top decks, Aggro MUD and Tyrant Oath, proved perfect prey to the rejuvenated Hulk Flash archetype. Hulk Flash was now on top, and everyone knew it. 

### April – Would You Like Some Cheese with that Wine?

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_rain_of_tears.jpg) April may be the cruelest month, but it was also the whiniest for Vintage players in 2008. Message boards bristled with complaints as the [Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) / [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) combo ascended through the metagame. 

One Vintage player said:


> 
> I've played Stax with 4 [Trinisphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Trinisphere). I've played Long with 4 [Burning Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) and 4 [Lion's Eye Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond). ![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_desperately_seeking_combo.jpg) Chris had identified an explosive two-card combo made possible by *Shadowmoor*: [Painter's Servant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Painter%27s+Servant) and [Grindstone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grindstone). [Painter's Servant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Painter%27s+Servant) changes every card in every zone to the same color (in addition to whatever colors it already was). This allows you to mill your opponent's entire library on a single activation of [Grindstone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grindstone). Although this was a potent combo, it was not nearly as terrifying as [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash), with its instant speed, two-mana kill and powerful [Pact of Negation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Pact%2Bof%2BNegation). 

 What makes Painter combo better and an overall trump are the synergies, and specifically the abuse of [Red Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Red+Elemental+Blast). Chris ran a full suite of [Red Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Red%2BElemental%2BBlast) and [Pyroblast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Pyroblast) in his maindeck, slinging them like [Vindicate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate) darts at his opponent's spells and permanents.

 Think about what the two best decks of the year thus far had been: Tyrant Oath and Hulk Flash. It turns out that both of these decks are hosed by [Red Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Red+Elemental+Blast).dec. Hulk Flash relies on a blue instant as its combo. Tyrant Oath relies not only on the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-bond combo, but also on a blue creature to combo out. The Painter combo was not just a good combo; the metagame was perfectly situated for it to feed on. Painter Combo was the perfect metagame solution. The only question was: what was the best way to build it? 

 A week later, Andy Probasco, no stranger to innovative Vintage design and the 2005 Vintage Championship finalist, won the first SCG tournament of the year with this:

The significance of this design shell is that Andy had found a way to port the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-bond engine to yet another deck. In the process, he not only produced a more traditional looking control deck, but he gave Painter combo the most powerful draw engine in Vintage. The second place deck, Todd Scott's Tyrant Oath, epitomizes the changing dynamic in Vintage, with Painter smashing Tyrant Oath in the finals, the former best deck being ousted from its top spot.

Almost all of the major players we've discussed over the last six months or so are represented in the Star City Games Power Nine Top 8, including [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid), Workshop Aggro, and, of course, Hulk Flash rounding out the Top 8. Painter was the best deck, but Tyrant Oath, Hulk Flash, and Workshop Aggro were all metagame players, and decks like [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) were still capable of making Top 8. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_power_nine_fan.jpg)  
Painter combo had thrown a wrench in the Vintage metagame, and it wasn't clear where things would settle. What would be the next metagame shift? What weakness could be exploited? 

A week later nearly *two hundred* players traveled to Annecy, France to compete in Team SoLoMoxen's gigantic [Bazaar of Moxen](http://www.bazaar-of-moxen.com/) event. Over sixteen pieces of Power Nine were awarded, in addition to a plethora of other fantastic prize support. After another tumultuous tournament, [a Top 8](http://www.morphling.de/top8decks.php?id=820) emerged that confirmed the American experience.

Jose Antonio Alascio Lopez finished in first place with Painter Combo. In many respects, the Bazaar of Moxen Top 8 looked much like the SCG P9 Top 8, suggesting that the European and American metagames were in sync. Both tournaments were won by Painter Combo, and had similar Top 8s, down to the fact that Hulk Flash placed 8th in both. 

There was, however, one big difference. David Beduzzi, the man who had put Aggro MUD on the map in December of 2007 as a Gro-a-Tog killer, piloted his deck to a second place finish. He must have thought that Aggro MUD would be a metagame answer to Painter Combo. The great strength of Painter against Flash and Tyrant Oath, [Red Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Red+Elemental+Blast), would prove much less effective against a "mono-brown" Workshop deck. At the same time, the Workshop lock parts could shut down the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-bond draw engine. [Painter's Servant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Painter%27s+Servant) may be strong against Tyrant Oath and Hulk Flash, but what about the Workshop prison decks that helped prompt the emergence of these archetypes in the first place? The fact that David ultimately fell in the Finals might suggest that his idea of running Workshop Aggro as a solution to Painter combo was mistaken. It turns out that David's intuition was right. He was just running the wrong Workshop aggro deck. 

### June – The Vintage Apocalypse

Vintage enthusiast Travis LaPlante lured nearly a 120 Vintage competitors back to New England with the promise of a playset of [Mishra's Workshop](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Mishra%2527s%2BWorkshop) for the tournament winner, and a fistful of Power Nine and other prizes for the rest of the Top 8. 

After a fiercely competitive Swiss and Top 16 playoff, Ross Merriam emerged the winner with Mono-Red Workshop Aggro.

Mono-Red Workshop Aggro did much of the work that Aggro MUD had promised to do, but it was even more disruptive. This deck was a death machine, feasting on the remains of [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-bond players throughout the tournament, armed with [Leyline of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Leyline%2Bof%2Bthe%2BVoid) for [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) and [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid). With [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) and Oath under attack thanks to Painter, and with [Red Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Red%2BElemental%2BBlast) flying around, [Mishra's Workshop](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Mishra%2527s%2BWorkshop) return in a big way again.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_gush_protean_workshop_splash.jpg)  
In a sense, it was a return to where Vintage started at the beginning of the year. What would that mean for the Vintage metagame? Tyrant Oath and [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) had emerged in succession as a response to the ascendance of Workshop Aggro. This time, however, Painter Combo was there to hold them in check. Vintage appeared to be a diverse balance of archetypes, checking each other across a metagame web. Would this delicate balance hold? Or would yet another new Vintage archetype emerge, changing everything once again? 

These questions went unanswered. 

The DCI announced the most sweeping changes to Vintage since the [Tolarian Academy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Tolarian%2BAcademy) era. The Vintage restricted list, the DCI's running tally of Vintage's greatest offenders, was going to get a little bit fatter. [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash), [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush), [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merchant+Scroll) and [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) were each announced to join the Vintage restricted list by June 20th. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_banned_cross_fade.jpg)  
The metagame as it had developed over the last year was slated for demolition. Not only was the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-bond engine being taken out back Mafioso style, cards which had been key building blocks to Vintage for half a decade, like [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), were getting whacked as well. [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) hadn't even been legal nine months. And for good measure, [Flash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) was going with them. Whether intended or not, the DCI was sending a message to the Vintage community: blue was getting a makeover. 

The complex metagame dynamic of Tyrant Oath, Hulk Flash, Painter Combo, and Workshop Aggro was being wiped out. Since most of the decks in the metagame had been running the [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Merchant%2BScroll) engine, central to both the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-bond engine and Hulk Flash deck, I think the DCI could have achieved all of its objectives by just restricting [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merchant+Scroll) or [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merchant+Scroll) and [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm).

Nonetheless, the deed was done. What would emerge from this upheaval? 

### July – Back to the Future

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_time_machine.jpg)For a few weeks Vintage was thrown into chaos. Completely new archetypes were being tested and tried. Bizarre homebrew concoctions were showing up in Top 8s everywhere. Slowly but surely, a new metagame began to coalesce.

At first, the thinking in Vintage was that the primary benefactors of the June restrictions would be the decks that were untouched by the restrictions. According to this logic, [Mishra's Workshop](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Mishra%2527s%2BWorkshop) decks and [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) would be the new top tier. This logic proved flawed. 

Workshop decks thrived in a [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) environment since they were natural predators for light mana bases and free (or cheap) cantrips and draw spells. The excision of the [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush)-bond engine from the Vintage metagame would undermine Workshop Prison's reason for existence. As for [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid), it was not a deck whose performance is a function of the metagame dynamics in which it is situated. Rather, its performance is dependant upon the amount of sideboard cards that people devote to it. So long as people ran the same amount of anti-[Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) sideboard cards, there was little reason to think that it would be more successful than it had been before. Rather than boosting the fortunes of both decks, the metagame that emerged as a consequence of the June restrictions meant that these decks were going to be less significant metagame players than they had been in the previous year. 

Instead, the metagame that emerged was directly traceable to the metagame that existed before the DCI simultaneously unrestricted [Gush](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gush) and restricted [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) a year before. At the time, the best deck in Vintage was Meandeck Gifts, a [Mana Drain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Drain)-based combo-control deck built around [Merchant Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merchant+Scroll) and [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) that ended up winning the previous Vintage Championship. But before [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) transformed the Vintage, the two best decks had been [Dark Ritual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) Storm combo and the ever-popular [Mana Drain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Drain)-based Control Slaver. With [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) gone, many thought that Control Slaver, and the [Dark Ritual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) Storm decks especially, would not be able to mount a comeback. 

Forty brave souls traveled to Wisconsin in the middle of July to battle in a tournament that would foreshadow the 2008 Vintage Championship. Tommy Kolowith, the dark knight of Ritual-based storm combo and the 2006 Vintage Championship runner-up with the same, bested Mike Sheen in [the Finals](http://www.morphling.de/top8decks.php?id=859). It was a classic matchup of [Dark Ritual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) fueled Storm combo versus Control Slaver.

What the naysayers overlooked was that most of the other decks in Vintage also relied on [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm). The restriction of [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), in effect, did nothing in terms of the composition of the metagame. If almost every deck suffered the same loss, none truly suffered. In any case, cards like [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) and [Sensei's Divining Top](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) helped fill the gap, cards that were either not available or unplayed when Slaver and Storm combo were the best decks in Vintage. 

### August – The 2008 Vintage Championship!

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_mox_ruby_prize.jpg)The 2008 Vintage Championship was temporarily moved from its traditional location at Gencon Indianapolis to U.S. Nationals to help celebrate and commemorate the 15th Anniversary of **Magic**. Over one hundred players made the trip to Chicago to compete in Vintage **Magic**'s most prestigious tournament, many with dreams of taking home the prize to the left.

Without fail, the Vintage Championship defines the state of Vintage, clarifies the metagame, and sets the course for future trends. After an indescribably brutal Top 8 playoff, Paul Mastriano emerged victorious. If you've been reading this article carefully, you'll recognize most of the names of the Top 8 competitors. Full coverage is available [here](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/feature-vintage-championship-top-8-coverage-2008-07-14).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/21_jim_and_paul_splash.jpg)  
The Vintage Championship confirmed what we already suspected with unmistakeable clarity. The Perfect Storm and Control Slaver were the two best decks in Vintage. The Top 4 decks featured two of each archetype.

Paul's deck utilizes the absolute best cards ever printed. Egregious mistakes like [Necropotence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) and [Timetwister](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Timetwister) are featured prominently. This is no tin-foil combo deck. The Perfect Storm is highly resilient and impervious to most modes of attack, featuring a rock solid, basic-land heavy mana base, and a swath of free countermagic to protect its game plan and defend itself from opposing stratagems. It can race any deck, but it prefers to build card advantage and establish mana stability in the first couple of turns. From there, TPS builds a critical mass of spells and then executes an unstoppable kill, often capped with a massive [Yawgmoth's Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will). In case the [Tendrils of Agony](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony) kill is not possible, the deck can pull off the mortifying plan B of [Darksteel Colossus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Colossus) beatdown, made possible with [Tinker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker). (For more on the deck, I've written a three part primer—here are [Part I](http://www.starcitygames.com/magic/vintage/16270_So_Many_Insane_Plays_The_Perfect_Storm.html), [Part II](http://www.starcitygames.com/magic/vintage/16298_So_Many_Insane_Plays_Playing_The_Perfect_Storm.html), and [Part III](http://www.starcitygames.com/magic/vintage/16330_So_Many_Insane_Plays_Winning_With_TPS.html).) 

The runner-up, Jimmy McCarthy, was playing a Control Slaver variant designed by Brian DeMars (who also made Top 8).
