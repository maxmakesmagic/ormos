Legacy Coverage of Eternal Championships 2013
=============================================



 Posted in **Event Coverage**
 on November 2, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/wizards_authorpic_larger.jpg)
By Wizards of the Coast











![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/trophy.jpg)Here at Eternal Weekend in Philadelphia, it's been all about the old school. After fighting their way through a field of close to 400 players, it came down to Ari Lax and Osyp Lebedowicz in the finals of the Legacy Championships. After all of the hullabaloo made about the strength of Sneak and Show coming into this event, there were no [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) decks that made it through to the elimination rounds. Instead, the tournament was dominated by the so-called "fair" decks of Legacy. Variations on [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets), Jund, and White Weenie managed to attack their way to victory in two point chunks, all day long. In the end, it was Ari Lax, eschewing his trademark [Ad Nauseam](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) Tendrils for the mono-white Death and Taxes, that won the day, taking home the impressive [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) and the title.


Tomorrow is another day and another tournament. Bright and early, players will be returning to the Convention Center to upgrade to Vintage, where the real power lies. From [Black Lotus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Lotus)es to [Ancestral Recall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Recall)s, the decks will be fun to watch, and the strategy will be intricate. Check back in the morning as Chris Pikula and Randy Buehler stream live video coverage of the Vintage Championships here at Eternal Weekend from Philadelphia!

Saturday, 9:30 a.m. – What's in a Name?
---------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

 

One of the biggest pieces of news coming into Eternal Weekend, especially for the Legacy Championships, was the November 1 release of Commander 2013. While geared primarily towards the casual Commander format, cards from Commander 2013, as with Commander before it, are legal for play in Eternal formats. We have seen in the past the effect that this can have, as Commander's [Scavenging Ooze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scavenging+Ooze) and Flusterstorm had a definite impact on the complexion of Legacy upon their release. Commander 2013 is no different, boasting a powerful new addition to the Legacy format: [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis).



![True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=True-Name+Nemesis)

Potentially a powerhouse, [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) is going to be one of the biggest question marks this tournament. It is extraordinarily resilient, susceptible to only global removal spells and sacrifice effects. From the moment it hits play, it is a virtual [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) a turn, which is capable of quickly ending games. That said, it's also a creature, and one that costs three, to boot. In Legacy, when people tap three mana for a creature, it's usually [Griselbrand](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Griselbrand) or [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn). Paying three mana for something that doesn't effectively end the game on the spot is a risky proposition, yet [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) still has people buzzing.


In the proper shell, the card can be absolutely devastating. So far this weekend, I have seen it in Stoneblade, Shardless BUG, and, of course, Merfolk. It serves as a wonderful clock for control decks, ending the game in short order once the game has been locked up. But it truly shines in Merfolk. In a deck that is already packing a number of ways to both aggress and be disruptive, [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) provides an extra boost, supplementing the already strong core of the deck. Merfolk has been somewhat out of favor in recent months, though, as it has appeared ill-prepared to deal with the bulk of the Legacy format despite its strength against the currently-dominant combo strategies. It will remain to be seen whether or not [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) is able to breathe a little life into this deck, carve a niche for itself in the shell of another deck, or simply remain a role-player in this star-studded Legacy field.


Another of the interesting Commander 2013 cards that will likely make a splash this weekend (perhaps a larger one than [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis)) is [Toxic Deluge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Toxic+Deluge). It's rare that a cad comes around with the ability to completely sweep the board, skirt even protections as strong as [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis), kills creatures both large and small, and does so for only three mana. When investigating the most sought-after cards of the weekend, [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) came up quite a bit, but only half as much as the black sorcery did.

It's easy to see why it's powerful, but let's look at what it does in the context of Legacy. Contrary to popular opinion, Legacy isn't just teeming with gigantic, nigh-unkillable monsters. Yes, Sneak and Show and Reanimator have been both very powerful and consistent recently. But they overwhelming majority of the decks in Legacy involve beating people up with cards like [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets), [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), and [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager). As creature decks make up the majority of the field, idealized mass removal like [Toxic Deluge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Toxic+Deluge) is very important for any decks packing the mana to cast it. In addition to killing all of the little guys, [Toxic Deluge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Toxic+Deluge) even has the ability to kill the largest of monsters. Sure, it comes at great cost, but even [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Emrakul%252C%2Bthe%2BAeons%2BTorn) falls prey to the [Deluge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deluge).


It will be interesting to see whether these cards have a noticeable impact on the fabric of Legacy, although it appears early on that they will.

Saturday, 12:00 p.m. – Legacy Championships at a Glance
-------------------------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

 

More than any other format in **Magic**, Legacy is all about the decks. With access to over 13,000 cards, there are a tremendous number of options for players, including a number of interactions that were undreamed of when the cards were originally designed. You get decks like Sneak and Show, which take advantage of [Sneak Attack](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sneak+Attack) and [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) to get [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn) into play, usually ending the game soon thereafter. You have the various [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) decks that are an updated and deepened riff on the [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) decks that ran rampant in Standard for over a year. Speaking of modified Standard stalwarts, how about the Stoneblade decks that are derivatives of CawBlade, one of the most stifling decks Standard has ever seen. You have Elves combo decks hoping to avoid reanimated Elesh Norns. You have decks like [Ad Nauseam](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) Tendrils and Belcher, which feature virtually no lands, and Lands, which features virtually no spells. In Legacy, you really can do it all.


As it has garnered more and more player and organizer support over the last few years, Legacy technology has grown by leaps and bounds. It doesn't quite move as quickly as Standard does, but it moves and shifts quite a surprising amount for a format that sees such a small relative change in its card pool on a regular basis. There are certainly trends to track, and information to be aware of if you have any illusions of doing well at a high-profile Legacy event.


Coming into this event, the biggest mover and shaker has certainly been Sneak and Show. [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) decks have been a force to be reckoned with ever since the release of [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn). Able to end the game in effectively one fell swoop, the deck is capable of killing opponents before they get more than one permanent into play, or wiping the board clear against those that do. It has been doing incredibly well recently, helping a number of players to top finishes at some high profile Legacy events.


Here in Philadelphia, things are a bit different. First, here is a look at the metagame breakdown from the Grinder run yesterday:

As you can see, that is an incredible amount of variety. 22 players, 15 different decks. Welcome to Legacy. As expected, the only real concentration of decks played is in the [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) variant, though it's a different deck than expected. Considering the performances of Sneak and Show in the preceding weeks, it was interesting to see the Omni-Tell variant, which relies on getting an [Omniscience](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Omniscience) into play either via [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) or [Dream Halls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dream+Halls), before killing with a cast [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn). The variant is one of the other major decks to use the [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) engine, though it has seen far less success than Sneak and Show. Even more interesting, there were no Sneak and Show decks at all in the Grinder.


Ultimately, it was Keith Blackwell's Shardless BUG deck that ended up taking the Grinder down. This is a deck that has been around in force since last year, where it served to keep the dominant Stoneblade decks in check. The deck also comes equipped with reasonable answers to the Sneak and Show deck, between [Liliana of the Veil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) and [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor), as well as a boatload of permission and discard.


Taking a glance around the room during the third round, the breakdown of the Legacy Championships itself looks a little different than shown by the Grinders. A random sampling of ten matches from around the room showed the following breakdown:

This sampling is fairly representative of the field around the room. RUG [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) and Shardless BUG are clearly the two biggest decks in the room. Both decks feature aggressive cores backed up with divergent strategies. RUG tends to follow the effective [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s of [Insectile Aberration](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Insectile+Aberration) and [Nimble Mongoose](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimble+Mongoose) with *actual* [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s, while BUG rocks more of a control shell, featuring cards like [Ancestral Vision](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Vision)s and [Hymn to Tourach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach) to cascade into.


Other than those big decks, the things to note are the relative lack of Sneak and Show enrichment. As decks tend to do well, players key into this and there tends to be an enrichment at following tournaments. We saw it with the rise of Monoblack Devotion and Colossal Gruul at Grand Prix Louisville, one week after they made Top 8 of the Pro Tour. Legacy tends to avoid this effect, though. With the richness of the format, players tend to find one or two decks they like and simple tweak them to deal with variations in the metagame. As such, seeing the relative lack of Sneak and Show isn't a complete surprise. What'll be surprising is if it doesn't end up with a presence in the Top 8. It is the favored deck of many of the top Legacy players, making it likely to have at least one representative in the elimination rounds.


For those of you unfamiliar with Legacy, many of these names will be rather foreign to you, despite the fact that Legacy tends to be one of the more direct in naming its decks. As such, I'll take a look through these breakdowns here in a little bit and give you the skinny on what these decks tend to do.

Saturday, 3:30 p.m. – Legacy Guidebook
--------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

 

As promised, here's a brief run-through on some of the biggest decks in the tournament, with special attention paid to the decks mentioned in the previous metagame breakdown. There are about a dozen other major players in the format, but these are the biggest decks for this event that might be misunderstood by the uninitiated.


**Omni-Tell** – Almost always mono-blue, Omni-Tell centers around having [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) or [Dream Halls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dream+Halls) slip an [Omniscience](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Omniscience) into play. Once in play, the powerful enchantment allows the pilot to cycle through the deck using cards like [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder), culminating with [Enter the Infinite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enter+the+Infinite) or just the shortcut to [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn). In a pinch, [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Emrakul%252C%2Bthe%2BAeons%2BTorn) can substitute for [Omniscience](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Omniscience), riding [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) into play.

**High Tide** – Another mono-blue deck, [High Tide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide) centers around its eponymous instant, the mana-generating engine [High Tide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide). Once the first [High Tide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide) has resolved, cards like [Turnabout](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Turnabout) and [Candelabra of Tawnos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Candelabra+of+Tawnos) to amplify the mana generated, culminating with one massive [Blue Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blue+Sun%27s+Zenith) to finish things off.


**Punishing "X"** – The Punishing tag is associated with versions of typical archetypes featuring the [Punishing Fire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Punishing+Fire)/[Grove of the Burnwillows](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grove+of+the+Burnwillows) engine. This can be found in traditional midrange decks like Jund and Naya, all the way to more controlling decks like [Life from the Loam](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam)-based decks. This engine gives the decks a long-term, crushing inevitability, as well as some up-front protection from all of the aggro decks in the field.


**Maverick** – One of the less descriptive names in Legacy, Maverick and its variants are essentially GW-based creature decks, relying on the central [Stoneforge Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) engine and the versatility of a [Green Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) toolbox to present an incredibly resilient front. Capable of defeating creature decks thanks to [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte), combo decks with [Gaddock Teeg](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg), and Reanimator strategies with [Scavenging Ooze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scavenging+Ooze), Maverick has an answer to everything, and threats to end the game quickly.


**Pox** – [Pox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pox) decks utilize a resource denial strategy, relying on the unholy trinity of hand destruction, land destruction, and man destruction to ensure that opponents can't do anything, much less win a game of **Magic**. Cards like [Smallpox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smallpox), (big) [Pox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pox), [Innocent Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Innocent+Blood), [Hymn to Tourach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach), and [Sinkole](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sinkole) keep opponents under lockdown, while [Bloodghast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodghast), and eventually [Tombstalker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tombstalker), finish them off.

**12 Post** – So many posts! 12 Post is the Legacy version of Modern's Tron decks, a big mana deck that does big mana things. Between [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost), [Glimmerpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost), and [Vesuva](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva), this deck is capable of spewing out an unreasonable amount of mana, especially when aided by [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) and [Candelabra of Tawnos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Candelabra+of+Tawnos). In the end, this tremendous amount of colorless mana leads to a dealer's choice of Eldrazi.


**Ad Nauseam** Tendrils – ANT is the prototypical Legacy storm deck. With access to all of the mana accelerators in **Magic**'s history, ANT uses a myriad of tutor effects to put together a massive turn, often involving [Ad Nauseam](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam), culminating with a massive [Empty the Warrens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens) or [Tendrils of Agony](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony). Since most of the deck costs zero or one mana, Ad Nauseum provides a glimpse at virtually the entire deck, making it quite easy to assemble the pieces.


**Shardless** – Most commonly a BUG deck, Shardless decks are so named for Shardless Agent, notable primarily for being a three-mana cascade spell. The Agent provides a number of beneficial two-for-one effects thanks to cascade, from [Hymn to Tourach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach) to [Ancestral Vision](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Vision)s to [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). The aggression in the deck is generally relegated to [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), Agent, and [Deathrite Shaman](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) (that two life adds up), and the deck surrounds that with a shell of permission, removal, and discard.


**Elves** – A rare creature-based combo deck, Elves is one of the most unique decks in the format. Thanks to [Glimpse of Nature](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimpse+of+Nature), Elves is able to turbo through its deck, generating absurd amounts of mana through a combination of [Nettle Sentinel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel) and [Heritage Druid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid). [Quirion Ranger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quirion+Ranger) and [Wirewood Symbiote](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Symbiote) provide a stream of creatures, ensuring that the deck keeps going. Eventually, it uses that massive creature base and mana production to either drop a lethal [Craterhoof Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Craterhoof+Behemoth) or an [Ezuri, Renegade Leader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ezuri%2C+Renegade+Leader), into play. Opponents don't usually last much longer.

**Painter** – This vein of decks generally focuses on the [Painter's Servant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Painter%27s+Servant)/[Grindstone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grindstone) combo, where you make all of the cards in both decks the same color, allowing a single [Grindstone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grindstone) activation to mill an opponent out. There are variants of the deck featuring [Imperial Recruiter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperial+Recruiter) as a method of searching for [Painter's Servant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Painter%27s+Servant) or [Goblin Welder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Welder) to help assemble the combo. When in doubt, the deck is still capable of a large amount of mana production, and it can use it to power out a fast [Wurmcoil Engine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine).


**Dredge** – At one point in history, it was impossible to play Legacy without dedicating half of your sideboard to dealing with graveyard-based strategies. Dredge is the reason. Using the powerful dredge mechanic to fill the graveyard up, Dredge bypasses most of the "standard" parts of the game, just dumping cards into the graveyard before returning [Flayer of the Hatebound](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flayer+of+the+Hatebound) to play with [Dread Return](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return). This is usually accomplished by sacrificing [Narcomoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba)s that came into play for free, meaning that mana is not an issue. Combined with copies of [Bridge from Below](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) in the graveyard, [Flayer of the Hatebound](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flayer+of+the+Hatebound) ends the game in a flurry of triggers. Nowadays, there is considerably less hate around for the graveyard, but its utter reliance on dodging hate has kept Dredge from reaching the pinnacle in recent months.


**Death and Taxes** – Effectively a Legacy White Weenie deck, D&T relies on not allowing opponents to do anything, all while pressuring them with an increasing army of creatures. The myriad of creatures available to D&T provide all sorts of versatility to the deck. [Phyrexian Revoker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Revoker) functions as an attacking [Pithing Needle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle). [Thalia, Guardian of Thraben](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thalia%2C+Guardian+of+Thraben), and [Ethersworn Canonist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethersworn+Canonist) are nightmares for combo decks. [Aven Mindcensor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aven+Mindcensor) is just good against all of the various fetch lands and tutors running rampant in Legacy. At the heart of everything, the deck relies on [Stoneforge Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) and [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader) to provide the finishing punch.

Saturday, 4:30 p.m. – Eternally Supportive
------------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

 

Here in Philadelphia, Eternal is the name of the game. There are no frills, no strings attached, no distractions...just good old school Eternal **Magic**. Players have come from all over the world for their crack at the title and the one-of-a-kind prizes: blown up versions of trademark cards from the formats, each with recommissioned artwork. Among the players vying for their chances to win are some of the biggest names in each of the formats. After fortuitously finding them paired against each other in Round 5, I had a chance to speak with one trademark representative of each community.


Ari Lax is one of the best Legacy players on the planet. Over the course of three or four major events in the past couple of years, I can count the number of losses I've seen him accrue on one hand. He contends that this is because I've only seen him at events where he was playing ANT, a deck with which he has become synonymous. Still, his numbers and performances tell the tale of a player very confident with the format. Interestingly, Lax opted away from the ANT deck which has afforded him his greatest success, choosing instead the aggressive Death and Taxes deck for this Championship. It has served him well so far, as he entered Round 6 an undefeated 5-0.


His defeated opponent, Raymond Robillard, is the very example of a pillar of the Vintage community. Over a decade ago, Robillard began a tournament known as the Waterbury in Waterbury, Connecticut. Since its inception, this quarterly Vintage tournament has steadily grown in popularity and esteem, fueling the fire for hundreds of Vintage players across the east cost of the US. After getting married, Robillard had to let organizing events fall by the wayside, but his influence has still made a major impact on an entire generation of Vintage players.


*So how does it feel to be here at Eternal Weekend, with both the Legacy and Vintage Championships being showcased in a stand-alone event rather than part of some larger event?*


**Robillard** – I think it's awesome. I've alweays wanted to participate in it, but never had the chance. I went to Gen Con every year for a few years there, and sporadically as time has allowed recently. I would always plan to do all of these cool things! I'm going to do this or do that! And then I would spend all of my time in the **Magic** room and not make it out. It's nice having it be separate now, because I could focus on Gen Con at Gen Con, and I can focus on this now.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/robillard%20lax.jpg)  
*Raymond Robillard and Ari Lax* 
**Lax** – I am very happy. I love traveling to Indy, I think it's a great place to hold tournaments, but I hate Gen Con. I'm not really into a lot of the other gaming experiences. I play **Magic** for the competitive aspect, and that doesn't really exist outside of the tournaments at Gen Con. A lot of the other stuff there doesn't really appeal to me. At Gen Con, it can be kind of expensive having to purchase a badge, and you have to jump through hoops sometimes to get the tickets you need for events... I just want to play in a **Magic** event, and I get to do that here. It's also about nine hours less driving for me, so I'll take that.


**Robillard** – On a personal level, I'm also really good friends with Nick Coss, the TO for Eternal Weekend. He's a Vintage guy through and through, and he really understands what Eternal players want. I think he's doing a bang up job here.


*Eternal formats tend to be the most misunderstood formats in **Magic**. People just hear Vintage or Legacy and assume that it's all about doing something absurd and an utter lack of interaction between players. How would you guys respond to that?* 


**Lax** – From a fundamental standpoint, Vintage is way harder than any other format. Not only are you presented with so many options for cards, but Vintage also has all of the restrictions placed on it. Other formats have decks with like twenty individual cards. Vintage has that many one-of cards, tutors with an infinite number of options...every one slot matters so much, and every part of the game matters so much because there are so many plays that can cascade into a win so quickly. If you think this card is going to matter and it doesn't, you can just lose... If you battle a well-fought game, it can be incredibly interesting.


**Robillard** – I think the biggest difference between Vintage and Standard is that they are really two sides of the same game. It's like in golf. There is the long game and the short game. Standard is like the long game—whack the ball as hard as you can. You play this huge guy then I play this mythic then you play this huge mythic... Vintage is more about precision. If you turn your wrist ever so slightly when putting, you find yourself missing completely. It's the same thing in Vintage. If you misstep one little bit (pun intended), it's all over.


*How do you feel about the state of Legacy right now?*


**Lax** – I'm pretty happy with it. I mean, the big hype right now is that [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) needs to be banned, right? This is the same [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) deck that we ran out of the metagame like a year and a half ago. I'm not sure why people are complaining so much about it now.


**Robillard** – Until they produce a card that says "counter target spell," [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) is going to be dominant. Oh wait! There are a million ways to do that!

**Lax** – Yeah, we dealt with this deck and caused it to fade out about a year ago. It's good, but it's not stellar. There are a bunch of issues with [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) as opposed to some of the other combo decks, specifically mana mobility with your combo turn. Whereas Storm or [High Tide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide) can set up these sequences where they force opponents to really consider what's important to worry about, [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell) simply plays [Show and Tell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Show+and+Tell). **Robillard** – Wait, you do have a [Counterspell](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)? Ok. I guess you got me.


*So are you guys looking forward to Vintage tomorrow?*


**Lax** – I'm probably not going to play. I don't own any power, and I didn't want to go through the hassle of borrowing someone's power, playing with it and being responsible for it. If I did have access to some that I knew I could get easily, I would have loved to play more of the format to prepare for tomorrow.


**Robillard** – I have a funny story considering that I'm considered a big Vintage guy. I'm actually running a deck with no power tomorrow. I have always played in proxy tournaments, and for years, I had two full sets of power. Then I decided I wanted to marry my wife, and those two sets of power basically paid for the wedding. I've collected all sorts of other cards since them, but I've never gone back and collected the power since I've always just been able to proxy it. I always feel a little nervous about playing with someone else's power, so I just opted to play unpowered. If I'd known that there were going to be such strong security measures here, I might have considered it. It's great. I had a set of power stolen from me at Gen Con a couple of years ago, and Brian DeMars helped me get it back. It was an eye-opening experience for me, both in terms of how much it can hurt to lose something so valuable, but then the sense of goodness in the world that there are people that would help me get it back.


*How important have proxy tournaments been in helping to grown Vintage, especially considering the difficulty some players face in acquiring the cards to play sanctioned events?*


**Robillard** – I think the proxy tournaments have helped to grow the environment, but they also created a situation where it can be tough to go back to sanctioned. They bring people in, and they create an opportunity for people to play Vintage, which is great, but those same people lose the incentive to actually acquire the cards needed to play in sanctioned tournaments if they can just play in proxy tournaments. I think it's been a weird catch-22. I think proxy tournaments are necessary to have a vintage scene, but as long as there are proxy tournaments, there won't be as strong of a sanctioned Vintage scene. Those are really the two options. Do you want a proxy Vintage scene or no Vintage scene at all? I'll take the proxies.


*Let's close by telling us your favorite things that you can do in your respective formats!*


**Robillard** – I'm going to have to go with being able to kill someone on turn one. There is nothing that compares to that feeling—it's one of a kind. I think that it creates a real sense of excitement for players and spectators alike. There are players who complain that, without [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will), you can't possibly compete. That doesn't happen all the time. Both players should be coming in with that attitude, that they might both be able to pull of this turn one-kill. Sometimes I'll get it, sometimes they will. We can both get our rush. The slightest thing could mean the match. It's very unforgiving, but I mean that as a compliment. There's just so much strategy there.


**Lax** – I've got two very different things that I find really exciting. First is the one that everyone would expect from me—casting [Diminishing Returns](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diminishing+Returns). That card is sweet, you never know what's going to happen. Never tell me the odds. You just flip your cards, and you're like, "Ok, I win this time." The other one that I had happened today. I had a [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader) with a [Sword of Fire and Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) and an [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) on it. Trying to figure out how much damage that does on the first swing is impossible. I love that.

Saturday, 5:45 p.m. – Playing "Fair"
------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

 

Legacy is synonymous with big plays and overpowered cards. I mean, one of the biggest decks coming into this weekend is a deck capable of paying three mana to put a 15/15 creature with protection from more or less everything. Yet despite the obvious power in this format, the most-played decks in this format are centered around creatures. Decks like the various [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) decks, Jund, Shardless BUG...they all seem to want to play...well...fair.


"I mean, I wouldn't call this deck fair," Ari Lax said about his Death and Taxes deck. Based around an army of white creatures that all have some sort of ability that is detrimental to opponents, Lax's deck is a perfect foil for the various powerful combo decks in Legacy. Between [Ethersworn Canonist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethersworn+Canonist) and [Thalia, Guardian of Thraben](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thalia%2C+Guardian+of+Thraben), it's very difficult for decks like Storm and [High Tide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide) to get any traction. [Mangara of Corondor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mangara+of+Corondor) and [Fiend Hunter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fiend+Hunter) are great answers to Sneak and Show decks. [Phyrexian Revoker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Revoker) is ideal against the myriad of Planeswalkers throwing their clout around, as well.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/Lax.jpg)  
*Ari Lax* 
"It's just not fair," Lax laughed. "You just make your opponent not play **Magic**, then you somehow kill them. They just sit there and say, "Man, none of my cards did anything." And you get to ask them why they didn't build their decks better. I mean, I'm playing creatures with powers and toughness."


Creatures with power and toughness less than three, even! The smalles creatures seem to go in Legacy is the 3/2 [Insectile Aberration](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Insectile+Aberration), so seing Lax doing so well (he's 7-0) with a deck filled with two-powered creatures is such a wakeup call. Yet despite the fact that he's just playing stupid little creatures and attacking with them, he contends that his deck isn't playing fair.


"Your game plan is to get a [Sphere of Resistance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+Resistance) into play and then just destroy their lands with [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) and Mangara," he explained. "The difference is that your [Sphere of Resistance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+Resistance) gets to attack them. You want to negate your opponent's strategy, but not like a control deck does, where you are looking to have all of the right answers. Here, you want to have all of the right threats to make their cards effectively do nothing. That's why it's not fair. They don't get to play **Magic**."


Another player "playing fair" this weekend is Pro Tour champion Osyp Lebedowicz. His deck of choice for the Legacy Championship was a strictly UR version of [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets). Unlike most versions of the deck, which tend to dip into three colors, Lebedowicz's version of the deck is less vulnerable to two of the most powerful disruptive spells in the format: [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) and [Blood Moon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon).


"Being able to fetch out a first-turn basic Island is so good in many of the matchups here," Lebedowicz said. "Especially against the other [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) decks, having [Stifle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) up and not being vulnerable to [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) makes a big difference."


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/Osyp.jpg)  
*Osyp Lebedowicz* 
Perhaps the coolest aspect that separates this version of [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) is the addition of [Young Pyromancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Young+Pyromancer).


"Pyromancer has been a very good card," he told me. "It's basically taking the place of [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) in this deck. Against the decks that are trying to play fair, like other [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) decks or BUG, it can just kill them in three turns if they don't deal with it. Against the unfair decks like Sneak and Show, [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) isn't going to get any bigger than two-power anyway, so the Pyromancer actually packs more punch. Being able to cast it with [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) and [Daze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze) in hand, getting you four power on the second turn, is so powerful."


Lebedowicz also tested out the newest addition to Legacy: [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis). It has been quite a divisive topic thus far this weekend, as it seems just a touch too slow in many matchups to really shine. It has been taking the place of two-drops for much of the weekend, which has led to more than one lost match. As Hall-of-Famer Randy Buehler said during his commentary, "If you aren't impacting the board on turn two, it's really hard to win in Legacy."


"One of the biggest reasons I wanted to play this deck here was that Chris Pikula wanted me to test out [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) before Grand Prix DC," Lebedowicz laughed. "So far it's actually been pretty good. Again, it's really good against the fair decks, where they have to have an answer soon or it just kills them. It's a bit worse against the combo decks, but it isn't completely useless. If nothing else, you can always pitch it to [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will)."

Quarterfinals Roundup
---------------------


*by Nate Price*

 

### Richard Nguyen (RUG Delver) vs. Micah Greenbaum (Death and Taxes)


Nguyen's [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) deck came out strong against Greenbaum's Death and Taxes deck, getting an early [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets) into play and transforming it. He was also able to stifle (but not [Stifle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle)) Greenbaum's early development with a [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) that killed a [Karakas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karakas), stranding Greenbaum on one land.


"That [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) was pretty good," Nguyen said after the game. Greenbaum had an [Æther Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial) that he was able to get into play, but it was far too slow to keep up, especially when Nguyen added a 4/5 [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) to his side. Two turns later, and Greenbaum packed his cards in.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/QFNguyenGreenbaum.jpg)  
*Richard Nguyen vs. Micah Greenbaum* 
In the second game, Nguyen desperately tried to avoid Greenbaum's [Crackdown](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crackdown). Yes, you read that right. Against Nguyen's [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) deck, [Crackdown](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crackdown) would effectively keep all of his creatures tapped down, alleviating a great deal of pressure. If, that is, they ever reached three power. Nguyen tried to dodge hitting threshold and failed to reveal a card for his [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) to transform it in an effort to keep his offense alive.


In the final game of this match, things went incredibly well for Greenbaum, but only after they had gone incredibly poorly. After getting his [Æther Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial) countered with [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) and his [Karakas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karakas)[Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland)ed, stranding him on one mana, Greenbaum was able to rally, drawing the cards he needed to not only get himself back into the game, but prevent Nguyen from winning. It took drawing the perfect set of cards, including a [Swords to Plowshares](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares) for an [Insectile Aberration](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Insectile+Aberration), as well as Nguyen being unable to get his [Nimble Mongoose](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimble+Mongoose) into threshold. Eventually, Greenbaum found a [Stoneforge Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) and a [Batterskull](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull), eking out a very close game to take the match.


### Osyp Lebedowicz (UR Delver) vs. Brad Jarman (RUG Delver)


Lebedowicz thought he had the advantage in this matchup, as his mana is much better than that of Jarman's RUG [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) deck. While that may be the case, it was Lebedowicz's aggression that carried the day in the first game, quickly overrunning Jarman with creatures.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/QFJarmanLebedowicz.jpg)  
*Brad Jarman vs. Osyp Lebedowicz* 
The second game featured a bizarre moment where Lebedowicz accidentally played a second land at one point. Things were very confusing between multiple [Daze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze)s from both players, but it was all fortunately caught on camera. These days are long, and it is easy to make mistakes at the end of them, and the judges were willing and able to rewind the clock to before that point and progress from there. Rather than take that, however, Lebedowicz chose to concede the game, admitting that he shouldn't be allowed to win this game if he had made such a big mistake. That wonderful display of sportsmanship sent things to a tense Game 3.


With everything in the balance, Lebedowicz managed to get the aggressive start he needed to put himself in the driver's seat. Holding a [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) at the end of the game, Lebedowicz played it safe rather than go for the throat, giving Jarman an extra turn to draw out, but it didn't end up mattering in the end, as Lebedowicz was able to hold on and burn Jarman out.


### Matt Tocco (Ad Nauseam Tendrils) vs. Greg Price (Legacy MUD)


Tocco's first game in the Top 8 was anything but standard. Since this is Legacy, that should be a good thing. Unfortunately, he found himself in an unfavorable situation in an unfavorable matchup. Price's MUD deck comes equipped with a number of potent answers to Tocco's storm deck, including a full set of [Chalice of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) and [Trinisphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinisphere)s. Even with that on his side, Price didn't need them. He had a terrific starting hand, with a [Darksteel Colossus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Colossus) and the [Metalworker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Metalworker) needed to power it out. Faced with this dead end, Tocco took to his deck in an attempt to go off. He was missing a few of the pieces required to power to victory, but he had the eponymous Ad Nauseum, which he used to reveal card after card. And as the cards peeled off, he grew even more desperate, failing to find the kill card needed to actually win as life slipped away. Before he actually drew a kill condition or a way to go find one, he drew himself out of life, essentially killing himself before Price would get the chance.


In the next game, Price did draw his powerful hate cards, landing both a [Chalice of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) for one and a [Trinisphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinisphere). This would have been enough to lock up the game if it weren't for the pair of [Abrupt Decay](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay)s in Tocco's hand. This gave him a glimpse of light in an otherwise dark game, but the light was soon snuffed out. A second [Chalice of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) for one delayed Tocco just long enough that Price's [Kuldotha Forgemaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kuldotha+Forgemaster) was able to put out a hasty [Sundering Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sundering+Titan), clearing Tocco's board.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/QFToccoPrice.jpg)  
*Matt Tocco vs. Greg Price* 
The game seemed unlosable for Price, as Tocco had no cards in play and he was showing a lethal attack. Instead, Tocco managed to convert a single [Underground Sea](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underground+Sea) into tons of mana via [Infernal Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor)s and [Cabal Ritual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Ritual)s, thankfully in threshold. Eventually, thanks to [Past in Flames](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Past+in+Flames), he was able to fetch out a [Tendrils of Agony](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony) to steal the second game.


In the final game, Price once again found the right cards to deal with the situation. [Lodestone Golem](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lodestone+Golem) is very potent against the ANT deck, functioning both to slow them and to put a quick clock down. In addition to this, Price had a [Phyrexian Revoker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Revoker) locking down a pair of [Lion's Eye Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond)s. In the end, the combination was too much to bear, and Tocco folded to the pressure.


### Paul Lynch (UWR Miracles) vs. Ari Lax (Death and Taxes)


Lax said earlier that his deck was about not allowing his opponent to actually play **Magic**, and he set up the first game of his quarterfinal match with that in mind. Against Lynch's Miracles control deck, Lax set up a pretty silly cycle with [Mangara of Corondor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mangara+of+Corondor) and [Karakas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karakas), where he was able to exile one of Lynch's lands every turn without losing his Mangara. With an [Æther Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial) on three counters, this was able to continue until Lynch had had enough.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/QFLaxLynch.jpg)  
*Ari Lax vs. Paul Lynch* 
In the second game of their match, it all came down to one tense turn. Lax had managed to deal with all of the major threats that Lynch was able to play. The only thing left on Lynch's side of the table that wasn't a land was a seemingly useless [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace). On the final turn of the game, Lynch managed to find a [Helm of Obedience](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Helm+of+Obedience), which would combo with the [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) to completely mill Lax out. When he went for it, Lax used [Æther Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=%C3%86ther+Vial) to slip in a [Flickerwisp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flickerwisp), resetting the [Rest in Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rest+in+Peace) and giving him the breathing room. Lax had a [Sword of Fire and Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) for his [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader), representing approximately a billion damage. Lynch took a swing with his Helm, looking for a creature that would allow him to block, surviving the turn and giving him the chance he needed to get to the [Terminus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminus) on the top of his deck. Instead, he found a [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland). Game and match to Lax.

Finals - Osyp Lebedowicz (UR Delver) vs. Ari Lax (Death and Taxes)
------------------------------------------------------------------


*by Nate Price*

 

It's rare in a tournament like this to have the two most accomplished players in the field meet in the finals. There are so many pitfalls along the way that it is almost a certainty that one will fall prey to them. Not so here at Eternal Weekend in Philly, though.


Osyp Lebedowicz, Pro Tour Champion and man-about-town, brought a UR [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) deck that he was hoping to test before the upcoming Legacy Grand Prix in Washington DC. It is safe to say that his testing bore fruit. This was originally orchestrated as a chance for him to test Commander 2013's [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) in a RUG [Delver of Secrets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Delver%2Bof%2BSecrets) shell, though it ended up dropping the green for a better mana base. In the end, *Nemesis* ended up being an all-star against the fair decks, and [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) bait against the unfair decks (read: Sneak and Show and Storm).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/finals_osyp.jpg)  
*Osyp Lebedowicz* 
In his way was Ari Lax, a stalwart and perennial top finisher at Legacy events, with multiple top finishes at the upper level of competition. Rather than his usual [Ad Nauseam](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) Tendrils deck, Lax opted for Death and Taxes, a mono-white aggro deck aimed at disrupting everything that opposing decks could plan on. It was interesting to see two "fair and interactive" decks playing against each other in a field that has previously been dominated by "unfair" Sneak and Show decks.


### The Games (Kind of...)


Lax opened with one of the best possible cards in this matchup, or any matchup for that matter—[Stoneforge Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic). It was two Mystics, in fact, allowing him to search up not only an [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte), but a [Batterskull](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull), as well. Lebedowicz had a [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) to stop one before it could activate, but not the second. This let him get Jitte into play, dominating Lebedowicz's [Young Pyromancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Young+Pyromancer) in the early stages of the game. Even with Lebedowicz's [True-Name Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=True-Name+Nemesis) presenting three points a turn, it was better served as a defender against Lax's equipped attackers. Unfortunately for Lebedowicz, Lax also had a [Mother of Runes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mother+of+Runes) in play, allowing him a path past the *Nemesis*. This combination proved too strong for Lebedowicz, and when a [Phyrexian Revoker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Revoker) shut down Lebedowicz's [Engineered Explosives](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Explosives), he just packed it in.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/eternal13/finals_lax.jpg)  
*Ari Lax* 
The next game was even more quick and painless than the first. Lax had his first-turn [Mother of Runes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mother+of+Runes) hit with [Daze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze), setting Lebedowicz back a turn in mana, but eliminating the stupidly powerful protector from the game. Lebedowicz even had the [Stifle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) to stop Lax's attempt at a second-turn [Stoneforge Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic), preventing it from searching out the game-winning [Batterskull](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull). When Lax simply tapped two mana and put the [Batterskull](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull) that he already had in his hand into play, Lebedowicz laughed. When Lax followed that by [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland)ing Lebedowicz's second land, the laugh turned to a quick concession.


"That's about enough of that," he said, picking up his cards.


Ari Lax has defeated Osyp Lebedowicz in a quick two-game match to become the 2013 Legacy Championship winner!
