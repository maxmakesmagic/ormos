Anything but Standard
=====================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on November 4, 2010 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20170709072651im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











Last week we talked about a variety of [Survival of the Fittest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest) decks.

  
Just after the States / Provincials we got our first look at one of the rising stars of **Magic: The Gathering**—the various $5K and Open circuits at least—in Nick Spagnolo.

In case you missed it last time, here is the short video I did with Nick at New York States:

|  |
  
![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/twtw/twtw101_nicPic.jpg)Now, at the time I interviewed Nick, I didn't know that he was going to go on the meteoric run that he is right now riding. He was just a player who had beaten me fair and square in the Swiss and made the Top 8.

As you now know, Nick went on to win the New York State Championship with his Blue-Black Control deck. Since then he won back-to-back TCGPlayer.com $5K days (both Standard and Limited portions), and to take it back to the first sentence of this article ... Nick won the Legacy portion of the StarCityGames.com Open in Charlotte ... With a Survival deck!

But that isn't the topic of this article.

Pulling back one day to Standard, Nick also finished 3rd the previous day with that signature Blue-Black deck.

The defining features of this deck are the offensive combination of [Frost Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) and [Trinket Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage). Nick's [Trinket Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage)s can get any of the following:


> * [Brittle Effigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brittle+Effigy)
> * [Elixir of Immortality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elixir+of+Immortality)
> * [Everflowing Chalice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice) (2)
> 

The most important of these is [Elixir of Immortality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elixir+of+Immortality), which allows Nick to re-buy all his countermagic and win a long game against basically any deck. 

The current version has moved away from [Memoricide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memoricide) main (originally there for a [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)-dominated metagame) for [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) (anti-control). 

Nick's performance in the month of October is dizzying. He won three different $5K events, made Top 8 of another, and won the New York State Championship. Just prior to that, he finished Top 16 of TCGPlayer's New York Standard tournament (albeit with a White-Blue rather than Blue-Black deck). Not long prior to *that*, he ousted Building on a Budget columnist Jake Van Lunen for a Pro Tour Qualifier Blue Envelope.

I have been quite impressed by Nick's run, and have tried to figure out how anyone—even someone playing great **Magic** like he must be—could perform at such a high level in such sustained fashion.

Then I realized it: He not only always plays [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain), he plays [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) way better than the rest of us do!

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td115_preordain.jpg)  
Nick shared an article on how to play blue spells on TCGPlayer.com last week, which was one of the best theory articles I have read in forever. But in particular I was impressed with how he plays [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain).

Most people I know snap-keep land light hands that simply have [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) in them. Nick not only calls keeping a one land hand with a [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) "a trap" he says to *never cast [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) blind*. This part was particularly eye opening for me:

*"[Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) generates more value the longer you wait to cast it. A late game [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) is usually game-winning, while an early game [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) is little more than a cantrip."*

Anyway, if you want to read the whole thing, I felt like it was worth drawing attention to:  
[Casting Blue Spells](http://magic.tcgplayer.com/db/article.asp?ID=9237) (TCGPlayer.com).

Daniel Jordan won the StarCityGames.com Open Series tournament.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td115_3Cards1.jpg)  
His deck main lines the Block Constructed three-card tag team of [Lotus Cobra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra), [Oracle of Mul Daya](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oracle+of+Mul+Daya), and [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor). [Oracle of Mul Daya](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oracle+of+Mul+Daya) is a savage combo with either of the other two cards (massive mana with [Lotus Cobra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra); relentless card advantage and mana acceleration with [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)) ... Obviously all three together for more than a turn or two will generally set up an insurmountable positional advantage.

So what do you do with all that mana?

Candidates include the aforementioned [Frost Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) and Block Constructed (and TurboLand) favorite [Avenger of Zendikar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avenger+of+Zendikar). Speaking of which, [Avenger of Zendikar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avenger+of+Zendikar) + [Oracle of Mul Daya](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oracle+of+Mul+Daya) is a combination itself!

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td115_destructive.jpg)  
Here we see even more [Frost Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) action. [Frost Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frost+Titan) being a superb post-[Destructive Force](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Destructive+Force) threat (you can keep whatever minuscule mana the opponent has locked down). The one thing that I found puzzling while looking at all these Blue-Green-Red decks is that of the four, Hon's is the only one with even one [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)!

Christopher Cannon played a mono-red Goblins beatdown deck.

This deck hybridizes the disparate camps of metalcraft and Goblins tribal/linear. The deck puts together combinations like [Goblin Bushwhacker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker) + [Devastating Summons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Summons) and [Goblin Cheiftain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Cheiftain) + everything else.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td115_rebirth.jpg)  
All these seemingly disparate elements are glued together by a little spell by the name of [Kuldotha Rebirth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kuldotha+Rebirth). [Kuldotha Rebirth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kuldotha+Rebirth) works brilliantly with both the Bushwhacker and Chieftain haste elements of the deck. All the artifacts that fuel the Rebirth link arms and power up the metalcraft parts of the deck. 

[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Kuldotha+Rebirth)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kuldotha+Rebirth)  
Creatures like [Memnite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite) make for both cheap metalcraft synergy (costs nothing, but makes [Mox Opal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Opal) and [Galvanic Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast) look good) and, of course, more and more free damage in conjunction with [Goblin Bushwhacker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker).

Many of the rest of the artifacts are essentially interchangeable ([Trusty Machete](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trusty+Machete) for example), but the cards really build on one another in a snowball—err *fire*ball—of violence.

Rounding out this Top 8 is a [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) combination deck.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td115_pyro.jpg)  
The fundamental deck is not so dissimilar from the superb combination deck pioneered by Jake Van Lunen over the summer, though a deck leading up to this list has recently been discussed by Patrick Chapin. The exit of ***Magic** 2010* took with it two important cards: [Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp) and [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder). Of the two, [Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp) is the more glaring exit. After all, most games decided in favor of [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) over the summer featured a seemingly limitless stack of [Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp)s powered by [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) and [Call to Mind](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+to+Mind). But today, with no [Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp)s, the deck has to play a much blunter game.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

The "modern" [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) has become a blunt instrument. All four copies of [Foresee](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Foresee). Card advantage. Selection. Like a stone being thrown through an expensive department store window. Fill up your hand, power up Ascension, and throw all the cards at your opponent. The deck can theoretically do tremendous damage with multiple copies of [Call to Mind](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+to+Mind) and the burn suite, but realistically you only need about four actual pieces of cardboard to kill the average opponent. 



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

While [Time Warp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Warp) is the more obvious missing link from the previous format, [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) is probably the more important one. As you can see from the viability of this deck in general, taking infinite turns is, in a sense, overkill. You can get 80% of the way there just drawing way more cards than your opponent and pointing burn spells. What [Ponder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) offered, on the other hand, was a pair to [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain), and an incentive that almost no other deck in Standard could boast: The ability to keep almost any hand.

If you read Nick Spagnolo's strategies around [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain), great! Like I said before, I felt it was a great article and had a lot to teach. However in my opinion, Nick's rules don't apply to combo decks like [Pyromancer Ascension](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension) the way they do against progressive control decks that intend to draw and play lands every turn, and win after interacting with the opponent and his cards. A combo deck will often be able to determine exactly what it wants out of its cantrips just by looking at its opening hand. As we can see from week to week, tournament to tournament, there are all kinds of different, exciting, decks you can play! There are all different kinds of beatdown decks, up to and including Goblin/metalcraft hybrids.
