The End of a Season, the Beginning of an Era
============================================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on December 8, 2005 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/worlds05/mori_trophy.jpg)*2005 World Champion Katsuhiro Mori*The [2005 **Magic** World Championships](/en/events/coverage/worlds-2005-japans-crowning-achievement) concluded last weekend in Yokahama, Japan. The Japanese capped a year littered with Pro Tour Top 8 appearances, Grand Prix bum rushes onto American soil, and the emergence of a couple of new stars on the scene with a definitive finale to the 2005 Pro Tour season. Despite a confident start by the best American team in years, the World Championships's host nation left the **Magic** universe stunned via a clean sweep of individual titles of World Champion and Player of the Year, simultaneously unseating Team USA in three-man competition. Home court advantage served Japan well... Though these highly skilled and dedicated players have received the lion's share of online hype over the last 12 months, it was actually the French who took the majority of the individual titles... Pierre Canali's [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager)s dodged Shuhei Nakamura's [Pillage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillage)s in the Columbus finals, last year's Player of the Year Gabriel Nassif and his team Nova bested *this year's* eventual Player of the Year Kenji Tsumura and his One Spin squad on the way to the team win in Atlanta, and Antoine Ruel defeated both the storied Tsuyoshi Fujita and the ubiquitous Kenji before taking his Champion's crown at Pro Tour Los Angeles. Tabling for a moment the fact that 2005's dominant Tsumura *also* made an appearance in Pro Tour Philadelphia's finals (losing to 2005's lone American champion Gadiel Szleifer), only Shu Komuro managed to actually bring home (or *keep at home* in the case of the Pro Tour *Nagoya* winner) a first place trophy for Japan… despite continuous Top 8 finishes care of superstars like Masashi Oiso, Tsuyoshi Fujita, Ryuichi Arita, and of course eventual Player of the Year Tsumura. Chalk one up for World Champ Katsuhiro Mori.



![Glare of Subdual](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Glare+of+Subdual)
*It's no [Opposition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opposition), and it's no joke either.*
Not surprisingly, Japan's strong weekend started in the tournament's first format: Standard. The field was defined by Mono-Blue Control (well, maybe not "*mono-*" due to a universal Black splash), but the Japanese rocked it rogue style with a new deviation called GhaziGlare. As its name implies, GhaziGlare marries disparate *Ravnica* threats [Vitu-Ghazi, the City-Tree](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vitu-Ghazi%2C+the+City-Tree) and consensus best Limited open [Glare of Subdual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glare+of+Subdual). The deck can function as a reasonable G/W beatdown deck with Vitu-Ghazi as a finisher, or can create an [Opposition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opposition)-inspired board advantage via tons of creatures and [Glare of Subdual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glare+of+Subdual). GhaziGlare has many unique elements, such as choosing [Seed Spark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seed+Spark) over [Naturalize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize) as main deck removal (why not make two [Icy Manipulator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Icy+Manipulator)s while killing that opposing Jitte?) and the underrated [Congregation at Dawn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Congregation+at+Dawn)... What Boros deck can survive midgame topdecks of [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch), [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch), and – you guessed it – [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch)?


As with many creative Japanese decks in the past, GhaziGlare incorporates multiple distinct and effective strategies. It is on the one hand the Vitu-Ghazi deck we have been waiting for since *Ravnica's* unveiling of the heir to [Kjeldoran Outpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost), the successful weapon that Fungus Fire and other such decks have been approaching, but have failed to reach until now. It is an offensive Jitte deck, with mana acceleration and efficient threats, echoing some of the best elements of the TOGIT Three-color Control deck from *Kamigawa* Block, all the way to the double Yosei lock, especially after boards. Over the course of three games, GhaziGlare can even play a [Greater Good](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Good)/fatty plan, much like some of the States-dominating Champs decks we looked at a few months back.


A loyal and effective weapon, GhaziGlare both took eventual semifinalist Tomohiro Kaji to a 6-0 Day One record and handed Katsuhiro Mori his giant $35,000 check. Here are two looks at the deck from Day One and overall big winners:

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/worlds05/f_karsten.jpg)Sharing Kaji's spot at 6-0 on Day One was eventual Finalist Frank Karsten. Karsten had many cards in common with the GhaziGlare 75 -- Yosei, [Greater Good](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Good), and *Kamigawa* Block's dominant land, good old basic Forest -- but went with a [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) engine instead of focusing on weenie creatures, Jitte, and the beatdown. Karsten's deck incorporates many of the effects we have come to associate with one another in not dissimilar decks over the course of *Kamigawa* Block and Standard tournaments -- [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top), [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder), and other shuffle effects -- and can defend itself with tons of removal cards. After sideboarding, Karsten's Greater Gifts can switch into a more conventional [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) engine with [Hana Kami](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hana+Kami) and Arcane/Splice tricks.

![](http://archive.wizards.com/global/images/mtgcom_daily_mf71_picMain_en.jpg)Javier Dominguez missed Top 8 on tiebreakers, finishing 11th, but his Extended deck was flawless in *its* performance. Dominguez's deck is a hybrid between perennial Extended favorite The Rock and Standard and Block Constructed powerhouse [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven). [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) (the card, rather than the deck) super charges The Rock, answering a lot of questions while accelerating the long game elements that the normally ponderous threat deck often has difficulty catalyzing. Case in point: [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis). [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) is often a dorky [Durkwood Boars](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Durkwood+Boars), at least initially... The Rock has to get [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) killed somehow -- many times going down in cards in the short term -- in order to start reaping long term card advantage. [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) allows The Rock to "tutor" for both [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) and [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) (as well as two more targets), to get its machinery going a turn or more early.


It doesn't really matter where the opponent puts [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) relative to [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis). If both go to hand, Dominguez can play [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) four-into-five and follow-up with [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) and a flashback. If [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) hits the graveyard, [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) comes online a turn more quickly, and if [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) goes to the graveyard for whatever reason… everything is great to begin with.


I don't for the most part like Wish sideboards, but Dominguez's is fairly practical. His [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) can get [Llanowar Wastes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Wastes) (the deck is mostly Black and Green, though a successful Wish presupposes Green mana in the short term) and increases his access to the [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) hating [Withered Wretch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Withered+Wretch) in Game One. One interesting singleton in Javier's sideboard is [Stabilizer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stabilizer). This card may be a late season sleeper to hose the rising [Astral Slide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Astral+Slide) and continuingly popular [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) engine decks.

Interestingly, given our data at the time of this writing, no deck claimed more than one PT slot this week (though I suspect when this goes up, you'll see more than one first place NO Stick). That said, Boros Deck Wins seems like one of the best decks for battling through the Swiss rounds, leading all decks in Top 8s and equaling six other archetypes with one Blue Envelope on the week. The CAL, arguably the most powerful deck in Extended – and a notorious candidate for [Stabilizer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stabilizer) to squash – finished just behind Boros, along with Raphael Levy's aggressive Rock deck. It's funny how the simple strategies of clocks + [Molten Rain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molten+Rain) (from Boros) and aggression + disruption and potentially [Withered Wretch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Withered+Wretch)es can trump much more powerful engines.

![Mystic Enforcer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mystic+Enforcer)

I always take a second look at hybrid decks, and Adam Ragsdales' from Osawa, Ontario is a fascinating example. I love Threshold decks, and this Threshold-Zoo-[Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) mish-mash has all the old favorites… even sometime superstar [Mystic Enforcer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Enforcer)! [Mystic Enforcer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Enforcer) was the distinguishing bomb in sickestever.dec (Super-Gro, whatever you want to call it), the eventually dominant deck of Extended past, and has unstoppable buddy [Nimble Mongoose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimble+Mongoose) along for the ride. The best offensive two-drop in **Magic** history and the synergistic [Call of the Herd](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) round out the *Odyssey* Block love fest in this versatile weapon. Dredging [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) is very good with both the Threshold and Flashback elements, fuels [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer)… and of course helps to draw extra cards. There are subtle elements in this deck, too, like being able to match Brushhoppers with [Balancing Act](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Balancing+Act), or recover [Nantuko Monastery](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nantuko+Monastery) attack after attack. I'm not sure if Adam's deck is the best beatdown available… but it's at least fabulously cool.

As far as archetype decks, I still think Heartbeat is the best choice for winning a PTQ (although, or perhaps in support, I played Rift/Slide myself last weekend). Lots of decks just can't beat Heartbeat, at least in Game One (Rift/Slide, Boros, and so on), and it is a lot better against NO Stick than most players – even Heartbeat players themselves – realize.


Good luck this weekend, no matter what you choose to play!
