... Almost Got There
====================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on December 18, 2008 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td17_team.jpg)With wins in [Kuala Lumpur](/Magic/Magazine/Events.aspx?x=mtgevent/ptkl08/welcome) (Jon Finkel), [Hollywood](/Magic/Magazine/Events.aspx?x=mtgevent/pthol08/welcome) (Charles Gindy), and most recently [Berlin](/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/ptber08/welcome) (Luis Scott-Vargas), the good old U.S. of A. was mere inches from closing out every Pro Tour win in 2008.

In the team event, the American trio led by U.S. National Champion Michael Jacob did their job, with Pro Tour superstar Paul Cheon and Sam Black knocking down all necessary pins to emerge team champions. The U.S. were once again at the top of the teams.

The team format this year was broken down into three different Constructed partitions, with each national team sending a representative in each of the Standard, Extended, and Legacy formats. 

Extended
All eyes will be shifting to Extended soon for the upcoming Pro Tour Qualifier season, and the team competition is a rich source of Extended technology (these numbers are all pulled from the teams Top 4 deck lists).

Sam's Elves list was heavily metagamed... He had an [Orzhov Pontiff](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orzhov+Pontiff) starting for Elves mirror matches. If the opponent started to go off, Sam could ride [Wirewood Hivemaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Hivemaster) to several Insect tokens generated by the *opponent's* Elf plays. These he could use to set up a [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) to banish the opponent's entire squad with the aforementioned Pontiff, even if he were "tapped out."



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Interestingly, Sam played both [Predator Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Predator+Dragon) and [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) as kills despite the Scott-Vargas win in Berlin on [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot). [Predator Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Predator+Dragon) was the most popular kill in Berlin (assemble lots of Elves, [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) or [Birchlore Rangers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birchlore+Rangers) to make a [Predator Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Predator+Dragon)—which has both trample and haste—eat lots of little Elves and Insects, attack for ~200); [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) was a less popular but arguably more elegant path to victory. The Elves can produce massive amounts of mana with [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid) by tapping one mana to grant everyone all creature types, thereby allowing the Insects to play Elf and set up ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif). This mana goes into the Mirror Entitiy, and anyone who started the turn on the Elves' side can crush for huge amounts of damage. Why is it arguably more elegant? In addition to a pure high-power kill, the "all creature types" clause allows this version of Elves to pick up [Wirewood Symbiote](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Symbiote)—as it's now an Elf—to break the "play this ability only once each turn" rule. This makes for massive mana production and life gain in concert with [Essence Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Warden).

Of the two Black-Blue 'Tron decks in the teams Top 4, I prefer Masashi Oiso's to our newly crowned Rookie of the Year's ... at least for an Elves-defined Extended metagame. Nicastri's deck is capable of a fast (turn three or so) Night of Soul's Betrayal (which can certainly put the brakes on Elves), but Oiso's interaction suite is much more varied and synergistic (again speaking to an Elves-defined Extended).

First of all we see four main-deck [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void). These can be set to one, making it impossible to play all those one mana [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel)s, [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid)s, and so on that make Elves so impossible to overcome. [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void) is also an artifact, so you can pitch it to [Thirst for Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge) if you don't want to play it.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

But the other big incentive to a Black 'Tron deck comes from not just Night of Soul's Betrayal (which Oiso's deck packs), but [Persecute](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persecute). With [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) and [Dimir Signet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dimir+Signet), Oiso's deck is capable of a fast [Persecute](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persecute), which can cripple many decks. We've seen how terrible Elves decks can look after eating one [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) ... But the whole hand?

What a cool deck!

Former U.S. National Champ Paul Cheon ran a deck reminiscent of Next Level Blue (from Extended) grafted onto LandStill (from Legacy). The result is a controlling deck that draws lots of cards and runs about three soft combinations.

The typical strategy of this deck is to control the board with conditional permission, and get in there with 2/2s: [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) and [Mishra's Factory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factory). Sounds simple, right? But those aren't actually very powerful things to say. This deck generates advantages with its soft combos.

**[Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) + [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance)**



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

The classic. Against an opponent with a tight array of mana costs, this combination can counter basically everything.

**[Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill) + [Mishra's Factory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factory) and [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland)**



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Paul's deck can go on the offense with [Mishra's Factory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factory) or disrupt the opponent's board with [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland)*without every having to play a spell*. On the play this combination can be an absolute nightmare for an opponent who did not play a threat on his first turn.

**[Phyrexian Dreadnought](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Dreadnought) + [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) and [Trickbind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trickbind)**



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

[Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) and [Trickbind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trickbind) are powerful reactive spells in the format due to the many "fetch" lands played from *Onslaught* ... plus they shut down storm combo decks. But in addition, you can use these spells to   
 remove the trigger on [Phyrexian Dreadnought](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Dreadnought) ... 12/12 for two anyone?

The first two soft combos generate the card advantage that lets a deck with conditional counterspells and mostly 2/2 creatures compete in a format full of, well, much more powerful effects. The last soft combo is the [Dread](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread)still deck's own powerful forward-moving play, allowing it to win in as few as two attacks!

This is one of the coolest decks I have seen in some time. The beater is [Magus of the Tabernacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magus+of+the+Tabernacle), which has a gigantic rear end for blocking, and can eliminate any and all opposing creatures provided there is no land ... Conveniently, the deck is full of land-eating [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon)s.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

The [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) defense is everywhere. [Flagstones of Trokair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flagstones+of+Trokair) gives the deck an immediate land back. [Crucible of Worlds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crucible+of+Worlds) works overtime, and also locks arms with [Dust Bowl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dust+Bowl) and [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland). Why [Mox Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Diamond) instead of [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox)? [Crucible of Worlds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crucible+of+Worlds) again ... They work as a team.

Over time, a soft lock of [Smokestack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smokestack) and [Crucible of Worlds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crucible+of+Worlds) can eat all the opponent's permanents but keep the Prison deck with *just enough* to stay not, you know, locked itself. In the mana-poor environment generated by this deck's spells and strategy, [Magus of the Tabernacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magus+of+the+Tabernacle) and [Ghostly Prison](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghostly+Prison) can keep this deck from ever being attacked successfully. Actual victory, though, will probably take a while.

This deck is very similar to CAL and Loam decks we have seen in Extended over the past couple of years. It has a nice quick kill with [Countryside Crusher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Countryside+Crusher) or [Terravore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terravore) into [Burning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) for [Devastating Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Dreams) ... Or it can play a longer game based on [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) card advantage and a [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) kill.

The one lift this deck gets over the Extended version is [Mox Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Diamond) (similar to Akihiro Takakuwa's [Crucible of Worlds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crucible+of+Worlds)) ... [Mox Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Diamond) has essentially no drawback in a [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) deck.

Michael Jacob's Black-White Heights deck ran all manner of token-generating creatures and spells, including [Marsh Flitter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Marsh+Flitter), [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger), [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession), and the ubiquitous [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom). These all generate three or more creatures immediately (save [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), which creates more than three creatures, but not immediately), making for superb synergy with [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Windbrisk+Heights)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights)Many top players are now touting [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) as "the best card in Standard," but with no access to red mana, Michael went with the classic Goldmane version of Ajani. This Planeswalker could increase the size and stature of his token creatures in much the same way as a [Glorious Anthem](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glorious+Anthem), turning his zillions of 1/1s into much more dangerous creatures.

The Black-White Heights deck is very rich in disruption. In addition to a quartet of [Tidehollow Sculler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidehollow+Sculler)s main, Michael ran a pair of [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize)s, with two more in the side. [Head Games](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Head+Games) is a card we haven't really seen since the 2007 U.S. National Championships, but that [Persecute](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persecute) stand-in, though more expensive, can really ruin the opponent's hand when it gets in.

All-in-all, a strong new deck that is *not* the same-old Fae and Five-Color Control, Kithkin and Demigod decks ... A nice change of pace that can compete with the top of the metagame.

I think that Black-White Heights (also called Black-White Tokens) is going to be an emerging strategy in Standard as it is quite good against the [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge)-based decks and plays loads of powerful synergies that give it game in many matchups. Five-Color Control decks have arguably the greatest flexibility of any decks in Standard at present due to their many Vivid lands and [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool)s—plus high mana counts—which allow them to play essentially any spells.

Case in point, Edel ran a red-white planeswalker, a blue planeswalker, a green-white two drop, and a triple-colored-mana blue-black six as his creatures in the same deck as both [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) and [Jund Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jund+Charm). The remarkable thing? No one bats an eyelash. That's just how these decks are set up.

Note the [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom)s in Willy's sideboard...
