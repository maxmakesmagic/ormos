Three for Three
===============



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on April 29, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






We join this Standard Pro Tour Qualifier season, already in progress ...

Three **Magic Online** PTQs down, three victories by White-Blue Tap-Out.

We haven't talked about Standard in several weeks. While I fully expect Jund to lead Top 8 appearances once paper **Magic** PTQs start getting tallied, the early leader—in particular in the one statistic that really matters—is White-Blue Tap-Out.

These decks are all about the creatures. All three PTQ-winning decks have four copies of [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) (we missed her, what with that Boss Naya lockout), [Knight of the White Orchid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid) (see below), and [Sphinx of Jwar Isle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx+of+Jwar+Isle) (largely as a concession to Jund).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td88_cross1.jpg)  
These white-blue decks are called "Tap-Out" because they play powerful cards on their own turns. In addition to the creature base, all these decks play about five planeswalkers—a two [Elspeth, Knight-Errant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant) / three [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) set—and a variety of ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20160829100442im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/x.gif) spells, [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup)s and [Mind Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring)s. Earlier I said that these white-blue decks are all about creatures .... That extends to their creature *suppression* as well, apparently. [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup) is a potentially backbreaking spell in that it can undo quite a bit of investment in mana and cards on the part of one player ... simultaneously deploying a dragon's worth of power serving the interests of the other. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td88_coupWithSoldiers.jpg)  
Caveat: Now while these decks did well with four total [Martial Coup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martial+Coup)s and [Mind Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Spring)s (on either a 1-3 or 2-2 split), don't be surprised to play against all eight.

\_ShipItHolla, who won the first PTQ, played two copies of [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) main-deck (which was down to one copy by the time \_\_SipItHolla won); both bolov0 and \_\_SipItHolla played two copies of [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate) main deck. The rest of the cards in these Tap-Out decks really are meant to be tapped out for on your own turn.

So what exactly are you tapping out for? 

The first action on the first turn might be [Fieldmist Borderpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fieldmist+Borderpost). [Fieldmist Borderpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fieldmist+Borderpost) is the glue that holds the White-Blue Tap-Out decks together. It both supplements the primary mana sources of these decks (or did you not scratch your head at only 22-23 lands in a deck chock full of 7+ mana costs?), but it hooks up with [Knight of the White Orchid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid) for card drawing and mana acceleration.

All three decks played between one and three copies of [Everflowing Chalice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice); again, this is just a "Signet" that helps ramp the deck to the seven mana ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20160829100442im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/x.gif) spell sorceries, or can help set up a third-turn [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) or planeswalker.

The mirror card on two mana—and arguably the more important one—is [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas). I feel like [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) is a Top 10 card in Standard, barely out of the Top 5, and arguably more central to the success of most blue decks than some of the obvious "bomb" banner-bearers. [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas), as a cantrip, helps these relatively land-poor decks draw into more lands; but, more importantly given the composition of the metagame, [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) can help slow down the Jund deck. It is that simple act of slowing down [Putrid Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech) by one turn by applying a [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) to [Savage Lands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savage+Lands) that helps to make white-blue decks viable in the first place.

Earlier versions of this deck, coming out of the Pro Tour in the hands of well known players like Mark Herberholz, Gabriel Nassif, Patrick Chapin, and Brian Kibler, played an additional engine: [Halimar Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Halimar+Depths) + [Treasure Hunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Hunt). [Treasure Hunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Hunt) would have been over [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas), with all the [Cancel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cancel)s and all four copies of [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor). One advantage that this strategy offers is a [Treasure Hunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Hunt)-driven mana advantage, making [Tectonic Edge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge) look better than in almost any other deck.

White-Blue Control decks are not the only ones packing [Celestial Colonnade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade) and [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor). A [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) / Bant strategy employing many of the same cards as the white-blue variants took drVendigo to an online PTQ finals.

The core main deck differences between Mythic and a "regular" Bant [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour) deck is, again, a focus on mana. Zvi's deck packs 27 lands and four more mana-producing creatures ([Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra), essentially over [Dauntless Escort](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dauntless+Escort)). 

Zvi's deck also goes a little bigger; note the [Rampaging Baloths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampaging+Baloths) and [Admonition Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Admonition+Angel)s (in the sideboard).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td88_balothWithBeasts.jpg)  
Playing against these decks is going to be relatively similar. The opponent is going to try to tax and overload your removal, and stick—and ultimately kill you with—a single big threat. In that sense [Rampaging Baloths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampaging+Baloths) is a perfect finisher. The opponent has already drawn out your removal capable of dealing with a big monster—you know, your [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring)s and [Doom Blade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade)s and what have you—with the intention of "killing you" with the, ahem, "last fatty."

One strategy that you may have missed—but that will probably be in force at the real-life PTQs due to its combination of relative budget-friendliness and fun factor—is Naya Allies.

Allies is a classic linear creature deck. Almost all the creatures are Allies. First turn [Hada Freeblade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hada+Freeblade) comes down on turn one; [Kazandu Blademaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kazandu+Blademaster) (always good) makes the Freeblade look really good. Then [Kabira Evangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kabira+Evangel) makes it look like the best of the Rebels back in 2000; no one blocks. At that point any Allies off the top will keep your squad punching through. 

You can of course substitute [Oran-Rief Survivalist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oran-Rief+Survivalist) or even [Harabaz Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Harabaz+Druid) as your two-drop, and use the Druid to play out lots of stuff (hopefully all pumping each other) ... but what about the non-Allies?



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Bloodbraid Elf | Ranger of Eos |

[Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) is ... [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf). Not an Ally, but good enough that we can cross our fingers and hope it flips over one of the good ones. [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) might be the even more interesting four mana non-Ally.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Hada Freeblade | Goblin Bushwhacker |

Of course you can grab a pair of [Hada Freeblade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hada+Freeblade)s for the double pump, but a more interesting one might be grabbing a [Goblin Bushwhacker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker), which would send everyone on your squad into the Red Zone, that turn. And because of the cumulative force that the Allies deck can generate in a single turn, giving all those new, +1/+1 counter-producing Allies haste *too* should make for a wild—usually lethal—answer to the classic Boros Bushwhacker finale.

While we approach this PTQ season already in progress, the online data is less than perfectly descriptive; after all, this is Eldrazi Week, and where are the 15/15s?

Patrick Dickman finished first at a National Qualifier with an Eldrazi-flavored look at Mythic. Mythic has always been about big creatures, but with *Rise of the Eldrazi* ... well ... now they are really big.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td88_lostAlaraArt.jpg)  
He replaced the some of the big pump effects / the top of the curve with an unusual threat card: [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara). At only 4/5 for six mana, this Spirit seems out of character for the usually-[Rampaging Baloths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampaging+Baloths)-packing Mythic strategy. So what's changed? For one thing, the card has exalted, which makes it at least interesting in the context of lost Rafiq or lost [Finest Hour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Finest+Hour). But the Eldrazi-appropriate final curtain to this article is the [Godo, Bandit Warlord](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Godo%2C+Bandit+Warlord)-like effect that [Sovereigns of Lost Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sovereigns+of+Lost+Alara) brings down, hammer-like, onto the battlefield.

Yes, that is [Eldrazi Conscription](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Conscription).

![Eldrazi Conscription](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Eldrazi+Conscription)  
Pretty nifty when you don't have to pay *anything* for it, isn't it?

That's +10/+10 *and* annihilator 2, and trample makes the +10/+10 chump-block-proof. In sum, the biggest deck seems to have gotten even bigger.

I hope to be able to report on some great new decks and unexpected victories next week, so good luck this weekend! (Yes, even those of you still playing Jund.)
