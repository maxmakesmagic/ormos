A Dredged Deck
==============



 Posted in **Serious Fun**
 on November 27, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/astybs_authorpic.png)
By Adam Styborski




 Stybs has played Magic the world over, writing and drafting as part of the event coverage team and slinging Commander everywhere his decks will fit. 







M**agic** is a collectible game. Whether you're trying to get every copy of a specific card you can find, filling out storage with complete sets, or heading to a Grand Prix to have an artist sign cards, building out the stash of cards you own is a challenge. It takes time, effort, patience, luck, and investment. 


 It's easy to forget that after spending years [gathering the cards I like into a handy box for deck building](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/game-breaker-2012-09-04) that there was ever a time I'd have to scrape, scour, and dredge enough for a deck. The human brain loves to take shortcuts, and mine often bypasses looking at lots of cards for "What's in the box?" 


 This is Golgari Week, meaning I have a good reason for going deep. Last week's [walkthrough of my process for building decks](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/minds-eye-2012-11-20) took the aforementioned cheat, using my Commander Box to let me focus on the mechanics of construction. Not all of you can take the shortcut. As GeekyDad [asked in the forums](http://community.wizards.com/go/thread/view/75842/29501923/11202012_SF:_The_Minds_Eye&post_num=5#526231391): 



> 
>  How do you build a deck if you don't have a bookcase full of cards, but only, say, a backpack full, with mainly commons and uncommons? Especially if Commander's supposed to be a casual format to get new (or returning) players into the game? 
> 
> 
> 


These are tough questions. Answering them requires getting hands dirty, suspending squeamishness, and being honest with ourselves. I'm on board for all three.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf223_ghave_guru_of_spores_2.jpg)
[Ghave, Guru of Spores](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghave%2C+Guru+of+Spores) | Art by [James Paick](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22James%20Paick%22%5D)



### What are we Answering?


Before we get too far ahead of ourselves, let's look at what I'm trying to solve for building a Commander deck:


* How can we use more commons and uncommons to full effect?
* How can we show newer players, using newer cards from newer sets, how to get in?

These, put another way, are restrictions. They will limit what players can do, and how they can do it. Translating these into specifics:


* "Mainly commons and uncommons" I took to mean without the splashy, powerful rares that are harder to find and acquire. I used the decklists and games featured in [recent Grand Prix events coverage](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Events.aspx) as a guide for what's hot and desirable as rares to absolutely avoid. I also looked at preconstructed decks and promotional cards from Prerelease events to see what may be more abundant.
* "New (or returning) players" I took to mean those who only have access to a small, recently released pool of cards—or, in other words, [Standard legal](http://www.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=judge/resources/sfrstandard).

These limits are not small challenges. Commander decks usually carry 60–70 unique cards if you're using basic lands as the majority of your mana base. Currently, Standard has 1,139 nonbasic cards to choose from, so 65 cards is almost 6% of everything eligible. And it gets worse.


 Commander restricts the color identify of cards to what your commander has. This means your choice of commander affects your options outright. If you want to check out Standard to build a deck around [Mikaeus, the Lunarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mikaeus%2C+the+Lunarch), your options drop to fewer than 300—or more than of 22% of Standard-legal cards with a white or colorless color identity. 


 Since I believe choosing your commander is so important, and something that's desirable to pick from outside of Standard, I decided on a three-color commander from last year's ***Magic: The Gathering** Commander*  release: [Ghave, Guru of Spores](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghave%2C+Guru+of+Spores). Why [Ghave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghave)? 


![Ghave, Guru of Spores](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ghave%2C+Guru+of+Spores)
  
* Of the three color pairs in [Ghave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghave) (green-white, white-black, and green-black), two are featured in *Return to Ravnica*.
* Green gives us access to many ways to find lands and fix our mana, two things the color does naturally and helps us work our deck in games.
* Black and white give us ways to brings things back from the graveyard. Recycling our "best" cards is a smart play anywhere in **Magic**.


[Ghave, Guru of Spores](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghave%2C+Guru+of+Spores) is just for an example. The commander you choose can be built around in the same way. 


### How are We Answering?


 The process for building can be as simple as what I like to do: grab the piles of cards you already have and start pulling out what you want to use. For today, I followed a more complex route: I pulled up every card in every set in Standard, looked over what it did, and [wrote down every card I liked and why](https://www.dropbox.com/s/v2o46sw3dnnss3q/CMDR.txt). If you want to see the raw work, it's there, but we'll make sense of it below. 


 The only way to pull together a deck from scraps is to understand what all of the cards you have can do for you. Thanks to [Limited lunches](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/wacky-winchester-2012-07-17), I had a handle on some of the lesser-seen cards in Standard inasmuch as I'd tried using them before. By gathering up [different groups of cards classified](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/fun-numbers-2012-07-31) in similar ways, we can [assemble a deck through modules of effects](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/angels-wings-2011-07-26), all while minimizing the rares we'd want to include. 


Let's get started.


The first thing I wanted to get out of the way was which rares would be the easiest to ensure we had for the deck.


* [Moonsilver Spear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moonsilver+Spear), [Corpsejack Menace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corpsejack+Menace), and [Grove of the Guardian](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grove+of+the+Guardian) are all Prerelease promos, ensuring these are cards we already have or can find among friends.
* [Sunpetal Grove](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunpetal+Grove) has been reprinted in the core set each year since its debut in **Magic** 2010. Tracking one down is reasonable, and I encourage  [good mana to ensure good **Magic**](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/tribute-2012-09-25).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/sf/sf223_rares.jpg)
*[Moonsilver Spear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moonsilver+Spear), [Corpsejack Menace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corpsejack+Menace), [Grove of the Guardian](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grove+of+the+Guardian), [Sunpetal Grove](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunpetal+Grove)*

 I can't think of an easier set of rares to find. The question is whether the rest of the deck could be built without more. [Ghave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghave) is forgiving and gave us multiple themes to work with: 


* Tokens—lots of little 1/1 green [Saproling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saproling)s—can help swarm a battlefield.
* +1/+1 counters—things that make and use them—are handy.
* Sacrifice—packing in our own creatures—can lead to good things.

This isn't your typical Commander deck. In fact, I'm not very happy with where we ended up. I like a lot more creatures than what we found, but with enough mana we can rely on Ghave, Guru of Spores to help us through. There's plenty of removal, which should help keep the game moving slower. Our plan of attack is going to be tough since we want our commander in play all the time, but fighting the bigger guys while getting our mana going will let us explode over a turn or two.


What the deck can do isn't up for debate, but how well it works is another tale. The problem with restrictions is that they limit with absolutes what is possible. We don't have fancy Dragons and dominating Demons. We don't have battlefield-clearing spells to reset the playing field. We don't have the cards that are even better in the themes and goals we've set out.


What we do have is a coherent deck with ways to interact with opponents. We can disrupt their plans. We can break up the cards holding us back. We can wait to surge our resources when the time is right. What we didn't set out to do was maintain parity with decks without these restrictions. It's not reasonable to ask the same power and might from cards that aren't the rarer, splashier varieties.


 The comparison I imagine is the difference between a machine built for mass production (the usual Commander decks one sees) and a [Rube Goldberg machine](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rube_Goldberg_machine) (what we've created here). Given time and stability, we can engineer something marvelous. In many other games, our pieces just won't fall into place. 


 Can rares spice things up and help our deck perform better? Yes and no. Going back further in time will yield more commons and uncommons that help. From ***Magic** 2012* , the previous core set: 


* [Spirit Mantle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spirit+Mantle) makes any creatures unblockable.
* [Diabolic Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Tutor) finds anything we want.
* [Reassembling Skeleton](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reassembling+Skeleton) is an engine with Ghave.
* [Jade Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jade+Mage) is another way to produce lots of tokens.
* [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) is the ancestor to [Farseek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek).
* [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) lets us smash for a ton given the tokens.
* [Manalith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manalith) is another artifact to fix mana with.
* [Swiftfoot Boots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swiftfoot+Boots) combines the best of [Ring of Valkas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ring+of+Valkas) and [Mask of Avacyn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mask+of+Avacyn).

Ultimately, it's up to you and your play group to determine if decks built like today's are enough to get by, or if joining forces to find more cards and level out everyone's decks together makes more sense. We can't find the true unknown of the entire world of Commander decks, but we can build a machine to help us get there.
