Unveiling Extended and Other Stories
====================================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on October 18, 2007 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






The skies parted. The venue flooded. Graveyards got washed away. The Pro Tour went on. 

![Flood](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/ptval07/flood_5.jpg)By now most of you know about the rainstorm that hit [Pro Tour–Valencia](/en/events/coverage/fortier-reigns-spain). As a fan of the game, I was actually heartbroken (okay, "heartbroken" is maybe overstating it but you get the picture) waking up on Friday morning only to learn that I would have to wait another 24 hours before finding out whether or not boogeyman Dredge got the Pro Tour... or at least Day One. 

It turns out that while Dredge was one of the most popular decks in the tournament, it did not make the break to Top 8. Reason prevailed, hatred bared its pointed fangs, and graveyards everywhere were emptied before they could perpetrate *too much* mischief. Bill Stark wrote a very informative breakdown of the Valencia metagame for the official tournament coverage; you can read it [here](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/extended-metagame-breakdown-and-rogue-decks-2007-10-14).

### CounterTopGoyf – Remi Fortier

At the end of the abridged two day tournament, the top deck was a tuned look at CounterTopGoyf, one of the most popular decks on **Magic Online**, "first place" in more than one week of online analysis.

This deck does not differ significantly from the decks we have seen previously. Fortier's deck plays a little more land (which, from my own testing, is exactly what it needed), and three copies of [Threads of Disloyalty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Threads+of+Disloyalty) starting; on the other hand, Remi played only two copies of [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) in his main or anywhere. Going a little old school, the Valencia champ ran four copies of the classic, [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell), but none of the new kid, [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare).

The key to this deck is [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top). The Top is probably the deck's most important bounty by way of [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage), a complement to both [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) and [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant). With [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) online, [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) does a lot less collateral damage. At the same time, [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) and [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) is a soft lock... at least against decks without extravagant mana costs.

### Graveyard Resistance

Main deck: 3 [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) + 1 [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt); Crypt recursion via [Academy Ruins](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Academy+Ruins)  
 Sideboard: 2 [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt)

### [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) – Andre Müller

Another popular archetype in the tournament was Enduring Ideal. Most Enduring Ideal decks seen in Extended over the past year have been hybrid decks including the [Draco](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Draco) + [Erratic Explosion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Erratic+Explosion) combination or [Balancing Act](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Balancing+Act), but finalist Andre Müller ran pure, straight acceleration-into-enchantments.

Enduring Ideal plays numerous mana-ramping cards, like [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom), [Pentad Prism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pentad+Prism), [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song), or just its lands, many of which explode for more than one mana. Andre used the extra mana to make it to his normally prohibitively expensive signature card, [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal). With epic online, the [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) deck can with the game from multiple different angles.

![Solitary Confinement](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Solitary+Confinement)One plan would be to lock out the game with [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement). Stuck under epic, Andre wouldn't "need" his card per turn anyway... Then again, stuck under [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement), he wouldn't be getting one. Enter [Honden of Seeing Winds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honden+of+Seeing+Winds). One card feeds the other; most decks can't bust through a Confinement, and [Form of the Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Form+of+the+Dragon) wins in four or fewer. [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) is another lock card. With [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) in play, you can't really break out of [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) unless you play with very specific cards like [Ronom Unicorn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ronom+Unicorn)... even when you "have" the answer. Note the synergies between [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) and Müller's sideboarded copies of [Boseiju, Who Shelters All](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boseiju%2C+Who+Shelters+All)... Boseiju can force through [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) against permission... But there is always the question of being raced while a counter deck answers subsequent epic copies; the answer is to just go and get [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) first. Another option is to drop [Dovescape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dovescape) before [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal)... You can follow up playing spells *using* [Boseiju, Who Shelters All](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boseiju%2C+Who+Shelters+All). You get both the doves *and* the spell.Enduring Ideal is a very interesting gap choice for Extended. Müller's build runs many of the cards that make elite deck TEPS "go" ... but it is not badly hosed by anti-storm measures. It is a fast combo deck, if not the fastest, but it is immune to the graveyard hate that defined much of Valencia's anti-combo resource dedication. Subtly, the mere presence of [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) helped to defend against cards like [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), normally poison for combo when played in a fast enough deck.

The advantage of the previous default Domain Aggro deck was Gaea's Might, specifically Gaea's Might aimed at a [Boros Swiftblade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Swiftblade) ("take twelve"). This version only has one way to specifically exploit the many faces of its mana base (specifically that [Steam Vents](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steam+Vents)), [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames). The rest of the colors in Koike's deck actually just, well... They actually play cards, cast spells, etc. Red makes [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer). Green kicks [Kird Ape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape) and enables [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). Black is there for [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) and [Vindicate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate), white for the other half of the latter.

Domain Aggro is very versatile and high powered. Most specifically, it has nearly the punch of an actual red deck, but gets to compliment [Firebolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firebolt) with [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames) ("take five") and [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) (basically the best card of its class and cost ever printed).

Koike's version of Domain Aggro does not play [Savannah Lions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savannah+Lions), so he has less of a chance to run the optimal aggro opening (three two-power creatures on the second turn), but [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) is just a great and versatile card... Especially as it can stifle [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below).

### Gifts Rock – Tine Rus

It's The Rock; he plays [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven)! Midrange control plus one of the best card drawing / selection instants in the history of the game makes for a deck capable of dealing with basically any type of opponent. The addition of [Collective Restraint](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Collective+Restraint) is a powerful one. On a fast draw, Rus can hit that enchantment on the third turn, flat out winning numerous matchups, and at least slowing [Dredge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dredge) down.

The presence of multiple types of tutor / engine cards gives Rus the ability to find any card in his deck, even a singleton, often at instant speed. For example, [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) for [Eternal Witness](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness), [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish), and [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate) will basically ensure that you get [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate) because you will either get the [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate) itself, or the Witness (Witness proxy [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish)).

Well, now it has a superb finisher that happens to cost two mana (as does almost any other deck that wants to play it). 

As for the rest, Barra's deck is a pretty straightforward update to the classic Jelger Wiergsma / Sol Malka favorite... a couple of creatures, some cheap discard, a fair amount of creature elimination, four [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed)s... a classic, as I said. White comes by way of the *Onslaught* / *Ravnica* Block mana marriage.

*Is* [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) the greatest creature of all time? What other card has reached its massive level of demand in the secondary market *while legal for Standard?* It forces black-blue to play green and fits effortlessly into a deck known for its large amount of colorless mana costs.

[Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) is particularly good in Affinity... The lands are almost all artifacts, the creatures are artifacts, half the artifacts are all jumping into the graveyard by themselves... Then there's the one-mana sorceries, defensive enchantments, &c. Stein didn't play it, but [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) seems very good with [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)... It's kind of seven damage.

It's great to see the best aggressive archetype of all time near the top again.

These decks harness the mana production of [Urza's Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Mine), [Urza's Power Plant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Power+Plant), and [Urza's Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Tower) to play extravagant threats like [Sundering Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sundering+Titan) and [Mindslaver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mindslaver). On the way, they play U/W control, using light permission ([Condescend](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condescend) and/or [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand), which actually help dig up the UrzaTron) and board control to get to their end games.

While these two decks had quite a bit in common (including main deck graveyard resistance in four copies of [Tolaria West](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tolaria+West) to get one copy of [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt), [Tolaria West](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tolaria+West) also being pretty good at assembling the 'Tron), but what strikes me, especially as Mihara and Nakamura had some playtest partners in common, is how different their implementations ended up. Mihara ran three [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand)s... Shuhei none at all! [Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction) or [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven)? They cost the same, they do *kind of* the same things... but these two instants play like night and day. 

### A Lesson from MTGO

For the first part of this week's MTGO section, I will make an ass out of you and me... well *me* anyway. You know what they say about what happens when you *assume* something! Last week I credited a deck played by one PVDDR to Fan Favorite Paulo Vitor Damo da Rosa (I know, how could I?); however it has come to my attention that PVDDR is *actually* former JSS winner Arthur Stewart. Sorry!

Here's something interesting... I have been lamenting the lack of good life gain in Standard recently. We have gotten spoiled on our [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch)s, [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix)es, and [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel)s. [Brion Stoutarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brion+Stoutarm) and [Knight of Meadowgrain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Meadowgrain) seem pretty awesome, but they can go into a comparatively narrow number of decks... and not really the decks that need the life gain as much. Look at the *Ravnica* counterparts: [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch) was great in green-white creature decks like Ghazi-Glare... and better in midrange control decks. [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) made Boros great and Zoo less painful... but allowed Angel decks to dominate creatures. Is there a solution?

Last week's fifth place in the metagame chart by Solar Flare came largely on the back of this deck, by t\_shrk. It must be good; t\_shrk and his deck were present in the first *three* PE Top 8s that I tallied for last week's article. See anything interesting, clever, or out of place for the archetype?

### When Strategy Goes Wrong

In lieu of the short strategy and tactics lessons that with which we have been ending Swimming With Sharks, this week I am just going to put something out there inspired by a conversation I had last week with my friend Greg Weiss, on the subject of strange sideboard card interactions. Probably the biggest conceptual leap a player makes going from complete novice to being capable of winning in the competitive tournament setting is understanding that he has to have a *plan* (this is the nature of *strategy*, specifically, in **Magic**). This is actually easiest for combo players because they can only win in a very finite number of ways, but it applies to every deck playing interactively, too. You need to know what cards in what order you have to draw and sculpt in order to win. The next leap (and this is actually much harder to make because figuring out how to play strategically gives most players such a positive delta that they actually entrench themselves, in some cases becoming *too* single-minded) is to understand that top-level **Magic** play requires fluidity, rapid adaptation, and throwing the plan or unique cards out the window when they cease performing (for example, PT–Osaka winner Ken Ho ran [Still Life](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Still+Life) in his Blue-Green versus [Mutilate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mutilate), but by the end of the tournament stopped siding it in, having figured out a superior way to beat Mono-Black mid-tournament). 

Open your imagination up a little as you consider this card: 

![Price of Glory](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Price+of+Glory)This could be pretty scary for blue, right? Forcing [Price of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Price+of+Glory) into play early enough puts a big hurt on a blue player's ability to defend himself with [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell). That's the idea, anyway. This card gets increasingly less good the later in the game you are able to stick it. Moreover, there is the problem of Blue knowing what you are planning to sideboard... What happens when a blue player sideboards this right back?

![Sacred Ground](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sacred+Ground)No, no. Think *harder*. Let me help you with this one:

![Ghitu Fire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ghitu+Fire)If your opponent has [Price of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Price+of+Glory) in play, and you have [Sacred Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sacred+Ground) in play, and you have [Ghitu Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghitu+Fire) in your hand... He's pretty much dead. You can tap a land for mana, bin it to [Price of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Price+of+Glory), get it back with [Sacred Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sacred+Ground), rinse and repeat, until you have sufficient mana to kick it lethally, instant-style. 

A more recent example is this card:

![Twincast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Twincast)A green mage would work so hard to entwine his seven nine-mana spell, probably even dropping [Boseiju, Who Shelters All](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boseiju%2C+Who+Shelters+All) along the way in a sideboarded game, in order to waltz right past a hand full of counterspells. The problem is that [Twincast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twincast) doesn't *counter* a spell, it copies it. What many [Tooth and Nail](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) players learned—the hard way—is that many blue mages would devote their sideboards to anti–[Tooth and Nail](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) measures. All of a sudden Uyo, the Silent Prophet would appear to copy yet another "uncounterable" [Tooth and Nail](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail), or [Triskelion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Triskelion) and [Mephidross Vampire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mephidross+Vampire) would wipe the board. Very messy.

Many times, as players—human—players, we get excited about drawing a sideboard card and put all of our eggs into that basket, thinking "I can't lose now!" I know that I've thought those very words more than once... and found myself experiencing the wrong side of the unpredictable when I didn't see the opponent's foil.

Consider the latest incarnation of the sideboard auto-win...

![Manabarbs](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Manabarbs)This card is awesome, right? It helped Greg Poverelli to [win this year's MSS](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/mss-finals-blood-martyr-2007-07-29) in Baltimore. It strikes fear into the hearts of wizards of midrange analysis and consistently gets a good red deck close *enough* to finish a game*.* Like Price of Victory, though, the dream of completely taking out the color blue or a ponderous control deck can turn into a nightmare quickly if the opponent plays the wrong cards.
