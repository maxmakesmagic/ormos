By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic."

In PTQ **Magic**, information is king. You will often see huge swings in player trends from week to week, such that over the course of a single season, the decks that do well in the first weeks look nothing like those that win the fifth- or sixth-week PTQs. This resource can help you track what decks will be popular, what you have to prepare to beat, and what kinds of decks have shown viability around the country.


For example, last week, Brian's column unveiled the "Teen Titans" deck, a brand new deck that had a powerful impact in Week Two of this season. Without this little page, you might have never heard of Goblin Welder Reanimator, whereas given that knowledge, Goblin Welder decks made a big splash at…


### #3 Grand Prix–Boston


(Newest news) Grand Prix–Boston was an event that grew into a story that had little story kids, it was so interesting. Basically the coolest part was the Japanese invasion of an American Grand Prix. At first this was surprising, until you dial back the fact that some years ago, it was common practice for American pros to storm foreign Grand Prix and steal money spots. Brian Hacker, Randy Buehler, and even a couple of the Deadguys would routinely score Top 8 finishes on the international level, while Alex Shvartsman's legendary Top 8 count was built almost entirely on his willingness to travel. Now that many of **Magic's** top superstars are from Japan, is it any surprise that they are willing to stretch across oceans to compete on American soil?


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/gpbost05/fin.jpg)*Masashi Oiso took the Boston trophy back to Japan.*If there's one thing that I know about this Extended format, it's that no one knows what deck is best, or even what kind of deck. Over the course of 11 or so rounds in Boston, I played against the same deck only one time! If your opponent plays [Polluted Delta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta) and [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), he could be not only Mind's Desire, Psychatog, or Reanimator, but [Aluren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aluren)… and [Underground River](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underground+River) might make him Affinity!


So anyway, on Day One, the question came up, "what deck is going to win this Grand Prix?" Gabe Walls, a top U.S. Nationals and Worlds competitor joked "whatever Masashi Oiso is playing, because he's the best." Oiso showed that he really is the best and [took his Aluren to the No. 1 spot](/en/events/coverage/japan-strikes-again). Believe the hype.


Oiso's victory was far from certain, as his back was up against the wall going into the Top 8. In Game 3 of the last round of Swiss, Masashi was forced to mulligan to five against John Fiorello with Mind's Desire. After a few turns, Oiso played a [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed) with his [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) and [City of Brass](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=City+of+Brass), leaving up [Havenwood Battleground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Havenwood+Battleground). You can often tell a lot about a player's skill by how he taps his mana, and this choice was a signal to Fiorello that Oiso could defend against a combo kill, because he could break the Battleground to Deed for two to destroy any [Sapphire Medallion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sapphire+Medallion) or [Sunscape Familiar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscape+Familiar) mid-combo.


Fiorello forced the issue the next turn by sequencing [Snap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snap) and [Cloud of Faeries](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloud+of+Faeries); Masashi traded his [Havenwood Battleground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Havenwood+Battleground), [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed), and [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) for an enabling [Sapphire Medallion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sapphire+Medallion), stopping a potential combo kill (or was it just a bait?). This play, which may have been what Fiorello wanted to be honest, left Oiso with only a [City of Brass](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=City+of+Brass) and [Volrath's Stronghold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volrath%27s+Stronghold) to Fiorello's far superior board.


And Oiso came back.


[Volrath's Stronghold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volrath%27s+Stronghold) eventually allowed Oiso to not just survive a [Brain Freeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze), but use his graveyard to increase his virtual hand size by dozens of cards. Under pressure from a flurry of Cabal Therapies, Fiorello's relatively large -- but not lethal -- [Brain Freeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze) was designed to strip all the [Aluren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aluren)s out of Oiso's deck… but John had no was of knowing Masashi had been holding one since the beginning of the game. Oiso carefully put a [Raven Familiar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven+Familiar) and [Cavern Harpy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy) back on top of his deck, allowing him to set up his combo and win in short order. Even after nearly 10 years of playing around top-level **Magic**, I found Oiso's composure and decision-making in this match humbling.

I distinctly remember being told that Lucas was playing Cephalid Breakfast, but every time I walked by him, he seemed to have Life combo pieces in play. The reason? Lucas was playing *both* decks!


Cephalid Breakfast seeks to play a [Cephalid Illusionist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cephalid+Illusionist) and a [Nomads en-Kor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nomads+en-Kor). The [Nomads en-Kor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nomads+en-Kor) targets the [Cephalid Illusionist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cephalid+Illusionist) multiple times, until its controller's library is completely depleted by the Illusionist's triggered ability. At that point, [Krosan Reclamation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Reclamation) is flashed to put [Exhume](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exhume) and [Reanimate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reanimate) back as the only cards in the deck, while [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) serves the dual purpose of removing the [Cephalid Illusionist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cephalid+Illusionist) from play and clearing the opponent's hand of resistance. It is key to remove the Illusionist because if the opponent targets it, the Cephalid Breakfast player will lose his [Exhume](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exhume) and [Reanimate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reanimate), such that he is unable to complete the combo.


[Exhume](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exhume) or [Reanimate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reanimate) will then set up [Sutured Ghoul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sutured+Ghoul), which leeches on (traditionally two copies of) [Krosan Cloudscraper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Cloudscraper); [Dragon Breath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragon+Breath) provides haste, and 20+ points of immediate trample come crashing through the Red Zone.


Glavin's deck sort of notices that Cephalid Breakfast and Life have a lot in common. Both are creature-based combination decks that use similar creatures and mechanics to set up their wins. While Lucas may be playing a slightly inferior Cephalid Breakfast deck he is *also* playing a slightly inferior Life deck!


 



![Krosan reclamation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Krosan+Reclamation)

Cephalid Breakfast is mediocre against Red Deck Wins (because of that deck's ability to burn a [Cephalid Illusionist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cephalid+Illusionist)) . . . but Life is great against Red! Osyp Lebedowicz had Masahiko Morita dead to rights in the last round of the Swiss . . . but then drew with him because the Pro Tour–Venice champion *wanted* Life in the Top 8. While Osyp's deck is powerful against green-white Life, his match with Glavin showed vulnerability to the other side of Lucas's combo deck. If you think about it, it makes sense: Osyp wants to run Lucas out of cards . . . Lucas *also* wants to run Lucas out of cards! Unless Mind's Desirecan set up a timely [Deep Analysis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis), decking Cephalid Breakfast, with its powerful [Krosan Reclamation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Reclamation), simply sets up a Ghoul kill the next turn.

Once again, the card that makes all of this possible is [Aether Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial). Morita's Life deck cheats [Meddling Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meddling+Mage) and [Gilded Drake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilded+Drake) into play with the ubiquitous Vial, and Lucas's deck uses it to fix the mana in an abominable base that includes [City of Brass](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=City+of+Brass), [Forbidden Orchard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Orchard), [Tarnished Citadel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarnished+Citadel) . . . and not a single basic. [Aether Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial) has at this point gone from being just in Affinity to joining the Goblins to making Life more resilient, to just playing a mana-fixing [Mana Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Vault) in the great combo deck tradition.


Like Morita's blue creatures, other decks from GP–Boston also showed small innovations with big impacts. [Unspeakable Symbol](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unspeakable+Symbol) lets B/W Life win a turn faster than [Test of Endurance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Test+of+Endurance) in G/W Life. Osyp out-gamed the metagame by playing no copies of [Sphere of Law](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+Law) in his Desire sideboard. The theory goes like this: Mind's Desire is faster than Red Deck Wins; Red Deck Wins sideboards [Pyrostatic Pillar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrostatic+Pillar) to beat Mind's Desire; [Sphere of Law](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+Law) trumps [Pyrostatic Pillar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrostatic+Pillar), so Red Deck Wins says "hey, it was a good idea while it lasted," and dumps the Pillar tech because it expects to lose to [Sphere of Law](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+Law); [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) ignores [Sphere of Law](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphere+of+Law) because it is faster than Red Deck Wins not playing [Pyrostatic Pillar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrostatic+Pillar); no one plays either sideboard card and we are back to “Mind's Desire is faster than Red Deck Wins.” Got that?


My favorite part about the Boston Top 8, though, has got to be Daniel O'Mahoney-Schwartz's triumphant return with "the never-changing face of" blue-green Madness. Dan is a Generation 1.5 pro from New York's Golden Age. He is brother to legendary Rochester Draft champion Steve O'Mahoney-Schwartz, and a former Antarctica teammate of the great Jon Finkel. Seeing Dan win, and win with slightly older cards printed before the defining powerhouse [Exalted Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel)s, [Aether Vial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial)s, and [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox)es, brought a smile to my face. I cheered for Dan up until his squashing at the hands -- or is it giant feet? -- of Keith McLaughlin's [Sundering Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sundering+Titan)s. It is heartwarming to see a (now) Boston resident show up to his local Grand Prix with the cards that he had three years ago take names all the way to the Top 8!
