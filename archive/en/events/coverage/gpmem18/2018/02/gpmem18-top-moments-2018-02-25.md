Top Stories of Grand Prix Memphis 2018
======================================



 Posted in **Event Coverage**
 on February 26, 2018 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/chapman.jpg)
By Chapman Sim











With Standard all shook up, 1082 players arrived at the Memphis Cook Convention Center for a weekend of Standard. Only 217 made it through to Day 2, and only one stood tall at the very end. Let's take a look at the top stories of Grand Prix Memphis and relive the weekend in a matter of minutes.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2018/events/gpmem18/gpmem18_highlights_small.jpg)


### Day 2 Metagame Breakdown


217 players made Day 2 of Grand Prix Memphis, and it was clear that the Standard metagame was incredibly diverse, allaying concerns resulting from the recent bans. With the overly-dominant decks nerfed, the playing field was leveled for other archetypes to emerge. There were over 30 strategies represented, and most of them had distinct game plans.

First things first, there were a lot of [Hazoret the Fervent](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hazoret+the+Fervent)s and [The Scarab God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Scarab+God)s out there. Players looking to attack the metagame will need to have a plan for these key cards. [Vraska's Contempt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vraska%27s+Contempt) was one card that was very well-positioned, as was [Ixalan's Binding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ixalan%27s+Binding). Mono-Red aggro was the most dominant deck in Day 2 by far. There were also 23 Black-Red Aggro decks, some of them merely splashing for [Scrapheap Scrounger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scrapheap+Scrounger)s and [Unlicensed Disintegration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unlicensed+Disintegration)s, while others had [Hour of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hour+of+Glory), [Dread Wanderer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Wanderer), and [Bone Picker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bone+Picker). Even if we grouped these decks, that's still less than 30% of the entire metagame.


[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hazoret+the+Fervent)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hazoret+the+Fervent)[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=The+Scarab+God)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Scarab+God)
As for the Blue-Black players, there are two easy ways to tell them apart. They're either playing with a lot of counterspells and removal or eschewing those for more threats. Grixis Energy was the next most popular archetype. Since players weren't allowed to pair [Whirler Virtuoso](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whirler+Virtuoso) with [Rogue Refiner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rogue+Refiner), they decided to hold hands with [Glint-Sleeve Siphoner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glint-Sleeve+Siphoner) instead.


Moving down the list, we see a refreshing blend of old and new. Mardu Vehicles, Grixis Control, Pummeller, and Vampires were not unfamiliar archetypes, but Monsters and White-Black City's Blessing were fresh, and only made possible with numerous cards from *Rivals of Ixalan*. Then, there was also a cluster of Ramp decks featuring [Mastermind's Acquisition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mastermind%27s+Acquisition), to go over the top. Isn't having such a diverse Standard metagame beyond your [Wildest Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildest+Dreams)?


### Cool Decks, Cool Plays


Now that we've showcased the wide variety of Standard decks let's take a closer look at some of the more unique ones. All these players might be wielding unorthodox weapons, but they were all potent enough to make it through to Day 2.


Chris Botelho calls himself a “deck building enthusiast," and he continued to delight us with his masterpiece. There were other versions of Mastermind Ramp, but his version aimed to win with [Approach of the Second Sun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Approach+of+the+Second+Sun) instead. If you're trying to ramp your way into big spells, you might as well have a copy of [The Immortal Sun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Immortal+Sun) or [Torment of Hailfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Torment+of+Hailfire) in your sideboard.


Or, [Zacama, Primal Calamity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zacama%2C+Primal+Calamity).

Zhou Zirui was fresh off a team [Grand Prix win at Indianapolis](https://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/gpind18) and continued his chase for Pro Points here in Memphis. Showing up with something genuinely unconventional, he surprised everyone with his tribal deck.


The plane of Ixalan was populated by Merfolk, Pirates, Dinosaurs, and Vampires, so the Artificers of Kaladesh was not a tribe which was on anyone's radar. Perhaps that was why he was able to shock and awe.


[Inventor's Goggles](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inventor%27s+Goggles) can be tapped to help cast [Metallic Rebuke](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Metallic+Rebuke) and [Maverick Thopterist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maverick+Thopterist) before jumping onto any of his Artificers, and he even had the combo of [Decoction Module](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Decoction+Module) and [Whirler Virtuoso](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whirler+Virtuoso) for potentially an infinite amount of Thopters. He didn't need that many though. [Saheeli Rai](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saheeli+Rai) copying [Maverick Thopterist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maverick+Thopterist) was sweet enough to seal the deal!

### 20 Archetypes in the Top 32


After fifteen rounds had ended, we further analyzed the Top 32 results, while also posting all these [Top 32 Decklists](https://edit.magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/gpmem18/gpmem18-9-32-decklists-2018-02-25) for your perusal. The first observation was the unprecedented number of archetypes in the Top 32. Five archetypes made the Top 8, but fifteen other archetypes made Top 32! That makes a total of twenty unique strategies, which were not only viable but also competitive. As we close our curtains in Memphis, it's worth noting that this picture of Standard is a welcome sight, and it seems that even a month into this new format, diversity is here to stay.

The Grand Prix was the ultimate test that even hundreds of Magic Online leagues can't provide, and the Standard format delivered in spades here in Memphis. Mono-Red was considered one of the top archetypes entering the event, but it seems players came ready and prepared to answer [Hazoret the Fervent](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hazoret+the+Fervent). From [Ixalan's Binding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ixalan%27s+Binding) to [Sunscourge Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscourge+Champion) to even [Hour of Glory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hour+of+Glory), the dominance of the red deck failed to materialize in Tennessee.

After fifteen rounds of *Rivals of Ixalan* Standard, the field of 1082 was reduced to just one. Cruising through the Swiss Rounds and steamrolling through the Top 8, it was Tyler Schroeder who towered above all with his Red-Green Monsters deck.  
  

Overruning Aaron Barich in the finals with a horde of resilient beaters, he also dispatched Matthew Kling in the semifinals, and even had to get past Pro Tour Hall of Famer Eric Froehlich in the quarterfinals.
