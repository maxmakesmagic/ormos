Detour
======



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 16, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







With the Pro Tour coming up on the American West Coast [this weekend](http://www.wizards.com/magic/tcg/events.aspx?x=mtg/event/protour/dragonsmaze13), a greater-than-normal number of top players chose to take a detour to Portland, Oregon, for last weekend's [Modern Grand Prix](/en/events/coverage/its-pardee-time). As fans of the game, we got to enjoy the triumphs and heartache of some of the world's greatest players on a pretty big stage... in anticipation for **Magic**'s greatest stage.


Portlandia wasn't the only detour, of course; Modern itself is a detour of sorts from the standard of Standard (and the Pro Tour itself is going to be *Return to Ravnica* Block Constructed). In this foray back to Modern we got to see Pro Tour Champions and Hall of Famers battling with some of their favorite strategies, although it was Sam Pardee and his Melira-Pod deck that took down the whole event.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td247_chart.jpg)### Melira-Pod


Melira-Pod was the big deck of this tournament; it put three players into the Top 8, including Grand Prix Portland winner Sam Pardee.


Melira-Pod is an infinite combo deck but can also play as a value midrange deck. Like other The Rock-esque midrange decks, it plays good creatures like [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) and [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), and flexible removal or disruption like [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) (Dan MacDonald) or [Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay) (Nass and Pardee).

But like a combo deck it plays weird cards—specifically creature cards here—that are not particularly popular in other strategies but work well in the context of a [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling)/[Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) deck designed to assemble a specific sequence of cards.


There are many potential routes to victory for Melira-Pod, but the core incentive is the interaction between [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast) and persist. Melira prevents -1/-1 counters from being put on your creatures, so when a persist creature like [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) or [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap) comes back from the dead... it does so freshly, rather than with a -1/-1 counter on it.

Consider a fairly common sequence of [Viscera Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer) (one mana) → [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast) (two mana) → [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) (three mana).


When all three creatures are on the battlefield, you can sacrifice the [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) to [Viscera Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer) to scry... as many times as you like (the [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) will never lose its ability to persist as it never gets any -1/-1 counters). You can gain essentially infinite life because you can do this over and over and over again and/or set up the top of your deck so your four-mana play is [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap)... and then you can rinse and repeat but instead brain the opponent for 20 instead of "merely" gaining tons and tons and tons of life.


Cards like [Cartel Aristocrat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cartel+Aristocrat) or [Bloodthrone Vampire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodthrone+Vampire) give Melira-Pod redundancy at different mana points, which can be useful because this is a [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) deck. You want to be able to chain up to different mana points to find combo pieces! When using [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) to chain up, the resilient lone [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) can grow up to be a [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap) (obvious three-four chain, as above); a [Linvala, Keeper of Silence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Linvala%2C+Keeper+of+Silence) (to lock out another deck's creature utility, perhaps preempting its own Melira-Pod combo); or a [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) (to get [Viscera Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viscera+Seer) and another one-drop, which might in turn become [Melira, Sylvok Outcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Melira%2C+Sylvok+Outcast)).

Affinity is an aggressive linear deck. It is offensively minded but built to take advantage of both the low mana costs and the synergies and incentives from both *Mirrodin* and *Scars of Mirrodin*.


Affinity can come out fast. It has acceleration from [Springleaf Drum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Springleaf+Drum) that becomes buffing with [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) and [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating).

Affinity can be overwhelming. It can drop its hand very quickly and send in for tons of damage either in waves ([Signal Pest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Signal+Pest)) or with singularly potent attackers [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager)/[Master of Etherium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium)/[Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating).


Affinity can surprise you. Not only can it brain you for the max consistently with [Galvanic Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast), but [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) wearing [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) doesn't care what life you are on! It can recover from sweepers due to an eight-pack of man-lands and the one-shot ability of [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) in concert with a virtual-haste attack.

### Scapeshift


Essentially all [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) decks have the same endgame incentive: use green mana acceleration like [Search for Tomorrow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Search+for+Tomorrow) and [Farseek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek) to generate a critical mass of lands in play. Then use [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) to bring in a bunch of Mountains and [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) to combo-kill the opponent with one big shot.


Many [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) decks have other incentives, options, and side plans. Some of them can play like midrange decks *a la* The Rock. Others can overwhelm you like [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker) decks. Still others play almost Draw-Go-esque with their card drawing and permission... although many can transform into midrange lifegain decks.


Grand Prix Portland gave us a treat with two different, but powerful, looks at [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift).

[Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) is brilliant in [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift); it is both a speed bump and an accelerator. It takes away one of the opponent's attacks while driving forward the [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) deck's "get a critical number of lands into play" strategy. Rizzi adds [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) to re-buy essentially any of his other spells. It can be more card-draw, another [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) or [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) for defense, a counterspell—even a re-bought [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)! Heck, it can be another [Izzet Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Izzet+Charm) (i.e., any and all). As Rizzi's is a [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) deck with tons of green acceleration, [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) is quite unburdened in its flexibility here.

[Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) is far from an automatic combo deck.


Rizzi has to be a bit careful with his various Mountains so he can get enough in play during and post-[Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle) to get value out the Molten Pinnacle. This, of course, has to consider the opponent's life total and position, which lands he has out already, and so on.


One card that can make life much easier is [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen).



![Prismatic Omen](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Prismatic+Omen)

 

With [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) in play, every land is a Mountain, including [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) itself. You could, for example, [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) any seven lands, getting all three copies of [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle) (all Mountains) and four other lands and... well, I can't actually count that high, but all of the [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle)s would count all of the other lands as Mountains and it would probably be a hot mess of damage.


[Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) also gives you these really interesting/weird/powerful midgames and long games when you just have a bunch of lands and [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) in play. How saucy is "play [Misty Rainforest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Misty+Rainforest)/brain you; sacrifice it for basic Island/brain you again?" If Joe Demestrio's opponent countered his [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) or even got them all with a [Slaughter Games](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) or some equivalent, he would still have a compelling progressive plan thanks to [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen).

This deck draws on a number of traditions, coming together as a celebration of WU victories from across the ages. [Batterskull](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull) we have, and it performs... even without the [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) that made it so fearsome in Standard. [Batterskull](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull) actually gets the nod here 2:1 over [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) (in the sideboard).


There is exactly one [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter); a nod to the Extended adventures past of Nick West and others. [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) + [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) is simply unbeatable for some decks; I can't imagine many want to play against [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) + [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) or even [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) + [Wear & Tear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wear+%26+Tear). [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer), perhaps surprisingly, can create similar headaches, given the right opponent.

Craig Wescoe is a deck designer who has become closely associated with the White Weenie archetype. I suppose his WB Tokens deck is technically a collection of small white creatures!


(...if modified for the dangers and combo decks of the larger Modern world.)


Craig's deck is chock full of efficient token-producing effects. [Raise the Alarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raise+the+Alarm) can produce two creatures for two mana using only one card (and can potentially mug attackers); [Gather the Townsfolk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gather+the+Townsfolk) can do the same, but more if your life total is pressed. [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls), of course, is the grand daddy Cadillac of token-producing cards, a cross-format All-Star that has won big events in almost every format up to and including Legacy... every format but its own Block, where it was preemptively banned before it could take down the Block Pro Tour. Not to be outdone at the three-spot is [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession), the six-drop in disguise.

So Craig has all these tools to create card advantage (if you are willing to buy 1/1 tokens as "cards"). He can trigger all kinds of annoying lifegain with [Auriok Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auriok+Champion); and [Auriok Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auriok+Champion) can be a hell heaven of a blocker in the right matchup.


But Craig isn't satisfied with his hellish divine layer after layer of lifegain and raw-material advantage... with [Honor of the Pure](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honor+of+the+Pure) and [Intangible Virtue](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intangible+Virtue), the White Weenie specialist's White Weenies can get quite big and burly.


As we said before, Craig's deck isn't just a teeny weenie token maker but is also built to defend itself in the Modern world; [Tidehollow Sculler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidehollow+Sculler) and [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) can help Wescoe buy time against powerful combo decks or rip the right answer out of a control player's hand.

Rumor had it that Finkel was going to rock the robots *a la* fellow Pro Tour winners and/or Hall of Famers Rietzl and Mowshowitz... but to the surprise of no one, the greatest in the game went with his favorite Modern strategy—UR Storm.


Finkel's strategy is to tear through his library with card draw and filtering while generating net mana via [Desperate Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desperate+Ritual) and [Pyretic Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyretic+Ritual). He can filter colors with [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose) and eventually end it via a lethal [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot) or the overwhelming snowball of [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension).

So... here's the thing.


You are sitting across from Jon Finkel; on turn two he summons a little 2/2 Goblin—the only creature in his deck: [Goblin Electromancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Electromancer). Choose your own adventure!



![Goblin Electromancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Goblin+Electromancer)

 

Do you...


1. Kill it, or
2. Play whatever proactive thing you were going to play otherwise?

It's an interesting dilemma, isn't it?


The initial knock on [Goblin Electromancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Electromancer) is that it is the only guy in Storm, meaning it is going to make the opposing deck's kill good all the time.


But here's the thing... if your opponent kills it, you basically get a [Time Walk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Walk). Your opponent killed it instead of doing something proactive on turn two... you can get another land drop or some card draw or both.


But what if your opponent doesn't kill it? What if your opponent makes an [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) or something?


Wow!


That's even better for you!


All of a sudden your [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose)s actually net mana and your [Desperate Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desperate+Ritual)s and [Pyretic Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyretic+Ritual)s become red [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual)s. The game is well and truly afoot!


Storm is a tough deck to evaluate in the abstract. What you should know if you are sitting across the table from one is that while its mana might look precarious (Jon only played sixteen lands and mulligans finally caught up to him in his Top 8 win-and-in), this is a strategy that can basically draw the whole deck and throw it at you in the first couple of turns.


It's Jon Finkel's consistent favorite *for a reason.*

PSulli joked all weekend that [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) was "an honorary red card" in his deck. Sullivan is, of course, an Nth-level Red Deck specialist with multiple PTQ wins, a Standard Open win, and a slew of other top finishes—including multiple *Legacy* Open Top 8s—all behind the little red men.


In Modern, this Five-Color Zoo is what amounts to a Red Deck, apparently!


To be fair, PSulli has not only the [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s but [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix)es via white... and the other colors make his two-mana [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames) utterly devastating. [Might of Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Might+of+Alara) joins as a +5/+5 [Giant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Giant+Growth).

The openings to this deck are an aggro player's dream: First-turn [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch) can give you second-turn [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft)... which can be attacking as a 3/3 even though it is joined by a 4/4 Angel! And that might not even be the best! [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) is quite the first-turn play; when you have played a couple of lands it can switch modes to becoming quite the little [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock)-on-a-stick.


[Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) rounds out a squad that, again, makes [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames) look good... you know, again and again.


For any of you who think aggro decks are easier to pilot competently than control decks, I challenge you to take a couple of draws with Patrick's. He has black one-drops with two total black-producing lands (which sac land do you break to grab which shockland?)... green one-drops, blue and white instants, blue and white (virtual) two-drops... and a high end terminating at ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif) for [Elspeth, Knight-Errant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant) and ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif) for [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite). Meanwhile, he will need ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) and/or ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) just to keep the [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman) lights on.
