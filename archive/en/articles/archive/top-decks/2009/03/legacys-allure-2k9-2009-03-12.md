Legacy's Allure 2K9
===================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on March 12, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






In this week's installment of Top Decks, you will find...

  
1) Some gushing about the largest ever North American Grand Prix.  
 2) More—yes, more—about Gabriel Nassif.  
 3) A little something for the Extended Faerie-Wizards metagame.

Let's get to it ... er ... *them*:

So this past weekend in [Chicago](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/coverage-grand-prix-chicago-2009-03-07) was a record-breaker for North American Grand Prix, drawing over 1200 players from around the world to shuffle up and compete in the seldom-featured Legacy format.

Since we are talking about a huge jump, nearly half again up from when Steve Sadin and his Billy Moreno-designed Hulk Flash deck took down [Grand Prix–Columbus](/en/events/coverage/hulk-flash-smashes-columbus) (the last time we saw a big Legacy event in North America), it probably bears speculation... *What makes Legacy so special?* What's the especial allure?

The biggest chunk of answer is that you get to play with lots of cards! 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td29_pileOfLegacy.jpg)  
Specifically, you get to play with cards that you aren't allowed to play with anymore in more common competitive formats like Standard or Extended, or in a broader sense, cards that have almost *never* been legal for broad-based competitive tournament play ... at least not this decade.

Huh?

The easiest way we can illustrate what I am talking about is—not surprisingly—with a deck.

This is Kurtis Droge's deck from Grand Prix–Chicago. Kurtis finished in the money with this very interesting take on a classic "Monkey May I?" style of Blue Zoo, opening up undefeated on Day One.

What is interesting about this deck is that even though the fancy blue spells—[Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), [Daze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze), and [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will)—are unavailable in Extended today, the left-hand column is something that we could accomplish with the available resources ... but no one has tried to do thus far.

The idea is a simple one. This is a quality creature deck with an intriguing reload: [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) keeps Kurtis's threats available. He merely goes and gets [Kird Ape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape) and [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td29_rangerKirdNacatl.jpg)  
The thing that makes a deck like this viable is the land mix. [Tundra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tundra). [Volcanic Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Island). Laced together with [Polluted Delta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta) and [Windswept Heath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windswept+Heath).

In Extended, the peers are [Hallowed Fountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Fountain) or [Temple Garden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temple+Garden) ... and we have a deck archetype that plays every single color and routinely gets five different basic land types into play. But the cost is great. We joke about Zoo-style decks "starting on 14," which can be lethal in a fast-damage format like Extended (at least sometimes). But in Legacy, there is very little life downside beyond breaking the *Onslaught* dual lands for a mere one life. In fact, the presence of [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) (and [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) in other decks) makes things infinitely more interesting, not at all prohibitive.

| Brainstorm | Ponder |

  
The result is that decks even remotely similar to what Kurtis tried to do have to play with single-minded offense. They are almost universally Domain Zoo, [Gaea's Might](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Might) Get There, or Naya Burn based ... because Extended's land mix puts players behind the eight ball in such a way that they almost *have* to race, not play a methodical CounterSliver-style game or mess around with cards like [Rushing River](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rushing+River).

This Legacy land flexibility makes so many different things possible. We have not seen a true "Monkey May I?" ("Monkey" being "Ape," as in Kird; "May I?" asking for, you know, *permission*) since ... can it be the Type I Pro Tour of 1996? Splashing [Island](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Island) lets players run [Daze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze). All those *Onslaught* fetchlands make [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) wonderful. A wide palette of amazing one-drops ... gives us the ability to play them with the permission spells, land fixing, and [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Brainstorm).

The same format gives players the opportunity to play all-in attack decks, offensively overloaded discard decks, lightning-quick combination decks, and of course the darling of the format, [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) + [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) decks of every spot and stripe.

| Counterbalance | Sensei's Divining Top |

  
As you look at the decks in this article (or study [the coverage of Grand Prix–Chicago](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/coverage-grand-prix-chicago-2009-03-07) or other Legacy events) you may be surprised by the format's comparatively low land counts (unless we are talking about a 41-land deck, that is!). Part of that can be attributed to the extremely wide palette of available cards. When a color like green can play two different 3/3 creatures for one mana ([Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl) and [Nimble Mongoose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimble+Mongoose)), there is sufficient selection to shave a couple of points off the average curve. However, the more interesting wrinkle comes from the numerous, stackable, and relishably redundant suite of one-mana cantrips (and thereabouts).

For about the past 12 years the rule of thumb in Constructed has been that for every four one- or two-mana cantrips, cycling cards, or equivalents, a player can cut two lands from his or her deck. Therefore a deck like Gabriel Nassif's, which plays only 20 lands (and of those, only 12 *mana-producing* lands) operates more or less like a deck boasting more than 25 lands thanks to [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder), [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top), and some nonzero (if not immediate) impact from [Dark Confidant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Dark%2BConfidant).

For anyone who has ever claimed about chronic mana problems, "always being mana-screwed" or whatever ... this might be the deck for you! While it's not *impossible* to draw no lands with this deck, it sure ain't *likely*.

So why might a deck like this one be good?

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td29_landCross.jpg)  
The lands in this deck tend to "do something" beyond just tapping for mana. [Barbarian Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barbarian+Ring) is a way to win and also creature defense. [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) might as well be [Strip Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine) in a format with few if any basic lands. [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland), um, also might as well be [Strip Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine) .... but like an even better one contextually. The deck can play more than one land per turn thanks to [Exploration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exploration) and [Manabond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manabond), so you can actually get ahead with your [Strip Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine) proxies. The format leans heavily towards [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) ... which can't really deal with a relentless assault of [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village) and [Mishra's Factory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factory).

And it has a basically inexorable card-drawing engine in Life from the Loam.

![Life from the Loam](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Life+from+the+Loam)[Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) works here much as it does in Extended decks, *Onslaught* duals like [Wooded Foothills](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wooded+Foothills) plus *Onslaught* cycling cards like [Tranquil Thicket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tranquil+Thicket), and it's doubly effective here due to the ability to play more lands while drawing lands and sometimes spells. Typically playing [Gamble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamble)—especially for a key card like [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam)—is a gamble ... but since your hand will be mostly lands (which can be recouped with [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) if you are forced to discard it) or [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) itself (which doesn't care if it is in the graveyard) there is little down side.

So how effective can a deck that is more than two-thirds lands actually be? 

[Maze of Ith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maze+of+Ith) is a pretty good solution to a single [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) or [Phyrexian Dreadnought](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Dreadnought), but not necessarily to a [Nimble Mongoose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimble+Mongoose). The card advantage is undeniable, but might be dicey against a [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) deck with another [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) on top. The really difficult proposition is not a creature or a control deck, I think, so much as a combo deck like [Ad Nauseum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseum). Decks like this one typically have problems they can't say "no" to ... and [Ad Nauseum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseum) presents those kinds of problems.

Kolowith's deck was the most successful combination deck of the Grand Prix. It works in much the same way that the Storm decks you are probably more familiar with work: that is, it plays lots of spells (probably mana acceleration to start) into a big finale. In this deck, the kill card (main-deck) is [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony). So it's all about cheap manipulation and mana access until that Tendrils (which is hopefully inexorable thanks to the storm mechanic). The special thing about this deck is [Ad Nauseum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseum).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td29_adAndFriends.jpg)Look at the mana costs ...

[Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) is one.

[Burning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) is two.

[Cabal Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Ritual) is two.

[Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox), [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond), and [Lotus Petal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Petal) are nothing at all.

So there is relatively little down side to drawing *lots* of cards with [Ad Nauseum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseum); you can probably get away with more than ten, depending on the opponent's clock.

Six's deck is halfway between an improved Red Deck Wins and a modern Naya Burn. The creatures (beyond the excellent onetime staple [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer)) are exactly what you might see in one of this weekend's PTQ Top 8s. The spells, though ... The spells are just the best stuff ever, spanning the full breadth of **Magic**'s many sets. From Alpha there is [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), *Legends*
[Chain Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+Lightning), *Visions*
[Fireblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireblast) ... all the way to cards from the last few years' blocks such as [Magma Jet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magma+Jet), [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix), and [Rift Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt). The really cool, special, and devastating burn spell, though, is [Price of Progress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Price+of+Progress).

| Chain Lightning | Price of Progress |

  
Named in honor of the Pro Tour's most celebrated practitioner of red beatdown decks, Price of Progress is that special something tailored perfectly to the Legacy metagame. Let me explain .... Just going over the [Grand Prix–Chicago Top 8](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/2009-grand-prix-chicago-top-8-decks-2009-03-07) we see a total of 30 basic lands between the eight decks ... and more than a third of them are in a single All-In Red deck. That means that most of these guys are going to be taking 4, 6, or even more damage from a [Price of Progress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Price+of+Progress) ... for just two mana! You don't need a lot of beatdown to drop an opponent with that kind of finishing reach. Not when you can speed it up while cutting your losses with a [Fireblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireblast).

### Boat Brew

Just kidding!

What is not a joke is the recent performance of failed retiree Brian Kowal.

The former Righteous Babe "quit **Magic**" a few months ago before accidentally winning a PTQ and inventing the über-popular Boat Brew / Red-White Vengeant [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) deck on the way to qualifying for the [Game in the Gulf](/en/articles/archive/news/game-gulf-2009-03-02). From there it took no less than eventual Pro Tour Champion Gabriel Nassif to hand Kowal so much as a Constructed match loss on the Pro Tour, barely keeping him out of the Top 8.

And now, even with another Nassif win, even Gab couldn't keep Brian from shining. Just a week after his first individual Pro Tour Day Two, Kowal finally made his big break, the elimination rounds on the Sunday stage.

I guess that's what happens when you combine arguably the strongest active player with the consensus best strategy: another big tournament win.

By now we all know the synergies.

[Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) + [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) .... No damage from Bobby, a constant stream of new cards.

[Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder), [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), and [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) + [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) ... The first set of cards tell you what is going to be on top; the other half of that plus sign ruins the opponent's day when you can match mana costs. The really tricky players run the first-turn [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) or [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), then tap for the [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) and look all innocent when the opponent's spell gets countered. "Guess I was just lucky."

The cool feature of this deck is that [Trygon Predator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trygon+Predator). [Trygon Predator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trygon+Predator) is a three-mana spell, and therefore not automatically so easy to counter with [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance). Once it is in play, Nassif would have a recurring source of [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) or [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) suppression.

![Trygon Predator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Trygon+Predator)And as for the 15 one-ofs in the sideboard? I have nothing to say about those beyond the jaw dropping admiration you already had yourself.

The next biggest winner in the tournament (and a guy who probably did more actual winning, having taken down Trial #6) was Andy "Brassman" Probasco.

Josh's deck was superb.

He beat almost every kind of deck over the course of the day, up until his nightmare match in the finals. Josh was particularly adept in the Faeries mirror, bringing in [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Mage+of+Zhalfir) to lock out the game.

Josh's deck features a number of cards that should be staple in a Faeries-style deck if you want to be successful at this stage of the PTQ season. First of all there are lots of [Riptide Laboratory](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Riptide%2BLaboratory) in this deck; I actually think that four copies is probably right due to being so important in the mirror match. In particular, [Riptide Laboratory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Riptide+Laboratory) can suppress the effectiveness of [Sower of Temptation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sower+of+Temptation), which can potentially produce large swings in board position (imagine the nightmare of having *Teferi* wiggle over to the other side). Previously we thought of Storm as being a difficult deck for Faeries to beat, but between 4 [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) and 4 [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique), that kind of a combo deck is pretty manageable. The Edison PTQ was a week where we predicted heavy Affinity activity, which explains Josh's many [Hurkyl's Recall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Hurkyl%2527s%2BRecall) in the side. I don't know that he would repeat those given the lower Affinity tenor of recent Top 8s.

If you don't recognize Corey's name ... the man loves a Kithkin. He made something like five of six Block PTQ Top 8s over the summer—and won the Philly $5,000—all with Kithkin. Unbelievably, Corey sleeved up [Wizened Cenn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wizened+Cenn) for Extended and ran through the Swiss fairly effortlessly.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td29_loadedKithkin.jpg)  
Zoo? He met that kind of deck with first-turn [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender), followed up with a [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Umezawa%2527s%2BJitte).

Affinity? I didn't personally see this, but rumor had it that his [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) powered out a [Fracturing Gust](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fracturing+Gust) that was hiding under a [Windbrisk Heights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Windbrisk+Heights).

We know from All-In Red testing that [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) can dominate many opponents sticking on any random 1/1. But Corey's deck has a special creature that might seem a little out of place (but he assures us is quite awesome): [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger)!

Chuckling?

Why?

[Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger) is a definingly awesome threat in the Standard version of this deck. Why not the Extended? As he explains it, the Faeries decks don't actually have a whole lot of ways to contain a [Cloudgoat Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger). Think about it ... Josh's deck has what? Two [Cryptic Command](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Cryptic%2BCommand)? Most have nada. You tap for it, and the Ranger will often stick. *And then* what are they going to do?

With [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) as an available four-of to shore up what was once Kithkin's only real weakness (no available and affordable point removal), Kithkin has become a much more versatile choice for basically the most diverse metagame in recent memory.

And just how diverse? Diverse enough that Osyp Lebedowicz *finally* got to play his pet deck again ... and it was good.

If you want to beat Faeries (and you probably want to if you are looking to win a PTQ)... This is a superb option. The card advantage available to this deck is equal to anything that Faeries can muster. The enchantments here are simply unstoppable for any deck that plans to win with any kind of an attack. Even tapped out the Slide deck can defend itself.

Imagine this scenario: You are playing Faeries. Your opponent runs out a [Cloudthresher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudthresher) at the end of your second main phase. It is going to kill one [Spellstutter Sprite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellstutter+Sprite) if you let it stick. But he paid only four mana (tapped out) .... Seems like a bit of a waste of a [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) if you fight it. Are you going to fight it? You had better! Because if you don't, don't be surprised if the opponent sacrifices [Flagstones of Trokair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flagstones+of+Trokair) after you are out of your [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) window and Slides out the [Cloudthresher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Cloudthresher) with [Edge of Autumn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Edge+of+Autumn).

I would caution you a little bit if you don't have any Extended Slide testing under your belt: It is not whatsoever intuitive to play. The mana is difficult to manage and your best action often includes pointing [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) at your own non-[Flagstones of Trokair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flagstones+of+Trokair) lands. But if you're good, it's fun to play and rewarding...

...As long as you don't get paired up against combo. Because you can't beat a Storm deck.
