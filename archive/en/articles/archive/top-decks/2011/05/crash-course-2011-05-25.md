Crash Course
============



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on May 26, 2011 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











This weekend is a rare Grand Prix devoted to the now-popular Legacy format. I still recall a few years back, when Billy Moreno brewed up the first winkings of his [Hulk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Protean+Hulk)[Flash](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flash) deck on the big wooden blocks in my living room, not yet knowing that a returning Steve Sadin would—maybe a week later—emerge a [Grand Prix champion](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/events.aspx?x=mtgevent/gpcol07/welcome) with arguably the greatest single-tournament deck of all time.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Once upon a time, Legacy was played infrequently at a large scale and the highest levels (maybe one North American Grand Prix per year) and catered to a comparatively small and specialized audience; but today, the Star City Games Open Series highlights a competitive Legacy event almost once a week. As a result, we have a format that is full of lively, week-over-week, innovation and give-and-take, with many of the greatest minds in the game devoting time, care, and technology to curating the still-emerging metagame.

Keep in mind there are probably forty different decks you can play in Legacy that are all capable of winning a big tournament. Yes, some are much more likely to win (a sick combo, Green-Blue-Black, or one of the Mono-Blue Control decks) than others (Goblins, Zoo, or other beatdown decks that don't include [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will)), but ultimately, there are far too many decks to discuss in a single crash course.

But we'll nevertheless give it a go.

Part of what has enabled this overwhelming diversity is the banning of this card:

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Survival+of+the+Fittest)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest)  
A few months ago we devoted an entire article to the various decks that you could make based on this card ([Survival Survival Survival Survival](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/114a)), but the reign of [Survival of the Fittest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest) was an oppressive and ultimately stifling stranglehold. The DCI banned the little enchantment, and now we have a crazy and medium-unpredictable mishmash of decks.

Legacy is a format of constricted time. There are combo decks that can win on the second turn, and others that don't win until turn four. There are control decks, but they don't generally play for a position of inevitability (but of those, they will incorporate combo-like elements); many play more like CounterSliver than Fortress. Of beatdown decks, there are plenty; all different colors and all different flavors.

With forty-plus different decks, what else would you expect?

The banning of [Survival of the Fittest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Survival+of+the+Fittest) occurred hand-in-hand with the un-banning of mighty [Time Spiral](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Spiral). Unsurprisingly, [Time Spiral](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Spiral) immediately won a big Open event:

[High Tide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide) is an upgrade to the famous Extended deck from the last century (the upgrade coming care of [Candelabra of Tawnos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Candelabra+of+Tawnos)). Essentially you want to play [High Tide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide), then net mana with [Turnabout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Turnabout), [Candelabra of Tawnos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Candelabra+of+Tawnos), and [Time Spiral](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Spiral). More and more untaps and taps for bonus blue give you the juice you need to kill the opponent with [Blue Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blue+Sun%27s+Zenith).

[High Tide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide) was the generally accepted deck of choice for most successful Pro Tour players (on the outside), but *New Phyrexia* has gone a long way in helping to shift the metagame once again. New card [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep)—a card that can be played by almost any deck—seems perfectly positioned to throw a monkey wrench into the bicycle wheels of [High Tide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mental+Misstep)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep)

Master deck developer Gerry Thompson unleashed the above on the metagame the first week [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep) was legal; both Gerry and Open Series mainstay Drew Levin made Top 8 with this deck, remarkable in part for its inclusion of [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td144_mental.jpg)[Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep) | Illustration by [Erica Yang](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;artist=+%5B%22Erica+Yang%22%5D)

Gerry's is a cool little reinterpretation of the [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill) archetype, enabled by [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep). [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep) (along with [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare) and [Repeal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Repeal)) give the deck a great deal of action against early game threats. Basically you want to play [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill) at any point that you are not behind / under pressure. Likely the opponent will have to break the [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill), resulting in a kind of [Ancestral Recall](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Recall).

When playing this kind of a deck you can get proactive without breaking [Standstill](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Standstill)... Much of your offense runs though [Mishra's Factory](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factory), for one thing; in addition you have the [Crucible of Worlds](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crucible+of+Worlds) + [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) interaction that allows you to not only draw extra cards but actually make big moves against the opponent; again, without actually casting any spells.

Remember what we said about Legacy being a format of constricted time? The Green-Blue-Black Control decks—often called "BUG"—are good examples. These are "control" decks relative to the potential speed of the metagame, but having access to an enormous card pool (essentially every set, ever; and with that, both the best mana and card access) gives the deck the opportunity to cherry pick from the best spells only.

BUG Control's [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s are all super fast... [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) and [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep). Its threats are top of the line as well: Tarmogoyf and Dark Confidant. But the real issue? The discard! Green-Blue-Black Control pairs the power and consistency of blue with the sheer jerkwad-ness of [Hymn to Tourach](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach)... These games are only fun from one side of the table!

I had been a bit unexcited around the topic of the Obliterator, specifically because I didn't think it could do anything in Standard the way things are. A fourth-turn giant monster is not impressive when the opponent can just respond with [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor), and most of the best decks coming in to *New Phyrexia* were interacting with creatures via [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn), [Oust](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oust), [Into the Roil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+Roil), or [Tumble Magnet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tumble+Magnet), anyway. But paired with [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) and the damning pall of Hymn (both unavailable in Standard), [Phyrexian Obliterator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Obliterator) has me pretty enthusiastic. This creature is the realization of all of [Papa Negator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Negator)'s dreams, the son that can accomplish all the father left behind, leaving his ancient red masters crimson in their own blood.

[Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) is pretty cool here, obtaining [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) as well as [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte). The Equipment packages are in particular awesome with newcomer [Mirran Crusader](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader), which has both double strike and a bad attitude.

I mean, who is going to block a [Mirran Crusader](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader) (protection from black and green) wearing a [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) (protection from red and blue)?

For that matter, who is going to block [Phyrexian Obliterator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Obliterator)? Ever?

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td144_force.jpg)[Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) | Illustration by [Terese Nielsen](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;artist=+%5B%22Terese+Nielsen%22%5D)

Legacy's tremendous mana availability allows you to combine many different cards with similar themes; also the same cards in different color combinations. For example you will see [Hymn to Tourach](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach) in lots of different decks... but also [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary), or [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte).

The Zoo decks exemplify the best in offense, stretched across close to twenty years of **Magic** set releases: awesome one-drops, [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)s, a little card drawing, great removal... and [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary).



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

The Zoo deck uses [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) primarily as a giant, though it can also pick up a [Horizon Canopy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Horizon+Canopy). On the other hand, Junk is a bit slower, but can do more stuff. Junk has good creatures, but not really one-drop beaters. Instead, Junk's [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) can get more specialty cards like [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) or especially [Karakas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Karakas) (which can handle every legendary creature, up to and including the normally slippery [Emrakul, the Aeons Torn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emrakul%2C+the+Aeons+Torn)).

Legacy's expansive view of all of **Magic** allows players to run all manner of historic Goblins, Fish, and Robots from Standard and Extended times past, as well.

Argh!

Yeah.

Dredge is probably going to win. :(

The deck's goal is to put lots of cards in the graveyard, flip over multiple copies of [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba), flash [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) with [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) in the bin (making lots of Zombie tokens), return [Flame-Kin Zealot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame-Kin+Zealot), and smash you all in one stroke.

But if it doesn't get that draw, Dredge can just hassle with [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid), or exploit the free [Cabal Therapies](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) it is getting every turn.

This deck is super hard to beat in Game 1, but anyone who really, really wants to GG the graveyard, can.

I like Cephalid Breakfast even more than Dredge. It is faster (just wins on the spot on turn two), and it plays the two best cards in Legacy ([Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will)). We haven't seen a Breakfast finish since *New Phyrexia*, though, and like [High Tide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide), this is a deck intimately vulnerable to [Mental Misstep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mental+Misstep). Anyway, Breakfast—despite being the fastest and potentially most powerful deck in Legacy—has always been vulnerable to the most stuff. Even a [Swords to Plowshares](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares) can break it up!
