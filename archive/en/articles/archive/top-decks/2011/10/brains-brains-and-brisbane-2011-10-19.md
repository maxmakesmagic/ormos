Brains! Brains! (and Brisbane)
==============================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on October 20, 2011 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











I don't know why I called this article "Brains! Brains! (and Brisbane)" other than it is Undead Week (and zombies, a popular type of undead, like—in some media—to utter "brains / brains"... which is their favorite thing to eat), and it probably took a lot of brains to come up with some of these new decks, many of which showed up at [Grand Prix Brisbane](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/gpbri11/welcome).

I don't have 100% of all the 2011s (State and Provincial Championships), but we nevertheless have quite a few deck lists to discuss from around these tournaments (and some from some pretty impressive players)... plus a Grand Prix. Let's start with that:

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td165_chart.jpg)  
So RE: the Brisbane elimination rounds, there were two big and obvious decks for the most part. The more numerous of the two was—and this was more-or-less to be expected from an educated perspective—Wolf Run Ramp. After seeing Brian Sondag's Star City Nashville Open-winning deck, it seemed to me, at least, that this strategy would be this Standard format's early breakout deck. It has big threats, drops lands, and a clever endgame. What's not to love?

There were some variations between Wolf Run Ramp decks, but I grouped them all together.

For practical purposes, these kinds of decks are green acceleration decks that play spells like [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) and [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith), and creatures like [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum) and [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan). The point of the deck is to stick either [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) itself or [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) for [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) to start hunting out lands. Which lands is very important. What you typically do to set up the endgame is to obtain at least one [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) and at least one [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run). The theory is that an opponent who deals with every [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) and planeswaker is still dead to [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) + [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus).



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

Many Control decks in particular don't even have enough removal spells to deal with 4 [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus)es, let alone the extras produced via [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) cheats. Then, eventually, the arm-in-arm team of [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) and power-pump from [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) do their damnedest to, you know, send the opponent to Undead Week status.

Huang's build was pretty close to what we saw from Brian Sondag's original... though he did vary the counts on [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) (up to four, from three), to the detriment of one [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine). Huang played one [Garruk Relentless](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Relentless) instead of all five-mana Garruks. Among other things, he could piggyback his [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) or [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) to hit Garruk faster in the mirror.

It would probably be an exaggeration to say that the *only* really important and groundbreaking innovation to come out of the 2011's came out of Owen Turtenwald's Wisconsin-winning deck (which he elsewhere credited to the traveling troupe of Martin Juza and Shuhei Nakamura), but I am just going to wink really prominently in that deck's direction and let you draw your own conclusion (which should inevitably be that Owen's Wolf Run Ramp is a legitimate game-changer).

The big difference here—the dramatic level-up relative to the Sondag trendsetter and 75% of the Brisbane Top 8 builds, and the difference that made the difference relative to the immediately preceding Pranoto one—is the inclusion of *four*[Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)s.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dungrove+Elder)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)  
Where previously we could see [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) as a point of influence, and accommodated by land choices, it was essentially a bullet, something different to find with [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith). In Owen's deck, it is a legitimate plan, an angle of attack that *is* going to come up, game after game over the course of the tournament.

Not only is [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) equally good as a *card* as it might be elsewhere, with Owen's mana base and spell selection we see problem layered onto problem for opponents. Like, when you shift from one [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) (classic Sondag, essentially itself a two-mana [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith) target) to six one-drop accelerators, what does that do?

*(don't worry, I am going to tell you)*

For one, you are going to hit a bunch of turn-two [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)s on the play. That is horrendous for many opponents, especially when you can back it up with [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth), [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum), the regular old top of your deck, etc. Owen's mana is *extreme*. Pranoto played 18 [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s but still ran 4 [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) and two red sources. Owen had all of one [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) and two little infectious Nexuses. His deck just wants to cast turn-one [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) (which you need [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest) to do if you aren't going to lower yourself to [Copperline Gorge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperline+Gorge)), and once [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) is in place, you really just want more and more [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s... like twenty of them.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td165_elder.jpg)[Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) | Art by [Matt Stewart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;artist=+%5B%22Matt+Stewart%22%5D)

Playing six one-drop accelerators (and to be fair, Pranoto, above, played five) also gives your opponents something to chew on. Like, if the other team has a [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) or some other "Edict" kind of effect, even when he needs to have one, the presence of another small body can soak that up and keep the Elder alive. Moreover, a deck like this, which not only has 20 [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s but 4 [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth), 4 [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum), and 4 [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan), can easily stay ahead of the default non-targeted creature-sweeper we see outside of [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment): [Black Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Sun%27s+Zenith). I mean seriously, how high is X going to have to be?

Jeremy Neeman played a Blue-Black Control deck that is very different from most of the decks we have seen in the *Scars of Mirrodin / **Magic** 2012 / Innistrad* Standard to date: Lots of counterspells rather than an unending stream of point removal, 27 lands, powered up by the incomparable [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage).

Neeman's deck is instant-tacular, with nearly 50% instants in a deck that *also* has nearly 50% lands (a mere nine creatures, four of which are [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage)s, plus one [Black Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Sun%27s+Zenith), are the only "slow" stuff). Interesting in particular is the inclusion of [Wring Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wring+Flesh) as a one-mana removal spell.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

[Wring Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wring+Flesh) can handle a [Stromkirk Noble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromkirk+Noble) on turn one, and mostly "turn off" most every fast threat in the format. It's obviously very solid as the format moves to more [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) and [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves), as well. As the cheapest of the point removal instants, [Wring Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wring+Flesh)—this strange, seldom seen little inclusion—helps set up a magnificent defensive curve for Neeman's deck. To wit:


> * Turn one [Wring Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wring+Flesh) your little dude.
> * Turn two [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) your next thing.
> * Turn three [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) to re-buy [Wring Flesh](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wring+Flesh) (for your next little dude).
> 

[Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) and [Forbidden Alchemy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy) help give the deck long game card draw, with [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx) and [Grave Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grave+Titan) (along with ye olde [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine)) providing the endgame beatdown.

Worth mentioning: [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard). Neeman has four after sideboarding! Another way to win, that can't be countered, that many decks can't handle at all. More on this card in a bit...

If there is one immutable truth of **Magic: The Gathering**, it is that former Player of the Year Shouta Yasooka loves a Blue-Black Control deck; and in particular in recent months, he has enjoyed a partnership with planeswalker [Tezzeret, Agent of Bolas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tezzeret%2C+Agent+of+Bolas).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Tezzeret%2C+Agent+of+Bolas)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tezzeret%2C+Agent+of+Bolas)  
No "[Torpor Orb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Torpor+Orb) song" this GP Top 8 around, but Yasooka and Tezzeret have continued to lock arms into success from a little-bit-different angle.

Rather than the [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage)s we have seen in every other list (and will see in yet another), Shouta's deck is much more about playing permanents, and most of his cards come down on his own turn. That is, we see [Despise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Despise) controlling the game rather than [Dissipate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dissipate), and he has a "mere" three [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s instead of the all-out overload on permission we might see in some [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) decks. In return, we have the synergy between Tezzeret and artifacts in general (go get a [Batterskull](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Batterskull), defend yourself with [Tumble Magnet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tumble+Magnet), make [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) a 5/5).

Just for the fun of Undead Week, we see [Army of the Damned](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Army+of+the+Damned) as a potential game-winner in a State Championship-winning deck, played by the veteran Adam Prosak.

On one hand this is yet another look at the blue-black "[Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) + permission and removal" macro archetype, going all-out with permission ([Psychic Barrier](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychic+Barrier) and [Steel Sabotage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+Sabotage) in addition to the commonly run [Dissipate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dissipate) and [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak))... On the other, we have a very strategic inclusion of [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Nephalia+Drownyard)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard)  
In other contexts, [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard) is... a card. It has some text. It taps for mana. You might get someone with it... sometimes.

In one of these instant-heavy blue-black decks (Neeman, Prosak, et al), it is actually an engine enabler, helping to set up [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) like a kind of [Demonic Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Tutor) ([Diabolic Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Tutor), really, given the two mana premium on [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage))... and sometimes you will flip [Army of the Damned](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Army+of+the+Damned) and produce lethal Zombies with essentially a free card. Yes, dear readers; the probable target of the average [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard) activation is *yourself*.

And in a deck with [Visions of Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Visions+of+Beyond)?

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Visions+of+Beyond)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Visions+of+Beyond)  
*Mise!*

[Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard) is the ~~John Stockton~~ Steve Nash (Adam is from Arizona) to [Visions of Beyond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Visions+of+Beyond)'s hard finishing power forward.

I mean, "Brains! Brains!"

Wolf Run Ramp and Blue-Black Control decks are "obvious" ... but this seems like a pretty good format, meaning we have potential play from some non-obvious sources... like Tim Fondum's Green-White Tokens / [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) deck.

We see some great use of new "[Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves)" [Avacyn's Pilgrim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacyn%27s+Pilgrim), helping Tim accelerate to the turn-two [Blade Splicer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blade+Splicer) along with [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise). Lots of great token generators here (I am in particular a fan of new (gigantic) [Siege-Goat Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudgoat+Ranger)[Geist-Honored Monk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist-Honored+Monk)), and solid use of planeswalkers (token-generating planeswalkers, of course) where we have seen them fall a bit out of fashion in other decks.

Cool to notice: In a deck full of free little cards, [Mortarpod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortarpod) can give you some pretty good looks at both creature removal and finishing games.

Speaking of finishing games, [Gavony Township](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township) and [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) can end them pretty handily when you have a lot of little guys on the battlefield (which this deck usually should).

The format sure has been interesting so far, from aggressive red and white decks performing early, into the rise of [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) and now [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) (both carrying with them some color-specific requirements). Every color of mana is currently being represented, and across a fair number of different strategies.

You know what might require some *real* brains (brains!)? Keeping it shifting and swirling this way!
