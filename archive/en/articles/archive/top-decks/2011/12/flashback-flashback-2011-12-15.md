Flashback to Flashback
======================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on December 15, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







Yes, yes, we have some very nice flashback today.


Patrick Chapin says that [Desperate Ravings](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desperate+Ravings) is so good we will someday consider it a kind of [Preordain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain) and players as successful as a Gerry Thompson or a Jon Finkel will bend their decks to accommodate it. [Think Twice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) and [Forbidden Alchemy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy) do—if not everything a blue mage wants—enough, such that Nick Spagnolo once confessed that he just wants to play with them "forever."

And then there is [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), which doesn't in fact have flashback, but makes it so that everything else does.



![Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Snapcaster+Mage)

 

Yes, we have some very nice flashback and I am sure you all love playing with it.


But—and this is especially for the newer players out there—flashback has a long and storied history in **Magic: The Gathering**, and today's article is going to talk about some of the many exciting flashback cards of yesteryear... The ten best, in fact.


But before we get to those—[Alter Reality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alter+Reality).


Long before I was the columnist on Top Decks— or its predecessor column Swimming With Sharks— any writing I did on this website was actually more casually focused. My original not-column was focused on Mental **Magic** (and you can peruse the very beginning of [my archives](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Archive.aspx?author=Mike%20Flores) to see what was going on there).


I even did a whole series on using flashback proactively in Mental **Magic**, which you can [read here](/en/articles/archive/mental-magic-proactive-flashback-2003-05-23), and among the many power cards referenced in that one was the seemingly humble [Alter Reality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alter+Reality).


This is what I said about it back on May 23, 2003:



![Alter Reality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Alter+Reality)


> 
> **[Alter Reality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alter+Reality)**  
> 
> The power of this card stems from its innocent face. It doesn't do *anything* does it? Nope, [Alter Reality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Alter+Reality) doesn't do anything except win the counter war that would have ended with Burnout; counter [Execute](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Execute), [Perish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Perish), Soul Rend, and [Slay](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slay); and generally leave your opponent tapped out in the middle of his own turn. One of the great joys in Mental Magic is walking your opponent into an unwinnable counter war in which you are going to fight that impending [Red Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Red+Elemental+Blast) with a card in the graveyard that he or she ostensibly already dealt with.
> 
> 
> If there is one single card that I try to cast every game, it is this one.
> 
> 
> 


### And Now, the Top 10 Flashbacks of Yesteryear!


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td173_card10a.jpg)Yes, yes, this is a "Top 10" list and yet I open it up (already) with a savage cheat, essentially unable to figure out which is better: [Firebolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firebolt) or [Lava Dart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart)?


The question has bedeviled even the strongest Red Deck strategists for years. Which is the more compelling? [Firebolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firebolt) with its clear route to card advantage (and 2 points) or [Lava Dart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart) with its speed and cunning?


When playing the mirror match, you can tap out on turn three or four or five with a [Lava Dart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart) in your graveyard. The most important thing (other than [Cursed Scroll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll) control) is to just not get hit by [Blistering Firecat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blistering+Firecat), and [Lava Dart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart) has you there. You can use [Lava Dart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart) to clear more blockers, faster, as well.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td173_cross1.jpg)But look at [Firebolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firebolt)! Part of what makes it so compelling is who was playing it. Off the top of my head I can summon up a couple of Hall of Famers. Three even!


When the two were legal at the same time, there was no consensus over which was the right one-mana flashback burn spell to run... so I see no reason to break this tradition with my Flashback Week Top 10 list.


*Uncertainty it is!*

There are quite a few different decks that played [Quiet Speculation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quiet+Speculation) into [Moment's Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moment%27s+Peace), but Patrick Chapin's 2002 Michigan State Finalist deck dovetails best into my next segment, so there you have it.


In this deck, [Moment's Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moment%27s+Peace) was in some cases better at controlling creatures than [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), such as when the creatures were [Blistering Firecat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blistering+Firecat) or other haste attackers. Really, what you wanted was to prevent the *damage* the creatures had pointed at you (and in a sense killing creatures is really just one of many ways to prevent their bonking you for 7 or whatever).


In Patrick's deck, [Moment's Peace](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moment%27s+Peace) was there to buy time so he could activate [Krosan Verge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Verge) to accelerate, eventually setting up [Time Stretch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Stretch) (or [Burning Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) for [Time Stretch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Stretch)), at which point it was all about [Crush of Wurms](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crush+of+Wurms) and winning in extra turns.


*[Crush of Wurms](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crush+of+Wurms)? That didn't make the list, did it?*


No. Rest assured, there is no [Crush of Wurms](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crush+of+Wurms) in this Top 10, although between it and [Firecat Blitz](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firecat+Blitz) we have quite some odd (but conditionally deadly) flashback threats. In Patrick's deck, [Mirari's Wake](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirari%27s+Wake) would help him produce so much mana that, thanks to [Time Stretch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Stretch), he would have ample opportunity to make some huge amount of power... and actually attack with it for lethal.


And if not?


...there was always another [Time Stretch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Stretch)!


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td173_card07.jpg)Look at these handsome faces!


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td173_faces.jpg)This quick interview was from Grand Prix Milwaukee 2002, where Patrick finished second and even yours truly finished in the money.


We had such an enormous amount of respect for [Roar of the Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm) that, if enabled by [Quiet Speculation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quiet+Speculation), there was a fear that it would be too good. And that wasn't really that far out there: I actually played [Roar of the Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm) in that Grand Prix (enabled by [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) and [Merfolk Looter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merfolk+Looter)), and it was already quite good.


Later decks ran it with stuff like [Breakthrough](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breakthrough) or [Careful Study](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Careful+Study), and it represented just what we would today think of as an [Abyssal Persecutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abyssal+Persecutor) that didn't fly.


But again, if you had [Quiet Speculation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quiet+Speculation) going, [Roar of the Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm) was *three* 6/6 giants for one card, and each of them only cost four mana. And if you actually had seven for the front end? It would be pretty difficult to lose a war of attrition, don't you think?


[Roar of the Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm) ultimately contributed to any number of highly effective and ultimately famous decks, primarily green-bluedecks with [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel). It was a sideboard card in control decks as a Plan B, and a rule breaker in all the best senses of that term. With [Roar of the Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm), green-blue decks could shatter the age-old paradigm that Aggro-Control would always lose to beatdown; in those days, green-blue Aggro-Control could, in fact, contend with red-green and their little Lavamancers.


This is how respectable [Roar of the Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Roar+of+the+Wurm) was:

The Solar Flare decks in the original wave were all perfectly happy to [Zombify](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zombify) after [researching compulsively](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=researching+compulsively).


But if you had [Dread Return](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return), you could [Zombify](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zombify) at no mana cost, provided you had three creatures.


No problem, right?


Um...


The problem is, sometimes it is easier to string together three creatures than it is to rustle up four lands. For example, say you are very lucky (and also very cruel).


You might...


Play a first turn [Nomads *en*-Kor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nomads+en-Kor), then follow up with a second turn [Cephalid Illusionist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cephalid+Illusionist) (I told you, you were lucky).


You repeatedly target the Illusionist with the Nomad's damage prevention ability, setting yourself up to put every card in your deck into your graveyard, if you so choose (you more-or-less so chose). Along the way, you flip three copies of [Narcomoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba), all of which happily hit the battlefield.


When your graveyard seems jazzed enough, you flash back the *one* copy of [Dread Return](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) (you only *need* one as you are going to get it inevitably by binning your entire library) by sacrificing the three [Narcomoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba)s. Then you have a [Sutured Ghoul](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sutured+Ghoul) of enormous size, wearing, incidentally, a [Dragon Breath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragon+Breath).


And you kill the other guy.


Turn *two.*

To be fair, at the time we got to play with [Fact or Fiction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction), and it wasn't immediately obvious that [Deep Analysis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis) was that good. Kibler played the one copy— in his sideboard no less— simply so he could call his deck something uncouth (notice the three copies of [Probe](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Probe) in the main deck).


Yes ladies, even nine years ago, he was a dreamboat of unparalleled wittiness.


Well, it turns out that that one [Deep Analysis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis) was better than anyone had anticipated. You could discard it to [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) or [Probe](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Probe) and it would be a fine two-mana draw-two. Other players were even more focused and aggressive with [Deep Analysis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis), pairing it with faster discard outlets like [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel), [Merfolk Looter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Merfolk+Looter), or [Aquamoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aquamoeba).

Michael Krumb won this Grand Prix with Friggorid; the Top 8 featured a US National Champion in Antonino De Rosa, a future R&D member in Tom LaPille (both playing Friggorid), and eventual Caw-Blade master Dave Shiels with The Rock.


Anyway, before there was Dredge, there was Friggorid. The general idea was that you would bin various dredge cards with [Zombie Infestation](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zombie+Infestation) (this could occur on the first turn with [Chrome Mox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox)) or other outlets, such as [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) or [Tolarian Winds](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tolarian+Winds). Then you would, you know, *dredge*. Over the course of the game you would put enough black creatures in the graveyard "passively" to continuously attack with your [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid)s, which were pretty resilient to creature removal. [Deep Analysis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis) was not the central focus of the deck, but again, you would be dredging cards into the graveyard, and could "passively" put [Deep Analysis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis) there. Once down, [Deep Analysis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis)— for the bargain price of two mana— could ship another twelve or so cards to the graveyard (provided you had the requisite number of available [Golgari Grave-Troll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll)s), which would keep the whole thing going, until somebody put an eye out (you know, with an [Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) attack).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td173_card03.jpg)Possibly the most elegant and beloved of the flashback cards is [Call of the Herd](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd).


This is a card that was equally adopted by red-green beatdown decks (which just cast it on the second turn with [Llanowar Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) or [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)), midrange decks like some versions of The Rock, and even dedicated control decks. A 3/3 for three mana can be format-defining (depending on the format), and [Call of the Herd](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) just gives you an extra 3/3—pure, simple, card advantage. [Call of the Herd](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) could block red creatures, soak up burn, or of course threaten to win the game. In later years, its Elephants would carry [Sword of Fire and Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice), or rumble into the Red Zone bearing a [Moldervine Cloak](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moldervine+Cloak). It was a favorite of green-blue (though never "Green-Blue Madness"), red-green, and anyone else with a Looter, a [Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mongrel), or just four lands to string together.


[Call of the Herd](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) was the honest player's look at flashback, but like any and all could be spun up to a wild level of excitement when paired with a little bit of library manipulation.


The sorcery hit the scene at its first Pro Tour—ironically, in Extended—where it made it all the way to the finals while being fueled by [Intuition](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intuition) (for four Elephants). The only thing that could stop the stampede of so many 3/3 threats?


Kai Budde.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td173_card02.jpg)As we near the conclusion of this Top 10 list, we hit what many consider the most skill-intensive card of all time: [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy).


We have already seen [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) in some of the above lists. In Friggorid (or Dredge, or in concert with [Narcomoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba) in Cephalid Breakfast), you could cast it for free. Can you imagine how frustrating it must have been to be attacked by some number of 3/1 hasted creatures—none of which were particularly eligible targets for your removal, all of which were coming back next turn—but instead of just dying like they were supposed to (per their text boxes), the opponent had the audacity— nay, *cruelty—* to sacrifice one to [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) you?


[Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) forced you to read your opponent; it wasn't like [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), in that you got the information (although it worked well with [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), with one setting up the other).


As an aside, I once missed with both a [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress)*and* a [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy)*in the same turn!*


I won the next turn though, as all I really cared about was how big my [Quirion Dryad](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quirion+Dryad) got.


End Aside.


One of my favorite questions came from Antonino De Rosa and Gerard Fabiano at an Extended Grand Prix. The duo were playing Ant's Red-Black Goblins deck, which splashed black for [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy). Gerard polled everyone he could find:



> 
> "Your hand is pretty scripted. You are going to play [Goblin Piledriver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Piledriver) into [Goblin Warchief](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Warchief) into [Goblin Ringleader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Ringleader). Your plays after turn one are pretty much all made for you. Your only choice is on turn one, when you cast [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy). You don't know what the opponent is, so it is going to be blind...
> 
> 
> *"What do you name?"*
> 
> 
> 


This was the kind of game **Magic** was at the height of [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy). I really had to think to answer that one, but I think I got it right with "[Vampiric Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)" (that is what Gerard actually named, and he won the game).


There is a similar story involving being hit *by* a blind [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) with [Vampiric Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor) in hand.

Part of what made [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) so skill-testing was not just the ability to hit the opponent, but the attitude you took when trying to hit. Some players bragged about their hit percentages ("I get [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) 75% of the time!"). Others named *what they would lose to* rather than what they thought the opponent had in hand. "Who cares if you hit an irrelevant card—even if they have it —if it isn't even going to beat you?"


[Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) was a rare card embraced by beatdown (as the Goblins we referenced a moment ago), combo (to protect their combos and even enable them—for example, the flashback being used to trigger an [Academy Rector](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Academy+Rector)), or as a combo breaker. It was used in control paired with [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), or run solo by the truly confident.


[Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) still sees some play in Legacy, typically alongside [Narcomoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba) or Bridge From Below, generally in areas where it is going to be free, and devastating.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td173_card01.jpg)My favorite flashback card of all time happens to be legal once again, thanks to being reprinted in *Innistrad*.


So you can go out and play it!


What put [Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) over a card like [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy), which is thought to be the most skill-testing card of all time? Or over a beloved instrument of fair play, like [Call of the Herd](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd)?


Simple: No other flashback card has legitimately been "the best card in the format" except [Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge).

Appropriately, Fortier's [Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge)s were splashed in a blue-black deck with a [Seat of the Synod](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seat+of+the+Synod) and a basic [Plains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains). Typical.


There have been Extended formats where really all you needed in life was an [Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) and a little luck, and you could take on the world. [Isochron Scepter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter)? Thanks for making my two-for-one a three-for-one (if not four-for-one). [Lotus Bloom](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom)? That still hits play sometime before you actually move to your main phase, right? Because if so...


Affinity? *Check*.


Opposing [Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jitte)s and Swords? *Check*.


That other joker's one [Seat of the Synod](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seat+of+the+Synod), or [Chrome Mox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) package, or even [Sensei's Divining Top](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) when we get the timing right? *Check*, *check*, and— believe it or not— *check* (sometimes).


Even today, you can play [Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) as part of an overall package of artifact hate that can send a [Tempered Steel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tempered+Steel) mage's head spinning. Four [Acidic Slime](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Acidic+Slime)*and* four [Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge)? Yeah, like *that's* realistic.


But you know what? It can be.


[Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) is for a surgeon.


[Call of the Herd](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) is for a value player.


[Dread Return](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) is for— let's face it— a gigantic meanie jerk.


But [Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge)? That card dominates— and dominates often— regardless of what else is in your sixty. Gerry Thompson advocated adding the [Copperline Gorge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperline+Gorge) to play it in red-blue. Patrick Chapin stretched his Olivia mana all four ways to include it in his sideboard. Many a blue-black [Psychatog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) deck has leaned on the interaction between *Onslaught* fetch lands and *Ravnica* dual lands to borrow just one green mana.


Why?


[Ancient Grudge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) is that damn good.
