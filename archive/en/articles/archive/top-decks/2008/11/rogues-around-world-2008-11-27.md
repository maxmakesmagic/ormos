Rogues around the World
=======================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on November 27, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic."

So we have spent a fair amount of time talking about some of the more mainstream decks in the Standard format, including the really big [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) decks like [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool) Control and the Fae; and the beatdown suite, including the Red Deck, its cousin [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) Beatdown, Kithkin, and/or other White Weenie decks.


This week I wanted to highlight some of the more unusual decks out there, that while a bit left-of-center, are still quite viable, and in fact, are actually *winning tournaments* all over the world!


First up is a "Boat-Brew" by my old Righteous Babe teammate Brian Kowal.


Brian used this inventive homebrew to win a spot on the Game in the Gulf cruise.

 There are three main themes going on in this deck:



![Ranger of Eos](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ranger+of+Eos)

**1. The [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) Engine**


The primary forward-moving plan in this deck involves choosing a number of cards eligible to be plucked out of the æther with Antoine Ruel's Invitational card, [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos). The primary candidates are [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) and [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny). [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny) is of course one of the most popular threats in Standard, and is even played in Extended Zoo decks. This card has humble beginnings but can rise to Akroma size to win the game all by itself. Its companion, [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic), is one of the most popular one-drops of all time. Though a little on the outs in the popularity department right now, [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) is a key tool against the dominating Fae deck in Standard (last week Pennsylvania State Champion and  

 Fae expert Brett Blackman called it a "two-for-one"). Finally, Brian played a lone [Burrenton Forge-Tender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burrenton+Forge-Tender), the embarrassing 61st card. This card is not something you necessarily want to draw in every matchup, or in every game, but it is a one-card insurance policy against [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout)*and* a superb tool against Red Deck Wins.


**2. The [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) Synergies**


Many of the creatures in Brian's deck were chosen for their size up front ... specifically, they have 2 or fewer points of power. [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap), for instance, was chosen as the deck's nod to spot removal. It is slower than a [Lash Out](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lash+Out) and can't even slow down a [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge), but as a 2-power creature, [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap) has long term synergies with [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark). [Siege-Gang Commander](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander) is a fine threat in and of itself, but can have completely worn out the opponent's defensive capabilities by the second time around.


**3. The Mana Acceleration**


This deck plays essentially twice the mana acceleration of any other deck in the format. [Mind Stone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Stone) can help put the deck in [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) position on the third turn ... and you know how I feel about [Knight of the White Orchid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid). There are some matches where you will silently pump the fist at *losing* the die roll, and [Knight of the White Orchid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid) can actually exploit the fact that this deck runs a little light on lands.

Brian feels that [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) is just about the strongest card in *Shards of Alara*, and while we think about it primarily as a weapon for thumping Five-Color Control decks (they are often creature-poor and have difficulty attacking a planeswalker), Ajani is just fine against, say, the Red Deck. Think about it: You can ding a [Boggart Ram-Gang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Ram-Gang) with the "[Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix)" ability on Ajani, and the opponent will *still* have to send a burn spell in Ajani's direction, or will probably lose to its time management sooner rather than later.


All together, this deck attacks from several different angles: early pressure, [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos) card advantage, [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) card advantage, and sticking a planeswalker whenever the opportunity arises. Combined with the massive number of 1/1 and 2/X dorks that can muck up the Red Zone and block potential threats to Ajani's wellbeing, it can be a difficult deck to overcome, especially if you are not prepared for it. Few opponents will be.

 Nathan's deck, which is reminiscent of the [Fellowship of the Ring](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Y5G9ZjYo8-k) deck featured during [Pro Tour–Berlin](/en/node/502416), is a classic example of hating anyone and everyone.


For the most part, the true control decks are vulnerable to planeswalkers; these decks, full of [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout)s and [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn)s, are simply not optimized to deal with this particular type of permanent. The planeswalker decks supplement the rest of their card space with—how do we say this—all kinds of ways to kill creatures.

Nathan's deck ran not just [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) but [Hallowed Burial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Burial), [Naya Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naya+Charm), and even [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) (which can kayo other people's planeswalkers). So the forward-moving parts of this deck are all about planeswalker offense; the balance is creature defense.


This deck has a tremendous amount of acceleration. In addition to the [Mind Stone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Stone)s we already espied in Brian Kowal's deck, Braymore's planeswalkers can ride [Fertile Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fertile+Ground) for acceleration and additional synergy (turn-two [Fertile Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fertile+Ground) not only makes turn-three [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker), but Garruk can untap that [Fertile Ground](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fertile+Ground)-enhanced land for even more free mana, turn after turn).

 Ian Woodley presented some of the strongest cards from Alara's Naya shard and combined them with a planeswalker sub-theme that allowed him to attack his opponents from multiple angles.


[Woolly Thoctar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woolly+Thoctar) is a card we don't see very often, but it seems like a gigantic beating. 5/4 for only three mana? This one is bigger than [Ravenous Baloth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Baloth) for one mana less. Thanks to [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise), this gigantic threat can be in the Red Zone on turn three.

But the really exciting card in this one is [Realm Razer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Realm+Razer). We have talked a lot about the game-winningness that is [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) in Five-Color Control, but [Realm Razer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Realm+Razer) does a lot of the same job, arguably more, for a little less mana. You drop it—potentially with [Guttural Response](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guttural+Response) backup in Game 2—and the opponent might not be able to play spells ever again, let alone actually win the game. After this big [Armageddon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon)-type effect, you can clean up with your [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus) or Thoctar, and crush the enemy in just a couple of attacks.

 This deck is a synergistic combination of largely Bant cards, many or most of which are both green and white.


Being both green and white, creatures like [Rhox War Monk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rhox+War+Monk) and "The New Girl" herself [Stoic Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoic+Angel) benefit greatly from teaming with [Wilt-Leaf Liege](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wilt-Leaf+Liege). Now [Wilt-Leaf Liege](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wilt-Leaf+Liege) is itself an interesting piece of the metagame puzzle. A few weeks ago we were cheerleading the [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) Beatdown strategy (and I still love that strategy) ... but it should be pretty clear that [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) would have a rough go of it against this volume of defensive, big back-ended, life-gaining creatures ... especially backed up by [Wilt-Leaf Liege](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wilt-Leaf+Liege), which is hell on the signature card, [Blightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning), itself.

[Wilt-Leaf Liege](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wilt-Leaf+Liege) is also very powerful against the card we have been fixated on from a strategic standpoint for the majority of our Standard discussions, [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum). Can you imagine the horror a Five-Color Control player would experience if you sacrificed, say, a [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) and discarded one—or more—[Wilt-Leaf Liege](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wilt-Leaf+Liege)s?


This deck also sports [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)! Due to its more efficient creatures, unlike most [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)-packing decks in Standard, I think that if it has a lead, The New Girl can simply seek to remain the beatdown by protecting its squad with [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command) rather than necessarily holding at least one for the opponent's [Cruel Ultimatum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cruel+Ultimatum) (but of course many games will dictate just that).


Having played Ian's deck a couple of times, I would respectfully suggest some tuning of the mana base. The New Girl has a huge number of [Yavimaya Coast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yavimaya+Coast)s, [Adarkar Wastes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Adarkar+Wastes), and so on, and I found myself—especially against beatdown decks—in a position to lose close games due to drawing too many of these pain lands.


Still ... a very synergistic and enjoyable deck to play. There are a good number of games when you have the initiative where a beatdown player can simply never keep up with all your life gain. [Stoic Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoic+Angel) herself is quite the asset in my experience, a solitary attacker even without the true exalted stamp.
