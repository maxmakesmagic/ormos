All in all an interesting thirty-two deck lists...


Blue envelopes go to [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud), Green-White [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp), Dredge, Loam, and Mono-Red Burn; interesting paths for all of these decks. [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) has probably never seen the level of popularity that it "deserved" in this metagame, but it picked up a blue envelope in one of its two Top 8 appearances. Green-White 'Wisp has quietly become "a real deck," picking up a win where two looks at the same deck faced off in the finals for the Hollywood invitation. Dredge remains one of if not *the* most decorated decks of the season , picking up another win, albeit via its *only* Top 8 appearance of the week (strange in and of itself). Mono-Red Burn [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast)ed its way to its second (to the best of this writer's knowledge) North American invitation in a week, where it, too, was down representation (at least compared to a few weeks ago). No deck posted more than one win.


So what can you expect? There will be PTQs this weekend, not to mention the much anticipated [Grand Prix–Philadelphia](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/grandprix/philadelphia08), this spring's glittering gem in the City of Brotherly Love.


Last week, Blue-Green 'Tron was the only double PTQ winner.... This week, it was tied for the most Top 8 appearances, but had no blue envelope. First turn *Invasion* sacrifice land? How do you know if it is TEPS or [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal)? Right now, I would bet on TEPS... It was one of the most popular decks in terms of Top 8 appearances, twice as popular as old buddy [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal). Domain Zoo, a consistent Top 8 performer since the first week, eclipsed TEPS and Goblins (with four Top 8 appearances each); everything else, including recent Grand Prix winner Previous Level Blue, was pretty scattered.


### How Not to Beat Previous Level Blue


Okay, what about our title this week? How *don't* you lose to Previous Level Blue? (Hint: Thinking about playing Previous Level Blue? Don't get sloppy!)


I was recently asked by a former PT player who will remain nameless if two [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter)s didn't just flat-out beat the blue deck *du jour.* Missing something? All of the deck's obvious finishers—those four [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)s and that gigantic [Rude Awakening](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rude+Awakening) that sticks out like a sore thumb in every list—are *green.* While the deck technically has a fair bit of *access* to green with eight or so *Onslaught* dual lands, in terms of physical green sources... there are typically only the two [Breeding Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool)s:

![Ghost Quarter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ghost+Quarter)

There are a couple of issues here; certainly if you can contain the first [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), managing to take out both [Breeding Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool)s may be an issue for especially the sloppily unprepared Previous Level Blue player. However if the opponent has even a little foresight, you're probably not going to be able to catch him, thusly, with his green jeans down. If you're the Previous Level Blue player and you have an open shot to break your [Polluted Delta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta)—and your opponent has already shown you the willingness to trade [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) to cycle you into your next [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island)—you might not want to go and get the other [Breeding Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool) even if he hasn't shown you a second 'Quarter yet. I know, *I know*, that's how you ran it in testing but every game is different (it says so right on the packs) and you have to alter your tactical game play on a situation-by-situation basis in order to not, you know, walk directly into the other guy's spider web.

So one of the reasons this is not an automatic win strategy is that the opponent can simply refuse to let you lock him this way. If it were me, I would wait until the turn I was planning to win with [Rude Awakening](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rude+Awakening) to expose my other green source... at least if I could help it. I remember the old Sunday afternoons at Neutral Ground, in the days of the Grudge Match, when deck design in New York was at an all time high, watching Eric Kesselman over his shoulder playing his Eye-Go deck, refusing to play down lands against [Fires of Yavimaya](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fires+of+Yavimaya), discarding [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s and [Vendetta](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendetta)s rather than expose the lands in his hand, winning, refusing to make himself a target for the uncounterable [Obliterate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obliterate). Ugly **Magic**, maybe, but the kind of play that can cut off the opponent at the knees.


Say you have "the read" and you are going to run this game plan against Previous Level Blue anyway. Both the [Breeding Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool)s are waiting to get Quartered and you have the juice.... *You still have to play with follow through!* Remember that Previous Level Blue has got seven main deck [Control Magic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Control+Magic)s, including the recurring savagery of [Vedalken Shackles](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vedalken+Shackles). *Do not* show the opponent a card he can use to win. Congratulations, Mage: Your plan is now likely to win by decking! Count 'em up before you go down the [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) route.... An errant [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) might make winning a little harder than the automatic breeze boulevard you anticipated against the [Breeding Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breeding+Pool)s.


Finally—and this is awfully important given that you likely have to win via decking unless you can contain the [Control Magic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Control+Magic)s—Previous Level Blue can just set up to win with [Ancestral Vision](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Vision)(s). Those suspend sorceries are usually there to help the blue mage get ahead in cards and keep his hand full of annoying responses, but in a pinch, he can direct one or more in your direction to finish you off Extended *1999* style.

Hansen's Green-White 'Wisp beat Houston's in the Wichita final.


So how does this deck work?



![Tallowisp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Tallowisp)

The central character in this Selesnya drama is [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp). Many of the archetype's card choices—from metagame bomb [Phantom Centaur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantom+Centaur) to unusual defensive measures like [Otherworldly Journey](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Otherworldly+Journey)—are crafted around the [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp)'s specific ability. The 'Wisp will typically find [Armadillo Cloak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armadillo+Cloak) (which makes [Phantom Centaur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantom+Centaur) unkillable and solves all the problems on [Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic)) but can also set up the [Griffin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Griffin+Guide) singleton to send one of the deck's monsters to the air or defend against even Dredge's [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%2C+Angel+of+Wrath) with [Temporal Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temporal+Isolation)... at instant speed.

Remember, changeling [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus) is *also* a *Spirit*! Early in the season, many Doran decks were sideboarding [Mystic Enforcer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Enforcer) for the mirror match. Between [Phantom Centaur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantom+Centaur) and [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus), the Green-White 'Wisp deck has a ton of protection from black to match and even dominate creature decks like Doran on the ground. Add [Armadillo Cloak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armadillo+Cloak) to [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus) (and get an untap)... Don't be surprised to see a 22-point life swing!


Both decks pack the maximum number of [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg)s, which make the deck very difficult to beat for certain combo or control decks that rely on four-mana spells ([Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction), [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return), [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God)); Hansen's deck packs even more than the usual four, with [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) giving him access to a virtual six, as well as main deck access to [Spike Feeder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spike+Feeder) and [Yixlid Jailer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yixlid+Jailer) against Dredge, [Harmonic Sliver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Harmonic+Sliver) against artifacts and enchantments, and [Selesnya Sanctuary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Selesnya+Sanctuary) in a mana pinch.


All in all, this archetype has been sneaking up on the metagame for most of the season, but it actually seems well positioned against many of the other top decks in the format. The [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) matchup is probably rough even with [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) ([Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed) in addition to spot removal), but it doesn't take a lot of imagination to see [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) and the sideboard dominating Dredge, [Armadillo Cloak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armadillo+Cloak) burying Mono-Red Burn, and so on. Definitely underplayed on a national level.

An interesting choice from Larson was deciding to play no dedicated anti-Dredge—no [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt)s in this deck, nor [Engineered Explosives](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Explosives). However, check out those four copies of [Echoing Truth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Truth)... With two mana open—and provided his hand hasn't been wrecked by a triad of flashed Cabal Therapies—[Echoing Truth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Truth) is a very serviceable answer to [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) and its never-ending cadre of Zombie tokens (for one turn, at least).

We've seen Domain Zoo decks all season, and this week, the strategy remained one of the most popular. However, even when these decks have played Gaea's Might (usually three copies) they have erred on the side of [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) and [Vindicate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate)... We have seen few if any of the [Boros Swiftblade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Swiftblade)s that helped buy Hall of Famer Raphael Levy his back-to-back Grand Prix wins.


Well, that trend ended with Richard Townley's deck from Burlington, VT. Townley not only played the full four Gaea's Mights, he borrowed a couple of Red Deck Wins's [Reckless Charge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckless+Charge)s and laid them down next to his returning [Boros Swiftblade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Swiftblade)s for even more erratic explosiveness. Imagine... It's turn three and the opponent is tapped out from removing your [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). You lay down Swifty, tap another ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif), and bam! Eight to the face!

This deck is a look at an archetype Brian David-Marshall featured in greater detail in [a recent edition](/en/articles/archive/week-was/rogue-adventures-2008-02-29) of [The Week That Was](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/columnarchive&column=TheWeekThatWas). Essentially a [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) combo deck, Weiss Spice simply goes about the storm setup in a different way than TEPS or Heartbeat.

While the deck has [Rite of Flame](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rite+of+Flame), [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song), and [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom), similar to TEPS, Weiss Spice has a unique method of generating storm copies: [Quiet Speculation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quiet+Speculation). You can [Quiet Speculation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quiet+Speculation) for, say, three [Lava Dart](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart)s. The turn you want to go off and win, you set up with your mana, then flash the [Lava Dart](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart)s (possibly with [Pyromancer's Swath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath) already on the board), *then* play [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) or whatever storm card you want (with the [Swat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swat)h down, [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot) might just be game). The [Lava Dart](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Dart)s not only deal damage but produce as many as three incremental storm copies at no mana cost... You just have to sacrifice some [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s, but the game isn't going to last the next turn anyway, the [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s are in all likelihood tapped already.


[Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top)*into* [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) offers another fairly unique opportunity to generate Storm copies; [Final Fortune](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Final+Fortune) is there to smooth out what looks like a low-margin game into a story for the grandkids.

It should come as no surprise that Faeries—already a solid archetype—has been improved by *Morningtide*. Both the winner's deck and the finalist's were upgraded with [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), kind of an aggressive Faerie [Phyrexian Arena](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Arena). [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) can generate a steady stream of threats compatible with "Lords" like [Scion of Oona](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scion+of+Oona) and set up big prowl spells like [Notorious Throng](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Notorious+Throng), even when it looks like the deck is completely locked down with permission or mass removal. Don't forget that against bigger creatures, [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) tokens can *block*, allowing you to trade 1 life for the much greater chunk of your total that an aggressive [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) or [Countryside Crusher](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Countryside+Crusher) might demand.


Ruel went absolutely crazy with his *Morningtide* improvements, adding [Oona's Blackguard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oona%27s+Blackguard) and [Notorious Throng](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Notorious+Throng) in addition to [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom).


Apparently, [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) is so good, you can run it outside of Faeries, in a three-color mid-range aggressive deck:

![Reveillark](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Reveillark)

Ishimura's [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom)s could combine with the "[Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun)" from [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) or the fear from [Profane Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Profane+Command)... and it's just good enough to run on its own merits.

The really exciting deck if you haven't seen it yet is a [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) combo deck played by former Player of the Year Kenji Tsumura, as well as standouts Akira Asahara and Kazuya Mitamura. This deck can play like any other Blink deck... but [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) can play another, extremely compelling, combo.


Imagine that using your copious [Careful Consideration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Careful+Consideration)s and [Mulldrifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter)s, you have got [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) in the graveyard and a [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) in hand along with [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity). Provided you have something productive in your graveyard (a [Riftwing Cloudskate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Riftwing+Cloudskate), say, or simply an evoked [Mulldrifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter)) you can "go off" with this deck. The next step is to activate [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) for zero as many times as you'd like, all in response to one another. When you finally let the first [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) roll, what happens is that everyone dies, including the [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) and the [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity). When the 'Lark bites it, return the [Body Double](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Body+Double) copying [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) (and something productive) and let the next [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity) activation resolve. Now reverse it and pick up the original, and so on interchanging the two 'Larks, then repeat until you've returned all of the opponent's cards, drawn your deck, whatever you like based on the cards available to you... provided it can be accomplished via repeated access to two 2-power creatures. Your advantage on the board at the end of it all should be massive one way or another, plus the deck will often be in a position to win with a single attack powered by [Mirror Entity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirror+Entity).

Finally, the Top 8 was rounded out by a pair of Black-Green Elf Aggro decks, each with a unique addition from *Morningtide*. Masuno's was the more obvious, with [Bramblewood Paragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bramblewood+Paragon) powering up a succession of [Civic Wayfinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Civic+Wayfinder)s and [Imperious Perfect](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperious+Perfect)s, whereas Fujimoto's pair of [Wolf-Skull Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wolf-Skull+Shaman)s interacted nicely with every non-[Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) creature in his deck.
