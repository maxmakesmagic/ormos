The End of Fun
==============



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on December 10, 2009 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











The 2009s, a.k.a. state championships (the most fun tournaments of the year, in large part) were this past weekend, but I want to wait a week before trying to digest the many deck lists and snapshot of Standard that will come out of it; not all states have reported yet, and I want to make sure we can take a longer, fuller, view of the results as a whole when we move to wrap-up mode.

So for this week we have a respite of full-on fun before going over the number of just how many Jund decks littered Top 8s across the continent.

But still in Standard, still in the context of the 2009s, I decided to highlight some fun and different—but still viable—decks that made Top 8, or even won!

I know that we mentioned the "Spread 'Em" strategy [last week](/Magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/td/67), but with Ben Tash's victory in Kentucky (and additional Top 8 performances elsewhere), I figured that it might be worth a more in-depth look at this frustrating, sometimes silly—but again sometimes really effective—control strategy.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td68_seas.jpg)  
Spread 'Em plays twelve Cascade cards: the ubiquitous [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), its common sidekick [Captured Sunlight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Captured+Sunlight), and Extended staple [Ardent Plea](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ardent+Plea). [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) and [Captured Sunlight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Captured+Sunlight) can *only* cascade into [Ardent Plea](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ardent+Plea) or one of the two-mana spells; [Ardent Plea](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ardent+Plea) can only cascade into one of the two-mana spells. So a 3/2 body, 4 life, or an Exalted enchantment are all there essentially as redundancies funneling the Spread 'Em draw into those two-mana spells.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Spreading+Seas)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)  
And there are only two kinds of two-mana spells, the namesake [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) and additional redundant backup [Convincing Mirage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Convincing+Mirage).

As the title of this section goes, [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) is very nearly a real card. 

I know it looks a bit silly, but a two-mana spell that replaces itself—regardless of its effect—is often playable. Is turning a land into an [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) more or less valuable than, say, 2 life? Because Renewed Faith (generally deployed for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif) with the text "Gain 2 life. Draw a card") was a staple of Standard and block, and heavily played even in Extended! There are many such examples.

[Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas), alone, has a relatively insignificant effect on the game. It doesn't actually *destroy* one of the opponent's lands, just makes it less efficient. You can still tap the target for mana ... as long as you can do something with ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif). However when about 60% of a deck's cards become [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas), you have the potential for some rock and roll. 

[Convincing Mirage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Convincing+Mirage) is far less desirable than [Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) (because you don't get to draw a card), but in the context of being one of so *many* "[Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas)," it can make up for the card advantage ... with card advantage. 

That's how mana denial works, after all.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td68_mirageoversavage.jpg)If you put a [Convincing Mirage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Convincing+Mirage) on, say, a tapped [Savage Lands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savage+Lands) on your second turn (lucky you, you went first), your Jund opponent has almost no opportunity to make a play on the second turn, land lying fallow. Now you cast another on a [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) or whatever, and still no plays from your opponent, with a hand is full of [Putrid Leech](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech)es, Bloodbraid Elves, [Broodmate Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broodmate+Dragon)s ... all cards that require more than one color of mana. By denying one color of mana, you might be cornering your opponent into an unenviable [Mind Twist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Twist) situation, where he or she might *think* that hand is full of cards ... but has no capability to play any of them.

The Spread 'Em deck only works because of the discipline of playing *only*[Spreading Seas](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spreading+Seas) and [Convincing Mirage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Convincing+Mirage)s on the two. You literally *can't* play other cards like spot creature removal, [Negate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Negate)s, or what have you without foiling the redundancy provided by the cascade spells. Then you just have a *regular* bunch of, you know, [Convincing Mirage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Convincing+Mirage)s.

The Spread 'Em deck has a transformative anti-beatdown sideboard. The theme of playing what look like "silly" spells in the main carries over to the sideboard, with the now-[Convincing Mirage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Convincing+Mirage)-less Spread 'em Cascading into—believe it or not—[Deft Duelist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deft+Duelist).

To be fair, the Duelist can be quite the [Goblin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Guide) containment unit!

This article is supposed to be about fun (but still competitive) decks ... and as far as I can tell, Roger Sampson's deck is a full-on pack of wild dogs.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td68_mesa.jpg)  
The unique feature of this deck is the presence of [Mesa Enchantress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mesa+Enchantress). When you have [Mesa Enchantress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mesa+Enchantress) on the battlefield, every spell you cast allows you to draw a card. Coincidentally, this deck is chock full of serviceable enchantments!

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mesa+Enchantress)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mesa+Enchantress)  
[Journey to Nowhere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Journey+to+Nowhere) and [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) have proven themselves to be playable in other decks in this environment. Players run them all the time as flexible answers to different kinds of permanents, or proxies for [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) with no drawbacks. In the context of a [Mesa Enchantress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mesa+Enchantress) deck, these enchantments go from being *about* good enough to being consistent card advantage.

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130614051355im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/td/td68_3Cards1.jpg)  
[Luminarch Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Ascension) is not so much "good enough" to play as a legitimate breaker. There are a good many decks in this format that shake and shiver at the thought of a turn-two Ascension. Again, in this deck, the turn two looming threat is *also* a kind of cantrip.

The last tier of main deck enchantments includes [Pacifism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pacifism) and [Sigil of the Empty Throne](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+of+the+Empty+Throne). [Pacifism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pacifism) is a card that has been played off-and-on, sporadically, over the last nine years or so, but has never really been a Tier One kind of card. Once it starts drawing extra ones ... [Pacifism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pacifism) starts looking more viable. 

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sigil+of+the+Empty+Throne)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+of+the+Empty+Throne)  
[Sigil of the Empty Throne](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+of+the+Empty+Throne) is a card that my good friend Brian David-Marshall has been trying to harness since it was first spoiled ... but at five mana with no initial utility (despite a deep throng of dependencies), [Sigil of the Empty Throne](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+of+the+Empty+Throne) has, to date, found itself with an empty dance card. However, with [Mesa Enchantress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mesa+Enchantress) in the mix, there seems to be more incentive to playing lots of enchantments ... both White permanents can share the possibilities, profitably.

Sampson's deck is one long list of creature murderers, with [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) and [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) bolstering the enchantment set of Journey, Ring, and [Pacifism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pacifism).

The cool thing about playing so many enchantments that are already "good enough" is that Roger's deck does not need to play a specific way. It can play cards off the top, trading one-for-one against creatures, eventually winning with [Baneslayer Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel); or it can fill its hand while doing the same thing.
