Gold Star!
==========



 Posted in **Serious Fun**
 on May 19, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_kelly-digges.jpg)
By Kelly Digges




 Kelly Digges has had many roles at Wizards over the years, including creative text writer, R&D editor, website copyeditor, lead website editor, Serious Fun column author, and design/development team member on multiple sets.
 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_scale.jpg)It's Solid Gold Week! What does that mean? No, we're not going to talk about carats and troy ounces (although I did once calculate whether some **Magic** cards might be [worth their weight in gold](/en/articles/archive/ask-wizards-april-2007-2007-04-01)). This week is all about *Alara Reborn*, pretty gold borders, and cards that take two colors to cast.

I've [written in the past](/en/articles/archive/serious-fun/traitor-we-trust-2013-03-19) about the ten two-color, "mono-multicolored" decks my friend Laura put together from my collection. They started out, before *Shards of Alara* was released, as *Ravnica* / *Shadowmoor* decks. Since then, I've been updating, most recently with all the golden goodies in *Alara Reborn*.

  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_goldCross.jpg)  
As you might recall, those decks have a "schtick" above and beyond being (originally) made of cards from just two blocks: every colored card in each deck is exactly two colors.

Quick aside: Unlike *Alara Reborn*, these five decks are not quite solid gold. They are, however, 100% multicolored. See, a number of the cards in them are hybrid. The rules treat hybrid cards and gold cards similarly—[Bull Cerodon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bull+Cerodon) and [Hearthfire Hobgoblin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hearthfire+Hobgoblin) are both red and white—but in practice, I think of them more as opposites. [Bull Cerodon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bull+Cerodon) takes red AND white to play; [Hearthfire Hobgoblin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hearthfire+Hobgoblin) takes red OR white. Anyway, these decks aren't all gold, but they are all multicolored. Close enough theme week purposes!

With such a big influx of new gold cards courtesy of *Alara Reborn*, it was time to give these decks (along with my five three-color shard decks) a major overhaul. And having overhauled them, it was time to actually use the five allied-color decks for their intended purpose: Star.

This deck has improved significantly of late. It gained an [Ith, High Arcanist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ith%2C+High+Arcanist) and [Grand Arbiter Augustin IV](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grand+Arbiter+Augustin+IV) that I picked up since the decks were made. I'm especially a fan of [Grand Arbiter Augustin IV](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grand+Arbiter+Augustin+IV), even if he does draw major hate. Making every spell in this deck ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161225105850im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless2.gif) cheaper and all my opponents' spells ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif) more expensive is just too much fun. The [Overrule](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrule)s came out in favor of [Offering to Asha](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Offering+to+Asha), the deck picked up Borderposts to help with mana, and, most importantly, four [Wall of Denial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Denial) came in to significantly bolster its early-game defenses.

This deck was overhauled substantially. The idea was that it could beat down one opponent with flying and unblockable creatures will milling another—a strategy uniquely suited to Star. It didn't quite work out that way, as you'll see, and my friend Dave concluded that the deck would work better if it focused on evasion creatures and left milling to a deck that could include [Forced Fruition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forced+Fruition), [Memory Erosion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memory+Erosion), and [Jace Beleren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren). This is the deck that, last time I wrote about it, I accidentally played with 74 cards. This time, there was apparently a different glitch: after the overhaul it only has 15 creatures, and the deck as a whole doesn't do much.

### All Star

As you may recall, in Star, five players sit in a circle. The two players across from you are your enemies, the two players next to you are your allies, and you win when your two enemies are no longer in the game. In the variant we play, the turn is passed across the circle and to the left.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_backOfCard.jpg)  
In its most classic incarnation, Star is played with five monocolored decks, arranged as on the back of a **Magic** card. And as on the back of the cards, each color is enemies with the colors across from it and friends with the colors next to it. You can play Star with any decks, but the color-based version is the original.

The two-color pairs—as with the five shards—can easily be arranged in a similar pattern. Green-white is friends with red-green and white-blue, the two pairs it shares a color with, and enemies with blue-black and black-red, the two pairs it doesn't share a color with.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_star1.jpg)  
(The same principle can be applied to the five enemy-color pairs, although I find that a little harder to wrap my head around. The tricky part is that you could argue either way as far as allies as enemies. It makes sense to say that white-black and red-white are allies because they share a color, but extrapolating that out, you end up with white-black's enemies being the two pairs that contain white and black's common ally, blue. Going the other direction, it would make an inverted sort of sense for white-black to *hate* red-white and black-green—enemy-color pairs are weeeeeird—but the color-sharing method makes more sense to me.)

With the five allied-color decks fully updated, I gathered four friends for a game of Star. I grabbed the green-white deck, since that's my favorite allied-color pair. Laura sat on my right with the red-green deck, and her friend Matt sat on my left with the white-blue deck. Across from me on the left was my co-worker Dave with blue-black, and next to him was another co-worker, confusingly also named Matt. For convenience, Ally-Matt is Matt W., and Enemy-Matt is Matt T.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_star2.jpg)  
We rolled two ten-sided dice to see who would go first. As my roll tumbled, Dave cried, "Multiply!" We all laughed about it and agreed, and I won the right to take the first turn, 32 to 21 to 15 to double zero. ("Multiply" is pretty harsh on standard ten-siders, which go from 0 to 9.)

Almost from the word go, this game of Star had a lot more interaction between multiple players—"multiplayer" activity, if you will—than many others I've played. Dave used [Agony Warp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Agony+Warp) to keep Laura's [Tattermunge Maniac](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tattermunge+Maniac) from damaging him while killing my [Qasali Pridemage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Qasali+Pridemage). Dave then put a [Helm of the Ghastlord](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Helm+of+the+Ghastlord) on Matt T.'s [Rakdos Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Guildmage) to help him beat up Matt W. and myself, because, as I mentioned, his deck was very creature-light.

### And Again?

That really hadn't taken very long, so we tinkered with our decks a bit and laid into each other again. I took out three [Sigil Blessing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sigil+Blessing) for three [Mercy Killing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mercy+Killing), realizing how tough it was that neither of Matt T.'s enemies had any removal in their decks.

Several of us got great openers this time—myself with two [Selesnya Evangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Selesnya+Evangel)s that set to work making an army, Dave with [Inkfathom Witch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkfathom+Witch) and [Wasp Lancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasp+Lancer), and Matt W. with not one, not two, but *three* [Wall of Denial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Denial). Laura wrecked Dave's opener with a [Branching Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Branching+Bolt), though, and Dave didn't do much else this game besides play [Deny Reality](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deny+Reality) three times on the [Deus of Calamity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deus+of+Calamity) she coughed up on turn five (cascading into [Agony Warp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Agony+Warp) for [Boggart Ram-Gang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Ram-Gang) one of those times).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_voidAndFriends.jpg)  
Matt T. didn't like what he was seeing across from him, so he played [Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Void) for 3, taking out two Borderposts of mine and all three of Matt W.'s Walls. I was very, very glad he hadn't opted to play [Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Void) for zero, which would have destroyed six Saprolings at this point and really set me back.

Even down on mana, I was in great shape, landing [Dauntless Escort](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dauntless+Escort) to protect my fungal squad and making ever more Saprolings. Laura was making the flashy plays, like suiting up [Deus of Calamity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deus+of+Calamity) with [Runes of the Deus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Runes%2Bof%2Bthe%2BDeus) and walloping Matt W.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_runes.jpg)  
All well and good. Amazing, actually. But the goal in Star is not only to beat your opponents; it's to beat your opponents faster than your allies beat theirs. Laura had also knocked Dave very low while trying to beat Matt—but not quite getting there—and I was able to take advantage of it. Before anyone knew what was happening, I was able to split up a huge assault force at Dave and Matt T., both of whom were at 5. Nobody was able to stop me, so I took out both my opponents in one turn, winning the game. With Saprolings.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf39_victory.jpg)  
### Good Times

As always, Star is a great evening of **Magic**. It's quick, it's brutal, and it's the fun kind of political, at least for me. I highly recommend it in its monocolor, two-color, three-color, or non-color-based incarnations.

I don't know how appealing it is to any of you out there to build decks that are 100% multicolor, but there is something to be said for decks in which every creature (or nearly every creature) can fully activate a [Shield of the Oversoul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shield+of+the+Oversoul), [Boartusk Liege](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boartusk+Liege), or, in the enemy-colored decks, [Nightsky Mimic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nightsky+Mimic). With *Alara Reborn* filling out the ranks of even enemy-colored gold cards, it's more possible than ever to go solid gold. I love the idea of a five-color, all-gold, all-creature deck packing [Ancient Ziggurat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Ziggurat), [Pillar of the Paruns](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pillar+of+the+Paruns), and [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool).
