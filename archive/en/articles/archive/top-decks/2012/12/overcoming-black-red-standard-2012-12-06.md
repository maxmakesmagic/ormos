Overcoming Black-Red in Standard
================================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on December 6, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 







Welcome back to Perilous Research, DailyMTG.com's exclusive **Magic Online** column. Today, we're going to discuss the ubiquitous Black-Red deck in an attempt to find its weaknesses. When *Return to Ravnica* first entered the fray of Standard, we saw a flurry of aggressive Zombie decks gain footing as the deck to beat. Midrange Jund decks quickly put those decks in their place. Out of those ashes rose three-, four-, and five-color [Unburial Rites](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Unburial+Rites) decks that abused [Angel of Serenity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angel+of+Serenity). These reanimation strategies quickly found themselves outmatched by Bant Control and four- and five-color control brews with many Planeswalkers. These hard control decks remained top dog for a few weeks until Adam Prosak and Gerry Thompson introduced the world to White-Blue Flash, a deck that performed exceptionally well against Bant Control in its original state. And so began the era of Black-Red Midrange. Almost identical versions of Black-Red won both [Grand Prix Charleston](/en/events/coverage/bolding-thunders-victory-charleston) and [Grand Prix San Antonio](/en/events/coverage/lytles-zombies-big-texas). 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/pr/pr224_knight_of_infamy.jpg)
[Knight of Infamy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) | Art by [Peter Mohrbacher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Peter%20Mohrbacher%22%5D)



 Black-Red Midrange decks weren't just winning Grand Prix. **Magic Online** was quickly inundated with the strategy and the deck began winning most Premier Events and posting 4–0 or 3–1 finishes with some level of consistency in the Daily Events. The deck seems to be very difficult to push out of the metagame. Let's take a look at one of the most recent Black-Red lists and discuss its strengths and weaknesses in the current Standard environment.

Why is this deck so menacing? The deck operates optimally at virtually every stage of the game:



* It is capable of blisteringly fast opening draws with eight 2-power, one-mana creatures to get things going.
* Then we have [Knight of Infamy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy), a creature that can attack into a telegraphed [Azorius Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azorius+Charm) free of care. [Knight of Infamy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) allows the Black-Red pilot to change gears if necessary. Many of the more aggressive decks in the format are Human-centric green-white decks that play a lot of white creatures. A single [Knight of Infamy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+Infamy) on the table can often hold back an entire squad while the green-white player is forced to find one of the [Garruk Relentless](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Relentless) he or she sideboarded in.
* As we work our way up the curve, we get to [Geralf's Messenger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geralf%27s+Messenger), [Falkenrath Aristocrat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Falkenrath+Aristocrat), [Hellrider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hellrider), and [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite). All of these creatures either have an "enters the battlefield" trigger, haste, or both. This severely hinders any type of strategy that plans on being reactive. Normally, you can simply outlast these kinds of decks by stocking up on removal and playing for a long game. However, players who want to play sorcery-speed removal like [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) are quickly discovering that they usually die to the constant bombardment of damage from haste creatures, [Hellrider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hellrider) triggers, and [Geralf's Messenger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geralf%27s+Messenger) triggers.

###  The Aggressive Route to Victory Against Black-Red


 So, where's the weakness? A quick look at the creature base in the Black-Red deck paints a very clear picture of the deck's major weakness. [Diregraf Ghoul](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diregraf+Ghoul), [Geralf's Messenger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geralf%27s+Messenger), and [Gravecrawler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gravecrawler) are all terrible on defense. These creatures either enter the battlefield tapped or can't block at all. As a result, many players have decided they can beat Black-Red by going underneath. The Black-Red deck will often stumble a bit in the early turns because it plays so many high-end cards. By applying extremely aggressive pressure in the earliest turns of the game you can put the Black-Red deck on its heels, force it off its aggressive midgame, and close the game with some type of reach. The deck that accomplishes this goal with the most consistency has proven itself to be Red:

 It seems like yesterday that decks with [Centaur Healer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Healer) and [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) were running amok in this format. Just a few weeks later, we've found ourselves in a surreal position, talking about Red's rise to tier one. Red decks have outperformed every other archetype in the most recent Daily Events. The deck is extremely straightforward: the goal is to kill the opponent before he or she takes over the game with big spells. The Black-Red Midrange deck has no choice but to trade blows with the Red deck and the Red deck is usually a full turn faster, giving it a huge edge in the matchup. 


 Red is surprisingly resilient given the current Standard environment, but the deck still struggles against cards like [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) and [Centaur Healer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Healer). Red is currently the biggest gamble in Standard. The deck performs very well against some of the most top-end control decks, Green-White and Black-Red. Unfortunately, there are a lot of archetypes in between that play [Centaur Healer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Healer), [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel), [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk), and [Huntmaster of the Fells](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Huntmaster+of+the+Fells). Decks that feature these cards are going to give the Red deck huge problems and there isn't much the Red deck can do about them unless it wants to go over the top by playing four copies of [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite), which isn't even an option unless you want to sideboard in additional lands. 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/pr/pr224_problems.jpg)
*[Centaur Healer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Healer), [Restoration Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Restoration+Angel), [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk), [Huntmaster of the Fells](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Huntmaster+of+the+Fells)*

  
Red is an excellent choice if you expect the right metagame, but the archetype has some very clear weaknesses and can often put you in the position of having an unwinnable matchup.


### Charging up the Middle Against Black-Red!


 Being more aggressive is definitely the best plan against Black-Red, but that strategy's weaknesses may outweigh its strengths in the long term, as the format diversifies itself again. There are a number of midrange decks that do reasonably well against Black-Red Midrange while still putting up game against the rest of the format. Remember, Black-Red Midrange is very similar to the decks from the earliest stages of *Return to Ravnica* Standard. 


 The original [Gravecrawler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gravecrawler) decks fell out of favor when Jund Midrange became the dominant strategy. Jund Midrange still sees some scattered success on **Magic Online**.  Once the dust settles, I expect Jund Midrange to be considered tier one for quite some time. The deck slaughters any deck that wants to win with creatures. The one weakness here is [Sphinx's Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation). The big mythic rare instant from *Return to Ravnica* can undo a lot of what this deck is working for and the deck doesn't have the aggressive route to victory that can go underneath the top end of the Bant Control decks. For this reason, Jund Midrange is forced to rely on [Slaughter Games](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Games) after sideboarding in an attempt to strip the opponent of any [Sphinx's Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation)s. Tapping out on the third or fourth turn to do something that doesn't affect the board isn't exactly what you want to be doing against the Bant Control decks, but at this point there doesn't seem to be a better option.

### Over the Top of Black-Red


As we've previously discussed, it's extremely difficult to fight Black-Red with answers because so much of the deck ends up whittling away the game via haste and damage triggers. Recently, we've seen a new breed of control deck consistently doing well in Daily Events and just beginning to poke it's head into the Top 8 of Premier Events.


 The Five-Color Control deck may seem like it's a disaster against Black-Red Aggro. Surprisingly, the deck performs much better than White-Blue Flash or Bant Control when trying to beat Black-Red. The matchup isn't necessarily good, but it's not quite as bad as Bant Control in this matchup, either. Two players with reasonable draws will probably find themselves in skill-testing matches that are usually won by the tightest pilot. However, if Five-Color draws multiple [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) and the Black-Red deck is able to find a timely [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite), the game may end out of nowhere.  Ramping into [Huntmaster of the Fells](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Huntmaster+of+the+Fells) and [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk) gives this deck the strength it needs against early aggression. Once this deck makes its way into the midgame, it continues to develop its mana while controlling the board with sweeping effects like [Terminus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminus) and [Supreme Verdict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Supreme+Verdict) while it establishes dominance via [Curse of Death's Hold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold), [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls), and Planeswalkers. The top end is the deck's strongest asset against Black-Red. [Gisela, Blade of Goldnight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gisela%2C+Blade+of+Goldnight) or [Griselbrand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Griselbrand) are both enough to punish opponents for trying to squeak in a few points and can usually win the game on their own. 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/pr/pr224_midgame.jpg)  
 I really like this deck because of how strong it is against the control decks while still maintaining a reasonable matchup against the aggro decks. The deck struggles with the speed of Mono-Red, but that deck will likely fall out of favor when [Centaur Healer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Centaur+Healer)s and [Thragtusk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thragtusk)s become the flavor of the week again. 


 Black-Red may be the deck to beat right now, but there are many paths to victory against the current best deck in the format. **Magic Online** gives us a unique insight into the future progression of the format and lets us know which decks have been rewarded by Black-Red's popularity.
