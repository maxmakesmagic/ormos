Players Tour Phoenix Day 1 Pioneer Metagame
===========================================



 Posted in **Competitive Gaming**
 on February 7, 2020 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/hosler.jpg)
By Corbin Hosler











Pioneer has been inverted.


It's only been around for a few weeks, but the combo of [Inverter of Truth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inverter+of+Truth) and [Thassa's Oracle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thassa%27s+Oracle) has taken the Pioneer metagame by storm. It has been compared to the infamous "[Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin)" combo, and the field at Players Tour Phoenix has taken to the Inverter combo in much the same way.


[Thassa's Oracle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thassa%27s+Oracle) is performing across the board. In addition to the Inverter deck, it also functions as the win condition for the Lotus Breach brew, which uses [Underworld Breach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Breach) and [Lotus Field](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Field) to generate copious amounts of mana and use it to churn through the deck before winning with the Merfolk.


Pioneer's traditional staples has shown up strongly well. Spirits, Mono-Red, Mono-Black – all have posted strong numbers this weekend, as has the Sultai Delirium deck that Joel Larsson used to take down Players Tour Brussels last weekend. Further down the list, there are some wild brews, from Vampire tribal to the bizarre Sram deck that Ken Yukuhiro took to the finals of Players Tour Nagoya.

Some major takeaways:


Who Wants to Play Fair?
=======================


It wasn't that long ago that the top of the Pioneer metagame was dominated by "fair" decks – that is, decks like Spirits or Mono-Black that primarily sought to slowly and steadily (or perhaps a little less slowly in the case of Mono-Red) reduce their opponent's life to zero. There was even a brief period where Big Red, featuring expensive top-end cards like [Glorybringer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glorybringer) and [Chandra, Torch of Defiance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra%2C+Torch+of+Defiance), were leading the way.


But all that has changed in a few short weeks. At least in Phoenix, it seems that most competitors agreed that playing fair was not the way to go. By far, the most-represented archetype was combo, with about a third of the field bringing some form of combo deck.

The Changing Shape of Aggression
================================


For much of the young existence of Pioneer, Mono-Black has been the aggressive deck of choice, ranging from Vampire tribal variants to decks built entirely one and two-drops. But by the time players submitted lists for Phoenix, it seems that the black menace has begun to fall out of favor. Backed up by [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize), the deck was able to contest the combo decks of the format, but in the United States it seems that competitors have opted for a little bit of fire as well.


Thirty-six players brought Mono-Red Aggro to the tournament, just a shade under the number who brought some form of Mono-Black. With the combo decks leaning on [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time) to refill their hands after a barrage of discard spells, many players opted instead to simply fling Wild Slashes to the face.
