This is No Treasure Cruise
==========================



 Posted in **Command Tower**
 on October 2, 2014 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/astybs_authorpic.png)
By Adam Styborski




 Stybs has played Magic the world over, writing and drafting as part of the event coverage team and slinging Commander everywhere his decks will fit. 






When *Return to Ravnica* rolled around I was annoyed: It wasn't until *Gatecrash* that I could celebrate the return of my guild, the Boros. Learning about *Khans of Tarkir* and taking [the clan quiz](http://promos.wizards.com/chooseyourclan/) confirmed I had a new group of heroes to play as: the Abzan.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2014/images/daily/cardart_abzanwarrior_tok.jpg)
Warrior Token | Art by [Ryan Barger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=%5B%22Ryan%20Barger%22%5D)


While many of my Boros brethren have flocked to the call of the Mardu—unfocused and disorganized savages, in my humble opinion—the Abzan Houses are a comfortable new home for me, where long-term strategic approaches to winning combat are rewarded.


I mean, I always plan to outlast everyone in multiplayer, and that's precisely what the Abzan do best.


However, both the Boros and Abzan (among other guilds and clans) share a drawback: There aren't any Islands to be found. It isn't that blue is necessarily the best color, although I know plenty of players who would argue just that, but one of the most powerful utilities in multiplayer formats like Commander is the ability to draw cards.


Drawing Live
============


In a duel, you have one opponent typically drawing one card each turn. Compared to us, and our one card each turn, it's an equitable arrangement. The power of cards like [Sphinx's Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Revelation) lies in tipping that scale in the caster's favor and flooding a bunch of extra cards to the hand versus the opponent.


In multiplayer, the dynamic starts off tilted against any one player. With three opponents, all drawing cards for their turns, you're already down two cards *every turn* versus everyone else. It's why effects that hit multiple creatures and players, such as [Crackling Doom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crackling+Doom) and [End Hostilities](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=End+Hostilities), feel more valuable than [Hero's Downfall](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero%27s+Downfall).




[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=End%20Hostilities)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=End%20Hostilities)


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Hero's%20Downfall)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero's%20Downfall)





While every color has its own bag of tricks and abilities to pull from, it's blue that gets the biggest share of ways to draw extra cards. From [Divination](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Divination) up through [Treasure Cruise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise) and [Dig Through Time](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time), putting more cards into your hand is a shtick blue knows all too well. It's this talent that leads many newer players to focus on including and using blue in multiplayer decks. Constantly refilling your hands with amazing options and answers feels powerful.


The not-so-secret secret: It *is* powerful.


Ignoring blue's other skills, like countering spells and returning permanents of all types to owners' hands, the ability to draw extra cards means a lot in the average multiplayer game. Competing against multiple players with more total resources than you have available is tough, so naturally bumping up your own resource count helps. It's why ramping into more mana with [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) or something like [Abzan Banner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abzan+Banner) is also popular.


But for colors and decks without blue it can feel as though there aren't ways to rack up more cards in hand, even though it isn't true. Fortunately, you had a lot to say about refilling our hand with nary an Island in sight. Andrew's approach to [Glissa, the Traitor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glissa%2C+the+Traitor) covers some of the typical blue-free alternatives:


Drawing cards with no blue mana? No problem for my [Glissa, the Traitor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glissa%2C+the+Traitor) deck! This deck draws cards using three methods:


First, there's the classic black take on card draw that trades away other resources. [Phyrexian Rager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Rager) and [Phyrexian Gargantua](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Gargantua) trade life for cards while also giving me some bodies to work with. [Barren Moor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barren+Moor),


[Polluted Mire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Mire), [Slippery Karst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slippery+Karst), and [Tranquil Thicket](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tranquil+Thicket) trade a land for a card through cycling. These lands are Commander staples, and they do good work. Finally, [Phyrexian Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Vault) turns an already doomed creature or extra creature into a card.


This segues perfectly into the next method: death triggers. [Deathreap Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathreap+Ritual) is one of my favorite cards from *Conspiracy*, and boy does it do work. Creatures die a lot in Commander, and this deck can kill them with [Phyrexian Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Vault), Glissa, sacrificing Eldrazi Spawn tokens, and kill spells. It's not difficult to draw an extra card on every single turn with this out. The other stand-out card is [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum). Feeding "Sad Robot" to [Phyrexian Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Vault) with a [Deathreap Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathreap+Ritual) on the battlefield nets three cards for two colorless mana...


...And with Glissa, I can recur [Solemn Simulacrum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solemn+Simulacrum) whenever an opponent's creature dies. Glissa's engine combos with [Moriok Replica](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moriok+Replica), [Necrogen Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necrogen+Spellbomb), [Nihil Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nihil+Spellbomb), and [Horizon Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Horizon+Spellbomb) to potentially draw tons of cards while only using a few combo pieces. I also love mana rocks that do stuff, and [Golgari Cluestone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Cluestone) and [Dreamstone Hedron](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dreamstone+Hedron) are useful when you're short on cards but have plenty of mana.


With much contempt for the Mirrans,  

Andrew

Bobby, too, likes to play off trading bodies for cards:


All colors like to draw cards, but being outside of blue makes it difficult. It's what gets [Harmonize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Harmonize) run in every green deck in my group, while [Concentrate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Concentrate) is nowhere to be found. But the awesome people who make this game have been giving us card-drawing tools in other colors, and many of these tools are present in my Jund deck headed by [Xira Arien](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xira+Arien).


The first card-drawing card in the deck is the commander herself. Three mana is a fairly steep cost for just one card, but being out of blue but still having a commander that gives me a repeated source of card drawing is an amazing security blanket.


Lands are great, and my Xira deck is based around churning the lands out as fast as possible. The deck has received more than a few snickers for spending consecutive later game turns doing nothing but playing creatures that fetch lands. [Grim Backwoods](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Backwoods) takes advantage of these creatures I have laying around and turns them into more productive cards, sometimes removal, sometimes finishers, and if I'm lucky, more land-ramp spells.


My absolute favorite card-generating card in the deck is [Deathreap Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathreap+Ritual). I believe it is common knowledge that creatures will die in a Commander game, which means this morbid card will continue to draw me cards. It works incredibly well with [Grim Backwoods](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Backwoods) or [Perilous Forays](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Perilous+Forays), which turn my little land-ramp guys into cards, which in turn generates more cards.

For two-thirds of the colors of the Abzan Houses, Andrew and Bobby covered a lot of the bases. [Deathreap Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathreap+Ritual), [Fecundity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fecundity), and similar effects that key off destruction and death work wonders with decks that can abuse them.




[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Deathreap%20Ritual)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathreap%20Ritual)


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Fecundity)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fecundity)





While individual cards can be exciting to build an engine around, there are some mechanics that draw cards, too. Cycling, typically a one-for-one exchange of a card in hand for one drawn from the library, can be used to our advantage.


Or, as Jeremy put it into verse:


Cycling.  

Cycling [Krosan Tusker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker).  

Cycling [Krosan Tusker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker), knowing I'll get it back.


In haiku format:


Paying green and two  

to cycle [Krosan Tusker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker),  

that is how I draw.


As a player with similar appreciation for [Krosan Tusker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krosan+Tusker), that was delightful to find. There's another class of cards that are commonly used to draw extra cards, although it isn't the one-sided affair cycling and [Deathreap Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathreap+Ritual) effects provide. Adrin explains:


Blue draws a lot.


Blue always drew a lot, and blue will always draw a lot. But not all hope is lost for the nonblue colors! There is always a way, especially so in Commander! (While we're at it, let's exclude black as well, since it's pretty good in drawing as well.) Behold, the symmetric draw engines! ([Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine), [Font of Mythos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Font+of+Mythos), [Temple Bell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temple+Bell), etc.)


Yes, I know not everybody likes symmetric draws, and I would seriously recommend you don't do it in any of the following scenarios.




1. The power level of your play group is very high: You will probably die before your next upkeep.
2. Opponent has [Consecrated Sphinx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx) in play: Go away Sphinx. I don't like you.



On the other hand, symmetric draws work pretty well when:




1. The power level between play group members vary a lot: If [Azami, Lady of Scrolls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Azami%2C+Lady+of+Scrolls) is drawing four cards every turn, and everybody else is drawing one, might as well make everybody draw more and level out the playing field a bit.
2. You are not the archenemy: MWAHAHA fight among yourselves, my puppets!
3. You want to have more fun: More cards for everybody usually means that everybody gets to enjoy a bit more.



The following is a deck based on such philosophy. The important things would be making sure YOU profit the most from the effects, and not overfeeding the other players.


Just remember to get rid of your commander before your tokens arrive (by sacrifice or cloning him) or you'll be very sorry you let the other players draw in the first place!

I've played with [Font of Mythos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Font+of+Mythos) and [Howling Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling+Mine) plenty of times, typically in decks like Adrin's. Giving *too many* cards to everyone and ensuring you have ways to punish them for it embraces the unbalanced scale of resources in multiplayer games. I love [decks that take advantage of the game this way](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/ct/293).




[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Font%20of%20Mythos)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Font%20of%20Mythos)


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Howling%20Mine)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Howling%20Mine)





But don't let the splashy ways and classic solutions stop you from going exploring. Reggie has a decided different set of tools to keep his hand full:


Hi Adam, it was cool to meet you at Gen Con even if we never did get that game in.


I have a Karador enchantress deck that jumped to mind when I read your article, but after a bit I realized that enchantress isn't new or particularly different. Instead I want to show this.


This is a Saproling tribal deck and may seem to be a strange choice for the prompt of card drawing, but it's full of fun and interesting solutions to the problem of an empty hand. Obviously, [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp) is a powerhouse of card advantage, but I want to point to some more interesting choices: [Psychotrope Thallid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychotrope+Thallid) is really strong when the deck is built around Saprolings, and if you get it and [Utopia Mycon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Utopia+Mycon) out you pay two tokens for a card at any time, even after blocks are declared. [Overwhelming Instinct](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overwhelming+Instinct) means a free card every turn in a token deck because you don't care if all your tokens make it through a combat. [Woodvine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woodvine+Elemental) is also a great way to fill your hand, though everyone else gets to as well. [Druidic Satchel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Druidic+Satchel) can also a pseudo-card-draw engine if you hit a land and even if you don't it does everything that this deck is trying to do anyways! So while the card-draw options aren't necessarily the most powerful in all the game, I like them because they are suited well for this deck.


Have fun!  

Reggie

Tribal effects lead to plenty of ways to load up your hand, but Reggie went a step further with all the ways his tokens interact with drawing cards. As an admitted Forest lover, seeing a green-based way that isn't more of the same cards I already know surprised me, and it's inspired me to dig through other colors for ways to scrape cards together.


Except for blue, because that's just too easy.


Hand Dexterity
==============


If you're playing Islands you have a trove of ways to refill your hand. For everyone else, I hope this gives you some ideas on managing your hand in Commander.


While next week I will share some of the stars you've come to love, I'm looking further ahead than that. Here's a question I'd like to you have fun with and go off the deep end: **What are your favorite ways to make one card become another?**



* [Feedback via email](mailto:commandtowerstybs@gmail.com)
* 300-word limit to explain which card and why
* **Sample decklist or list of cards is requested** (does not count against word limit)
* Decklists should be formatted with one card per line with just a leading number, such as "3 Mountain"—just a space (no "x" or "-") between the number and the card name, *without* subtotals by card type (Submissions that don't follow this rule will be ignored.)
* Name and email required (non-personal information to be used in column)

There are dozens of ways cards can become something else in Commander. Everything from morph ([Hooded Hydra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hooded+Hydra) and [Willbender](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Willbender)) to transform ([Chalice of Life](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+Life), anyone?), and even champion ([Changeling Hero](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Changeling+Hero) to the rescue!) swap what opponents see as one card for something else. Those of you tricky and deceptive enough to rely on these ways, I'm counting on you.


Join us next week when our standouts stand tall. See you then!
