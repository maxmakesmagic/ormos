Post Fifth Dawn
===============



 Posted in **Feature**
 on July 19, 2004 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_alexshvartsman.jpg)
By Alex Shvartsman











Every time a new expansion set becomes legal in constructed, it shifts the metagame and re-shuffles the power level among archetypes. This constant flux is what makes **Magic** so much fun. Usually big sets like *Odyssey* or *Mirrodin* affect the metagame in a profound way, while smaller sets like *Judgment* or *Darksteel* have relatively minor impact upon the metagame. *Fifth Dawn* is acting as though it was a stand-alone!


While many of the archetypes you've come to know well over the last couple of months are still viable, the banning of [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp) in addition to the introduction of *Fifth Dawn* are forcing them to change. In addition, there are a few new archetypes and some old ones making a comeback. Here is a rundown on the decks you need to know in order to compete in today's Standard tournaments.


### AFFINITY


This is the deck players love to hate. It was kind of annoying before *Darksteel*, and absolutely ridiculous after. Removing [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp) should have helped quite a bit – except one major problem… *Fifth Dawn* introduces an artifact that – in this particular deck – is as broken as [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp) was. [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) is absolutely sick when every permanent you play happens to be an artifact. It would not be uncommon for an [Ornithopter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter) to attack for seven or eight points of damage on turn three. 


It is safe to say that if you take your current Affinity build and just replace [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp)s with [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating)s, the deck you end up with is going to be tournament quality. Of course, you can tune it a little more to account for this change. Be sure to play with four [Ornithopter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter)s if you weren't doing so already, and [Blinkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blinkmoth+Nexus) becomes an incredibly attractive option. Murray Evans played four copies of [Night's Whisper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Night%27s+Whisper) in his Affinity deck at Canada Nationals, earning a second place. He also played main-deck [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s – a good call when the metagame is pretty set on beating you with ridiculous cards such as [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon).

### TOOTH AND NAIL


Once upon a time Tooth and Nail did not play Elves. It was a deck that accelerated itself via cards like [Rampant Growth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth) and [Explosive Vegetation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Explosive+Vegetation). Then green mages came over to the dark side after discovering the joys of [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp). Elf and Nail was pretty clearly a superior build, but it was one of the decks that simply could not survive the loss of [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp). The archetype was not about to die out however – it simply went back to its roots. Tooth and Nail decks now rely on a variety of mechanisms to accelerate their mana development. Perhaps the most solid are the Urza lands, when combined with [Sylvan Scrying](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Scrying) to find the missing piece of the puzzle. ### BLUE-WHITE CONTROL


For a while this was a tier two deck. Blue-White Control decks have always relied heavily on [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), but [Skullclamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp) allowed weenie decks to recover too well for this powerhouse sorcery to be effective enough. Now the Wrath's back and so is this archetype.


There are many, many ways to build U/W control and only time and tournament results will show which build is superior. [Silver Knight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silver+Knight)s and [Pristine Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pristine+Angel) with [Worship](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Worship) is one way to go. [Eternal Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Dragon) and [Decree of Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Decree+of+Justice) is another proven path to victory.

### PROTEUS CONTROL


You can play Blue-White as a control deck, but you can also play it as a combo deck! Mauro Kina won his National championship with a Proteus Staff deck that was able to trade up a [Raise the Alarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raise+the+Alarm) or [Decree of Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Decree+of+Justice) token creature to a [Darksteel Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Colossus). This build's strength is that it retains virtually all control elements of U/W Control but its victory condition is more powerful. This deck's weakness is that it is forced to play some cards that are dead outside of the combo or when you draw them – such as your second [Proteus Staff](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Proteus+Staff) or a [Darksteel Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Colossus) you draw. [Thirst for Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge) is an answer to both scenarios – a way to get rid of the undesirable cards in your hand while digging deeper into your library.

### MONO-RED CONTROL


[Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) has change colors and its name is now [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song). You might not be able to get out a turn one [Hypnotic Specter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypnotic+Specter), but will you settle for a turn three [Rorix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rorix) or turn four [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon)? Mono-Red control is capable of doing just that. I played this deck in U.S. Nationals and, of course, it was pre-sideboarded to beat Affinity. In the current metagame I would move 4 [Damping Matrix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damping+Matrix) and 4 [Furnace Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon) into the sideboard, replacing them with the eight land destruction spells.
