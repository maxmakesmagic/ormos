Round 11 Feature Match Pascal Maynard (Abzan Megamorph Control) vs. Paulo Cortez (Esper Dragons)
================================================================================================



 Posted in **Event Coverage**
 on June 28, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/calderaro.jpg)
By Marc Calderaro











The point-chaser and “King of the North”–hopeful Pascal Maynard has been hanging around the top tables all weekend. He says that to have a real shot at Platinum and at beating his current Canadian rival (22) Alex Hayne to the World Championship, he needs to win out here. Sitting at 8-1-1, he's set himself up pretty good to do so. And now that Singapore is over, and Alex Hayne has not Top 8'ed on the other side of the world, Maynard has a chance to make some gains today.



Expecting someone to win out seems far-fetched, but for someone like Pascal, it's much less so. The man has been a Grand Prix Top 8–machine, and in the last year he's one of the safest bets to make it to the single-elimination rounds—as long as he doesn't get distracted by a [Tarmogoyf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf).



His opponent Paulo Cortez is no stranger to the spotlight either. In 2009, he played in three of the four Pro Tours, and finished in the Top 50 in Austin. Though he was qualified for the next year, he stopped playing the game to attend university. He came back to the game last year—once school ended—and at his first tournament earned a Pro Tour invite. Next up on his triumphant return, he finished in the Top 4 of Grand Prix Sao Paolo in his home country of Brazil. He's qualified for Pro Tour *Magic Origins* and we might be hearing more good things from Cortez very soon, now that the whole “school” thing is out of the way.



And “very soon” could be now, if he can beat down Maynard's Abzan Megamorph Control with his Esper Dragons.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/events/gpbue15/gpbue15_r11fm-cortez-maynard.jpg)  
*Paulo Cortez and Pascal Maynard*



**The Games**



Maynard's strategy was not a fast one, but it can be effective. He tried to strip enough cards from Cortez that he could slowly overwhelm him while his opponent spent resources recovering. Maynard's first [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) saw: [Silence the Believers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silence+the+Believers), [Dragonlord Ojutai](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonlord+Ojutai), [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize), [Go for the Throat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Go+for+the+Throat), [Elspeth, Sun's Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion), and one remaining land. His second took Ojutai, solidifying that strip-and-conquer strategy. Throughout his plays Maynard would check and re-check the amount of cards in his opponents' hand, making sure he could answer whatever remained.



He also established the first flag on the battlefield with a [Satyr Wayfinder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Satyr+Wayfinder), and tried to maintain an advantage there throughout the game. For Cortez, despite having no land in hand after his turn three, he had scryed a land to the top of his deck, then drew another, and was getting his hand online—at least, what remained of it.



Maynard killed a resolved [Dragonlord Ojutai](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonlord+Ojutai) with an Elspeth; then killed Cortez's return Elspeth with a [Hero's Downfall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero%27s+Downfall). Each time, he kept track of the cards left in hand—[Silence the Believers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silence+the+Believers) and [Go for the Throat](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Go+for+the+Throat) were still hidden from his sight.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/events/gpbue15/gpbue15_r11fm-maynard.jpg)  
*Pascal Maynard*



Eventually the black removal spells rotted in Cortez's hand, as they were all-but blank against the Soldier tokens of Elspeth. Cortez needed a [Bile Blight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bile+Blight), but just couldn't find it. The white 1/1s kept multiplying, and Cortez had no choice but to resign the fight.



In the second game, the Canadian's strategy remained the same. Use cards like [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) to take the key spells, and apply just enough pressure to keep the Brazilian off balance. [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) and [Thoughtseize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) working in concert can do extremely powerful things, especially when your own deck is designed around recursion and long-term advantage.



Cortez's hand was [Hero's Downfall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero%27s+Downfall), [Hero's Downfall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero%27s+Downfall), [Crux of Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crux+of+Fate), [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time), [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time), [Dragonlord Ojutai](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonlord+Ojutai)—with Cortez at three land. Maynard took the board sweeper, then tried to exhaust the creature removal spells—his [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino) and [Courser of Kruphix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Courser+of+Kruphix) ate the two [Hero's Downfall](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero%27s+Downfall). Cortez spent his first [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time) to refuel, while Maynard's board was not imposing.



But Maynard had been attacking with a [Satyr Wayfinder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Satyr+Wayfinder) since the third turn, and a turn or two later, had refilled his battlefield with another Courser and a [Deathmist Raptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathmist+Raptor).



Maynard had Cortez at 11 life before a [Dragonlord Ojutai](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonlord+Ojutai) from the Brazilian threaten to stabilize the board. But again Maynard had the answer and the biggest threat rolled into one card—[Elspeth, Sun's Champion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Sun%27s+Champion). It took down the Ojutai and grossly tipped the scales in Maynard's favor. If Cortez couldn't find an answer quickly, this six-mana ‘walker would end this game just like it had the last.


![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/events/gpbue15/gpbue15_r11fm-cortez.jpg)  
*Paulo Cortez*



Cortez sunk down to 6 life, then lower. He needed desperately to find a [Crux of Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crux+of+Fate) or some kind of mass removal spell. He cast a mainphase [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time) to find it.



And he bricked. When Maynard attacked with his team the next turn, Cortez extended his hand.



Pascal Maynard has kept his “win-out” streak alive and goes to 9-1-1. Paulo Cortez sinks to 8-2-1.
