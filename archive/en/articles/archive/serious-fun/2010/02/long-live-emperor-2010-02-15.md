Long Live the Emperor!
======================



 Posted in **Serious Fun**
 on February 16, 2010 










Quite some time ago I told you about a [Sealed Deck League I had been organizing](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/sf/68). It was a multi-week, over-the-holidays adventure of pack cracking and deck building that would lead up to the ultimate showdown of ultimate 40-card deck building in teams: [Emperor **Magic**](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/tcg/resources.aspx?x=mtg/tcg/resources/formats-casual#emperor) using Sealed pools. Here's the breakdown of what, exactly, Emperor **Magic** is:

**Emperor****In Brief:** Emperor is a team-based variant of **Magic** in which an Emperor is the central pivot to a team of Lieutenants (also called Generals), one on each side. The goal is to defeat the Emperor of the opposing team. Dealing with what the opposing team presents together as a team provides a unique spin on a larger-than-life multiplayer game.

**Rules Rundown:** There are two major rules that apply to Emperor games. The first is that Range of Influence varies between the Emperor and Lieutenants. Range of Influence is how "far" a player's spells and other effects can reach across the table to target (and, in the official variant, otherwise affect) opponents and their cards. Emperors start the game with a range that allows targeting out to the furthest opposing Lieutenant (a range of 2 normally). Lieutenants have a range of only 1—they can target their Emperor as well as the opposing Lieutenant, but not the opposing Emperor (until the intervening Lieutenant is dead) or either of the Lieutenants at the other end of the table.

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130605184016im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf78_eChartRange.jpg)  
The second major rule is the Deploy Creatures rule, which means that each creature has "{T}: Target teammate gains control of this creature. Activate this ability only when you could cast a sorcery." This allows creatures to be passed down to where they are needed most and shared among teammates. It's generally done by passing down to the next player on the team to have a turn, minimizing the "downtime" of a tapped creature.

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130605170744im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/sf/sf78_eChartCreatures.jpg)  
**Pros:** Emperor is a great way to freshen up a stale multiplayer experience. Not only does mixing and matching potential teammates change the game dynamics, but the potential to craft specialty decks with predetermined teammates can jumpstart creative engines.

**Cons:** Emperor doesn't scale well with random groups of players. You need at least six to get a game fired off, and having the four additional players to expand it out (to two Lieutenants on a side) works best: having only five or seven or some odd collection of players is better lent towards other multiplayer formats. And while having specialty decks with predetermined teams is nice to get games going quickly, pairing powerful, tested strategies against random, unprepared, new teams can create very lopsided games—something that's certainly unfun.

  
We had adjusted the point structure since I posted the league article, but that's just unnecessary details. At end of nine weeks, the "Top 6" from the league included a solid mix of different types of weekly casual players:

* Min, our resident casual DCI Judge, earned his way to the top on the back of a solid green-white ***Magic** 2010* pool featuring both double [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden) and double [Ant Queen](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ant+Queen) in his opening week.
* Dan, a fairly competitive player who focuses on both Standard and Legacy, had a mean, Vampire-laden, nearly mono-black *Zendikar* pool featuring almost Constructed-worthy numbers of [Hideous End](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hideous+End) and [Disfigure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disfigure).
* I came in third in rankings only through repeated punishment. Only Dan's blazing fast, hard-hitting deck had more Duel *wins* than my losses. Yeah.
* Shawn, a newer weekly player, had a solid ***Magic** 2010* pool that crushed quite a few in Free-for-All multiplayer games. A solid mix of red, green, and white gave him flexibility to sculpt a deck to suit the need at hand.
* Greg, the biggest [Planar **Magic**](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/feature/51) lover among the top, took a midrange *Zendikar* pool through multiplayer hoops as well.
* Russell, perhaps our most gregarious player, had a hybrid ***Magic** 2010* / *Zendikar* pool and used the pure flexibility to surprise other players at every turn. It was always a surprise to see what he was running week-to-week. Tim, the seventh if you're counting, used a very defensive *Zendikar* pool to stymie aggressive efforts each week. Flying creatures with 5 toughness make even a [Plated Geopede](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede) slow down considerably.

While it would have been a ton of fun to join in the Emperor melee, I decided to step down and just document everything, which is why Tim was able to get in on the action. Tim was just outside the cut point and had made an incredible effort over the weeks. It was a good run, and he deserved a chance to play one last game.

**Teams and Decks**

I divided everyone up sort of haphazardly, and I thought I was going to have to play after all until the true sixth player, Greg, came in late.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/sf/sf78_echartplayers.jpg)  
The traditional team approach to Emperor games is the "Sword and Shield" method of having the right Lieutenant play a defensive deck designed to stymie the opposing left Lieutenant, who usually plays an aggressive deck—hence, Sword and Shield.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/sf/sf78_russel.jpg)  
With the teams divided, there was a little confusion at the onset. I had originally planned for teams to share pools and create three decks from the combined 27 packs of cards but, as Min pointed out, this could cause some severely overbearing decks: more than four copies of [Doom Blade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade) on a team and one player can run them all. I agreed that this didn't jive well with the laissez-faire, "make a deck you want to play" approach we encouraged. That said, Team One accidentally followed through with my original plan, while Team Two did not. It was an inadvertent advantage that wasn't discovered until after the game had started.

Additionally, I had missed the link to the updated Emperor variant rules (despite having linked it in my original Sealed article) and was looking at some rules from when *Urza's Saga* had been released. Needless to say it wasn't too long until we realized that the old rules didn't make much sense. As a general principle in **Magic**, if a rule description involving creatures actively refers to "summoning sickness" as rules terminology, you've hit the WABAC machine. Some Internet searching later, and everybody was on-board with the correct (and modern!) rules.

As you can see, the decks utilize the best of both worlds in terms of availability from ***Magic** 2010* and *Zendikar*. A mono-black, removal-laden deck seems to be the type to punch through nearly anything—two [Black Knight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Knight)s is a fine insulating offense that will punish any white-based defense, with first strike always being relevant in Limited. The shield deck seems equally disruptive, especially with the multiples of [Sky Ruin Drake](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sky+Ruin+Drake) and [Shepherd of the Lost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shepherd+of+the+Lost). [Kor Hookmaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kor+Hookmaster)s can really put a damper on a quick offense. And, of course, Min's go-to plan of dropping [Soul Warden](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+Warden) followed closely by [Ant Queen](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ant+Queen) was a proven back-breaking strategy throughout the weeks. There was never enough burn and removal to stymie the flow of life-gain-triggering Insects.

While Team Two inadvertently locked into a disadvantage with unshared pools, it wasn't as through their decks were slouches. Greg has a solidly white-blue control build as a Shield deck. [Emeria Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria+Angel) and [Felidar Sovereign](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Felidar+Sovereign) promised long-term strength backed up by cheap, defensive fliers in three [Kor Skyfisher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kor+Skyfisher). Four [Kor Sancifier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kor+Sancifier), with their solid 3 toughness, looked promising to block with as well as break any awkward artifacts or enchantments—a nice dual focus. Of course, Dan took to the path of the Sword with his incredibly focused red-black deck and seemed primed to cut through quickly. Shawn's Emperor deck went a very peculiar route: nearly creatureless with all-in support for the men-at-arms. Burn, with two [Fireball](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fireball) available, backed up by deep card draw (four [Sign in Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sign+in+Blood) can just be unfair) seems a natural pair to the hyper-aggressive Sword. The real tech was the two [Act of Treason](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Act+of+Treason)—steal an opponent's creature, then overwrite the "return at end of turn" effect by sending it to the left!

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/sf/sf78_rumble.jpg)  
Of course, all the best planning and plotting doesn't guarantee anything, and awesome pools of cards follow the same rules for randomness every deck in **Magic** does.

**The Showdown**

It was a dusky, cold evening when the teams assembled. The cold wrapped around outside, setting the stage for a chilled reception between normally amicable *amis*. The two teams set up shop and readied their decks-at-arms.

Russell won the die roll to start, and only Shawn took a mulligan to six. With everyone on board things started off fairly quietly, with lands all around and some one-drops mixed in: Russell with [Quest for the Gravelord](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quest+for+the+Gravelord), Greg with [Steppe Lynx](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx), Dan with [Guul Draz Vampire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guul+Draz+Vampire), and Tim with [Kraken Hatchling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kraken+Hatchling).

Tim shrugged as he casually cast a [Kor Hookmaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kor+Hookmaster) to tap down the Nighthawk. **The Emperor Dark Side**

As it was clear to see here not every game of Emperor is a protracted back-and-forth duel. Sometimes it's a speedy beatdown after a team takes a stumble, as in this case, or other times it's an unexpected combination of cards that creates a must-answer threat. Well-formed plans and solid strategies can always meet unexpected bumps in the road or a blazingly quick opposition, and that's the risk—and the fun—of any team-based game of **Magic**.
