The Fastest Game Ever Played – Youmelia Gay vs Elie Pichon
----------------------------------------------------------


*by David Sutcliffe*

This feature match threw together two French players on 9-1 records, and two very interesting decks with two diametrically opposed views to mana. Elie Pichon was playing the [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam)/[Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony) combo deck, hoping to chain together a string of cheap mana-producing cards to create a huge Storm count and win the game. Across the table Youmelia Gay was playing with ZERO mana-producing cards. No lands, and very few spells, Gay was running an extreme variant of the Dredge deck we say James Allingham play last night, replacing lands and spells with more creatures like [Faerie Macabre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Macabre) and [Phantasmagorian](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmagorian) that would help him to fill his graveyard more rapidly.


Winning the dice roll, Youmelia Gay chose to play SECOND – yet another rule of 'good **Magic**' that the Dredge deck ignored. While Pichon began with an [Underground Sea](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underground+Sea), Giataxian [Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Probe) and a [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder), Gay drew a card and immediately discarded a [Stinkweed Imp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stinkweed+Imp) in his end step to go down to seven cards. A second turn saw Pichon [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) twice more and take another look at Gay's hand with a second [Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe) – the Dredge player had four [Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe) of his own, [Street Wraith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Street+Wraith), [Faerie Macabre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Macabre) and Golgaria Thug.


At the end of Pichon's turn, Gay cycled his [Street Wraith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Street+Wraith) to Dredge 5 cards, and hit paydirt immediately – an [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid), a [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return), and two [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below). Starting his own turn, Gay returned his [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) to play and began to Dredge again, played [Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe) and Dredged again, and again... and again... and again... once he'd done playing all four of his [Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe) there were 30 cards in Youmelia Gay's graveyard, and his possibilities seemed endless. Sacrificing his [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) to play [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) (naming [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond)), Gay spawned two Zombie tokens thanks to the two [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) in his graveyard. Casting a [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) he resurrected his Sphinx of the Lost Truths to Dredge even deeper into his deck, putting more [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba) into play and more [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below) into his graveyard. One [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) later and Gay had [Flame-Kin Zealot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame-Kin+Zealot) in play, handing all his newly-minted Zombies +1/+1 and Haste.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration//mtg/images/daily/events/gpams11/Round%2011%20-%20Gay.jpg)
*Youmelia Gay*
Devastatingly, Youmelia Gay had just pulled off a turn 2 kill without generating a single mana in the game!


Youmelia Gay 1 – 0 Elie Pichon


"I lost so I'm choosing to go second – you can play!", Elie Pichon already knew how Youmelia Gay's deck had worked before the round began, but he'd been handed a fresh demonstration of just why he should try to prevent his fellow Frenchman from getting to eight cards in hand.


The first game had been quick. The second was the quickest I've ever seen, perhaps the quickest ever played. Blink and you miss it.


Gay: "Ready?"


Pichon: "Yes, sure"


Gay: "Ok. My turn. Your turn", Gay had no land to play and only seven cards in hand so couldn't discard anything.


Pichon: "[Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy): [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba)."


Gay: "I concede"


Youmelia Gay revealed the three [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba) from his hand – discarding those meant that it would now be another three turns before he could ever get to discard a Dredge creature, and he knew that game would be long-over by then.


Youmelia Gay 1 – 1 Elie Pichon



*Elie Pichon*
Back in the happy territory of playing second, Youmelia Gay discarded a [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll) at the end of his first turn, while Elie Pichon swung for his dread [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy)... and... missed! Pichon continued to draw into his deck with [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) and [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), while Gay got his Dredge engine rolling, replacing his draw step with a Dredge 6 before happily discarding the [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll) again.


Pichon was rolling towards his own kill, though.


[Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond), [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual), [Cabal Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Ritual), [Infernal Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor).


Sacrificing the [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) with [Infernal Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor) on the stack, Pichon searched out his singleton copy of [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam). Casting his deck's key card, Pichon began the life countdown, flipping cards off the top of his deck, trading his life essence for dark knowledge... 18, 17, 16, 12, 11, 10, 9, 6, 5, 4... the year's Pichon's life flew past like pages from a calendar, but emerging from the other side of his combo, Elie Pichon had sixteen new cards in hand and was ready to begin again:

Deck Tech – Canadian Threshold
------------------------------


*by Tim Willoughby*

Canadian Threshold is a funny little deck. Beginning with a blue/green aggro control shell, sporting the usual suspects of [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) and [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), it gradually evolved to include [Nimble Mongoose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimble+Mongoose) (hence the threshold), and [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) (hence the Canadian?). That burn element pushed through and allowed the blue green tempo deck to have a great deal more reach, and removal that in many respects was interchangeable with the white removal employed by [Bant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant), on the understanding that a large proportion of the creatures hit by [Swords to Plowshares](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares) might just as easily fall to it, and that the life just might be relevant.


Over the years, Canadian Threshold has changed quite gradually, adopting some of the most powerful threats in the format (Jace, I'm looking at you), and adjusting to what is most powerful. With *Innistrad*, it has gained a few new weapons, which has propelled the deck to being the most represented here in Amsterdam on Sunday. Depending on how you look at it, this is either the next level of Canadean Threshold, or a whole new deck. Certainly [Nimble Mongoose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimble+Mongoose) has fallen by the wayside, but in spite of the sometime lack of any cards bearing the keyword, it still fits a very similar mould.


Have a look at this version run by [Johan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Johan) Steurs, 9-1 at the time of writing;

This build has many classic elements. Great disruption in the [Force of Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will)/[Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)/[Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare)/[Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) package, all of which can be flashed back thanks to [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage). That [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) is as good at stopping fetchlands as it is storm, making it a winner this weekend. Powerful threats in [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) and [Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique). Reach from [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer) and Fire//Ice. The inclusion of the one Chaos Warp is a nice touch. The rare from the Commander set provides the ability to deal with any permanent if needed, and in many cases with little to no drawback, given what a spell heavy format Legacy is.


Other builds of Canadian Threshold are being even more aggressive, running [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets)/[Insectile Aberration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Insectile+Aberration) as an additional threat. Best friends with [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), this little guy is being heralded by some as 'the new [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), who is *really* blue'. For a deck like Canadian Threshold, getting a creature on the board on turn one who can start beating while disruption is happening is a key component of its plan. The plan put more people in day 2 than any other deck, so it's probably one worth trying further.


For reference, here is another list – if you are looking to have a go at Legacy, and like attacking for 3 in the air on turn two, this is a good deck to do it with. More aggressive than the other build, this one has [Daze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze) and a full set of [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) to back up the [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) disruption, keying in on having a very cheap set of threats.

*by David Sutcliffe*

I watched Elie Pichon's [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) Tendrils deck in action against fellow Frenchman Youmelia Gay a couple of rounds ago, and after the match I cornered Elie and asked him to lead me through his deck. As much as any deck in Legacy, [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) looks like the classic combo deck – generating mana at a frightening rate and abusing powerful card drawing effects to create a quick kill.


The core of the deck is mana production, from cards like [Lotus Petal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Petal), [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual), [Cabal Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Ritual), and the poor man's [Black Lotus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Lotus), [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond). The [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) is a key card in the deck, moving from being a trash to take center-stage thanks to its interaction with [Infernal Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor). When you activate the [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) you add three mana of any color to your mana pool, but must discard your hand – usually that's a heavy price to pay for three mana, but if you have [Infernal Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor) on the stack when you use the [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) things are very different. The [Lion's Eye Diamond](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) activates the Hellbent keyword on [Infernal Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor), meaning you gain three mana and can immediately go and search your deck for a card and put it into your hand!

Usually that card will be your single copy of [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam), and you'll proceed to cast that with all the mana you had stashed away, then suck up a brand new hand of a dozen or more cards, paying life to draw with [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam). So you play more mana, cast another Tutor, and find [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony). By now your storm count is already into double figures thanks to all your [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual)s, [Lotus Petal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Petal)s and [Infernal Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor)s, and the [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony) sucks 20 life out of your opponent in one shot.


Like all good combo decks, it's a simple and pure design with lethal execution. Elie Pichon led me through some of the subtleties of building an [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) deck, though...


"A lot of the deck is fixed, like all the mana production," Elie told me, "You cannot mess with the engine in the deck so all the [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) decks look pretty much the same. One card I'm using that not many decks play is [Grim Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Tutor) – it's just a really hard card to get hold of so most [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) decks don't have it in, but it's an extra Tutor to help you find cards".




![Past in Flames](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Past+in+Flames)


"The other card I'm using that most don't is the new [Past in Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Past+in+Flames), and that is really good. A lot of the time your plan is to play discard to chew up counterspells, but against decks like [Bant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bant) and Merfolk you take too much damage while you're doing that and it means you cannot make use of [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) to help you get to your kill. [Past in Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Past+in+Flames) is like your [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) against aggro decks when you cannot afford to pay life – you get to reuse your graveyard again and can replay all your mana cards to add to your Storm count".


"I think maybe the only other big change in my version is that I'm running [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy). Most decks play [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize), but I am using the combination of [Gitaxian Probe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gitaxian+Probe) and [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) to see their hand then name the right card, and that's really effective".


With a lot of the main deck fixed, [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) players only get to start innovating when it comes to sideboarding strategies but there are even problems there, as Elie explained to me;




![Xantid Swarm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Xantid+Swarm)


"It's actually really hard to sideboard the deck, because so much of your main deck is essential you cannot really change too many cards. I really like my sideboard [Xantid Swarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xantid+Swarm) – against a lot of decks they take our their creature removal against you, and blue decks put in things like [Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce) and [Flusterstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flusterstorm), but those can't counter the [Xantid Swarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xantid+Swarm). Once you have [Xantid Swarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Xantid+Swarm) attacking there is nothing they can really do because they cannot play counters and took out the removal".




![Chain of Vapor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Chain+of+Vapor)


"Then there's [Slaughter Pact](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Pact) for [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg) and [Ethersworn Canonist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethersworn+Canonist), which are both a problem, and [Chain of Vapor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Vapor) as well helps to deal with those creatures. Actually I think you should probably always side in at least one [Chain of Vapor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Vapor), no matter what match you play – every deck puts in something that you don't want to play against and having a single [Chain of Vapor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Vapor) means you can Tutor for it when you have to".


"I've also got [Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens) in as another kill, but I don't really like it. I've never played it, and just put it in because a friend recommended it. I think 99% of the time you're better off just making sure your [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony) works rather than trying to get the Goblins into play".


With the deck discussion out of the way I asked Elie how tough the deck was to play – was it straightforward?


"No, definitely not. I've played this deck for a long time and it's pretty hard to play. You've always got to think about what your opponent has in hand so you can play around it. And you also need to plan two, sometimes three turns ahead – where you want your hand to be, what you want to do. When you are playing against the decks will all the counterspells and disruption it doesn't come easily. But if you know your opponent's deck then you know how to win – against discard decks you put down all your artifacts early and try to get to your kill quickly, but against counterspell decks you sit back with cards in hand, and try to create the gap to exploit".


Was there any good news?


"Well, I think every non-blue deck is a good matchup, it's the really disruptive and counter decks that are hard. Like you expect to play against a [Daze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze) and a [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) or something, but you can deal with those with the [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy), it's the decks with a lot of counterspells are a problem, or a lot of discard. Team America is maybe the worst matchup around at the moment because it had lots of discard as well as counterspells, and that makes it very difficult"
