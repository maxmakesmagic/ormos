All Over the Place
==================



 Posted in [Top Decks](/en/articles/columns/top-decks-archive)
 on June 2, 2010 






![](https://web.archive.org/web/20170709072651im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











This week is going to be an eclectic review ... a little bit of this, and a little bit of that. And that is because the results are all over the place!

Last week's Pro Tour–Amsterdam qualifier tournaments featured a wide variety of different qualifying decks; same format, more excitement, the Last Chance Qualifier down in San Juan featured four different—and in some cases "hot new"—strategies.

Finally, we'll take a quick dip into the PT results. My good friend Paul Jordan is going to do his usual statistical thang; but given how exciting some of the Block decks are—and how some of those strategies can be translated to the more mainline relevant PTQ format—they are probably worth a blink or two as well.

Let's go!

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td93_chart1.jpg)  
Once again, Jund looks to be the deck to beat—the most plane tickets to Amsterdam (damn!), as well as the most Top 8 appearances. 

Mythic Conscription—still quite a monster; close on both counts. It is possible the raw numbers that Jund is putting up compared to Mythic is based on the relative costs of the decks (one of them is pretty cheap, one of them is about as expensive as they get). 

Red-White-Blue Planeswalkers turned the tables on White-Blue Control—multiple PTQ wins versus ... um, *no* PTQ wins—even on appearances.

The decks that did best that week were all tried and true format favorites; let's have a look at some of the close calls:

Tristan Case came within one match of a trip to Amsterdam with the deck that all of us have had scribbled in the back of our idea notebooks. When [Ball Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning) was spoiled last summer, many players had the Dave Price classic as a natural partner to the already popular [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf). Tristan finally laced the two—or shall we say eight?—haste creatures alongside *twelve* more little beaters who ignore summoning sickness. Only [Plated Geopede](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plated+Geopede) is the slow poke in this beat down deck (but what a slow poke it is). 

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td93_cascade.jpg)  
Case ran even more haste-ology in the sideboard, the better part of the [Devastating Summons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Summons) + [Goblin Bushwhacker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Bushwhacker) combo, alongside [Goblin Ruinblaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Ruinblaster).

Of the decks that earned Last Chance Qualifications in San Juan, only Next Level Bant hasn't been discussed—in some cases to death, and others in this very article.

Next Level Bant is a [Vengevine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)-centered give-and-take of a deck originally designed by Patrick Chapin and Matt Sperling; this version was modified from the Grand Prix–DC build by Martell himself. You can check out a video of Tom talking about his changes here:

So how does this deck work?

Next Level Bant's best matchup is White-Blue Control and its kin; Tom played against White-Blue Control or Red-White-Blue Planeswalkers seven out of a possible nine rounds.

The deck is designed to grind out a White-Blue deck with [Vengevine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine) card advantage, but can also match a White-Blue deck's top end with essentially the same planeswalker suite—[Elspeth, Knight-Errant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant), [Gideon Jura](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon+Jura), and [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)—keeping even with the best part of the opponent's game. 

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20130614125527im_/http://wizards.com/mtg/images/daily/td/td93_3Cards1.jpg)  
Given the spread of the cards, Next Level Bant can obviously play a variety of different game plans.

You can hide behind [Wall of Omens](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens).



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

You can use [Sea Gate Oracle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle) to smooth out your draw (or hide behind it if the opponent is bringing with a mere 2/1 or 2/2). Due to its ability to manipulate your hand, [Sea Gate Oracle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle) is also a fine card to assist in [Vengevine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine) recursion.

This deck plays a full *two*[Scute Mob](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scute+Mob)s. Against removal poor decks or slower decks (after their removal has been overloaded, especially), these "one" drops can get very big.

| --- | --- |
|  |  |

A new card that Tom added to the Next Level Bant card mix was [Oust](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oust). This is much better than [Path to Exile](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) against cards like [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) or [Lotus Cobra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) (and sometimes even threats along the lines of a [Putrid Leech](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Leech)). You really don't want to be accelerating the opponent when his or her creature is a mana producer, but the tempo you can generate by screwing up the top of an opponent's deck (and ultimately forcing him or her to re-tap for a creature that is much less exciting after turn one or two) is as attractive as, say ... Blake Lively.

Okay! Let's switch formats to Block.

Remember, Block lacks the Alara powerhouses like [Blightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightning) and [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), and the *Magic 2010* superstars like [Baneslayer Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Baneslayer+Angel) ... but it still has a lot to teach us. Block formats past have given us the prototypes for our Green-Blue Madness and Affinity decks, our Astral Slides and any number of other Standard—and sometimes even Extended—standouts to-be.

These decks may also give us a hint as to how we can contain [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) and other mythic planeswalkers when [Bloodbraid Elf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) is no longer available.

There are a lot of obvious things about this deck. There are 28 lands; lands are good with [Lotus Cobra](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) and [Oracle of Mul Daya](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oracle+of+Mul+Daya) (not to mention [Avenger of Zendikar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avenger+of+Zendikar)); lands—lots of lands in play—make [Comet Storm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Comet+Storm) potentially un-fun (at least for the other guy), because an ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20160829100442im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/x.gif)spell like [Comet Storm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Comet+Storm) actually gives you something to do with all that mana. But the really soul-crushing interaction?



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

[Oracle of Mul Daya](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oracle+of+Mul+Daya) + [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor).

That's right: You can stack the top of your deck with two of those 28 lands with any given [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), then play them both. No, I don't know how many card advantages that is with even one activation. No, I can't count that high. The Oracle even keeps you even on mana when you are "stuck behind" a [Vapor Snare](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vapor+Snare). 

Can you imagine being "stuck behind" [Halimar Depths](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Halimar+Depths) or [Kazandu Refuge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kazandu+Refuge) with this Elf Shaman on the 'field? Some disadvantage!

(The same deck was played to one less Constructed point by Top 8 competitor Josh Utter-Leyton.)

[Day of Judgment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) as a sweeper, [Oust](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oust) and [See Beyond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=See+Beyond), [Everflowing Chalice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Everflowing+Chalice) as with some of the Standard versions ...

But with four copies of [Eldrazi Temple](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Temple), it is *also* an impressive Eldrazi machine! The deck can start off "low" with [All Is Dust](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=All+Is+Dust) supplementing on the [Day of Judgment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) side at a "mere" seven mana ... but Gardner gets actually big with the ten and eleven mana "I win" cards on the back of the Temple. This hybridization seems to me a fine place to mine as a Standard mirror-breaker.
