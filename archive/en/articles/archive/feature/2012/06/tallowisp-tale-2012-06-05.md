A Tallowisp Tale
================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on June 5, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorPic_GavinVerhey.jpg)
By Gavin Verhey




 When Gavin Verhey was eleven, he dreamt of a job making Magic cards—and now as a Magic designer, he's living his dream! Gavin has been writing about Magic since 2005. 







Modern is a format that consistently crosses the streams of past and present. With nine years' worth of cards at deck builders' disposal, synergies that designers never even dreamed of appear more often than ever. Some Modern-legal **Magic** themes overlap in weird places—like today's deck.


The overlap? *Kamigawa* and *Innistrad* blocks.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc198_blended.jpg)*Kamigawa* certainly had its Spirit tribal mojo going on. Spirits were a major theme of the block, and several mechanics interacted between them. On *Innistrad*, Spirits have a small tribal component as well.


Combined, the two create something entirely new and unique to Modern.


Take a look at this week's deck, sent in by Joe Lam:

### The Battle Plan


[Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) is a bit of an oddball card that received very little fanfare when it was initially printed. However, this certainly isn't the first time The Little Spirit That Could has made a run at Constructed.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc198_tallow.jpg)[Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) | Art by [Ron Spears](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22Ron+Spears%22%5D)


Let's set the dials back a few years here. Way back at [Pro Tour Honolulu](/en/events/coverage/living-heezy-street)—not the recent third one, nor the second one, but the very first one—Ben Goodman and his online team Cymbrogi debuted a deck that surprised everybody at the event: [Ghost Dad](http://www.wizards.com/magic/magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/daily/bb59).

Ghost Dad bucked all norms at the Pro Tour, playing cards as unexpected as [Enfeeblement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enfeeblement) and [Strands of Undeath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strands+of+Undeath). The key piece to the deck was the Aura-centric [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp), which ensured a steady stream of cards. It turns out that drawing extra cards with every spell you cast is still powerful even when the cards you're getting for free are as meager as [Enfeeblement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enfeeblement).


But it wasn't only Standard where [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) made a splash. In 2008, the card actually showed up in Extended as well! [Spirit Stompy](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/grand-prix-philadelphia-top-8-decklists-2008-03-16), an aggressive green-white deck that used [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) to find cards like [Armadillo Cloak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armadillo+Cloak) and [Griffin Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Griffin+Guide) for [Phantom Centaur](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantom+Centaur), showed up out of nowhere late in the season. Although many people laughed at the idea of a [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) deck being competitive in the high-powered Extended environment, that quickly changed as the deck put up a Top 8 finish at Grand Prix Philadelphia.


So, how does [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) fit into Modern?


Within the wide card pool of Modern, there exists a plethora of good Auras to go grab and plenty of exciting Spirits. The challenge with this deck is going to ensure it can survive against the most degenerate decks Modern can throw at it. The curve is going to need to be brought down; ideally I see this performing like a tempo deck full of efficient creatures and cheap countermagic. The Auras this deck will end up using either need to provide some radical bonus or serve a very specific purpose.


### Card Breakdown


Since this deck's vision needs to be refined a little, let's start by taking a quick look at each card in this deck to see if it's a good fit or not. Once we see what's left, we can work from there.


**[Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp)**


[Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) is a core function of the deck, and these won't be going anywhere.


**[Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft)**


Geist is one of the best cards in the deck. It's an ideal creature to suit up with an Aura, and it's also an extremely powerful card in its own right. Even though he's legendary, I'm happy to play with four. Turn-two [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) into turn-three Geist, searching up [Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead), is the ideal draw, creating a one-two punch that will leave many opponents down and out.

**[Drogskol Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drogskol+Captain)**


While not nearly as crucial as [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft), the combination of being a lord, being a Spirit, and granting hexproof so you can cast Auras with ease makes the Captain another solid choice for this deck.


**[Spectral Rider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Rider); [Kami of Ancient Law](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kami+of+Ancient+Law); and [Kataki, War's Wage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki%2C+War%27s+Wage)**


All three of these cards have something major in common: they're two-drop Spirits! They're cheap, have small bonuses, and carry Auras nicely. [Kataki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki) is especially nice for those times you discover you're playing against Affinity—how convenient!


My issue with all three of these cards is that I don't think they do enough for this deck. None of them are blue, so [Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead) doesn't give the unblockable bump. They don't have hexproof and are pretty easy to deal with overall. I would rather focus on the primary [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) plan with Geist and Captain—and there are better cards for that purpose.

**[Celestial Kirin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Kirin)**


It's really neat to see somebody trying out [Celestial Kirin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Kirin) in Modern—that's a card you don't see played very often! The intention is pretty exciting as well—systematically blowing up the opponent's drops at each turn.


Unfortunately, I think the Kirin falls flat here. It's not going to be reliable what you destroy, and, since a lot of your cards are within the same price range, you risk blowing up your own creatures or Auras. I'd rather have a card like, say, [Dungeon Geistss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungeon+Geistss), which is going to do something reliably.

**Dungeon Geistss**


If this deck wants a four-drop creature, [Dungeon Geistss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungeon+Geistss) seems like the one. It's an efficient Spirit—although it is a tad on the expensive side for what this deck will have to do so it can be competitive. I could see playing one or two, since it is excellent if your opponent is relying on creatures.


**The Heavy Hitters**


This deck features four huge, top-end creatures.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc198_four.jpg)The idea is sound—survive until you can slam down one of these, then proceed to victory. The problem is, that doesn't work as well as you might think.


Modern is full of efficient removal like [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile), easily nullifying a big threat. The format is fast, and filling your deck up with six-drops is going to cost you games when they are in your opening hand. Finally, the point of awesome Auras is mostly to enhance your creatures so you don't need to play individually huge creatures—you can make your own instead.


Because of all of those factors combined, these big guys don't fit here very well. They can be cut to better focus the deck's plan.


**[Moonlit Strider](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moonlit+Strider)**


While soulshifting back a Captain or a Geist is incredibly cute, this card is fairly weak overall. I'd rather be playing something more effective for four mana.


**[Temporal Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temporal+Isolation)**


This is an excellent Aura for a [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) deck. It turns a [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) trigger into a potential removal spell, which is certainly necessary. [Temporal Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temporal+Isolation) edges out something similar like [Pacifism](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pacifism) or [Guard Duty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guard+Duty) because it prevents all damage the creature would deal—shutting off effects like [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer)'s—and also has flash, which can be surprisingly valuable.


Additionally, Isolation has one other interesting upside in this deck—giving your [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) shadow! While Isolation shuts off the Geist's damage, it doesn't affect the Angel token at all. In a pinch, this can let you swing in for 4 damage a turn.

**[Threads of Disloyalty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Threads+of+Disloyalty)**


I love Threads as a midrange and control card in general, although usually it's something I prefer to sideboard. If I could search up [Threads of Disloyalty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Threads+of+Disloyalty) it would be a different story, but the card does *not* work with [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp), which makes it far less attractive to me in this deck. ([Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) searches specifically for Auras with enchant creature, and any variation of enchant creature—such as [Threads of Disloyalty](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Threads+of+Disloyalty) or [Daybreak Coronet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daybreak+Coronet)—are disqualified from that search.) It's something I might look at as a sideboard option, but I would rather just have more Isolations or [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) main deck.

[Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead) is phenomenal. Most beatdown decks have little to no ability to race a [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) wielding a Steel, and the card can win games all on its own. Steel is an Aura I'd be happy to play two or even three copies of just because of how many "Oops, I win" scenarios it provides.


[Angelic Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angelic+Destiny) is less spectacular than Steel in this deck, but it is still a target I'd like to keep. It sits much better on a [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) than Steel does, and it can do some slightly different things compared to Steel. It's much better when you're in the "make tokens with [Moorland Haunt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moorland+Haunt)" phase of the game or when you just have a [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) left.

[Infinite Reflection](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infinite+Reflection) is a card that really intrigues me, since with [Drogskol Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drogskol+Captain) it's completely insane. ### New Recruits


Now that the deck is down to its core elements, we can start adding in pieces that support the aggro-control plan it wants to play.


**[Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite)**


This deck could use another two-drop. There are no Spirits I really want to main deck that cost two. Fortunately, [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) works well in this deck. It protects your first [Drogskol Captain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drogskol+Captain) from removal spells, helps keep [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) alive, helps keep *you* alive by blocking, can redirect cards like [Maelstrom Pulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Pulse) that hit your enchantments, and so on.


Furthermore, [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite) can even go on the offensive sometimes! This 0/4 lacks defender and is only an [Angelic Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Angelic+Destiny) away from attacking for 4.

**[Mind Control](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Control)**


This deck definitely wants one stealing effect to search up when the game goes longer against creature decks. Out of all the options, the most straightforward looked to be the best. [Domestication](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Domestication)'s drawback is very real when staring down cards like [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), Krovikan Whisper's discount wasn't worth the drawback, and the other options were either worse or cost more mana.


If you have an irrational vendetta against Jace artwork, fear not—you can also use [Persuasion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persuasion) instead.


**[Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile)**


While you do have [Temporal Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temporal+Isolation) for removal, [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) is powerful and works as a good removal spell in this deck. Clearing out Geist blockers for only ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif) is excellent for pushing damage through, and the land you provide your opponent often isn't too relevant. I'm willing to play the full four copies.


**Countermagic**


The big piece this deck was missing for its game plan was disruption, and the best form of disruption in blue is countermagic! The counterspells this kind of deck wants should cost one or two mana and be good at gaining tempo.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc198_remand.jpg)[Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) | Art by [Mark A. Nelson](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22Mark+A.+Nelson%22%5D)


In any deck focused on tempo, [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) is one of the best "counterspells" you can have. In a deck like this, with cards such as [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft), [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand)ing spells for a turn can be the equivalent of 6 more damage!



![Remand](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Remand)

 

After [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand), the deck needed to have some more permanent countermagic. [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare) and [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) are good at different things, but a couple of each gives you flexibility in your draws and means you won't draw too many of any given one. Finally, the deck also has one [Disrupting Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disrupting+Shoal). Your opponent will always have to play around it once he or she knows you have it, and it plays very well with [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp).

I could see playing a couple, over the Shoal and a [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), but I wouldn't want the full four copies simply for mana cost reasons.

### Sideboarding


In a moment I'm going to give you a guide for how to sideboard with this deck—but before that, let me run down what each card in the sideboard is for.


[Kataki, War's Wage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki%2C+War%27s+Wage) is a Spirit, which works well with [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp), and it also happens to be pretty good against the Affinity deck. It's certainly not anywhere close to the powerhouse it once was when Affinity had all of its artifact lands, but it's still a good card against the deck. I would also bring it in against any deck that plays more than a couple artifacts, such as Red-Green Urzatron.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc198_kataki.jpg)[Kataki, War's Wage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki%2C+War%27s+Wage) | Art by [Matt Thompson](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoiler&method=visual&action=advanced&artist=+%5B%22Matt+Thompson%22%5D)


[Shining Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shining+Shoal) is excellent against damage-based sweepers like [Pyroclasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyroclasm), which is how a lot of people will try to remove [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft). Additionally, it's also powerful against beatdown decks since it wreaks havoc on their combat. On top of all that, it also triggers [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp), which provides an immediate replacement for whichever card you pitched to it.


[Spell Pierce](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Pierce) is to fight combo and control decks. You can load up on extra countermagic or swap them in for other pieces of countermagic, depending on the matchup.


[Vendilion Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vendilion+Clique) is also for control and combo, providing you with an additional avenue of disruption that lets you know if the coast is clear to cast an Aura. The 3/1 body is fairly threatening on its own as well. (Although it does trade with [Squadron Hawk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squadron+Hawk).)


The [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant) is just an extra card for opposing Affinity, to deal with [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) or that deck's huge creatures.


[Hyena Umbra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hyena+Umbra) is for board sweepers like [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment). You can tutor it up and put it on whatever your primary threat is (ideally Geist), then ensure it lives even if you know your opponent is playing a lot of ways to clear the board.


[Recumbent Bliss](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Recumbent+Bliss) is an extra [Temporal Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temporal+Isolation) against beatdown that has a small upside when you can spare the extra mana.


Finally, the fourth [Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead) is for the matchups where it plus Geist is an easy route to victory. There are several matchups that roll over to that combination, and you just want to give yourself the highest chance possible of drawing it.


Here is how I would sideboard with this deck:

Against Affinity, you will also want to bring in all three [Kataki](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki)s over [Mind Control](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Control), a [Spellskite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellskite), and [Disrupting Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disrupting+Shoal). Bring in the [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant) as well instead of fourth [Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead); Affinity hits you in such large increments that the life isn't always as relevant.


In this matchup, you are usually playing control by dealing with threats one at a time. Eventually you will land a [Tallowisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tallowisp) and start accruing card advantage, or play one of your more aggressive Spirits and start attacking past them. In many of these matchups [Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead) will be incredible, so you can try and assemble that for your endgame plan.

Depending on what kind of control deck your opponent is playing, a lot can vary. If it's red-based, you might want [Shining Shoal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shining+Shoal) to fend off red sweepers. ### Will of the Wisp


If you wanted to try different directions with this deck, I will once again mention I would look into splashing black for [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) and potentially [Dimir Cutpurse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dimir+Cutpurse). My concern is that the deck ends up clogged on three-drops without enough going on in the early turns—but it's something worth trying.
