Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







Decks and strategies from German and Japanese Nationals.
--------------------------------------------------------


*Lorwyn* is fast approaching, and with it will come not just [planeswalkers](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=magic/planeswalkers/home) busting out of their card frames but, doubtless, new archetypes for Standard... But before then, we still have the summer 2007 international Championships to complete!


This past weekend showcased two high-profile international tournaments, [Germany](/en/events/coverage/veteran-stages-triumphant-return) and [Japan](/en/events/coverage/champs-fall-rookies-rise-japan), homes to former winners—and *World* Champions—like Kai Budde and Katsuhiro Mori, as well as numerous other decorated winners, from two-time PT Champion Marco Blume to the genius's genius, Tsuyoshi Fujita (no pressure, new guys!). Just as National Championships around the world rage on, the Tier Two metagame shows no sign of letting up its martinet-like enforcement of deck diversity. Formats like Standard, where there are a lot of different playable cards at very similar power levels, allow for greater flexibility of deck choice than we have seen in most previous Constructed environments; even when you pick the "wrong" deck in Standard (or even Extended) the difference between the best deck in the format and the worst of the real contenders is pretty narrow. The metagame is therefore hard to predict, and awesome deck designers can push through uniquely effective weapons, even if they are effective only for one week or one tournament.

When I first started to look at these deck lists, I thought they might be identical (on the lists I have, they both began with two copies of [Golgari Rot Farm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Rot+Farm)); they're not. While they try to do the same things, and actually have a lot of functional similarities, they have subtle differences. Naoki plays a single [Pendelhaven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pendelhaven) to go with his [Ravenous Rats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Rats). He's also got [Slaughter Pact](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Pact) where the Champ has [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror) main deck. Kitayama has fewer creatures there... but also has two copies of [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation) main!


 



![The Rack](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=The+Rack)

These decks are updates to the Rat / Rack decks that have been played for some months, since the printing of *Time Spiral*. They try to empty the opponent's hand with [Funeral Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Funeral+Charm)s and [Stupor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor)s; they can steal creatures and nip at mana with [Smallpox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smallpox) while destroying the opponent's hand. Disruption is disruption, and effective when you are trading one-for-one, and certainly when you are getting two-for-one... but in these decks the major internal synergy is with [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack). That card is what makes people want to play discard in Standard. It can be a fast kill that is hard to escape.

The innovation, and one that we will see across Extended, probably Legacy, and certainly Standard next year, is the inclusion of [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) in the deck. Modern mana options make splashing [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) very simple, and the world's most popular Lhurgoyf makes for a fine addition. One of the biggest criticisms decks based on [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack) have received this year is that their creatures are laughable. Noted aggressive deck designer Pat Sullivan doesn't like [Call of the Herd](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) in general, but considers—or at least *considered—*it a fine sideboard card against [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack), which usually tries to play an attrition game. However, [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) is a great all around two-drop, one of the best ever, and shores up that one glaring weakness in [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack)'s strategy. With [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), this deck can win quickly—even more quickly—once it starts taking away the opponent's options.


The various sorts of discard spells are all synergistic with [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). [Stupor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor) is a sorcery; [Funeral Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Funeral+Charm) is an instant. Either or both put some other kind of card in the graveyard. [Smallpox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smallpox) is a sorcery, and counts for a land, some other card, and usually a creature, too. All of this makes for a happy [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf).


One of the things I like about Kitayama's deck is the inclusion of [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast) in the sideboard. I have been working on Extended this week, and you always see [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast) in the strangest places. In this deck it is quite clever... Can you think of a trickier or more consistently robust [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf)-on-[Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) option? Yours wins!

Solar Flare is... Solar Flare. The deck has not changed substantially in the last year or more since it rocked the world and dominated U.S. Nationals, putting Luis Scott-Vargas and Paul Cheon on center stage for the first time. Solar Flare can be considered the quintessential mid-range control deck for Standard. It does basically everything, some things quite well.


Solar Flare can...


 


* Draw cards with [Aeon Chronicler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aeon+Chronicler), [Court Hussar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Court+Hussar), and [Compulsive Research](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsive+Research) (and, being a highly customizable mid-range deck, everything from [Sift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sift) to [Tidings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidings))
* Deal with creatures, lots of creatures, with [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn), [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation), [Mortify](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortify), [Faith's Fetters](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Fetters)... (or [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror), [Slaughter Pact](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Pact), or anything, really)
* Counter spells, though not particularly well, using [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) and now [Pact of Negation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pact+of+Negation)

Solar Flare is a take-on-all-comers style of deck, using its permission largely for tempo, game preservation, or to force down threats. Because it defends from so many angles, Solar Flare is often considered a premier deck choice for undefined metagames; at the same time, the strategy is often vulnerable to highly dedicated opponents... Last year Solar Flare was generally considered a dog to land-destroying Vore or the combo deck Heartbeat, for instance. It is generally considered a superb anti-creature deck, though its reliance on *Ravnica* lands and signet mana production is often exposed by cards like [Cryoclasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryoclasm) and [Shattering Spree](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shattering+Spree).

What came first, the chicken or the egg?


Is this a descendent of Standard Dralnu, or Block Pickles? Block *Teachings* for that matter?


Yuuta's deck weaves threads from numerous sources. It contains a Teachings / Teferi plan, though that is obviously not the central strategy. He is serious about his Pickles combo! Four of this *and* four of that!


 



![Last Gasp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Last+Gasp)

This deck has two distinct plans: One is Teferi control. He has sixteen counters, more or less; he can counter most or all of the key threats in most opponents' decks, and lock the game up with [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Mage+of+Zhalfir). Additionally, he has the Pickles combo, facilitated in particular by Teferi, happy to cross the aisle and help his brothers out. [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental) skips down the hillside with [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter); no bad guys untap.

The proactive plans are quite obvious, but the defensive end intrigues me... [Last Gasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Last+Gasp)? Patrick Chapin credits YT with the [Last Gasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Last+Gasp)s—and [Volcanic Hammer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Hammer)s—in his Korlash control deck, but when I made those suggestions, the scary turn-two beaters in the metagame were [Scab-Clan Mauler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scab-Clan+Mauler) and sometimes [Watchwolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Watchwolf)... Does a topdecked [Last Gasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Last+Gasp) even kill a two-mana beater these days? The last time I checked, one of the main ones was 5/6 for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif) and the other was ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) for a 5/5 flyer. Especially in a Teachings deck, I would have not been surprised to see *some* [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror) action.


The thing that actually concerns me if you are looking to try this deck is that it has no card drawing at all. It has been up for debate since Yokahama in Tsuyoshi Fujita's blue deck—well, even before that, when Pat posted his Blue-Red-White Flag Burner deck for U.S. States—whether [Ancestral Vision](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Vision) was the right call for *any* deck, or if [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) was better. Yuuta's deck has neither, relying on *just* the two [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) for card advantage... Just something to look for if you are trying this one.

Makoto's deck is built on the same central concept as most or all of the Project X decks of the last year: [Crypt Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crypt+Champion) + Soul [Essence Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Warden) + [Saffi Eriksdotter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saffi+Eriksdotter) = infinite life. Loop in [Teysa, Orzhov Scion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teysa%2C+Orzhov+Scion), or replace [Essence Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Warden) with her, and you get infinite creatures instead.


The major additions here are [Magus of the Disk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magus+of+the+Disk) as a [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) target (quite nice at the end of the opponent's turn... untap and "Wrath"), and [Augur of Skulls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Augur+of+Skulls), presumably as a tutor-eligible [Stupor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor).

As soon as he touched down in the country, Brian David-Marshall, who was doing the online coverage of the Japanese National Championship, called me to look at this deck. "Mori is so good!" Ishikawa Ren was the beneficiary of a special tuned by former Japanese and World Champion, Katsuhiro Mori.

The main deck is similar to many of the [Hatching Plans](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hatching+Plans) combo decks we have seen over the past few months. [Pyromancer's Swath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath) helps [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot) to go storm-lethal very early; the card drawing synergies are all known at this point.


The difference is Mori's sideboard: He can twist his strategy to go in multiple different directions based on who his opponent is. [Pyroclasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyroclasm) isn't good enough against the modern green creature... but [Martyr of Ashes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr+of+Ashes) serves just fine. Against a cautious mid-range control deck with only three or four soft counters and two Pacts, Mori's sideboard can produce an end-of-turn [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Mage+of+Zhalfir), fight a little, not care if it wins or loses, then take the *actual* turn it wanted, following. With two [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental)s and two Shapeshifters... his combo deck can transform into a completely different combo!


**Blink**

PT Champ Komuro came to play with a re-hash of the deck that took *US* Nationals by storm this year. The strategy is simple: This is a U/G/W "good stuff" deck. The creatures are largely all very good, all do something profitable, from the card-drawing [Maro](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maro)[Aeon Chronicler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aeon+Chronicler) to the mischievous [Venser, Shaper Savant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Venser%2C+Shaper+Savant) to the onetime best card in Standard [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch) all the way to cover model [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). Many of these creatures have powerful 187 abilities -- Riftwing Cloudscate, Venser, Hierarch, and certainly [Mystic Snake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Snake) -- that can be played two or even three times with [Momentary Blink](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Momentary+Blink). [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) adds tempo, [Edge of Autumn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Edge+of+Autumn) bounces off the Flagstones or accelerates to third turn Hierarch. After sideboarding, Blink cheats with Akroma momentarily, the only targeting by a White spell she will likely see in a game.

This is quite a curious implementation of the Angelfire strategy. The German winner seems to have hybridized the two main branches of the archetype into a single deck. Usually you see a deck that came out of the Brian Kowal "This Girl" tradition (most recently resurrected by Osyp Lebedowicz and numerous MSS players in Baltimore, a deck with [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel)s that kills on [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire)) *or* you see a deck with one [Sacred Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sacred+Mesa), light permission, and big creatures that does *not* end on [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire), coming out of the Frank Karsten / Tiago Chan / Japanese Grand Prix tradition. Rosner has lighter permission than ever, with only two [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare)s and no [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand)s, and plays the same number of [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel)s and [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire)s!


The Firemane decks usually make a claim for Karoos on the draw or additional [Compulsive Research](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsive+Research) synergy, but the Blue-Red-White decks in general take their market share with card quality. All their cards are awesome. [Lightning Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Angel), awesome. [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix), awesome. [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), really awesome. Conventional wisdom has Blue-Red-White as the best anti-creature deck you can play... It has never been good against combo, and needs the right tools (either [Detritivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detritivore) or a well placed [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire)) to take on true control.
