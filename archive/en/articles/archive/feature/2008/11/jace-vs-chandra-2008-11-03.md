Jace vs. Chandra
================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on November 3, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_KenNagle.jpg)
By Ken Nagle




Ken Nagle was a finalist in the first Great Designer Search and joined Wizards of the Coast as a design intern. He has since gone on to work on twelve Magic expansions, including four of which he led, as well as leading the design of Archenemy and the first Commander decks.
 






[![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/features/11_box.jpg)](/en/articles/archive/duel-decks-jace-vs-chandra-2008-10-10)Two humans stand opposed. Two eyes squint and glow. The other's eyes are covered.

The air vibrates bluish around one. The other's hair flares and ignites. Suddenly, energies collide and catastrophes scar the landscape.

Wait—they're no mere humans, are they?

Salutations and welcome to *Jace vs. Chandra* Week! Or, at least, my *Jace vs. Chandra* article—it's actually Naya Week, but *Jace vs. Chandra* releases this Friday. I've been asked to share with you a behind-the-scenes account of the **Magic** R&D process that went into creating *Duel Decks: Jace vs. Chandra*.

### In the Beginning

While I was a Wizards of the Coast design intern during the first half of 2007, the development of *Duel Decks: Elves vs. Goblins* by Devin Low and Noah Weil happened right beside me. Lots of real and fake Elf and Goblin cards could be found strewn across the marble-lacquered, depleted-uranium tables that the collective hive mind of **Magic** R&D uses to test **Magic** cards that are far too dangerous for the civilian public.

Fast-forward to nearly a year later, and I'm sitting in my own float pod amidst a galactic senate full of ambassadors from across the **Magic** spectrum (still) far more important and influential than myself.

The givens of codename "Duel Decks 2: The Sequel" were many:




* *Duel Decks: Elves vs. Goblins* was a success, and we wanted to do another (also successful) one.
* It would follow the soon-to-be-familiar "EntityA vs. EntityB" paradigm.
* The packaging would be similar along with all that entails, with two showcased foil cards, etc.
* The product would use only existing card designs. (This is usually a given, but it never hurts to ask!)

The crack team assigned to "Duel Decks 2" included:




* Designer Kenneth Nagle
* Developer Erik Lauer
* Editor Del Laugel
* Creative Director Brady Dommermuth
* Art Director Jeremy Jarvis

### Brainstorming the Theme

For your own good, I'm going to skip this part. The only thing that matters is that we eventually chose *Jace vs. Chandra* for the product name and theme. The two premium foil cards would be [Jace Beleren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren) and [Chandra Nalaar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra+Nalaar).

### Creative

[![Demonfire](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/cardart/DIS/Demonfire.jpg)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Demonfire)Working directly with the creative team on projects is a social experiment on its own. Planeswalkers are our central, recurring storyline characters, traveling to different planes (a storytelling vehicle not too unlike *Star Trek*). **Magic** cards appearing in a random distribution, having no guarantee of how few or many a customer with encounter, and having a single frame of illustration with tiny, uniformly typeset flavorfully equates to quite an ineffective storytelling device.

That said, a "Jace deck" and a "Chandra deck" might execute flavor better. We slated an early specification of two additional Jace alternate-art cards and two additional Chandra alternate-art cards.

The download from Creative specified and extremely easy deck theme for Chandra: Fire. Anything and everything Fire-themed would work, and coming from someone who has researched every red card in **Magic** with a fire in it, there are a *lot*—so many, in fact, that Creative Director Brady Dommermuth postulates that his final minutes working at Wizards of the Coast will be spent staring at a Multiverse record labeled "Yet Another Red Burn Spell" just before he snaps, driven insane by having to concept one too many spells that burn something.

Jace was a different story. While I'm sure an "ice" theme has leapt into your mind, [Jace Beleren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren) is more adept with illusion and mental magic. This was not a happy thing for me as the designer to hear from Creative, but considering the Chandra deck theme was going to be a complete cakewalk, I decided to count my blessings.

### Design

There were considerable deck-building constraints for *Jace vs. Chandra*, including:




* Maximizing fun
* Ensuring back-and-forth game play
* Choosing an appropriate power level
* Choosing an appropriate complexity level (i.e. "keyword vocabulary")
* Allowing "the planeswalker cards to be stars"
* Choosing which cards to be alternate art
* Catering to the flavor-driven player
* Catering to the lapsed player
* Catering to the casual player
* Catering to the "bling bling" player (I'm one of these.)
* Catering to the new player
* Adhering to the Reserve List
* Adhering to Extended legality rules

That last one really goosed my gander. It's a carry-over criterion from *Elves vs. Goblins* that I frankly don't see any upside in. I've played more than my fair share of casual **Magic** games (it's a valuable viewpoint I contribute to the collective **Magic** R&D hive mind), and *not once* can I recall an Extended-legality check from our games. The unwritten rules from my casual groups were:




* You can't play more than 4 of one card except basic lands. (The basic deckbuilding rule of Magic that promotes variance.)
* You can't play with a card that the group has collectively banned (frequently [Armageddon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Armageddon) and its ilk).

Based on this, I appealed the legality requirement. The verdict came down from the heavens in favor, and that's why *Jace vs. Chandra* isn't Extended-legal.

The alternate arts were tackled early due to art commissioning requirements. As for the exact cards, we tried to leave ourselves leeway. The team settled on [Incinerate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incinerate) and [Seal of Fire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seal+of+Fire) for Chandra, locking in [Seal of Fire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seal+of+Fire) because the art would depict an actual "seal" like its previous artworks. Jace was a left a bit more open-ended, with "a card drawing spell" and "a counterspell" locked in.

### Playtesting

I began playtesting with prototype *Jace vs. Chandra* decks. The panging thought in my brain was always, "If I were [Jace Beleren](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren) and I knew [Chandra Nalaar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra+Nalaar) was trying to kill me, what creatures would I summon? What spells would I cast?"

Jace would summon and cast:




* [Narwhal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narwhal)
* [Blue Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blue+Elemental+Blast)
* [Ether Well](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ether+Well)

Chandra answered the question similarly:




* [Guma](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guma)
* [Red Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Red+Elemental+Blast)
* [Parch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Parch)

**Magic** is filled with these kinds of cards, and "metagaming" is the natural inclination for anyone who knows what their opponent will bring to the table. See *Jace vs. Chandra* developer Erik "Evil" Lauer's article [The Role of Hate](/en/articles/archive/latest-developments/role-hate-2008-10-31), which discusses color hosers in detail. Note that we would never put [Chill](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chill) or [Boil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boil) into these decks, as those two cards are far more powerful in this matchup than Jace or Chandra could ever hope to be, even more powerful than every other card in the game. In fact, the only usable hosers I found were [Ether Well](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ether+Well) and [Parch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Parch).

However, playtesting revealed that even [Guma](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guma) and [Narwhal](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narwhal) were invincible, unstoppable clocks that would require [Bottle Gnomes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bottle+Gnomes) or [Moonglove Extract](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moonglove+Extract)s in both decks as answers.

The intermediate decklists for *Jace vs. Chandra* are almost unrecognizable. Here's a few of the more whimsical cards that were cut in playtesting:

**Jace**




* [Belltower Sphinx](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Belltower+Sphinx)
* [Millstone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone)
* [Elixir of Vitality](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elixir+of+Vitality)

**Chandra**




* [Searing Touch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Searing+Touch)
* [Firecat Blitz](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firecat+Blitz)

I found these cards fantastically fun, but they were ultimately purged during "The Great Powering Up" of both the Jace deck and the Chandra deck. (What can I do, The Pit is full of spikes!) Jace received a smattering of morph creatures like [Wall of Deceit](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Deceit) to convey his illusion magic and a complement in secret red hoser [Spire Golem](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spire+Golem). Chandra's got a raw power-level upgrade with [Flametongue Kavu](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flametongue+Kavu) as well as her own sideways blue hoser in [Flamekin Brawler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flamekin+Brawler). All in all, Erik Lauer did an exceptional job developing the back-and-forth game play of these decks even at their increased power level.

The Chandra deck ultimately crossed the finish line as "the attack deck."

Chandra also has the largest blowout potential with [Hostility](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hostility) into Fireblast, creating 18 power of haste combat damage out of nowhere. Her [Keldon Megaliths](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Keldon+Megaliths) is an intentional unsolvable threat. [Chandra Nalaar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra+Nalaar) herself is a very dangerous board controller that will quickly take over the game (my personal favorite planeswalker design).

By contrast, the Jace deck ended up being "the defense deck."

### Conclusion

And that's how *Duel Decks: Jace vs. Chandra* came into being. If you weren't a fan of Jace or Chandra before, I can only hope that my reminiscing and primitive salesmanship skills have at least given you second thoughts. As a "bling bling matters" player, I've already put these promotional alternate art cards into my Elder Dragon Highlander decks.

Not to give anything away, but *Jace vs. Chandra* is evidence that we could do more planeswalker-themed *Duel Decks* in the future. Maybe someday Chandra will fight an ice-themed planeswalker, and my ultimate wish will come true: a Fire in Chandra's deck and an Ice in the other.
