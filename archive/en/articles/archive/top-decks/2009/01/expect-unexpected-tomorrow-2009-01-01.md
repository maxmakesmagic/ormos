Expect the Unexpected Tomorrow
==============================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on January 1, 2009 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







*Far and away my favorite article I wrote this year. This one was a whiz-bang smash hit among the readership, a solid intro to the Pro Tour complete with the "unexpected"—or at least unpreviewed—best deck running all over [Berlin](/en/node/502416), dominating, and ultimately taking first place. But hey! I did say to expect this to happen! Also it was my first Top Decks foray into video and people have liked that angle enough that I have continued to pursue it more or less every week. Promise to continue enjoying them and I promise to continue making them.*



![Kird Ape](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Kird+Ape)

Today you are going to read this issue of Top Decks, which is about the Extended [Pro Tour–Berlin](/en/node/640191). You are going to go night-night. When you wake up, it is very likely Pro Tour–Berlin will be well underway, and you can tune in here to **magicthegathering.com** and follow along with what promises to be a Wild West of [Kird Ape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape)s and [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager)s.


So this article is going to try to give you a little context as to what you might see starting tomorrow.


But Extended has been a notoriously difficult format to predict in recent years. Last year, for Pro Tour–Valencia, we all knew that Dredge was going to be the big boogeyman of the tournament... but that was kind of the thing. *Everybody knew.* So Dredge was ultimately unsuccessful, buried under an avalanche of [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt)s and [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate)s.


Few in the **Magic** media would have predicted not just one but two copies of The Rock in the Top 8 of the Valencia Top 8 alongside multiple versions of White-Blue UrzaTron in an eclectic final day including Affinity, Zoo, [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal), and of course Remi Fortier's [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) deck.


Fewer still would have predicted that the Barra Rock deck from that Top 8 would have survived the entire PTQ season, making nary a wave, until reemerging at Grand Prix–Philadelphia to take the title in the hands of Gerard Fabiano.


Next Level Blue?


*Previous* Level Blue?


What is the best third color to splash in The Rock?


Professional-level Extended has been defined by the unexpected, with almost every Pro Tour showcasing the reemergence of a forgotten strategy, and Grand Prix showcasing the odd Fujita [Sneak Attack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sneak+Attack) deck or groundbreaking Friggorid.


So no preview article can give you every single angle ahead of time... But there are still pillars of the format, holding the entire structure of countless decks and variations up. Top Decks might not be able to give you an eye on *every* top deck ahead of time this time, but we can certainly address the pillars, and the levers being pulled to shake up the format.


### 1. Goodbye to *Invasion*


The recent rotations have had tremendous repercussions on the Extended format. *Invasion* Block was the home to [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant), and with no [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant), there is no NO Stick, no Scepter-*Chant* per se.


Moreover, the *Invasion* sacrifice lands—[Ancient Spring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Spring), [Geothermal Crevice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geothermal+Crevice), [Irrigation Ditch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Irrigation+Ditch), [Sulfur Vent](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sulfur+Vent), and [Tinder Farm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinder+Farm)—and so on were fundamental to accelerating Storm and [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) strategies. These lands gave their home decks a lift of a turn or so in order to explode to their combination finishes before the opponent could kill them first.


Here is Andre Mueller's [Enduring Ideal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enduring+Ideal) deck from the finals of last year's Pro Tour–Valencia. Check out how his deck leans on eleven or so explosive Invasion sacrifice lands.

### 2. Goodbye to *Odyssey*


The days of [Wild Mongrel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) and [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) ruling Extended have been over for some time—at least as long as [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) has had its flag planted squarely in the center of the metagame—but even without defining the format with some of the best two- and three-mana threat creatures ever printed, *Odyssey* *still* managed to help define the format.


Why?


[Breakthrough](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breakthrough)  
[Careful Study](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Careful+Study)  
[Putrid Imp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Imp)



![Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cabal+Therapy)

While these cards didn't have the word "dredge" actually printed *on* them, they were essential for the Dredge deck to play on the first couple of turns. The number of times a player evaluated a hand, declared a [Breakthrough](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Breakthrough) for zero, and pitched everything... then won in short order with a dredged [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll) feeding [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba)s to set up [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return)... any number greater than "zero" is probably surprising to many of you.


[Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) was a weapon used *by* the hated Dredge deck, but just as much by middle-of-the-road do-gooder creature decks as well. Of all the cards in *Odyssey* Block, I would be surprised if this one were not the most fiercely missed.


Now does that mean goodbye to Dredge?


It certainly means Dredge is off most players' immediate "to beat" lists. Last year, the entire Pro Tour was prepared for Dredge, and it showed: the boogeyman was completely locked out of the Top 8. However this year, it seems that guards are down... meaning the clever graveyard mage just might make a metagame opening.


...Just a thought.


### 3. Goodbye to Sensei's Divining Top


Removing [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top) crippled numerous strategies, including basically every [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) deck... including last year's Extended Pro Tour winner. Without [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top), [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) becomes significantly less reliable; [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) gets quite a bit worse, at least in control decks, and, subtly, there is less incentive to play *Onslaught* fetch lands over, say, *Shadowmoor* filter duals.

### Affinity


Affinity has the pedigree of being the most powerful offensive strategy in the history of **Magic**—a champion in Columbus under Pierre Calali, and Top 8 again in Valencia alongside San Stein—and no kind of loser whatsoever.


Affinity combines many different cards that can piggyback artifact lands such as [Vault of Whispers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vault+of+Whispers) to create advantages... Spells with affinity for artifacts are undercosted when considered next to artifact lands, which essentially "tap for two mana" in this deck ... You only need two artifact lands to summon a [Frogmite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frogmite). [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) can eat artifact lands to get bigger and more powerful.

Sam's deck played a variety of cards that, while good, will probably not appear in this year's Affinity deck.


[Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), the best two drop in maybe the history of the game, will likely step aside in favor of [Atog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog). [Atog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog) is a ferocious attacker in Affinity, and next to [Fatal Frenzy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fatal+Frenzy), can sometimes produce a turn three kill.

A lot of us complain about bad luck, mana screw, poor pairings, and so on without evaluating what we could have done better in individual games and circumstances. Look back at about 3:40 in the video, where I play the [Arcbound Worker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Worker).


I had enough mana to play [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager). My plan was to crash with [Ornithopter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter)s and [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating)s (got there), and I didn't really consider using [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) as another primary threat. I missed damage due to the opponent's [Smother](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother), but if I had had the Ravager, I could have recouped at least a +1/+1 counter even if I essentially missed the attack.


Imagine I had lost a close one with the opponent on one instead of winning with those [Ornithopter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter)s and Platings... Would I have chalked it up to bad luck? The beauty of video is that *it's right there for me.* I could review the game and see where I had made a clear error. I guess thousands of you get to see it too!

Let's hope this helps both of us.

Anyway, I feel like Affinity will be one of the more popular decks of the Pro Tour. It's extremely explosive and has some nice options from the new set.

[Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge), [Kataki, War's Wage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki%2C+War%27s+Wage), and [Hurkyl's Recall](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurkyl%27s+Recall) are just a few potential headaches Affinity players will have to weather in Game 2 and Game 3 situations.


### Domain Zoo


What we today call Domain Zoo is a mashup of several decks, evolving over time from a Raphael Levy-style Gaea's Might Get There to incorporate the Chapin / Herberholz [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant)s and [Vindicate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate)s.


...Well, given the *Invasion* rotation, not the [Vindicate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate)s any longer.


Domain Zoo has the highest quality cards on a per-mana basis of any deck in the format... Everything from a variety of 2- and 3-power creatures on one mana, to the best two-drops of all time, to burn cards that can swing 6 life or just crunch through 5 points of bone for a couple of measly mana.

All-In Red harkens back to the days of some of the game's great black decks.


Early tournament players often loaded their decks with [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual)s and [Mind Twist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Twist)s (once upon a time you could play four and four, not none and none). This deck can accomplish a similar feat using a little mana acceleration to drop [Magus of the Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magus+of+the+Moon) or [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon); in Extended, there are very few decks with a lot of basic lands. Take the Zoo deck above. It is a variation on the red deck, but even Zoo can't play very much with only [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s. [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) paralyzes the opponent and blanks his or her hand, almost as if you had spent the same acceleration cards to actually force him or her to discard it with Mind Twist.

Many a black mage has used [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor) and [Entomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Entomb) to set up the perfect fat creature to [Reanimate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reanimate) into play... All-In Red does something similar, pushing all its resources into a single threat on the first or second turn; the only difference is that you don't bring a creature back from the graveyard, rather playing it directly from your grip after using mana acceleration to pay for it. A first-turn [Deus of Calamity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deus+of+Calamity) is nothing that can be ignored.





![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220216050541im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/img/interface/video-place-holder.png)[.](http://www.youtube.com/v/GR2TBa2L2LM&hl=en&fs=1&rel=0)



  
 

[![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/vi/wQHWKXYu3RY/0.jpg)](#)[![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/vi/6SKKAUaMVWo/0.jpg)](#)[![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/vi/GR2TBa2L2LM/0.jpg)](#)

All-In Red seems to be one of the most fun decks in the format, mixing potentially devastating offensive capabilities with speed and surprise... but it has its holes as well.


In the video I jokingly said that I lost back-to-back games against Faeries with first-turn [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon)s. Strange but true! The deck has no removal to speak of, so I fell behind a measly 1/1 [Spellstutter Sprite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spellstutter+Sprite) and [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte) in each. I later lost a game to Zoo thanks to a 1/1 [Kird Ape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape) and [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Umezawa%2527s%2BJitte). So while [Umezawa's Jitte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Umezawa%2527s%2BJitte) is no longer a fundamental staple of the Extended metagame, it seems to have a sweet spot against All-In.


Don't forget that even the aggressive decks can tussle with a Demigod if you don't have backup. It's not like Standard where you can easily play more spells! A first-turn [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) might get in for 10, but [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames), [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast), and so on can play adequate defense at low cost.


Still, more fun than a barrel of barrels; brutally quick, demoralizing, barrels.


### Other Decks


I am pretty sure about Affinity, Zoo, and All-In Red, but what about the many other options to be played in the format? The above are basically all attack decks (even if All-In Red rubs up against combo a little bit)... what about the true combo or varying control options in the format?

One possible combination deck is a porting of the [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll) strategy to Extended, simplifying the Standard [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) + [Dakmor Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dakmor+Salvage) to a single [Chain of Plasma](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Plasma). This is more-or-less a two-card combo; you target your Swans with [Chain of Plasma](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Plasma) and can then repeat the process over and over; because the Swans lets you draw three generic cards, you can draw your entire deck with just the two, feeding [Chain of Plasma](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chain+of+Plasma) until you're not interested any more... You've got it all! Finish however you like.

The *Invasion* lands might be gone, but chances are there will still be storm combo decks as long as there is [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) in the format.


Operation should be the same as always: generate storm count with mana accelerators, play some big storm spell to either set up another storm spell ([Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire)) or kill right then and there (say with [Pyromancer's Swath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath) + [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot)).


This year, the storm decks will likely be setting up post-sideboard games against control with [Gigadrowse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gigadrowse)—a replicate instant to be played during the opponent's turn—which does the double-duty of putting significant pressure on the number of [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle)s your opponent drew (that is, even if the [Gigadrowse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gigadrowse) is "countered" by [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle), chances are your [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) will be safe).

The mighty mighty mana base is still intact for this strategy (barring Adam's [Skycloud Expanse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skycloud+Expanse)s in the white-blue version), and [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) is still there to pull it through the middle turns. The speed of the beatdown decks may be an issue for the UrzaTron decks, especially with [Moment's Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moment%27s+Peace) rotating, but they have been the control staple of Extended—at least in flashes—for the past several years.


Perhaps we will see a return to White-Blue UrzaTron for [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) in the absence of [Moment's Peace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moment%27s+Peace), but more keyed to the "tenacious" [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) card-drawing setup instead of the immediacy of the rotating [Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction).


Speed in the face of those incredible beatdown decks is going to challenge the control side, but control decks have been counted out of Extended before... and we're still talking about them.


Extended can be a dark and scary place for sure, but I trust we served to shine a little light on it. I hope that you enjoyed this overview, and that it enriches your ability to follow this weekend's coverage.
