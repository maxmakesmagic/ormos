Everything to Everyone
======================



 Posted in [Top Decks](/en/articles/columns/top-decks-archive)
 on November 17, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






By the time you are reading this, the 2011 World Championships will be mere hours away. Some of the best players in the world—joined by some of the greatest players of all time—will battle it out across four days and multiple formats. For singles competitors, Worlds is a three-format tournament, combining Modern, *Innistrad* draft, and of course the Standard format over a three-day period. At the end of the three Swiss formats, we will cut to a Top 8, who will determine the 2011 World Champion via single-elimination Standard play. 


[![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td169_worlds.jpg)](http://staging.wizards.com/Magic/TCG/Events.aspx?x=events/magic/worlds)

Today in Top Decks we will focus on the myriad of different Standard decks that have defined—and continue to define—the Standard metagame. Ours is an incredibly rich Standard this year, with every color represented and numerous different angles of attack being exploited across everything from -1/-1 counters to deck exhaustion in addition to damage and card draw.


Let's check out what each different color is offering in Standard.


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td169_r.jpg)

Straight Red Deck Wins has quite a few tools and synergies. Fast beatdown including [Spikeshot Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spikeshot+Elder), [Grim Lavamancer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer), the [Liliana of the Veil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil)-frustrating [Goblin Arsonist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Arsonist), and of course [Stromkirk Noble](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromkirk+Noble) help set up [Stormblood Berserker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormblood+Berserker) on turn two.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Stromkirk Noble)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromkirk Noble) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Stormblood Berserker)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormblood Berserker)
The red decks in this format have strong finishers, including [Koth of the Hammer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Koth+of+the+Hammer) and [Shrine of Burning Rage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrine+of+Burning+Rage) (both cards with various kinds of counters) as well as the resilient [Chandra's Phoenix](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chandra%27s+Phoenix). Some versions of the red deck have swapped out [Brimstone Volley](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brimstone+Volley) for [Volt Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volt+Charge), with [Volt Charge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volt+Charge) helping the Shrine, the mighty Koth, and any number of +1/+1-counter-bearing beaters to level up.


That said, straight red seems less common in live tournament Top 8s than it was just a month or two ago. Straight beatdown with burn spells may take on a purple hue:

Brian Sondag gave the world its first look at Wolf Run Ramp when he won the Nashville, Tennessee Open event. Sondag's deck is like a post-*Zendikar* reimplementation of [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) principles; personally, I actually think that Wolf Run is a better structured deck than Standard [Valakut](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut), seeing as it is a green deck with actual green-producing lands rather than being [Valakut](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut)-locked into playing a million basic [Mountain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s (and—eep—[Evolving Wilds](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evolving+Wilds) and [Terramorphic Expanse](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terramorphic+Expanse)).


The deck itself is a Red-Green Ramp deck that plays [Green Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith), [Rampant Growth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rampant+Growth), and so on to get ahead of the opponent on mana, eventually laying bombs like [Garruk, Primal Hunter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk%2C+Primal+Hunter) and of course [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan). The Wolf Run Ramp's unique end game is driven by [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan). Sondag would go and get [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) and [Kessig Wolf Run](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) with the goal of eventually poisoning the opponent to death. Especially early in the format when Sondag broke the deck, four copies of [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) might actually overwhelm a Solar Flare's point removal base! It doesn't take many activations of [Kessig Wolf Run](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) to kill an opponent, not when these specialty lands have been set up by a [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Inkmoth Nexus)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth Nexus) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Kessig Wolf Run)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig WOlf Run)

Owen played a whopping 20 [Forest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s, cutting his mana base down to the bare minimum requirements to set up [Kessig Wolf Run](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) (one, along with one [Mountain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain) to activate it). Owen played only two [Inkmoth Nexus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus) main.


With so little red, Owen could no longer support [Slagstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slagstorm), and he chose to go with a more traditional [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) / [Llanowar Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) package rather than Viridian Emissaries.


What Owen got in return was the ability to break [Dungrove Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder).


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td169_dungrove.jpg)

[Dungrove Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) is absurd against a large portion of the field. You can play it on the second turn off a [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) or [Llanowar Elves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves)... and the presence of that one-drop will also give the Elder some basic defense against [Geth's Verdict](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geth%27s+Verdict) and its ilk (you know, because of having another body). Because you are green, you can usually keep your Elder bigger than the opponent's planned [Black Sun's Zenith](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Sun%27s+Zenith). Ultimately, most of the time, if you can slip a [Dungrove Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) down early, it's not going anywhere.

The difference here is the versatility of the mana base. Wolf Run Robots plays four copies of [Glimmerpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpost) to defend against beatdown (with [Primeval Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) setting up 6 to 14 life). The justification of the latter is that sometimes you are counter-locked by Blue-Black Control, and being able to naturally draw a [Kessig Wolf Run](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) makes every dorky mana creature into a legitimate threat.

[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Avacyn's Pilgrim)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avacyn's Pilgrim) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Gavony Township)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony Township)
Almost everything about this deck produces tokens. [Geist-Honored Monk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist-Honored+Monk)? Tokens. [Hero of Bladehold](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hero+of+Bladehold)? Tokens. *Both* planeswalkers? Tokens and tokens. Having a ton of tokens on the battlefield paves the way for a devastating [Overrun](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun)s, or, again, tremendous synergy with the new land [Gavony Township](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township).


Speaking of [Gavony Township](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township), [Overrun](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun), and whatever makes guys bigger, [Mirran Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mirran+Crusader) (despite neither being nor producing a token) gets double bonuses from anything that gives bonuses, on account of having the envelope-pushing two-word text combination *double strike.*

### Esper (Solar Flare) and Blue-Black


Blue is most commonly paired with black in this format, and that is largely based on the synergy between [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) and fast removal sells like [Doom Blade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom+Blade). Traditionally, Blue-Black Control decks (as opposed to White-Blue Control decks, which have traditionally had [Day of Judgment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) or previously [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God)) have suffered falling behind the beatdown for lack of removal tonnage. [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) just helps Blue-Black shore that up (without wasting a whole lot of extra slots on cards that are bad in the mirror).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Snapcaster Mage)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=SNapcaster Mage) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Doom Blade)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doom Blade)
Blue-Black may be a popular choice among the most highly skilled Worlds competitors. It is just chock-full of all the things control players love to play (Nick Spagnolo told me he just wants to play [Think Twice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) and [Forbidden Alchemy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forbidden+Alchemy) forever). This kind of deck draws cards, counters target spell, and finishes the game with amazing nails-in-coffins like [Consecrated Sphinx](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consecrated+Sphinx). Even its land—[Nephalia Drownyard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard), and all four after going to boards—is so irresistible in the mirror... where you of course mill *yourself* the first several times to set up [Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage), before eventually killing the opponent with it.

### Solar Flare


Especially early in the format, the control deck of choice was probably Solar Flare.


Solar Flare is almost "everything to everyone"... With many of the control trappings of Blue-Black, Solar Flare includes more white removal like [Day of Judgment](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment) or the flexible [Oblivion Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring), and a saucy one-two punch in [Sun Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan) + [Phantasmal Image](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image) as a finishing combo (you can double up on [Sun Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan)s there, or use [Phantasmal Image](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal+Image) to kill [Thrun, the Last Troll](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thrun%2C+the+Last+Troll)).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sun Titan)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun Titan) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Phantasmal Image)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasmal Image)
The down side is that Solar Flare's mana is a bit more difficult to hit consistently than... almost every other deck in the format, and all that flexibility can come at an additional cost (here we see Jonathan Medina's list, with nary a [Mana Leak](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) to its name):

Former US National Champion and GP Top 8 competitor Joshua Wagener showcased some poisoner's skills in 2011 with this Infect decklist.


Infect is... Infect. Straightforward, double damage; an inheritor of Phyrexian *Scars* Block Constructed dark arts. Big things to keep in mind: 1) [Phyrexian Crusader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Crusader) is a big game; it can attack right through many creature configurations and laughs off [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)s. 2) [Lashwrithe](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lashwrithe) is the insta-kill; if Infect hits the [Lashwrithe](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lashwrithe), it can come in for lethal poison in just one or two evasive moves quite often (no mana cost to equip + massive incremental power... ka-pow!).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Phyrexian Crusader)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian Crusader) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lashwrithe)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lashwrithe)
Better than it might look: [Virulent Wound](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Virulent+Wound).


![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td169_virulent.jpg)

This card might be better at what [Wring Flesh](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wring+Flesh) does than Neeman's [Wring Flesh](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wring+Flesh)! Obviously a great first play in Infect to take out a [Birds of Paradise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) on a turn where black has no proactive play. Between [Contagion Clasp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contagion+Clasp) and [Tezzeret's Gambit](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tezzeret%27s+Gambit), it might even be possible to win without rumbling in the Red Zone (but it's not like you should try that).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Virulent Wound)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Virulent Wound) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Contagion Clasp)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contagion Clasp)
Well, there you have them all.


All the colors.


Lots of different decks and unique strategies: A wide palette of possibilities for the finale of the 2011 season... and this list of lists isn't even everything. There are Blue-Black decks that focus on removal rather than control, Tezzeret decks, Ramp decks with white and probably other stuff we haven't seen yet; we didn't even talk about Humans!


I don't know about you, but I can't wait to see who's trading blows on Sunday afternoon.
