The Future Reflected Back
=========================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on April 22, 2016 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 






[Pro Tour *Shadows over Innistrad*](http://magic.wizards.com/en/events/coverage/ptsoi) is upon us, and the best players in the world have gathered in Madrid, Spain, in hopes of immortalizing themselves with a victory on one of the game's biggest stages. *Shadows over Innistrad* is the start to a new block, and as a result, the Standard format is going to shift around in a big way. With $250,000 in prize money up for grabs, players will be tackling the new format and bringing their best in pursuit of cash, Pro Points, and seats at the 2016 World Championship.


*Shadows over Innistrad* has drastically altered the Standard landscape. The absence of mana fixing from *Khans of Tarkir* will force players to be more frugal with their mana bases. The format has barely been played for a week and we're already seeing new and exciting archetypes climb to the forefront of the Standard metagame.


With tons of new restrictions and powerful card options like [Archangel Avacyn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+Avacyn), the new Standard format may seem like a whole different world. Today, we'll be taking a look at what we can expect to see in the Standard portion of the Pro Tour.

The most successful new strategy at the recent StarCityGames.com Standard Open in Baltimore was Humans, a deck that backs up the format's most aggressive creatures with tempo plays like [Declaration in Stone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Declaration+in+Stone). The white-blue version of the deck secured two spots in the Top 8 of the tournament and another two spots in the Top 16; mono-white and green-white versions of the deck also had quite a bit of success. Emma Handy managed a 14th-place finish with the deck and broke down the archetype quite well in her [recent column for GatheringMagic](http://www.gatheringmagic.com/emmahandy-041416-building-the-six-million-dollar-man/), The deck applies very fast pressure with a host of 2-power creatures for one mana that can often lead to starts involving three creatures in play on the second turn. This combination, especially when backed up by a removal spell like [Declaration in Stone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Declaration+in+Stone) — or with [Kytheon, Hero of Akros](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kytheon%2C+Hero+of+Akros) in the mix—can lead to some quick runaway wins against decks that are trying to do bigger and more exciting things.


The deck has a lot of powerful interactions that may not be obvious at first glance. [Always Watching](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Always+Watching) is great in a deck that aims to swarm the battlefield, giving the strategy a powerful offensive boost while also allowing the player to set up perfect blocks in a format without a ton of non-red instant-speed removal. [Always Watching](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Always+Watching) gets especially strong when played with [Archangel of Tithes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Tithes), taxing the opponent in every combat step.


[Declaration in Stone](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Declaration+in+Stone) may be the most powerful Constructed card in *Shadows over Innistrad*, and it's definitely strongest in an aggressive shell that punishes the opponent for spending two mana to simply draw an extra card in the earlier turns of the game.


This version of the deck has a pseudo-transformational sideboard that makes the deck match up very well against the piles of [Pyroclasm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyroclasm) effects that opponents bring in for post-sideboarded games. Humans can overcome the inevitable hate cards by going bigger with [Gideon, Ally of Zendikar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gideon%2C+Ally+of+Zendikar) and [Archangel Avacyn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+Avacyn).


Aggressive strategies perform very well at the Pro Tour, and Humans seem to be all the rage. Other versions of the Humans deck also performed very well in the first weekend of *Shadows over Innistrad* Standard, establishing the archetype well enough that we can expect to see a lot of [Kozilek's Return](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kozilek%27s+Return) and [Radiant Flames](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Radiant+Flames) playing cleanup out of midrange and control decks this coming weekend.

Bant Company was already a major player in Standard, and *Shadows over Innistrad* pushed the deck to new heights with additions such as [Duskwatch Recruiter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duskwatch+Recruiter) and [Tireless Tracker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tireless+Tracker). Jim Davis won the StarCityGames.com Standard Open in Baltimore with the deck and created a [good write-up about its contents and construction here](http://www.starcitygames.com/article/32759_All-About-Bant-Company.html). [Collected Company](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Collected+Company) has a very high raw power level, and cards like [Bounding Krasis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bounding+Krasis) and [Reflector Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflector+Mage) give the deck enough of a tempo advantage to take over the game. [Duskwatch Recruiter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duskwatch+Recruiter) gives the deck an entirely new angle, essentially allowing the controller to pay three mana to draw a card at will while also allowing the deck to play multiple spells off of four mana despite the incredibly high number of three-mana creatures. [Tireless Tracker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tireless+Tracker) is often a powerhouse if we're securing a two- or three-for-one opportunity, and it's a great target for [Collected Company](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Collected+Company). [Archangel Avacyn](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+Avacyn) provides the deck with a new top end that finishes games quickly and protects the team before flipping over to swing the tides of battle against opposing creature strategies.


Bant Company has a good amount of room to change before the Pro Tour. The deck's core ([Bounding Krasis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bounding+Krasis), [Reflector Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflector+Mage), [Duskwatch Recruiter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duskwatch+Recruiter), [Sylvan Advocate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Advocate), and [Collected Company](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Collected+Company)) will likely remain untouched, but there's a lot of room left for innovation in the remaining slots.

We all expected enemy color Eldrazi decks to do well in a format where enemy pain lands are an option. White-Black Eldrazi seems to be the best home for this type of strategy—taking full advantage of [Knight of the White Orchid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid) seems like exactly where we want to be. The deck plays some of the format's most powerful cards, including [Sorin, Grim Nemesis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sorin%2C+Grim+Nemesis), a planeswalker that threatens to take over the game entirely by itself.


[Eldrazi Displacer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Displacer) had a lot of people excited when it first got printed, and our hopes for the card are finally coming to fruition with *Shadows over Innistrad* joining the mix.


The new Standard will likely feature a lot of large battlefields where the action is stalled while one players tries to engineer a favorable opportunity to start attacking. In these situations, there's no better card than [Eldrazi Displacer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Displacer). If things get to the point where the White-Black Eldrazi player has [Eldrazi Displacer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Displacer) out alongside [Thought-Knot Seer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thought-Knot+Seer), then the [Eldrazi Displacer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eldrazi+Displacer) can be activated during the opponent's draw step on every turn to ensure that the opponent never finds the answer they're looking for.


Eric Froehlich [wrote about the deck](http://www.channelfireball.com/articles/deck-of-the-day-whiteblack-midrange/) for his popular Deck of the Day series on ChannelFireball and discussed its contents at length.

The deck that had everyone scratching their heads as it rose to the top of the standings in Baltimore was Todd Anderson's [Pyromancer's Goggles](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Goggles) deck. [Pyromancer's Goggles](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Goggles) has become a lot more powerful in the new Standard. This deck can maneuver its way through a lot of hoops to clean up the board before completely taking over the game with a copied [Magmatic Insight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magmatic+Insight) or [Tormenting Voice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormenting+Voice). [Drownyard Temple](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drownyard+Temple), a card that I was unreasonably amused with, is at its finest here, where it can be discarded for advantage with [Tormenting Voice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormenting+Voice) or [Magmatic Insight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magmatic+Insight) before being returned to the battlefield on the third turn to conveniently ramp us into five mana to cast [Pyromancer's Goggles](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Goggles).


The deck can also draw lot of cards with [Jori En, Ruin Diver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jori+En%2C+Ruin+Diver), and the amount of inexpensive removal present means that it matches up well against the format's more aggressive strategies—especially when on the play.


Eric Froehlich also [wrote about this deck](http://www.channelfireball.com/articles/deck-of-the-day-ur-goggles/) in his Deck of the Day series, so be sure to check out his take on this powerful new archetype.
