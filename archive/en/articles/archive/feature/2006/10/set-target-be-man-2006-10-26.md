Set the Target. Be the Man.
===========================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on October 26, 2006 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Champs is this weekend. 

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/tournamentcenter/champs06.jpg)I know, I know. Some Champs tournaments were actually *last* weekend, but for this article's purposes, let's just go with "Champs is this weekend."

 For most players, Champs is not just the opportunity to defend their local turf and swing big for bragging rights... It's the first chance to play with *Time Spiral* in sanctioned Standard. Most years, players come into Champs fairly cold, relying on skeletons of decks from the previous year's Standard, or cribbing from the immediately preceding Block Constructed PTQs... and Ravnica Block didn't have any of those. Theoretically, with the tidal wave of *Time Spiral* crashing into the already diverse landscape of *Ravnica*-influenced Standard with its multitude of dual lands, the format should be more random than ever. Fear not, wary traveler! Swimming With Sharks has something special for you. Last weekend, the stage was set by a couple of Standard tournaments around the world. We have a couple of deck lists that should serve as benchmarks, punching bags... and hopefully inspiration.

You may have caught a blog entry in the Kobe coverage that contained what Ted Knutson called "this season's debutantes." Here they are again:

This is a hybrid beatdown deck somewhat reminiscent of 8StoneRain.dec. The difference between Otsuka's deck and that Sea Stompy variant is that it goes white for [Watchwolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Watchwolf) and support cards, rather than blue for [Trygon Predator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trygon+Predator) and counterspells.

 The land destruction theme here is fairly light, but with drops like [Kird Ape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kird+Ape) and [Watchwolf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Watchwolf), any pronounced tempo in the early game can prove fatal for the opponent. Otsuka's deck has seven one-mana accelerators to facilitate his second turn [Stone Rain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stone+Rain) or [Cryoclasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryoclasm); [Avalanche Riders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avalanche+Riders) is a redundancy, potential threat, and, oftentimes, a [Fog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fog) for dangerous creatures.

[Loxodon Warhammer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Warhammer) seems an odd choice, but with sufficient mana, it can turn almost any creature into a legitimate threat. One thing to remember with this deck is that with the white splash, [Thornscape Battlemage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thornscape+Battlemage) can kill Signets, not just bears, so that the card is useful both against little beaters and as a semi-redundancy on the mana control theme.

This deck takes the typical Blue Snow theme and splashes Red for [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred), a card that was called the best card of the previous Standard. Hanaoka's mana base is actually more robust than it might initially seem, with its strange-looking *three* 
[Scrying Sheets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scrying+Sheets) and the atypical [Steam Vents](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steam+Vents) (which are not Snow lands) supporting a minor Snow theme. Unlike some Snow decks that play all Snow lands and many Snow creatures, this one is actually a one-for-one permission deck that can certainly profit over time when it has spare mana but has other avenues for card advantage ([Careful Consideration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Careful+Consideration) and [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice) are both self-contained card advantage and a combo with one another). 

The [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) splash makes the deck much stronger against opponents like Zoo than most decks of this stripe, but even with [Repeal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Repeal) and quick [Phyrexian Ironfoot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Ironfoot), this deck isn't looking for the aggro matchup. The fact that it can stick and protect a [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Mage+of+Zhalfir) helps to resolve [Rimefeather Owl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rimefeather+Owl), a sure game winner... if quite expensive.

This is a fairly straightforward [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel) control deck, albeit focused on board control like certain of the PT Charleston versions, rather than breaking Zur's Weirding long game with [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel) or trying to take "true" control like Kamiel's Top 16 deck from PT Honolulu.

While it does not play permission or lock elements, the Yano deck has tons of card drawing and even more creature kill. Between [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn), [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix), [Faith's Fetters](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Fetters), and [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), beatdown decks are in a lot of trouble. [Story Circle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Story+Circle) can force the opponent to overcommit... and then BAM! [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) gets card advantage. This may be the first look at Boros Deck Wins I've ever seen that has more burn spells than creatures... And what an unusual creature mix we have! I actually don't really understand the numbers on [Leonin Skyhunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Skyhunter) (2) versus [Skyknight Legionnaire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skyknight+Legionnaire) (3) or the absence of [Boros Garrison](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boros+Garrison), but besides these individual tweaks and odd balance on the proactive elements side, Abe's deck is a straightforward implementation. The Lions and two-drops (and three three-drops) soften the opponent up, then you put the scoundrel out of business with one or a flurry of those 23 burn spells. [Threaten](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Threaten) and [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire) are particularly suited to the job, [Threaten](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Threaten) because it invalidates the control "tap out for a monolith" defensive strategy and [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire) because, hell, *it's [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire)*, and not even [Honorable Passage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Honorable+Passage) or [Circle of Protection: Red](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Circle+of+Protection%3A+Red) will save you.

![The Rack](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=The+Rack)Adachi's deck has a massive amount of discard. He has [Stupor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stupor) and [Castigate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Castigate)... and eight of his creatures for discard. Now sometimes a discard deck can get the advantage in the early game, but because most of black's cheap creatures are nothing to write home about offensively, the opponent has a chance to draw out of the disruption or even get ahead if he is blue. Welcome to [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack)! [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack) helps to cut down the number of turns the opponent has. If you get him down... he's staying down. Beatdown decks especially have problems fighting [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack). The dilemma is that an opposing beatdown deck that has been halted in the short term is almost necessarily losing the race to [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack)... but playing out threats to fight back *just makes [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack) better* . On the other hand, holding back non-essential lands makes the discard better. It can be miserable.

In previous formats, and for about the past seven years even through Extended, beatdown decks - certainly the kind like we have in Standard with 3/3 creatures for two and a lot of card advantage and efficient burn spells - could race because the discard creatures suck(ed) in comparison, but [The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack) really changes that math.

Ohmichi's is pretty close to the default Rakdos 60 for Standard. This archetype is hell on anything relying on [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) and [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) due to [Shadow Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shadow+Guildmage), and can burn out decks that deploy turn four [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%2C+Angel+of+Wrath) with surprising defiance. I think that Nobuhito's deck is short maybe 1-2 lands (probably the fourth [Rakdos Carnarium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Carnarium) should be there), and I personally have had no luck whatsoever with [Gemstone Caverns](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gemstone+Caverns), which, by the by, is atrocious in multiples. You really want to hit ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) on turn one and ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif) on turn two, because that is what your vital early game drops cost to play. [Shadow Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shadow+Guildmage) gets worse as the game progresses but really retards a green deck's game plan if you can hit it on the first.

Shiotani's deck is almost Heezy Street, but with [Avalanche Riders](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Avalanche+Riders) on haste four in lieu of [Giant Solifuge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Giant+Solifuge). I can see the attractiveness of this choice in a world of Karoos, but Mark would not approve.

The switch of [Frenzied Goblin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frenzied+Goblin) and [Scorched Rusalka](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scorched+Rusalka) into [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) gave Shiotani an excuse to cuts lands (specifically the [Skarrg, the Rage Pits](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Skarrg%252C%2Bthe%2BRage%2BPits)) and also a more explosive [Burning-Tree Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning-Tree+Shaman) and [Call of the Herd](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) early game. Otherwise, this deck echoes the PT - Honolulu Champion's deck (efficient beatdown drops in the early-to-middle turns), but with a greater emphasis on burn cards to improve the control matchup Game One.

Finally we have the tournament winner! In case you didn't notice, the Japan Top 8 was largely creature decks, and G/W [Glare of Subdual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glare+of+Subdual) specializes in beating creature decks. Kai (You've got to love that name for a tournament champion!) upgraded [Wood Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wood+Elves) to [Yavimaya Dryad](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yavimaya+Dryad) for this version and added a host of new cards: [Saffi Eriksdotter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saffi+Eriksdotter), one [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%2C+Angel+of+Wrath), [Stonewood Invocation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stonewood+Invocation), and four big [Thelonite Hermit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thelonite+Hermit)s. The inclusion of the Hermits echoes old style [Deranged Hermit](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deranged+Hermit) + [Opposition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opposition) decks, and gives Kai's version a "9/9" threat to win quickly after tapping down potential defenders. 

 Overall this deck is straightforward [Glare of Subdual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glare+of+Subdual) with no dedicated search ([Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) or [Congregation at Dawn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Congregation+at+Dawn), a.k.a. "Three Stupid Elephants"), instead focusing on a quicker, almost guaranteed, kill via [Stonewood Invocation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stonewood+Invocation). This is a superb choice for creature metagames but possibly a gamble if the guy on the other side of the table is Solar [Flare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flare).

Here is an example of what classic U/W control might look like in *Time Spiral* Standard. The defense robust with [Faith's Fetters](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Fetters), [Condemn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Condemn), [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), and copious permission. The endgame is simply the best in the format, albeit requiring eight main phase mana, in [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%2C+Angel+of+Wrath). The innovation, or alternate kill at least, is [Sacred Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sacred+Mesa). With this down, Smith's deck can overwhelm another control player or run a frustrating defense for creatures that forces over-commitment into [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God).

This is a solid look at a post-Kamigawa, *Time Spiral*-legal version of the deck Frank talked about in [My Babykiller, Part 1](/en/articles/archive/my-babykiller-part-1-2006-10-04) and other articles. The interesting delta to me is removing [Into the North](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+North) and sideboarding [Skred](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skred) for main-deck [Stormbind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stormbind) and [Rumbling Slum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rumbling+Slum). I actually quite liked [Into the North](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+North) because it allowed the deck to play only one [Mouth of Ronom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mouth+of+Ronom) and increased the frequency of hitting [Scrying Sheets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scrying+Sheets) (which Woltereck has reduced to three copies). Like the U/R deck we saw above, this one runs a de-emphasized [scrying sheets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=scrying%2Bsheets) engine but can benefit over many games whenever it has spare mana ([Boreal Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boreal+Druid), [Ohran Viper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ohran+Viper), and [Stalking Yeti](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stalking+Yeti) bolster the mana base).

Out [Compulsive Research](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsive+Research), in [Phyrexian Arena](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Arena). Out permission, in discard. Out [Zur's Weirding](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zur%27s+Weirding), in, um, [Phyrexian Arena](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Arena) again.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/mtgcom/fcpics/features/mf117-4.jpg)The Arena serves multiple purposes here. First of all, it is the new draw engine over blue. It is vastly inferior in that it costs you life and doesn't discard [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel), and superior in that it is an essentially neverending supply of card advantage without mana requirement long game, rather than a three mana single shot at your next land or a raw +1. While it lacks the obvious synergy with [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel) that [Compulsive Research](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsive+Research) has, [Phyrexian Arena](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Arena) has what we call in the trade "a sick combo" where [Firemane Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firemane+Angel) essentially erases the Arena's drawback, setting the deck up for a robust anti-control long game and mitigating the potentially disastrous investment against beatdown by *some* margin.

[Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Void) is a card Mark Herberholz and I have been trying to break for close to a month. Mihealsick has an admirable redundancy over [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) here. His deck seems really nicely set up against creatures in the short term and has numerous ways to annoy control and win long games. I would not like to have to out-think his [Rix Maadi, Dungeon Palace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rix+Maadi%2C+Dungeon+Palace) in a twenty turn game.

Yeah, yeah. I think Mike just wanted to hit both sides of [Hide // Seek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hide+%2F%2F+Seek), too.

We have seen [Dimir House Guard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dimir+House+Guard) engines many times in the past. Adam's features [Plague Sliver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plague+Sliver), [Faith's Fetters](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%27s+Fetters), [Persecute](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persecute), the underrated [Bottled Cloister](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bottled+Cloister), and of course [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) as key targets. Otherwise this is a straightforward B/W control deck that plans to win a long game after generating incremental card advantage in the middle turns. Along with another deck in this Top 8, the only deck that was expressly more successful given Bowles's second place finish, Fader features *Ravnica* Block Constructed superstar [Skeletal Vampire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skeletal+Vampire) in what is sure to be an illustrious Standard career over the next twelve or so months.
