===========



 Posted in **Feature**

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 







This week we are going to do a split personality of an article, half covering Standard developments from some additional National Championships, and half returning to the lands of *Lorwyn* and *Shadowmoor* for the weekly PTQ update.

In honor of Elf Week, I elected to start with Dr. Chris Stocking's deck... which is anything but a stock Elf deck.

Stocking has a number of unique customizations going on in this list, including seven main-deck persist creatures. [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) is not so unusual—many pros consider it the best card in *Shadowmoor*—but [Murderous Redcap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Redcap) in an Elf deck is pretty far afield of the typical spell suite. Stocking made room by moving [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) to the sideboard and removing recent fan favorite [Civic Wayfinder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Civic+Wayfinder) completely off the island... err... [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest).


This Elf deck—in the main at least—is built on a similar model to Sam Black's from the [U.S. National Championship](/en/events/coverage/jacob-re-ignites-great-chicago-fire), that is, not dissimilar from Charles Gindy's deck from [Pro Tour–Hollywood](/en/events/coverage/gindy-puts-name-lights), but with no [Garruk Wildspeaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk+Wildspeaker) and many [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror)-like effects instead. However instead of the essentially ubiquitous [Nameless Inversion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion)s we see in so many decks Elf, Faerie, or other, Stocking went with Elf-stamped [Eyeblight's Ending](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eyeblight%27s+Ending) instead. So... Elves?

![Pyromancer's Swath](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath)

Here is a deck that hasn't seen a huge amount of online coverage, Swath burn combo. This particular version is built on a similar model to the Innovator [Dragonstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonstorm) deck that rocked the [2007 World Championships](/en/events/coverage/historic-wins-peleg-swiss-worlds)—that is, it is a Storm combo deck utilizing [Spinerock Knoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spinerock+Knoll) as an additional enabler (see below)... just without the [Dragonstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonstorm)s.

Instead, Orsini-Jones ran many other storm spells, all the big red ones, between main and side. A typical setup for this deck might be to suspend [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom), later [Rift Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt), let the pair warp in, [Rift Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt) dealing 3 damage, and putting two spells on the storm tally. Then come main phase, it might be [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) into [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose) (a painless extra spell), then [Pyromancer's Swath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath) and a "mere" [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock). The [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock) is 4 due to the Swath, meaning 7 with the earlier [Rift Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt), meaning that the [Spinerock Knoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spinerock+Knoll) that has been sitting off to the side since turn one is not online. Flip... It's [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot)! What a disaster. Let's see... [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom), [Rift Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rift+Bolt), [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose), Swath, [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock), [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot) itself... With the Swath, that's... in the business, *more than enough*. Good game.

Here is an interesting take on a common strategy. Davies's "Good Old Rock" is anything but the typical Rock deck. This deck has a tremendous amount of land-search mana acceleration, including a minor snow theme ([Into the North](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Into+the+North) for [Arctic Flats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arctic+Flats)). For a fairly ponderous polychromatic deck, it seems like hell on the red deck—[Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command), and Warhammer all... though Warhammer and Doran may border on non-bo.

Frank made what can only be considered an extremely reasonable choice in main deck: a Demigod red deck very similar to the one Michael Jacob used to win the U.S. National Championship a few weeks ago.


That sideboard is... unusual. But similar to a comment Frank made of Wessel Oomens as playing a "highlander" red deck, there isn't a bad card in the bunch; and while less consistent, maybe, than a conventional 4/4/4/3 sideboard (especially for a Red Deck), Frank's probably didn't require a *lot* of sideboarding, and the wild number of one-ofs could create some very heinous corner case situations ([Dragon's Claw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragon%27s+Claw) in the mirror, and so on).

![Zur the Enchanter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Zur+the+Enchanter)

This deck obviously runs what we would typically think of as the "Swans" combo—that is, it gets out a [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll) and a [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault), then starts discarding lands to ting the Swans to draw two cards. It draws until it can find a [Dakmor Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dakmor+Salvage), which can then be dredged as one of the draws; dredging [Gaea's Blessing](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Blessing) makes decking essentially impossible... Once the deck can set up two [Dakmor Salvage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dakmor+Salvage)s it can continually damage the Swans to draw cards, net, and discard to damage the opponent with the [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) (two draws, two Salvages to draw, carry on).

Like many Swans decks, Ruben's played numerous search spells, such as [Beseech the Queen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beseech+the+Queen), to find key cards / combination pieces. In addition, [Glittering Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glittering+Wish) is an additional overlay that allows the deck to find not only [Swans of Bryn Argoll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swans+of+Bryn+Argoll)... but [Zur the Enchanter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zur+the+Enchanter)!


Now [Zur the Enchanter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zur+the+Enchanter) is something very new and exciting, qualifying Ruben's deck as a hybrid control-combo deck. In addition to his Swans combo, Snijdewind can set up Zur to attack, grab the [Steel of the Godhead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+of+the+Godhead), and dominate offensively.


Conveniently, Zur can also complete the Swans combo by obtaining [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault).


All of the Netherlands National Championships Top 8 decks can be [found here](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/top-8-decklists-2008-08-16-0).

This deck is a core Elementals deck, mostly... The cool thing is the inclusion of [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower). Doran is of course one of the most efficient and powerful threat creatures printed in recent years, but the problem with him has always been the prohibitive mana cost... Well, Elementals is already chock-full of Vivid lands and [Reflecting Pool](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reflecting+Pool)s, so mise well.


The inclusion of [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower) is compelling thanks to the synergy with powerhouse [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark). Ka pow! No power on a Doran.

This is a really interesting deck that includes [River Kelpie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Kelpie). While the deck has a pretty "Mannequin" feel to it, but the inclusion of [River Kelpie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Kelpie) makes it something a little special...



1. [River Kelpie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Kelpie) has a natural synergy with the evoke mechanic and [Makeshift Mannequin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Makeshift+Mannequin) (or [Profane Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Profane+Command)) ... [Mulldrifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter) draws three cards, [Shriekmaw](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shriekmaw) moves from [Terror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror) to [Annihilate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Annihilate) with no incremental mana cost.
2. Both [River Kelpie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Kelpie)'s persist and that of [Puppeteer Clique](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Puppeteer+Clique) make opposing attacks sticky.
3. And then there's [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime)... By now you know that [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime) can be devastating, even when all it is doing is trading late game lands for your precious spells. But with [River Kelpie](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Kelpie) in play, [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime) actually feeds itself!

Not surprisingly, Faeries continues to dominate a good amount of Block success, but there continue to be some very exciting and different options to play that can still be competitive in the format.
