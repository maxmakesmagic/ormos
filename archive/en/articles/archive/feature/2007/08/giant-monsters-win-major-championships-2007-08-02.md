Giant Monsters Win Major Championships
======================================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on August 2, 2007 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






The Onslaught doesn't begin this week, but after seeing the finishes from both United States and Great Britain National Championships (and the Magic Scholarship Series!), my only impulse is to quote the Bard, ("the Bard" being onetime Grand Prix coverage prodigy Josh Bennett) and say that [Giant Idiots Ruin Games](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/feature/88).

The most appropriate fit is of course the deck played by US National Team member-turned-US National Champion Luis Scott-Vargas.

I actually had no idea that this was the weapon of choice for both last year's and this year's National Champions when I first saw it in action, watching MSS Day One competition. A young master in the Scholarship Series was surrounded by all manner of [Ravenous Rats](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ravenous+Rats), was being beaten down by a [Stromgald Crusader](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stromgald+Crusader), and had his back against the wall... make that against *[The Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Rack).* At the end of the opponent's turn, he calmly tapped a stack of lands and his [Wall of Roots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Roots), added that signature G, and queried "[Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling) for six?" I had no idea what could get him out of the spot (by his lands, I assumed he was U/G/W *Blink*, deck of the hour since the Kentucky Open)... then out popped [Crovax, Ascendant Hero](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crovax%2C+Ascendant+Hero). Everyone on the other side died, and the opposite of the [Ascendant Evincar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ascendant+Evincar) quickly fielded a fine comeback.

The next game was crazier. How do you get out from under an oppressive tsunami of discard spells? How about drawing four cards per turn? [Arcanis the Omnipotent](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcanis+the+Omnipotent) came to play via the same Chord for six.

While these singletons are flashy, situationally advantageous, and potentially dominating, we would be lax in our duties if we didn't point out the deck's Pickles pedigree. Much like the best deck in Time Spiral Block Constructed, Scott-Vargas's Standard deck can lock the opponent down with a Stasis-like lock, and thanks to [Chord of Calling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chord+of+Calling), can do so at instant speed. The Pickles combo is of course [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental) plus [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter). Flipping [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental) up steals an untap; flipping [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter) up, pointing at Brine Elmental, accomplishes the same, but will do so every turn as [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter) can turn face down each upkeep.  
 One of the really great things about this deck is that, like Project X, it doesn't have to play for its combo strategy to win a fair fight. Luis's deck has superb card drawing, arguably the best card in Standard in [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand), and a great set of creatures. You can beat Red Decks simply by laying [Wall of Roots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Roots) into [Loxodon Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Hierarch), sitting behind a couple of permission spells, and beating or blocking as the individual game situation dictates.

If you want to talk about giant idiots ruining games, it is hard to imagine a more gigantic idiot -- or more idiotic giant -- than [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon).

Greg Poverelli, the reigning New York State finalist in Constructed, walked away with the big trophy and the bigger scholarship at this past weekend's MSS Championship piloting an update to Steve Sadin's [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) Red Deck. Greg made room for the obvious [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) by splitting similar drops [Martyr of Ashes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr+of+Ashes) and [Scorched Rusalka](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scorched+Rusalka), and replaced [Volcanic Hammer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Hammer) with the obvious update [Incinerate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Incinerate). 

This Red Deck is deceptively strategic and methodical. It can play a straight game of creatures, trading, and jockeying for board position, but is almost a different deck in any game where it opens on [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon). The ostensible fight is the one on the board, with reasonable Red creatures -- joined by the inimitable [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) -- press the opponent's life total, but what is *really* going on is that the Red Deck player is looking for a spot to stick that Gargadon and strike for 13 or more damage in a single turn. Tap out for [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) when you're behind? You might just be dead.

[Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon), despite being probably the most gigantic giant idiot to define the present Standard format, is actually quite a cunning foe. Its presence in this deck essentially blanks key anti-aggressive spells like [Tendrils of Corruption](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Corruption) and really takes a lot of the tooth out of obvious foils like [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix). When the opponent points a life gain elimination spell at one of your creatures, you can sacrifice it to [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon), speed up that giant's appearance, and prevent the opponent from gaining life.

This site's own The Professor, Craig Jones, won the top spot in Great Britain with a similar deck, albeit slower and composed of ostensibly -- some would say *obviously* -- better cards.

Drake's U/R/W Blink deck abuses the synergies between 187 creatures, [Aethermage's Touch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aethermage%27s+Touch), and [Momentary Blink](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Momentary+Blink). For example, he can play [Aethermage's Touch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aethermage%27s+Touch) at the end of the opponent's turn *after "at end of turn" triggers have been put on the stack*, flip up a [Bogardan Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bogardan+Hellkite), nail the opponent for five, refuse to return the Hellkite to his hand (see [The Legacy of White Lightning!](/en/articles/archive/legacy-white-lightning-2006-08-31) to see why this giant idiot refuses to return to hand), fly over for another five come the next combat, then before the delayed [Aethermage's Touch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aethermage%27s+Touch) trigger fires, [Momentary Blink](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Momentary+Blink) the Hellkite, essentially untapping it *and* braining the opponent for five, never having to fulfill [Aethermage's Touch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aethermage%27s+Touch)'s now turn-old return condition. That would be one example. If you had enough mana you could just flash the Blink and end it.

Drake's deck has numerous cute synergies. For example he can play [Grand Arbiter Augustin IV](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grand+Arbiter+Augustin+IV) and then get away with [Numot, the Devastator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Numot%2C+the+Devastator) for only four total mana.

Project X remained one of the most underrated decks of the format this past weekend. Numerous commentators have stated that [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) blanks this combo deck, but from what I have seen, Project X can beat such decks "fairly" with Hierarchs, Walls, and the like without being forced into a game where everything depends on keeping an [Essence Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Warden) in play versus a recurring [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) (thanks [Crypt Champion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crypt+Champion)!). 

This doesn't have to do with Bennett's listing specifically, but is something I saw while wandering around over the course of the weekend... G/W Glare is typically considered to be a dog against Project X (similar deck matchup, one deck can gain infinite life, the other has little-to-no relevant disruption), but *just playing [Saffi Eriksdotter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saffi+Eriksdotter)* seemed to buy a fair amount of time. The Project X engine will return Glare's [Saffi Eriksdotter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Saffi+Eriksdotter) every turn, and The Legend Rule will make Project X Saffi recursion impossible.

[Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic) fans will love taking this deck for a spin. Michael packed his deck full of things that can be put on a [Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic), from Cloaks to Hammers to Invocations. It was called the perfect metagame deck. Jacob had himself a 4-1 favorite -- or better -- against essentially any Blink deck... and this past weekend, there were quite a few. [Scryb Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scryb+Ranger) is hell on two for a [Riftwing Cloudskate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Riftwing+Cloudskate), and [Silhana Ledgewalker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silhana+Ledgewalker) didn't get any *less* scary since the last time we took a pass on straight Green.

Besides a strong palette of efficient creatures at essentially every drop, Jacob's deck can play positively *Red* with its hasty Solifuges, [Timbermare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Timbermare)s ([Lava Axe](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Axe)s), and of course Ball Lightnings (Groundshakers). While Michael still *technically* had to close in combat, the presence of so much haste made it difficult for removal decks to be able to sit back and play scripted elimination strategies.

Conrad's deck was another superb metagame choice, even if it looks a bit outdated for the format. He benefited greatly from the lack of [Detritivore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detritivore)s in the room, losing only one game in the Swiss portion, and opening up 2-0 against Michael Jacobs's Green deck, though testing showed the first game to be close to impossible. Conrad's deck features numerous one-ofs, Silver Bullets appropriate to specific circumstances (though he eventually concluded that the lone [Dimir Signet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dimir+Signet) should have been a 27th land). 

The Kolos take on B/U can answer essentially anything, and can get ahead with [Haunting Hymn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Haunting+Hymn), but what might not be immediately obvious is that it lacks a hard counter. The [Rune Snag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rune+Snag)s get progressively worse, and especially in the first game, the deck will have problems with something along the lines of a recurring [Moldervine Cloak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moldervine+Cloak) even if the coast seems otherwise clear.

The former winner came in with a crazy Rakdos deck that looks more like some of the recent Gruul builds, making numerous decisions centered around [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon). The most obvious switch between major archetypes is [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) over [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf). [Kher Keep](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kher+Keep) plus [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon) is at least medium exciting. [Fatal Frenzy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fatal+Frenzy) on the Gargadon will almost always be game. Gargadon plus [Epochrasite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Epochrasite) is one that I hadn't seen before... but seems kind of obvious if you think about it.

Before you ask, the answer is *yes.* Ant got a little unlucky and flipped giant idiot [Greater Gargadon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Greater+Gargadon) with his clever little Bob Maher to lose his quarterfinal match. Lest you start wagging your fearful finger, though, don't forget that the potential non-bo got the 2005 Champion back into the Top 8 for the third straight year.

And from the Great Britain Top 8...

**Finalist Stuart Wright**

Sti seems to have perfected the Dan Paskins zen sloughing of The [Fear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fear). [Dredge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dredge)? Really? And in Great Britain, epicenter of all things Red Deck? Dan got past numerous [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s, and beat Rakdos in the quarterfinals (Rakdos is even worse than any other Red Deck for [Dredge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dredge), combining the [Bridge from Below](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bridge+from+Below)-cancelling power of [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) and especially [Martyr of Ashes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr+of+Ashes) with the strategy smashing [Withered Wretch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Withered+Wretch)), before eventually falling to The Professor's Red Deck-splash-[Troll Ascetic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Troll+Ascetic) in the big game.

The strategy behind post-Future Sight [Dredge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dredge) hasn't changed. It is still about dumping a good deal of your deck into your graveyard, piggybacking [Narcomoeba](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Narcomoeba) to set up a fast [Dread Return](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dread+Return) flashback targeting [Flame-Kin Zealot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flame-Kin+Zealot), and killing with a thumb thick wedge of Zombies... The only things that have changed are 1) [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic), thanks to X, is here to make the deck's worst matchups even worse, and 2) (more importantly, and perhaps because of the previous point) [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt) and [Extirpate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Extirpate) are no longer the two most popular sideboard cards. Let this deck go unmolested for four turns and you're almost certainly dead. 

**Daniel Godfrey**

The "Aussie Storm" deck seems to be the inheritor to the previous breed of [Hatching Plans](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hatching+Plans) decks. The big combo here is [Pyromancer's Swath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer%27s+Swath) + [Grapeshot](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grapeshot)... Together a relatively discreet Storm 7 will generally prove lethal; moreover, the strategy is no longer reliant on Ingnite the Memories, and won't therefore pack to cards like [Bottled Cloister](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bottled+Cloister).

Now *this* is a compelling mix of top decks. We have any number of viable strategies with every color represented, all the way to mono-Green... But at the same time, there are clear market inefficiencies that can be exploited by talented deck designers. It will be interesting to see how the card pool morphs and adapts as the summer Standard season continues.
