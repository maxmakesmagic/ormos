The Twinning End
================



 Posted in **Daily Deck**
 on May 12, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/Melissa_DeTora.jpg)
By Melissa DeTora




 A Magic Pro player and strategy writer, Melissa's career Top 8s include PT Gatecrash and 2 GPs. She's currently a member of the Wizards of the Coast coverage team. 






Combo decks run rampant in Modern. Despite the extensive [banned list](http://magic.wizards.com/en/gameinfo/gameplay/formats/modern), the Modern card pool is so deep that you can pretty much do anything. The deck we're featuring today plays two completely different combos. You may have heard of [Living End](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End) and [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), but Colten Anderon's Twinning End deck from the [SCG Premier IQ in Portland](http://sales.starcitygames.com/deckdatabase/deckshow.php?event_ID=45&t%5BT3%5D=28&start_date=2015-05-02&end_date=2015-05-03&order_1=finish&limit=8&action=Show+Decks&city=Portland) is playing both.



There are many benefits to playing a deck that can win in two different ways. The main reason is that your opponent won't know how to interact with you. Should they play creature removal for [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch)? How about graveyard hate for [Living End](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End)? Whatever they decide to do, it's very unlikely that they'll have a way to stop both of your combos. Having a Plan B is always beneficial when you're playing a combo deck.



[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Deceiver+Exarch)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Living+End)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End)


[Living End](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End) decks are always interesting to me because you can't play anything with a converted mana cost less than 3. If you play inexpensive spells, you run the risk of cascading into those spells instead of your [Living End](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End). Given that [Deceiver Exarch](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch) costs three and [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) costs four, playing this combo seems like a perfect fit. Your game plan is to cycle creatures early to draw into your [Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) combo. If your opponent has removal for your Exarch, you can cascade into [Living End](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End) and get your Exarch right back onto the battlefield!



One thing that I think is really cool about Modern is that the mana bases are so strong that you can play as many colors as you want. If you don't mind losing 3 life per land drop (1 for a fetch land, 2 for a shock land), playing a four-color deck with heavy mana requirements such as this one is very feasible. [Twisted Abomination](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twisted+Abomination) helps a lot in finding lands, as it finds any swamp card, such as [Blood Crypt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Crypt) or [Watery Grave](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Watery+Grave).
