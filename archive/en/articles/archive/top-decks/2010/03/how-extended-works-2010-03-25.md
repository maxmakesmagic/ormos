How Extended Works
==================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on March 25, 2010 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






The 2009-2010 Extended PTQ season hasn't wound down yet, but it sure is winding. It is during long stretches like this one that we can be particularly grateful that **Magic** is a dynamic game that changes in near-fundamental ways several times per year. And while we are at a point where most of the oohs and ahs of deck design have already been exhaled by an adoring fan base, **Magic** can still remind us of what we love—both as players and as fans of the game—with its sparkly-shiny surprises.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_mori.jpg)*Katsuhiro Mori*This past weekend's [Grand Prix–Yokohama](/en/events/coverage/katsuhiro-conquers-yokohama), for example, demands some bona fide hat doffing. While we can marvel less at the deck innovation of the finalists' weapons of choice, the fans among us have got to love the Finals finale between two of Japan's greatest Pro Tour Champions, battling it out for the big gold cup.


Katsuhiro Mori is a master of the World Championships, everywhere a World Championships, having made three Top 8s in a row, including a win (numerous strong Grand Prix Top 8s notwithstanding). This particular Top 8 (a win itself) featured Mori with the consensus deck to beat ... and no one proved capable of beating it.

We have looked at variations on this theme on numerous occasions this season. In our opinion ... It's still the best. The deck is a fully functional—and, perhaps more importantly, fully fast—[Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths) / [Vampire Hexmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Hexmage) combo deck, capable of one of (if not the) dumbest openings in modern Extended:


1. [Urborg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urborg), [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize) (taking your answer)  
 2. [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths), [Vampire Hexmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Hexmage)


... this is a furious threat deck, more dangerous, more quickly, than basically any other option.


[Vampire Hexmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampire+Hexmage) erases all the time required to set up the usually ponderous [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths), putting a 20/20 threat on the field, attacking, as quickly as turn three.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_token.jpg)  
That side of the deck also features [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) as a card drawing engine ... alongside quite a few other card-drawing spells, for example [Compulsive Research](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsive+Research) and [Thirst for Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge).

| Dark Confidant | Thirst for Knowledge |

The reason that this deck is so special is the hybridization with the [Sword of the Meek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+the+Meek) / [Thopter Foundry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thopter+Foundry) combo. Queue oohs and ahs. This side makes the deck extremely durable against Zoo and other beatdown decks, producing threats (or blockers) while netting life. The [Thopter Foundry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thopter+Foundry) option is vastly superior to, say, a [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom); a game-winner all by itself, a beatdown breaker.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |

Mori's build features a fair number of customizations, including slightly more basic lands for [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) resistance (though sacrificing some flexibility and the ability to win a long attrition mirror due to the absence of [Academy Ruins](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Academy+Ruins)); [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor); and a variety of creature defense options after sideboarding. ![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_kuroda.jpg)*Masashiro Kuroda*  
Masashiro Kuroda was the first Japanese Pro Tour Champion. He is the kind of pro who has stepped away from the game somewhat, but who demands choruses and choruses of cheers from us fans at home every time he does well. Putting his initial mark on the game by winning [PT–Kobe](/en/events/coverage/live-coverage-2004-pro-tour-kobe) in 2004 with a Tsuyoshi Fujita-designed Big Red deck in the face of full-on Affinity, Kuroda is exactly the kind of hero we love to follow from the virtual sidelines.


This time around, he went with what now passes for a fairly standard [Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis) deck. 


The core of this strategy remains unchanged: Go get a three-mana cascade spell, cast it, and you can *only* fire off the [Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis); everything else is too expensive. 


Recent innovations to the strategy include [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) and [Terastodon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terastodon), often [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) PLUS [Terastodon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terastodon) for even more comes-into-play [Vindicate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vindicate) shenanigans. Kuroda's innovations include two basic [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s (to escape [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) by playing [Violent Outburst](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Violent+Outburst)) and [Akroma's Memorial](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Memorial).

Holy [Rakdos Pit Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Pit+Dragon)!


My old *Dissension* preview card hasn't been seen in All-in Red (in the Extended version anyway) since the printing of [Deus of Calamity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deus+of+Calamity) and [Demigod of Revenge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demigod+of+Revenge) in *Shadowmoor* kicked [Arc-Slogger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger) off the island ... err ... *[Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)*.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_pitDragon.jpg)  
[Rakdos Pit Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Pit+Dragon) is pretty good even when you aren't hellbent, but with all the fast mana and overall cheap spells in this deck (minus the busty five-drops of course), it isn't hard to get empty. [Rakdos Pit Dragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rakdos+Pit+Dragon) has some challenges in this format given the commonality of cards like [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), but unopposed—and with sufficient mana dumps—it can kill even more quickly than this deck's iconic five-drops. For example, you aren't empty; you attack; your opponent shrugs; you play a bunch of mid-combat fast mana ... [Rite of Flame](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rite+of+Flame), [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song), a [Simian Spirit Guide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Simian+Spirit+Guide) even. All of a sudden you *are* empty. Your opponent might be dead on the spot.

So who's the real Boss?


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_stoneForge.jpg)  
Boss Naya standout [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) makes an impressive Extended appearance alongside the highly regarded [Doran, the Siege Tower](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Doran%2C+the+Siege+Tower) and a fair squad of barely "fair" fighters.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_3Cards2.jpg)  
[Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) allows this build of Doran to spread out beyond just a pair of [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte)s to a little Swordsmanship, and on demand.


[Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) is arguably even more blissful a beater than usual. Doran—unlike most Zoo decks—can actually tap a [Bojuka Bog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bojuka+Bog) for mana after it has laid low a [Living End](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+End). Per usual, [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) can appear against a looming [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths). As usual, there are man-lands to be managed.


One of the individual inclusions I really like about this version is [Great Sable Stag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Great+Sable+Stag) in the sideboard. Just like in Standard, the Stag is not the most efficient threat against all the decks, but against some ... it is unblockable if not unbeatable.

[Oona, Queen of the Fae](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oona%2C+Queen+of+the+Fae) is advantageous on many fronts. For one thing, she is not just big, but big and Black. Look at the typical [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths) / Thopter deck. [Smother](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother)s and [Slaughter Pact](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slaughter+Pact)s are useless. [Echoing Truth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Truth) and [Repeal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Repeal) are alternately temporary measures or conditionally onerously expensive (or both). Oona herself can match mana with essentially any other dump. Plus the "[Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone)" effect might just race the opposing deck if it can sufficiently stifle the Faerie Rogue plan. 


I would wager that the average sideboarded game will include both a deck that can't stop an Oona without its sideboarded [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation)s ... and leaving those [Damnation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Damnation)s in the sideboard.

[Hypergenesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hypergenesis) is one, but there are all manner of [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) and Zoo variants to run as well.

You might have a seen similar deck from Internet darling Gavin Verhey (Gavin himself credits John Treviranus). "Kenji Control" looks like a super-charged Standard deck, but it puts up a pretty good fight in Extended, too.


[Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) gets even better with lots of fetch lands. You can "[Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm)" away cards you don't like and shuffle them away, or set up lands or artifacts on top for [Thirst for Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge) or [Compulsive Research](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsive+Research).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_jace.jpg)  
The deck *looks* like it has only 23 lands, but the many artifacts help it play with bigger, faster, shoes. Kenji said in the coverage that he was a bye for Faeries, but the match-up gets a lot better when you can play, say, second-turn [Night of Souls' Betrayal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Night+of+Souls%27+Betrayal) (turn one land + [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox), [Dimir Signet](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dimir+Signet); turn two land, face-planting four). The deck has a great many powerful fours, and at least one of them—[Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor)—can help you recover your lost card almost immediately.

Here is a hybrid [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) deck with, once again, the [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic). 


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/mtg/images/daily/td/td83_reveillark.jpg)  
Sroges was particularly industrious, and used the Mystic to find [Sword of the Meek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+the+Meek) to set up a [Thopter Foundry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thopter+Foundry) combination! Unlike many other decks of this ilk, he can also win fair and square with his creatures or [Martyr of Sands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr+of+Sands) combination. Unusually for a deck like this, we don't see a deep emphasis on [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains) + [Emeria, the Sky Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria%2C+the+Sky+Ruin). Instead [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark) is responsible for the recursion, along with [Academy Ruins](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Academy+Ruins) (if not for creatures).

When Scapesift fires? Good night. [Adventuring Gear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Adventuring+Gear) makes even non-Lynxes positively meow. You know, like a [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl).

Similar variations on this strategy go ahead and play one [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle), but Gaard didn't play a whole lot of [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s. Just be aware that that is an option if you go with a landfall Zoo variant, and that when you are playing against one ... 18 or so life might not be safe, exactly, even when you feel like you are a sitting pretty behind a wall of blockers.


So while [Dark Depths](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Depths) / Thopter is still on top, there is no reason to play *just* it or one of the various Zoo builds. Even the great Kenji Tsumura is willing to wander. Standard transplants like [Reveillark](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reveillark), other creature decks like Doran, or even an explosive, combo-deck-like version of the [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) / [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) / [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) set. 


Maybe there still are some oohs and ahs to be had.
