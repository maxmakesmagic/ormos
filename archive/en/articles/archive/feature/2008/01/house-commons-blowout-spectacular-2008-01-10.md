House of Commons Blowout Spectacular!!!
=======================================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on January 10, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_chrismillar.jpg)
By Chris Millar











Welcome back, folks! I hope everyone enjoyed the holidays, or as I like to call them, Shovel-apalooza. You see, in Toronto where I live, we had something like 35 centimetres of snow unceremoniously dumped on us. Worse, it seemed like every stop on my holiday tour of duty had been blanketed by more and more snow with less and less ceremony. The heavens just opened up and said, "Deal with it." When I reached my final port of call, I was chagrined to discover that not only was I using terms like "port of call" and "chagrined," but that the poor town had been swallowed up by more than 50 centimetres of the white, fluffy stuff. For those not familiar with the metric system, that's the equivalent of about three miles. It was deep. At a certain point, it would not have surprised me at all if the neighbours started loading various forest creatures onto a snowmobile, two by two.

Sure, you might counter my bottomless self-pity by pointing out that all of my snow has already melted, and, yeah, my roommate could commit justifiable homicide if I were to take credit for doing any shoveling whatsoever, but the fact remains that, like a certain Boggart dwelling, I am very anti-shovel. (Yes, it took two paragraphs to get to that joke. It's so worth it, though.)

Judging by the amount of drool on my chest, either I've transformed into a napping Bill Bryson or *Morningtide* previews are live and in full effect. Luckily, it's the latter. Just check out some of [these beauties](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=magic/morningtide/preview). I know everyone is excited about all the new Elves you'll get to play with, but I'm sure many of you are probably wondering what you're going to do with all of those *Lorwyn* cards that have been gathering dust for the past three days. Have no fear! I'm sure someone will come to the rescue.

### Thirteen Little Piles

Beside me on my desk sits thirteen little piles of cards. How lucky. These aren't just random piles of leftover draft commons. No, sir. At least, not any more. 

![Dawnfluke](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dawnfluke)What I did was take four (and only four) copies of every *Lorwyn* common and use them to create thirteen—roughly equal, at least semi-playable—decks. The four-copy restriction meant that I couldn't put [Oblivion Ring](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) in every white deck, [Mulldrifter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mulldrifter) in every blue deck, or [Lash Out](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lash+Out) in every red deck (like you're supposed to), but it also meant that I had to find a suitable home for unmaindeckable dreck like [Bog Hoodlums](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bog+Hoodlums), [Soaring Hope](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soaring+Hope), [Herbal Poultice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Herbal+Poultice), and [Dawnfluke](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dawnfluke). To keep things in something resembling balance, there will have to be a lot of compromise. This is the main reason why bad cards happen to good decks during the course of this experiment. It would be much easier—and much less satisfying, I think—to build six relatively "great" decks and seven miserable decks.Why would I undertake such a bizarre, uh, undertaking? Well, I was spurred on by the fact that at Worlds 2007 I found myself drowning in *Lorwyn* commons. The reason? It turns out that people who draft a lot (and there were hundreds of drafts during the week) don't necessarily care about their cards afterwards. They just pick out the good and/or valuable ones and either give or \*gasp\* throw away the rest. How many [Boggart Forager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Forager)s does one person really need, anyway? And besides, if you flew some great distance to play in the main event, you need to leave some space in your luggage for plaques, oversized novelty cheques, and Toyota FJ Cruisers.

Still, the amount of cards that people left scattered on tables, on the floor, and piled in garbage cans was just crazy to me. I've spent most of my **Magic** career with a relatively isolated playgroup, where you had to wheel and deal just to get four copies of "chase" commons like [Terminate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terminate) or [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) or [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress). Surely something can be done with all of these perfectly good cards that will otherwise go to waste? I talked to some judges who said that they scoop up a lot of cards after events and give them away to needy players or those just starting out. I know I personally saved a dozen [Leaf Gilder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leaf+Gilder)s and countless [Aethersnipe](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aethersnipe)s from a fate worse than [Terror](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror). It just so happens that I have some friends who are interested in taking up the hobby, so I'll be able to find good homes for these forlorn [Adder-Staff Boggart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Adder-Staff+Boggart)s and [Elvish Eulogist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Elvish%2BEulogist).

The other reason I decided to do this was because I like deckbuilding restrictions (I hear they breed something, but I can't remember what). I also like the idea of working within a closed system (or at least, a system more closed than usual), whether it be [Rotisserie Draft](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/feature/433), the [**Magic** Super Limited League](/en/articles/archive/latest-developments/you-can-go-home-again-2007-07-27), or [Pauper **Magic**](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/daily/jm67). 

I've mentioned it before in this column, but I once built decks using every single card in my collection. Unfortunately, that would be way too time-consuming nowadays, and besides, that's not a "system" that has any particular meaning or is relevant to anyone but me. By using just *Lorwyn* commons, the experiment becomes repeatable and the decks become much easier to balance since the cards were meant to co-exist from the get-go. The end result will be sort of like [*Elves vs. Goblins*](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=magic/expansion/elvesvsgoblins), except with six other tribes fighting for supremacy.

On top of all that, I thought it would be fun and I wanted to see if I could do it. I expected it to be like a huge jigsaw puzzle, with the edge pieces (traditional starting points) represented by little pockets of card synergy, or "modules," that would give each deck a basic identity.

**Deck #1: Red-Black Goblins**

The first deck has one such module: [Facevaulter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Facevaulter), [Mudbutton Torchrunner](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mudbutton+Torchrunner), [Hornet Harasser](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hornet+Harasser), and [Lowland Oaf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lowland+Oaf). Your basic collection of willing sacrifices and sacrifice outlets. The deck is about as aggressive as a Goblin deck can be, considering the limitations, and can even play a bit of long game with recurring Torchrunners and [Lowland Oaf](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lowland+Oaf)'s ability to break ground stalls. I kind of regret that [Boggart Loggers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boggart+Loggers) didn't end up in a deck with [Amoeboid Changeling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Amoeboid+Changeling), but the deck needed an answer to [Bog-Strider Ash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bog-Strider+Ash), which would otherwise (swamp)walk all over it.

**Deck #2: Black-Red Elementals**

The second deck that I built united a pair of Elemental almost all-stars: [Flamekin Brawler](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flamekin+Brawler) and [Soulbright Flamekin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soulbright+Flamekin). Often seen with uncommon compatriot [Ceaseless Searblades](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ceaseless+Searblades), these two can smash some serious face once you hit six mana (enough to activate [Soulbright Flamekin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soulbright+Flamekin) three times). [Inner-Flame Acolyte](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inner-Flame+Acolyte) adds to the aggression, while [Spiderwig Boggart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spiderwig+Boggart) and [Caterwauling Boggart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Caterwauling+Boggart) make blocking difficult. A deck like this wouldn't normally want any seven-drops like [Faultgrinder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faultgrinder), but they have to go somewhere and you can always play them a turn early with [Soulbright Flamekin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soulbright+Flamekin). [Hurly-Burly](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hurly-Burly) can be a little awkward since it kills [Soulbright Flamekin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soulbright+Flamekin), but on the other hand, all of your other creatures survive it, it makes [Caterwauling Boggart](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Caterwauling+Boggart) better by clearing out tokens, and it's your only answer to a cloud of Faeries.

**Deck #9: Black-Green Treefolk**

Treefolk were are tricky tribe, since they are all fairly expensive and you can't just throw them all into the same deck the way you can with Faeries. My solution was to use the remaining changeling ([Skeletal Changeling](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skeletal+Changeling)), [Nath's Buffoon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nath%27s+Buffoon), and [Fertile Ground](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fertile+Ground) to stall the ground early and accelerate you to your big boys. [Nath's Buffoon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nath%27s+Buffoon) seems a little out of place, but it gives you an answer to an early Elf rush and, since it's a Goblin, it triggers your [Bog-Strider Ash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bog-Strider+Ash)es to recoup some of the lost life you will incur from a slow start. [Nameless Inversion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nameless+Inversion) also triggers [Bog-Strider Ash](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bog-Strider+Ash) (and [Battlewand Oak](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Battlewand+Oak), for that matter), plus Treefolk is the one tribe where it can act as a pump spell as often as removal.

**Deck #10: Red-White Giants**

[Springjack Knight](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Springjack+Knight) and the sorry [Soaring Hope](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soaring+Hope) are decent Giant enhancers (double strike and flying being good qualities for five- or six-power creature to have), and they have a bit of synergy between them. [Soaring Hope](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soaring+Hope)'s ability to put itself on top of your library makes your hopes of winning a clash, um, soar.

**Deck #12: Mono-Green Elves**

The other Elf deck is all about [Nath's Elite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nath%27s+Elite), combining it with the devastating [Lace with Moonglove](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lace+with+Moonglove) and a whopping eight ways to regenerate it. It's like [Thicket Basilisk](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thicket+Basilisk), [Lure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lure), and [Regeneration](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Regeneration) all over again, except more unwieldy and less likely. [Gilt-Leaf Seer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gilt-Leaf+Seer) can help you avoid drawing a handful of [Herbal Poultice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Herbal+Poultice)s and [Heal the Scars](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heal+the+Scars) or just dig up more land. One of the problems I've run into with [Lure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lure)-based decks is that you can lose the game to a single flyer. [Cloudcrown Oak](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudcrown+Oak) should help out in this respect, keeping those pesky flyers back on defense where they can be [Lure](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lure)d to their deaths.
