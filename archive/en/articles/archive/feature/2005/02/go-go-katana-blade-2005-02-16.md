Go Go Katana Blade!
===================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on February 16, 2005 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






[![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/mtgcom/fcpics/features/PTQPhil_decklists.jpg)](/en/events/coverage/pro-tour%E2%80%93philadelphia-qualifying-season-extended-top-8-decklists)This week's PTQ Top 8 decks give us a great look at how the Extended format is continuing to evolve. Decks from the Top 8 of GP Boston that were not a part of the expected metagame -- decks like the first place Aluren deck and especially the new Temporary Solution deck -- are picking up adherents, with Ben Dempsey's creation especially taking top positions and even a slot.


But in the rich tapestry of the Extended format, a format with maybe twenty distinct, viable, choices, two things stand out to me this week like nothing else.


### #1 Didn't these guys get the memo?


Over the ten sets of PTQ Top 8 lists that I've studied so far from the [latest batch of Qualifier decks](/en/events/coverage/pro-tour%E2%80%93philadelphia-qualifying-season-extended-top-8-decklists) (and by the time you're reading this, Greg Collins and company will probably have even more up), variants on The Rock are present 11 times -- more than one copy per Top 8 --and one even took the blue envelope!


Top commentators have been naysaying B/G since the Pro Tour, when The Rock was the most popular deck but failed to make Top 8. Yet PTQ players seem to be adopting The Rock anyway. The version that won this past weekend in Osaka actually met itself in a rogue Rock mirror in the finals.


Which brings us to #2


### The Japanese sure love their Swords


I got it when Satoshi Nakamura played [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) in his Squirrel Prison deck back in Columbus. U/G doesn't have any fundamental creature kill, and protection from red can really help against a deck like Goblins or Red Deck Wins, especially when your deck is full of tiny green creatures. In that creature heavy deck, [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) fills specific holes, and can turn wimps like [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) or [Wood Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wood+Elves) into legitimate threats. I got it a lot less last week, when I saw Jun'ya Iyanga's version of Red Deck Wins playing the Sword.

To me, the main advantage of Red Deck Wins is all the burn it can play, and Iyanga's finalist build lacks the deck's best burn spell, [Cursed Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll)... Yet it plays [Sword of Fire and Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice)! My friend Josh Ravitz posits that this creature poor Red Deck Wins makes up for its lack of burn with a solid Sword hit, and can even leave in [Jackal Pup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup) in the mirror. That I could accept... but Tatsuya Oonishi's deck?

Draco-Explosion is a cool combination deck that first showed up in the hands of Jinpei Hassaku at PT Houston two years ago. This deck works under the theory that most players will deal four damage to themselves with cards like [Wooded Foothills](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wooded+Foothills) or [Sulfurous Springs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sulfurous+Springs), and has been called the [Donate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Donate) that doesn't gain 20 life in the middle of the combo. 


The deck can use cards like [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) or [Scroll Rack](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scroll+Rack) (or in some versions, [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor)) to put [Draco](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Draco) on top. One [Erratic Explosion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Erratic+Explosion) later and the opponent has taken 16. Cards like [Fire/Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire%2FIce), [Earthquake](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Earthquake), [Volcanic Hammer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Volcanic+Hammer), or just plain [Faerie Conclave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Conclave) beats make up any remaining life.


Draco-Explosion was refreshing when it came out, and is distinguished as being a two-card combination. Most of even the most successful Extended combos require more than two cards (for example, Life requires say a [Nomads en-Kor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nomads+en-Kor), a [Daru Spiritualist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daru+Spiritualist), *and* a [Worthy Cause](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Worthy+Cause), even if it can interchange specific cards). Draco on the other hand has room for strong card drawing and legitimate control elements like [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell), not just combination pieces.


All of that said, my vote for the killer deck of the week is Kazuki Kurashima's Top 4 deck from Osaka. If you go through the Top 8 listings for this week, you will see many decks that aspire to the heights of Ben Dempsey or Nick West. Rather than just tweaking the number [Meddling Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meddling+Mage)s or [Exalted Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel)s or eliminating creatures entirely, Kurashima's deck is truly innovative. Much like Lucas Glavin [hybridized](/en/events/coverage/japan-strikes-again) Cephalid Breakfast and Life for [GP Boston](/en/events/coverage/japan-strikes-again), Kurashima saw the synergies between Nick West's NO Stick and *Odyssey* Block's Solitary Confinement deck. Kurashima's deck adds *a whole other combo deck* to the West skeleton. His version can not only just lock the opponent down with [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter), but with [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement). The loss of [Accumulated Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Accumulated+Knowledge) is considerable in this build, especially with its 21 land only, but Kurashima makes up for this with [Compulsion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsion) + [Squee, Goblin Nabob](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squee%2C+Goblin+Nabob).

![Isochron Scepter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Isochron+Scepter)The standard NO Stick deck seeks to set up [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) with a relevant imprint -- usually [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant) or [Fire/Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire%2FIce) -- to contain the opponent's game. Notice that although [Fire/Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire%2FIce) and [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant) are key components to NO Stick, Kurashima plays only three copies of each main deck. Three is a subtle number in this kind of deck design: it is exactly the number required to [Intuition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intuition) for a card as you would a [Demonic Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Tutor) (by getting all three copies and keeping one), but three also allows Kurashima to play a fourth copy of each instant in his sideboard. This fourth copy is a [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) target, meaning that Kurashima is actually playing more like six [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant)s than three.
 The core combination for the [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) side of the deck is [Squee, Goblin Nabob](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Squee%2C+Goblin+Nabob) + [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement). Most Extended decks have no way to beat a player with [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) in play other than to hope he runs out of cards. With Squee in the picture, [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) should never run out. As already stated, Squee serves other interactions, notably as a free card when broken by either [Compulsion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsion) or [Intuition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intuition). Squee gets even better with [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and any shuffling device ([Enlightened Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Enlightened+Tutor), [Flooded Strand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flooded+Strand), or a second [Intuition](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intuition)).


It is interesting to note that while the standard West deck is poor against Red Deck Wins, Kurashima's deck can create a hard lock with just a [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement) and a single Squee. Because he never has to draw again, the Confinement will win the game by decking the Red Deck Wins deck. That said, this version of U/W is even worse against The Rock than most. Kurashima has little resistance to [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) and [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy), and because his deck requires significant investment to the board -- [Compulsion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Compulsion), [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter), [Solitary Confinement](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Solitary+Confinement), etc., [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed) becomes even more deadly. That said, if the opponent is not prepared, or if he is playing a single-minded beatdown or combination deck, it is unlikely that he will be able to withstand this deck's powerful defensive measures.
