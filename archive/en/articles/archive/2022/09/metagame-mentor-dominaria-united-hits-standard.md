Metagame Mentor: Dominaria United Hits Standard
===============================================



 Posted in [NEWS](/en/articles)
 on September 8, 2022 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_FrankKarsten.jpg)
By Frank Karsten











Hello and welcome back to Metagame Mentor, the weekly column in which I highlight the decks to beat in Constructed on the path to the Pro Tour.


In this article, I will take a first look at the post-rotation Standard metagame, based on the results of several online tournaments. But first, I'll highlight last weekend's biggest events and how they tie into the Regional Championship qualifying rounds.


Modern at SCG CON Columbus
==========================


Last weekend, [SCG CON Columbus](https://scgcon.starcitygames.com/event/scg-con-columbus-2022/) featured two $20K Modern tournaments. Although *Dominaria United* was already available on *Magic Online* and *MTG Arena* last weekend, it is not yet legal in tabletop *Magic* until its release Septembet 9. So we didn't get to see any newly released cards in action, but the winning decks in both tournaments were surprising choices nevertheless, each using [Urza's Saga](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Saga) in a different way.

In [the first tournament](https://mtgmelee.com/Tournament/View/11110), both Corey Baumeister and Ross Merriam made the Top 4 with [Grinding Breach](https://mtgmelee.com/Decklist/View/241260), a combo deck that aims to repeatedly mill itself by sacrificing [Mishra's Bauble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Bauble) to [Grinding Station](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grinding+Station), which in turn allows you to recast the [Mishra's Bauble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Bauble) via [Underworld Breach](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Underworld+Breach), untap [Grinding Station](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grinding+Station), and loop until you win the game with [Thassa's Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thassa%27s+Oracle). Yet it's also perfectly capable of winning a fair game with its creatures, and it could even shave some of its combo pieces after sideboard if opponents overboard on artifact or graveyard hate.


Grinding Breach has been quickly cementing itself as a major player in Modern. Last week on Corbin Hosler's ["The Week That Was"](https://www.magic.gg/news/the-week-that-was-the-very-model-of-a-modern-major-series), you could read how Jesse Robkin used the deck to take down the $5K RCQ at NRG Series St. Louis. When two well-known players use it to crush the biggest Modern event of the subsequent weekend, then that's a clear sign that it's the real deal.

In [the second tournament](https://mtgmelee.com/Tournament/View/11111), the finals featured more [Urza's Saga](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Saga) decks: Brady Munroe's Prison Tron versus Duo Yang's Jund. In the end, a Tron-fueled [Walking Ballista](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Walking+Ballista) carrying [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) took it down for Munroe.


Congratulations to them and the other top finishers! The Top 4 players from both events earned an invitation to the [Dreamhack Atlanta *Magic* Showdown](https://dreamhack.com/magic/) on November 19-20, which is the Regional Championship for the United States. Regional Championships for all eleven geographical regions will be held late November or early December, and their format is Pioneer. Top finishers at these Regional Championships will qualify for the first Pro Tour in 2023.


![](https://images.ctfassets.net/ryplwhabvmmk/DJPNxAlrmrCNwY5ZB7Fc6/a1e2363f540575f16e5741b149b2280d/1080x1080-Magic-Premier-Play-Path-2022-2023-Infographic.jpg)


If you aspire to earn an invitation to this first round of Regional Championships, then you can do so up to and including September 25. To find qualifier events around you in a variety of formats, you can use the [store and event locator](https://locator.wizards.com/) with the filter "Regional Championship Qualifier" (RCQ) and/or visit your [regional organizer's](https://magic.gg/pro-tour?section=regional-championships#pro-tour) website.


After this first qualifier season closes, [qualifiers for the second round](https://www.magic.gg/news/qualify-for-the-second-pro-tour-of-2023-and-get-these-promos) of Regional Championship start. This second qualifying round runs from October 1, 2022 through December 18, 2022. So, RCQs held in that period do not qualify for the aforementioned Regional Championship in November or December but rather for the second round of Regional Championships, which will take place in the first quarter of 2023. This second round of Regional Championships, which qualifies players for the second Pro Tour in 2023, will feature the Standard format.


Which brings me to the format for today's metagame roundup.


Standard Metagame Breakdown
===========================


Standard rotates every fall. With the release of *Dominaria United*, the oldest four sets (*Zendikar Rising*, *Kaldheim*, *Strixhaven*, and *Adventures in the Forgotten Realms*) rotated out, giving rise to a fresh, unexplored format. Moreover, for the first time since January 2017, no cards are banned in Standard anymore.


To analyze this new Standard, I used the decklists from *Magic Online* Challenges on [Saturday](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-standings/standard-challenge-2022-09-04) and [Sunday](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/mtgo-standings/standard-challenge-2022-09-05), in addition to all Standard decklists with non-negative net wins from [MTGMelee events](https://mtgmelee.com/Decklists) held since the release of *Dominaria United*, including [Crokeyz's tournament](https://mtgmelee.com/Tournament/View/11654). Net wins is given by a deck's number of wins minus its number of losses in its tournament. In total, I analyzed 224 decks. That's not a large sample size, but it can offer a valuable first glimpse.


The most-played cards overall (i.e., the cards other than basic lands with the largest sum of copies across all main decks and sideboards in my data set) were [Fable of the Mirror-Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fable+of+the+Mirror-Breaker) and [The Meathook Massacre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Meathook+Massacre). This should not come as a surprise, as they were already prominent staples of the format before the rotation.

These top six new-to-Standard cards paint a clear picture: In the early days of *Dominaria United* Standard, black is king, by a large margin. Keep in mind that these are very early results from the first days of a new format, and the metagame is unlikely to stay this way.


To provide a useful breakdown that combines both popularity and performance, I assigned archetype labels to all decklists and awarded to each deck a number of points equal to its net wins. For example, a deck that went 5–1 in the Swiss in a Challenge event followed by a loss in the quarterfinals was assigned three points. The sum of these numbers for every archetype was then used to determine its record-weighted metagame share, which represents its share of total net wins.

Ten Standard Decks to Beat
==========================


To figure out what a good, typical list looks like for top-tier archetypes, I used a proprietary aggregation method that combines popularity and performance. The core of the method was explained in [an article](https://strategy.channelfireball.com/all-strategy/mtg/channelmagic-articles/magic-math-a-new-way-to-determine-an-aggregate-deck-list-rg-dragons/), but I have since extended it by considering win rates, sideboards, land counts, and other relevant aspects, inspired by the theory behind artificial neural networks. It provides a systematic way to pinpoint the "Decks to Beat" in Standard right now. Let's start with number one.

Mono-Black Midrange uses some of the best creatures, the most powerful planeswalkers, and the most efficient removal spells available in Standard. It's all packaged in a smooth mana curve that starts on turn one and goes up to [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair) on turn five. The quadruple-black cost in that sorcery is one of the key reasons to stick to a pristine mono-color mana base.


From *Dominaria United*, this deck gained [Evolved Sleeper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evolved+Sleeper); [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil); [Sheoldred, the Apocalypse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sheoldred%2C+the+Apocalypse); and [Cut Down](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cut+Down). With these flexible cards, the deck has the tools to go on the offense and close out a game quickly, but it can also defend itself well and grind back over a long game. [Evolved Sleeper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evolved+Sleeper) and [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil)'s +1 can also give you something to do with excess lands in the late game, which has become more important now that creature lands have rotated out. All in all, it results in the prime midrange deck in the format.


If you don't want to play black yourself but are looking for ways to beat these decks, then there are many angles to explore. Here are three ideas, each of which will come with an example decklist later in this article:


* You could go over the top with a strategy that revolves around powerful noncreature spells and artifacts. This allows you to turn Mono Black Midrange's removal suite into dead cards in Game 1.
* You could overpower them by putting [Titan of Industry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Titan+of+Industry) or [Sanctuary Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanctuary+Warden) onto the battlefield on turn four or five. These creatures are too large to be swept by [The Meathook Massacre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Meathook+Massacre), enter the battlefield with a shield token to shrug off [Infernal Grasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Grasp), and bring along a token to sacrifice to [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) or [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair).
* You could go under them with a hyper-aggressive strategy that contains the right evasive, recurring, and/or properly sized threats. You need to be able to bash through [Sheoldred, the Apocalypse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sheoldred%2C+the+Apocalypse) while not folding to [The Meathook Massacre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Meathook+Massacre). This means that you may have to stray from more obvious card choices, but I don't think it's impossible.

Rakdos Midrange is very similar to Mono-Black Midrange. It gives up perfect mana and the black one-drop creatures to gain access to [Bloodtithe Harvester](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodtithe+Harvester) and [Fable of the Mirror-Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fable+of+the+Mirror-Breaker).


Whether or not that is worth it is up for debate. What I can say is that this mana base can cast the red spells and the quadruple-black [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair) consistently, but it does come at the cost of pain[/autocard] and speed[/autocard]. There are also a few Rakdos Midrange decks that went deeper on red and cut [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair), but that was only a small fraction.


If you were to combine Mono-Black Midrange and Rakdos Midrange and add up their respective 15.6% and 14.9% record-weighted metagame shares, then nearly a third of the winner's metagame is comprised of the same core of black cards. That's a lot, but it's a small sample size, and the first days of post-rotation Standard are rarely a good indication of how the format shakes out long-term.

Rakdos Sacrifice will be a familiar archetype to players who played Standard before the rotation. [Oni-Cult Anvil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oni-Cult+Anvil) and [Voltage Surge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Voltage+Surge) will still happily much on [Experimental Synthesizer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Experimental+Synthesizer) or [Voldaren Epicure](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Voldaren+Epicure)'s Blood token for value. Although [Blightstep Pathway](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blightstep+Pathway) and [Deadly Dispute](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deadly+Dispute) were lost in the rotation, [Sulfurous Springs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sulfurous+Springs) and [Reckoner's Bargain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckoner%27s+Bargain) are decent alternatives.


From *Dominaria United*, the deck gained [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) and [Braids, Arisen Nightmare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Braids%2C+Arisen+Nightmare) as powerful new three-drops. Braids in particular synergizes well with [Oni-Cult Anvil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oni-Cult+Anvil): When you sacrifice an artifact to Braid's ability, [Oni-Cult Anvil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oni-Cult+Anvil) will trigger to keep the train rolling. Moreover, opponents usually won't control an artifact, leaving them with no choice but to lose 2 life and grant you a card.

Grixis Midrange was one of the top decks before the rotation, so it's not surprising that it made the transition. In a way, you could see it as Rakdos Midrange that gains several blue cards in exchange for a slower and more painful mana base. The overarching strategy, however, is similar. As [Corpse Appraiser](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corpse+Appraiser) shows, the goal is to generate favorable resource exchanges over time.


Notable new *Dominaria United* cards in the aggregate main deck are [Sulfurous Springs](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sulfurous+Springs); [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil); [Cut Down](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cut+Down); [Shivan Reef](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shivan+Reef); [Sheoldred, the Apocalypse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sheoldred%2C+the+Apocalypse); and [Ertai Resurrected](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ertai+Resurrected). Yet there is no consensus whatsoever on the best version. Some Grixis players were going heavier on [Ertai Resurrected](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ertai+Resurrected); others were trying out [Sol'Kanar the Tainted](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sol%27Kanar+the+Tainted) instead of [Evelyn, the Covetous](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Evelyn%2C+the+Covetous); others were investing deeper in Vampires. Additionally, there are a lot of different possibilities for your interactive spells and your mana base. In the early days of Standard, everyone is trying out different options.

There are many ways to build Jund Midrange. Some of them look like Rakdos Midrange with a tiny green splash, but the aggregate list shown above is a breath of fresh air. Gone are [Tenacious Underdog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tenacious+Underdog); [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil); and [Sheoldred, the Apocalypse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sheoldred%2C+the+Apocalypse). Instead, we have a package of [Teachings of the Kirin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teachings+of+the+Kirin); [Old Rutstein](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Old+Rutstein); [Jaya, Fiery Negotiator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jaya%2C+Fiery+Negotiator) and [The Elder Dragon War](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Elder+Dragon+War). These are far from universal inclusions, but they featured prominently in the best-performing Jund Midrange lists.


Most of these cards, as well as [Fable of the Mirror-Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fable+of+the+Mirror-Breaker), allow you to discard or mill yourself. There are several payoffs for doing so. First, [Soul of Windgrace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul+of+Windgrace) can use lands in your graveyard to ramp. Second, the third chapter of [The Cruelty of Gix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Cruelty+of+Gix) can return [Titan of Industry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Titan+of+Industry). Thanks to these new *Dominaria United* cards, your graveyard turns into a valuable resource.


Additionally, the creature tokens from [Teachings of the Kirin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teachings+of+the+Kirin) and [Old Rutstein](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Old+Rutstein) can be sacrificed to opposing [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) or [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair), and they also enable [Jaya, Fiery Negotiator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jaya%2C+Fiery+Negotiator)'s -2 ability. So, all cards work together as a coherent package.

Like Grixis, Esper Midrange was a popular archetype before the rotation, and it made the transition fairly easily. Reaching WUB mana for [Raffine, Scheming Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raffine%2C+Scheming+Seer) on turn 3 gets a bit more difficult without Pathways, but [Adarkar Wastes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Adarkar+Wastes) and [Caves of Koilos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Caves+of+Koilos) pick up the slack. I am a bit surprised to see almost no [legend-heavy versions](https://mtgmelee.com/Decklist/View/242121) with Plaza of Heroes, but perhaps these will be discovered over the next few weeks.


At the moment, there is no consensus on the best Esper Midrange build, but looking over the aggregate decklist, several white *Dominaria United* cards stand out. [Resolute Reinforcements](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Resolute+Reinforcements) may not be as inherently powerful as [Luminarch Aspirant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Aspirant), which was lost in rotation, but the two tokens do enable a bigger Raffine trigger on turn three. Meanwhile, [Serra Paragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Serra+Paragon) and [Archangel of Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Wrath) provide staying power in the mid-game.

Orzhov Midrange was yet another popular archetype before the rotation, but there's no consensus on how to build it with *Dominaria United* in the mix. Some builds look close to an aggro deck; others have more of a control plan. Roughly half of the Orzhov decks exploit [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair) along with a lot of tapped lands to fix the mana. The aggregate list above, however, embodies the belief that adding [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair) to a deck with double-white planeswalkers is too taxing.


The biggest question for Orzhov players after the rotation was how to replace [Luminarch Aspirant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Luminarch+Aspirant). The aggregate list shown above answers this with pairs of [Cathar Commando](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cathar+Commando) and [Raffine's Informant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raffine%27s+Informant), but these creatures represent massive big drops in card quality. Orzhov didn't put up impressive numbers, and other black decks looked more powerful to me at first glance.


Having analyzed seven black archetypes in a row, you may be feeling a bit tired of that color by now. Fortunately, many nonblack options are viable in Standard. The remaining three decks in today's article won't feature [Tenacious Underdog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tenacious+Underdog), [Graveyard Trespasser](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Graveyard+Trespasser), [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil), [Infernal Grasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Grasp), or [The Meathook Massacre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Meathook+Massacre) anymore. Instead, we have Mono-Red Aggro, Naya Reanimator, and Jeskai Control. These are archetypes numbers 8, 9, and 14 from the table, and each exemplifies one of the angles of attack I outlined when discussing Mono-Black Midrange.

Mono-Red Aggro is always around, no matter the format. Yet I was surprised to see that the best-performing builds for early *Dominaria United* Standard relied on [Ancestral Anger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Anger) and [Reckless Impulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reckless+Impulse). It makes sense, though. Most red two-mana creatures would not be able to attack profitably into [Graveyard Trespasser](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Graveyard+Trespasser) or [Sheoldred, the Apocalypse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sheoldred%2C+the+Apocalypse), and they easily get swept away by [The Meathook Massacre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Meathook+Massacre). But [Electrostatic Infantry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrostatic+Infantry) is an exception! You just need enough spells to enable the Wizard.


All other *Dominaria United* card choices make sense in the context of a black-infested metagame as well. [Phoenix Chick](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phoenix+Chick) will fly over all of black's creatures and is the perfect sacrificial fodder for [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) or [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair). [Defiler of Instinct](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Defiler+of+Instinct) can usually attack into Sheoldred thanks to first strike. And [Lightning Strike](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Strike) is a no-brainer in an aggressive red deck that needs to trigger [Electrostatic Infantry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Electrostatic+Infantry). If you like blazingly fast starts, then this list may have the right card choices for the current metagame.

The dream with Naya Reanimator starts with [Fable of the Mirror-Breaker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fable+of+the+Mirror-Breaker) on turn three. Then on turn four, you discard [Titan of Industry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Titan+of+Industry) or [Sanctuary Warden](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sanctuary+Warden) to its second chapter, attack with the Goblin to create a Treasure, and finally cast [Invoke Justice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Justice). The reanimated creature, as I mentioned before, will be too large to be swept by [The Meathook Massacre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Meathook+Massacre), can enter the battlefield with a shield token to shrug off [Infernal Grasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Grasp), and can bring along a token to protect it from [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) or [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair).


This is an awesome strategy, and it even gained an important new tool from *Dominaria United* in [The Elder Dragon War](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=The+Elder+Dragon+War). While most decks would be primarily interested in its first and third chapters, the second chapter is essential for Naya Reanimator because it allows you to put [Titan of Industry](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Titan+of+Industry) into the graveyard.

What is Mono-Black Midrange filled with? Creature removal spells.


What nonland permanent type is ignored by [Invoke Despair](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Invoke+Despair)? Artifacts.


So what do we do? Build a creatureless control deck that just sweeps all of the opponent's threats, casts [Timeless Lotus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Timeless+Lotus), and wins the game with enormous X-spells. Perhaps you first cast [Silver Scrutiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silver+Scrutiny), shuffle back [Light Up the Night](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Light+Up+the+Night) with [Witness the Future](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Witness+the+Future), and finally point the red spell at your opponent. For Games 2 and 3, when opponents board out their removal spells, you could surprise them with [Hullbreaker Horror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hullbreaker+Horror).


It's a brilliant feat of deck building, but I expected nothing less from Eliott Boussaud. When there is a clear Deck to Beat, it only becomes easier to target it in a dedicated way. With fascinating options like these, I am excited to see where how the Standard metagame will develop in the coming weeks and months.
