New Year’s Deck Extravaganza
============================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on January 3, 2007 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_FrankKarsten.jpg)
By Frank Karsten











Happy 2007! **Magicthegathering.com** went on a short holiday break the last two weeks, but the online tournaments went on regardless. So today I will try to play catch-up and cover the new decks and metagame swings of the last three weeks of December. In order to start the new year with a bang, I have put a ton of decklists in today's column!

The second column shows the popularity percentage of three weeks ago, the third column shows the popularity percentage of two weeks ago, and the fourth column shows the popularity percentage of last week. Because people who collect decklists – thanks Josh! – are also entitled to a Christmas break and because events disappear from the cache rather rapidly, I only had access to about half of the events of week 51, so that data will not be completely representative, although it should be close to accurate. The last column shows the average weighted popularity over the three weeks, giving only half a week's weight to the results of week 51 because of the missing data. That's a lot of information, so let me try to summarize what has been going on. As I predicted [a few weeks ago](/en/articles/archive/comparing-real-life-and-online-metagames-2006-12-06), the Standard events are largely populated with Boros Deck Wins, [Dragonstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonstorm), and TriscuitTron. Boros Deck Wins is the best aggro deck. Almost everyone plays Paulo Vitor Dama Da Rosa's World Championships Top 8 deck version (available with the other Worlds Top 8 decks [here](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/decklists-top-8-decks-2006-12-01)), or a version that is very close to it. [Dragonstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonstorm) is the best combo deck. Almost everyone plays lists that are very close to Makahito Mihara's World Championships winning deck. TriscuitTron is the best control deck. With access to loads of Urzatron mana, blue control cards, and [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God), it should fare well against Boros Deck Wins and [Dragonstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonstorm). Most versions look similar to Katsuhiro Mori's World Championships Top 8 deck. However, Yasher\_Huyasher's version that splashes [Demonfire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonfire) and acoimbra's version with the [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter)/[Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental) combo in the sideboard are nice innovative refreshments.

But we've also had a couple “newcomers” to the Standard playground lately. One thing is for sure: Dimir decks are making quite an impact.

![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Desert)

















This deck was introduced by Guillaume Wafo-Tapa, who posted a 5-1 record with it in the Standard portion of the World Championships. It took a while for the online players to catch up, maybe because they didn't know that [Rewind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rewind) should be in the deck at first. You just have to know that there was a typo in [Wafo-Tapa's decklist](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/decklists-top-standard-decks-2006-11-30); the list there shows no [Rewind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rewind), whereas I am sure he included it in his – I was paired against him. 

![Dralnu, Lich Lord](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dralnu%2C+Lich+Lord)The deck is a typical “draw-go” style deck, which almost never plays a spell at sorcery speed. The deck is full of countermagic, so if your opponent plays something threatening, you should be able to stop it. If you happen to have mana open at the end of your opponent's turn, you can cast [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) or [Think Twice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Think+Twice). [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) can even fetch another [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings), which ensures that you never run out of gas. Eventually you play [Teferi, Mage of Zhalfir](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teferi%2C+Mage+of+Zhalfir), flashback [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) to get [Dralnu, Lich Lord](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dralnu%2C+Lich+Lord) (which has flash thanks to Teferi and can therefore be fetched) and re-use all the [Rewind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rewind)s you have in your graveyard. The kill card is the lone [Skeletal Vampire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skeletal+Vampire). In case a creature has slipped under your countermagic, you can either [Repeal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Repeal) it or tutor for a [Last Gasp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Last+Gasp). The sideboard holds [Persecute](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persecute) against [Dragonstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonstorm), and [Bottle Gnomes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bottle+Gnomes) against Boros Deck Wins, amongst other cards.Saikyo a.k.a. oOPraiz a.k.a. Perhaps you remember the original [Soggy Pickles deck](http://boards1.wizards.com/showpost.php?p=10691021&postcount=36). The goal of a Pickles deck – and I blame the deck's creator Brian David-Marshall for coming up with such a stupid name – is to deprive your opponent of untap steps with [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental) and [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter). The original Soggy Pickles version played white for [Court Hussar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Court+Hussar), as well as [Temporal Isolation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Temporal+Isolation) and Grand Arbiter out of the sideboard. But the deck didn't actually need the white.

The above deck is the result of someone (Stuart Wright first, rumor has it) making the connection between a Soggy Pickles deck and the [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) / Teferi engine. The list you see is made by mikeman29 and fob (Luis Scott-Vargas). Playing [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) allows you to go down to 1 [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental). It is a weak card in every stage of the game except when you can combo-win, which makes it the perfect tutor target. In fact, the current build is much less of a [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental) combo deck. Rather, it is a blue-black control deck that counters some stuff then draws two extra cards a turn with [Vesuvan Shapeshifter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuvan+Shapeshifter) plus [Fathom Seer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fathom+Seer) (the defining combo of the deck now, perhaps), and fetching a [Brine Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brine+Elemental) for the kill is a mere afterthought. So maybe I should start calling it U/B Morph or U/B Shifter instead. Any suggestions? In the sideboard we find [Bottle Gnomes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bottle+Gnomes), which works wonders against Boros Deck Wins, and a wide array of tutorable one-ofs.

To me, this deck is the most interesting to come out of the last couple weeks. The original Soggy Pickles deck was fun and innovative, but not good enough to be competitive. I think that with the addition of [Mystical Teachings](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystical+Teachings) and Teferi, the deck got the punch it needed. If I had to pick a deck for a Standard tournament right now, I would definitely give U/B Pickles a try. Then again…maybe the Dralnu deck is a good choice as well, as Saikyo told me he was beating Boros Deck Wins and U/B Pickles with it. And in case you like beatdown decks, Boros should be your safe deck choice.

Not technically a newcomer, this deck deserves a special mention because it has skyrocketed in popularity in the last week, and you have to keep out an eye for those trends. I can only assume the reason for this to be its good matchup against the blue-black control decks.

This aggressive tempo-oriented blue-green deck abuses the synergy between [Scryb Ranger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scryb+Ranger) and [Spectral Force](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Force), which offers the possibility of attacking with an 8/8 trampler every turn. Apart from that, the deck hopes to get the turn-one [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves), turn-two [Call of the Herd](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Call+of+the+Herd) opening and can punch through with [Stonewood Invocation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stonewood+Invocation) or [Psionic Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psionic+Blast). Other versions typically play [Ohran Viper](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ohran+Viper)s and fewer lands.

Trinket Angel has emerged as the most popular and best performing deck of the month December. Most versions look very similar to Gabriel Nassif's original [World Championships list](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/decklists-top-extended-decks-2006-12-02), with little innovations. Affinity has also risen to the top ranks and appears to perform as consistently as ever. TEPS Desire has also become a mainstay, but hate cards like [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) prevent it from dominating. Still, the deck's capability for turn two or turn three kills shouldn't be underestimated.

Blue-White Urzatron also remains one of the top decks. Lately an interesting variation on Blue-White Tron has emerged, which uses [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) and [Vesuva](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva) instead of the Urza lands as their mana engine. The [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) and Urza versions include mostly the same spells; the only real difference is the mana base. Which is better? Well, let's compare the two. An advantage the [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) version holds over the Urza version is that it produces more mana with the same amount of lands. Two [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)s yield more mana than two Urza pieces. The difference between two mana and four mana is huge. Three [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)s also provide more mana than three Urza pieces, and so on.

Furthermore, the [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) version only plays eight colorless lands (4 [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost), 4 [Vesuva](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva)), as opposed to twelve in the Tron version, which provides more room for blue or white producing lands, decreasing the chance of color screw. The major disadvantage of the [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) version is that your lands come in play tapped. Where the Tron version can go Island, [Urza's Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Mine), [Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) on turn two, the [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) version mocks you as you put down your comes-in-play tapped land. The [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) configuration also falls apart when you don't draw a [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost). Imagine you sit there with just [Hallowed Fountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Fountain)s and [Vesuva](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vesuva)s (which come in play tapped even when you're not copying [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost)) in your hand. Not good. So, now that I have listed the advantages and disadvantages, which configuration do I recommend? I honestly don't know. It's really close in my opinion. I'm leaning towards the [Cloudpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) version because it is innovative and refreshing, but I'm not sure.

The Boros Deck Wins menace appears to be contained. There are still plenty of [Savannah Lions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Savannah+Lions) and [Grim Lavamancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grim+Lavamancer)s around, but the overwhelming number of Trinket Angel decks keeps Boros in check. With [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix), [Umezawa's Jitte](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Umezawa%27s+Jitte), [Silver Knight](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Silver+Knight), and [Exalted Angel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel) in its arsenal, Trinket Angel has a great matchup against Boros. Furthermore, other popular decks like TEPS Desire, ScepterChant, and GiftsRock also tend to beat Boros more often than not.

[Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens) remains strong in combination with [Rite of Flame](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rite+of+Flame), [Goblin Warchief](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Warchief), and the usual goblin suspects. Interestingly, many players have ditched the Dirty Kitty variant, which splashed green for Fecundity, for the more straightforward mono-red Goblin Storm version.

“If I had to play in an Extended tournament tomorrow, I would probably choose TEPS Desire.”If I had to play in an Extended tournament tomorrow, I would probably choose TEPS Desire, because it should be able to beat Trinket Angel (the #1 deck) consistently. Yeah, they have [Meddling Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meddling+Mage) and [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle). But I have played TEPS Desire a lot lately and I dare to say that with enough practice and the right sideboard cards ([Empty the Warrens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Empty+the+Warrens) and anti-[Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) measures), you should win against Trinket Angel. TEPS should also fare well against the rest of the field. Okay, maybe not against Scepter/Chant, but you can't win everything.

Furthermore, I will show the two decks that have experienced a steady popularity growth in the last couple weeks, as that is an indication that it might be the next big thing: Aggro Loam and [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid).

I don't think this deck needs an introduction, as the synergy between [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager), [Frogmite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frogmite), and artifact lands has been around since *Mirrodin* Block. The above version is the way I would build an Affinity deck now. It based on Winx Club's list, but tuned to my own preferences. I wouldn't play [Fire // Ice](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fire+%2F%2F+Ice), [Somber Hoverguard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Somber+Hoverguard), painlands, or many of the non-artifact cards that I often see. I like to play at least 48 artifacts in an Affinity deck, in order to maximize my [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager)s and [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating)s. This is one of the most important things to keep in mind when building an Affinity deck, in my opinion: always play enough artifacts. Don't forget it. The sideboard can change a lot and should be viewed as flexible. For example, if you expect no TEPS Desire and a lot of Goblins, you should include [Engineered Plague](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague) over [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle).

The arch-nemesis of this deck is [Kataki, War's Wage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki%2C+War%27s+Wage), although Affinity has [Pyrite Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrite+Spellbomb) and [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast) out of the sideboard to kill it. Affinity should beat TEPS Desire in a match (based on a fast clock and sideboard hate), and should also win against Blue-White Tron and Trinket Angel, because, well, it's Affinity! It is as consistent and powerful as ever. If you read this and currently don't have Kataki in your sideboard, you'd better put it back in, since Affinity is on the rise online. Or just play the following deck to beat Affinity with [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed).

Gifts Rock adds [Gifts Ungiven](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) to the green-black mix, which is essentially a double [Demonic Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Tutor) as you can easily toss in [Eternal Witness](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness) and [Living Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) (which can get Witness) in the mix to ensure you get the cards you want. Typical Gifts setups also include [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) and [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy), aiming to start [Genesis](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis) recursion.

Niv Shmuely, a.k.a trunks123, based his list on Luis Scott-Vargas's. Niv has made a lot of minor tweaks to the deck and has put up great results with it. [Harmonic Sliver](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Harmonic+Sliver) in the sideboard as a wishable [Naturalize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize), maindeck [Global Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Global+Ruin) and [Engineered Explosives](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Explosives) – it's the little things that make a deck good. He changes the list sometimes according to the metagame, but no more then 1-2 cards in the sideboard.

I have [featured this deck before](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=mtgcom/daily/fk3), but that has been so long ago that I figured showing a more recent Aggro Loam list would be nice, especially now that the deck is rising in popularity. Aggro Loam still abuses the amazing synergy between [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) and cycling lands. The deck runs 3 [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) and 3 [Burning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) to get access to the deck-defining sorcery. Aggro Loam also has an aggro element with [Terravore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terravore) and friends. The fast creatures can deal a lot of damage quickly, at which point the [Seismic Assault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seismic+Assault) can finish it. The best matchups are Rock variants and Boros Deck Wins, and the worst matchup is Scepter/Chant, as the deck has no out to [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant) imprinted on [Isochron Scepter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Isochron+Scepter) before board.

I have also seen versions with [Wall of Roots](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Roots), [Vinelasher Kudzu](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vinelasher+Kudzu), and [Devastating Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Dreams) maindeck instead of [Werebear](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Werebear), [Wild Mongrel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel), and [Firebolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firebolt). According to the\_bestdude, who gave me the above list, that version is not as good versus combo decks and control decks, although it may be good in a metagame full of aggro decks. He found [Devastating Dreams](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devastating+Dreams) risky and situational – better suited as a [Burning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burning+Wish) target.

![Ichorid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ichorid)This deck attempts to break the dredge mechanic with [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) (and, to a lesser extent, [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)). The plan is to get a dredge card (preferably [Golgari Grave-Troll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Golgari+Grave-Troll)) in the graveyard via [Putrid Imp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Putrid+Imp) or [Zombie Infestation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Zombie+Infestation). Then you never draw a card again. Dredge, dredge, dredge, and get as many [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid)s and black creatures into your graveyard as possible. Every upkeep you return the [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid)s in play, smash, and after combat sacrifice them to flashback [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) for good measure. The onslaught of 3/1s (plus [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)s, Zombie tokens, and assorted other creatures) should overwhelm the opponent in short order.[Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) was the best deck in Extended about a year ago, but it had faded out of existence when people started playing graveyard hate like [Morningtide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Morningtide), [Leyline of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+the+Void), or – more recently – [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt). This is an obvious weakness in the deck's strategy, but lately people have been cutting back on graveyard hate because Ichorid wasn't that popular. And even if people play graveyard hate now, it is usually [Tormod's Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt), for which this deck has 4 [Pithing Needle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle) at the ready after board. That's why [Ichorid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ichorid) has resurfaced lately.

The above list is courtesy of Ryo Ogura, who went 6-0 in the Extended portion of the World Championships with it, and rhoaen has put up multiple Premier Events with it since. So my parting word of advice is to put some graveyard hate in the sideboard of your Extended deck, unless you want Ichorid to catch you with your pants down.
