2006 Legacy Championship: Repeat After Me
=========================================












On Friday, August 11 one man stepped to the forefront to claim the title of best “Eternal” player in the world. Who was this masked man and how did he become the first person ever to hold the titles of Vintage and Legacy World Champion at the same time? Read on, my friends, read on.


### LEGACY FORMAT


More than 8,000 thousand cards are legal in Legacy tournaments, dating all the way back to the creation of **Magic**. For a recap of the 63 banned cards for Legacy format, click [here](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=judge/resources/sfrlegacy).


The sun shone brightly in Indianapolis on Friday … or did it? I have no idea really – I was inside the giant convention center for the entirety of Friday and Saturday, and honestly, you're not reading this for a weather report, are you? You want to know about Legacy. What's the format like? What are the exciting new decks? Who took home the big prize? We'll get there soon enough, I promise. First however, I suppose an introduction is in order.


Legacy as it currently exists is a relatively recent format. The modern version of this format was formed last year when **Magic** R&D separated the Banned and Restricted from “Type 1” and “Type 1.5” (giving players Vintage and Legacy, respectively). The format is considerably more restrictive than its slightly older and more inclusive cousin, but considering that you can play *nearly* every single card ever designed in **Magic**, I still expected it to be a bastion for brokenness. Happily, as it turns out, I was wrong.


Thus far in its young life, Legacy has been dominated by three decks: Goblins, Threshold, and Reset High Tide, though glacial control deck Landstill has featured prominently in the metagame from time to time, and last November Chris Pikula introduced a spicy little black-white control deck he called Deadguy Ale to shake things up. This isn't to imply that these are the only decks in the format, nor even the only successful decks – a format that basically includes all the cards should hopefully be diverse, and it is. However, these particular archetypes are the ones that have shown up in the Top 8 time and again at big Legacy tournaments over the past year. Here are stock lists of each archetype from recent Star City events held in June, plus Pikula's original:

The Legacy Championship opened with 183 players, a very solid total boosted further by 104 competitors battling for a single two-round bye in the Trial the night before. The early rounds progressed rapidly, and players were not only interested in the event, but excited about being there. It made for an interesting contrast to the business-as-usual atmosphere of most Grand Prix, forming a mélange of casual competitive players plus format enthusiasts you don't often encounter at big events. Here's the metagame breakdown for the event:

As you can see, the field was fairly diverse, with a substantial “random” element to the field with people playing tons of homebrews or archetypes that have fallen out of favor. That said, the top 6 archetypes listed above made up around 57 percent of the metagame, so a little over half the time players wound up playing something with which they were reasonably familiar. Of the archetypes listed above that have not already been covered, Burninator are decks that featured a ton of burn plus a smattering of Sligh-esque creatures such as [Jackal Pup](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup), [Ball Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ball+Lightning), and even [Spark Elemental](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spark+Elemental). The White Weenie decks were mostly disruptive builds in the same vein as blue-white “Fish” decks from Vintage, with fast, disruptive beats topped off by [Exalted Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Exalted+Angel) to try to pull ahead of most Goblin and dedicated aggro builds.


Fast-forwarding through most of the Swiss, nearly every player in the Top 8 had a chance to be knocked out in the last round, depending on who played and how certain matches turned out. We pick up the action in Round 8, with notable slacker and Wisconsin **Magic**ian Brian Kowal versus 39-year-old pizza parlor and card shop owner Doyle Bledsoe. Bledsoe was playing Extended classic Aluren while Kowal battled with a homebrew Life from the Loam deck. Both players had a chance to squeak into the Top 8 if they won, but would definitely be knocked out with a loss.


### Round 8: Doyle Bledsoe vs. Brian Kowal


Kowal called shenanigans on himself on his first turn, yelling “Uno!” as he cast three [Mox Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Diamond)s, and then still made his land drop on turn two. [Life from the Loam](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) brought all of his discarded lands right back to his hand, showing his very intriguing engine right from the get-go.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/tournamentcenter/2006/legacychamp/fm_kowal.jpg)*Brian Kowal in a make-or-break match.*“I call it… Mishra's Stompy,” noted Kowal ironically, as he bashed for four with Factories. “Unfortunately I'm not going to know what he's playing if I kill him this quickly.” For his part, Bledsoe cast [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and [Impulse](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse) on the first two turns, digging for blockers, before putting [Eternal Witness](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness) into play.


Kowal used [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) to keep Bledsoe off of any white or black mana, dredging Life from the Loam each turn to fill his graveyard with useful items, and tossing in a [Hymn to Tourach](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hymn+to+Tourach) for good measure. [Wall of Blossoms](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Blossoms) from Bledsoe settled in to block, but he just couldn't deal with the Factory beatings backed up by [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt).


“That's sickening,” lamented Bledsoe.


“Thank you,” quipped Kowal.


Both players mulliganned for Game 2, with Kowal leading off the spellcasting with a [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) (hitting [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and revealing [Wirewood Savage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wirewood+Savage), [Wall of Blossoms](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Blossoms) and two lands). Bledsoe cast [Intuition](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Intuition) for three [Raven Familiar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven+Familiar)s, getting one to dig for combo pieces, and then attacking with it a turn later only to see it head to the farm via [Swords to Plowshares](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares). Kowal had no action at all however, and Doyle used [Living Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish)es to fill his hand with [Cavern Harpy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy) and [Raven Familiar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven+Familiar). Combine that with the [Cavern Harpy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cavern+Harpy) in his hand, and Bledsoe was able to go off over the top of Kowal's [Swords to Plowshares](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/tournamentcenter/2006/legacychamp/fm_bledsoe.jpg)*Doyle Bledsoe, with Living Wish on the brain.*About 30 cards into the loop, Bledsoe realized that he might have sided out his third [Living Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) and might not be able to finish Kowal, and stopped drawing cards. Luckily, Bledsoe had the wherewithal to start attacking with his creatures and killed Kowal a couple of turns later with plenty of cards left in his library.


“Why did I say anything about that Living Wish?” mused Kowal. “He might have just kept going and drawn his whole deck.”


“Yeah, thank you for that. I almost cost myself there,” Bledsoe answered as he checked his sideboard – he had definitely sided out the third Wish.


Kowal got off to a solid start in Game 3 with very little time on the clock, disrupting Bledsoe's hand heavily and trying to start the beats with [Mishra's Factory](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mishra%27s+Factory) and [Eternal Witness](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness), but his deck wasn't exactly suited to dispatching an opponent quickly. The game ended with Bledsoe at 7 life when time was called.


When neither player was willing to concede, the match ended in a pyrrhic draw.

Chang was the only player in the field to run any sort of traditional Madness build and it paid off for him. Minus [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will), [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) (it’s still hard to believe Brainstorm rotated out), and the manabase, the maindeck is Extended legal. Force and [Wasteland](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) add additional disruption elements to a deck that was already pretty good at protecting its beaters or keeping combo down. The sideboard for this deck is also focused almost exclusively on disrupting the common decks in the metagame, with graveyard hate, Aether Vial hate (Needle), and Goblins hate ([Blue Elemental Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blue+Elemental+Blast)) all present in reasonable quantities.

I’m quite certain a substantial portion of the **Magic** playing public just became very excited as they realized you can play The Rock in Legacy as well.

Thomas Lee has a serious fetish for some [Auriok Salvagers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auriok+Salvagers). Most of his success in Vintage has come via Oath of Druids Salvagers, and this deck tries its best to recreate that deck in the slower, Goblins-and-Threshold-filled Legacy metagame. It’s really just a dirty combo deck disguised as an excuse to get a crappy four-mana 2/2 into the graveyard so it can just win. Strangely, it’s quite effective at doing this, but it doesn’t feel much like you got sleazed out by brokenness when you lose to it. The same cannot be said of losing to IGGy Pop.

This was a deck that nearly broke through back at Grand Prix-Philadelphia, but certain new additions from *Guildpact* and *Dissension* (Leyline and [Infernal Tutor](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infernal+Tutor), respectively) have pushed it over the top. For those who don’t know the synergy, [Ill-Gotten Gains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ill-Gotten+Gains) with Leyline [of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=of+the+Void) already in play is a one-sided [Mind Twist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Twist) for four mana that also Ancestrals the caster in the process. That’s the kind of disgusting stunt you’d expect to see Divine pull in a John Waters movie, and it’s decidedly unkind on opponents in the game of **Magic**. I found myself coming back to matches involving this deck again and again on Friday, surprised by how nasty it could be as well as how resistant it is to both control and heavy disruption strategies. Michael Bomholt’s creation is a thing of insidious beauty.

Surprised to see this slow-as-molasses control archetype (it kills with friggin’ lands!) make the Top 8, I asked Trudeau point blank, “Isn’t that deck dead?” Trudeau’s performance answers the question in the negative. [Stifle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) wrecked people all weekend long, I really like the Monastery beats, and playing three [Fact or Fiction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction) with cards this good feels like cheating. This deck is the one obvious and direct control deck in the environment, and while its clock can only be described as glacial, the fact that it continues to do well is a good thing for the format.

Threshold is too good an archetype not to get at least one player into the elimination rounds. Apparently Mike Flores and BDM’s favorite Extended archetype is much better when you get to add cards like [Lightning Bolt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will), and [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) plus dual lands to the decklist. Then again, what deck isn’t? Threshold is consistent, hate resistant, and thus persistent. Expect it to be around for, oh, basically ever, though some former Extended players might (rightly) point out that it’s just like Madness but with worse animals.

I’ve said all I’m going to say about IGGy Pop above. In fact, just thinking about it again makes me feel like I need to go take a shower.


Eight decklists and seven different archetypes was more than a pleasant surprise, especially considering the fact that only one each of Goblins and Threshold made it to the elimination rounds. Looking at archetypes that made it into the Top 16, you can see nearly every solid Extended deck from the game’s glorious past has a chance to do well. Affinity, 9-land Stompy, and Suicide Black with a splash of red all put players one match win away from the elimination rounds. Further down in the Top 16, you could also find a modified version of Vintage hit Uba Stax, and even Burning Bridge, so it looks like there’s a lot of design space yet to be exploited in Legacy. Thus worries that Legacy is merely a three- or four-deck format (largely spawned from the Grand Prix results at Lille and Philadelphia) appear to be overblown.


As mentioned above, the only deck to put two players into the Top 8 was IGGy Pop, a dirty combo deck that was hardly well represented but is nevertheless very powerful. Designed to exploit the synergy between [Leyline of the Void](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leyline+of+the+Void) and [Ill-Gotten Gains](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ill-Gotten+Gains), IGGy Pop is disruptive enough to blow through many control decks in the field while still fast enough to get past the flood of Goblin decks one can expect to see in the Legacy metagame. In the past, both Legacy and Vintage players have seemed to shy away from explosive combo decks, but judging from the results at Gen Con, success may force a change in that trend.

### Semifinals: Thomas Lee vs. Brandon Todesca


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/tournamentcenter/2006/legacychamp/sf_lee.jpg)*Thomas Lee brought Auriok Salvagers into the semifinals.* Lee won the all-important die roll and chose to keep. Todesca kept as well and hit the saucy start of first-turn [Goblin Lackey](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Lackey). It died to [Innocent Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Innocent+Blood) before inviting any friends to the party. [Goblin Piledriver](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Piledriver) from Todesca a turn later was also good, and he managed to stick around for a bit, while [Rishadan Port](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rishadan+Port) allowed Todesca to keep a land a turn on lockdown. [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) from Lee for [Goblin Ringleader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Ringleader) hit, showing [Goblin Matron](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Matron) and [Goblin Incinerator](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Incinerator) remaining in Todesca's hand, while on the other side of the board [Mogg Fanatic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic) was now keeping Piledriver company on the attack.


[Gamekeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamekeeper) for Lee served double duty, providing both a sacrifice outlet for [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) and allowing him to put [Auriok Salvagers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auriok+Salvagers) into play for free. Another [Gamekeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamekeeper) two turns later with Lee at 9 dramatically slowed the game … but that was really just Todesca figuring out combat math with the Goblin Warchief and Piledriver he was about to cast. The little red men were shipped directly into the red zone. Lee dropped to 1, but the [Gamekeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamekeeper) going to the graveyard triggered its ability, which then flipped [Lion's Eye Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) into the graveyard. That allowed Lee to end the match after his untap via recurring [Lion's Eye Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond), [Auriok Salvagers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auriok+Salvagers), [Chromatic Sphere](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Sphere) digging for [Pyrite Spellbomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrite+Spellbomb), and then [Pyrite Spellbomb](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrite+Spellbomb) recursion for the kill.


Todesca freely admitted he had no idea how to play against this combo deck.


Game 2 opened with a first-turn Goblin Lackey from Todesca eating another [Innocent Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Innocent+Blood), while Todesca put a pair of [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial)s on the board for his second turn. [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) for [Goblin Ringleader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Ringleader) whiffed, revealing Warchief, Fanatic, and [Goblin Matron](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Matron). Todesca drew his required land off the top, and cast the Warchief, putting Fanatic into play along with it, while Lee wiped the board clear a turn later with Infest. The board remained that way for a turn until Todesca went for the greedy play, tutoring up [Goblin Warchief](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Warchief) with his Matron (instead of [Goblin Ringleader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Goblin+Ringleader)) and then putting it into play, casting Piledriver, and smashing for 8. Another [Infest](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Infest) lay in wait for this new crew of Goblins, and they were quickly toe-tagged and sent to the morgue.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/magic/images/tournamentcenter/2006/legacychamp/sf_todesca.jpg)*Exhaustion got the better of Brandon Todesca.* A pair of dirty plucks for Lee let him get past Todesca's dual [Rishadan Port](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rishadan+Port) lockdown, giving him a [Bayou](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bayou) and Forest so that he could cast [Living Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) and then [Gamekeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamekeeper) on successive turns. An end-of-turn [Siege-Gang Commander](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege-Gang+Commander) looked to give Todesca the win, except he decided to throw up all over himself instead. With Lee at 7, all Todesca needed to simply attack, stack damage and sacrifice two of his tokens before damage resolved and he would win the game. Instead, he decided to sacrifice a token and his Siege-Gang *before damage*, putting Lee at 1. Lee cast [Innocent Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Innocent+Blood) on his turn, sending the Keeper to the bin and then flipping his entire combo over with the trigger. It had been a very long day, and Brandon's play was clearly the result of exhaustion.


Thomas Lee advances to the finals of the Legacy Championship with a 2-0 win over Brandon Todesca.


### Finals: Thomas Lee vs. Roland Chang


Roland Chang has a chance to be the first holder of both Vintage and Legacy World Champ titles and not only that, if he wins here, he will hold both at the same time (for a day, anyway…call it the Roland Slam). Thomas Lee is also a notable Vintage player, sporting at least one StarCityGames.com Power 9 Top 8 on his resume.


 



![Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cabal+Therapy)

Both players kept their openers and they were off. [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) from Lee met a [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) in response, with Chang protecting [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will). The Therapy whiffed on Mongrel, revealing two [Wooded Foothills](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wooded+Foothills), [Brainstorm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm), [Arrogant Wurm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arrogant+Wurm), [Basking Rootwalla](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basking+Rootwalla), [Deep Analysis](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deep+Analysis), and [Aquamoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aquamoeba). Chang cast the ‘Moeba on his turn and then protected it from [Innocent Blood](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Innocent+Blood) with [Basking Rootwalla](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basking+Rootwalla). [Living Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) from Lee resolved, giving him [City of Brass](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=City+of+Brass), and perhaps indicating that he already had [Gamekeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamekeeper) in hand.

[Gamekeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamekeeper) a turn later ran right into [Daze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze), earning a big frown from Lee.


Chang meanwhile kept casting angry animals and turning them sideways. The [Daze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze) plus a Force in hand was more than enough to buy Chang time for his burly beaters to win, with even a [Circular Logic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Circular+Logic) left in hand to spare.


“Now is when the matchup gets really bad… if I can't win Game 1, there isn't much hope,” lamented Lee.


Both players kept their hands for Game 2, and Lee led off with a [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress), hitting one of a pair of [Circular Logic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Circular+Logic)s in Chang's hand. [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) a turn later ripped Chang's hands to shreds, nailing a pair of [Aquamoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aquamoeba)s this time, leaving Roland without a madness outlet. On Lee's third turn he cast [Living Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish), and then used his [Lion's Eye Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) for three white mana before the Wish resolved. Chang Dazed this to set Lee back at least a turn, since Lee was obviously paying for the [Daze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze), but now could not put his [Auriok Salvagers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auriok+Salvagers) into play that turn. On Chang's turn he merely cast [Aquamoeba](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aquamoeba) and then passed it back, while as expected, Lee's Salvagers merely resolved a turn later than anticipated.


 



![Circular Logic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Circular+Logic)

Chang used two [Circular Logic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Circular+Logic)s to make sure a recursed [Lion's Eye Diamond](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lion%27s+Eye+Diamond) did not resolve, trying to buy more time to draw into either [Pithing Needle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pithing+Needle) or [Tormod's Crypt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tormod%27s+Crypt). It was not to be, and Lee combo'd out a turn later to even the match.

Chang kept his starting seven for the rubber game, but Lee had to pitch his hand back, and then watched in horror as his first [Polluted Delta](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Polluted+Delta) was [Stifle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle)d. [Basking Rootwalla](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basking+Rootwalla) from Chang kicked in the beats, followed by [Wild Mongrel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) a turn later. [Duress](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress) cleared Chang's hand of a [Daze](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Daze), but he drew a [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will), and was basically a blue card short of guaranteeing a win for himself, especially with a second angry dog joining its brother on the table.


[Living Wish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Living+Wish) from Lee got him [Gamekeeper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gamekeeper), but [Cabal Therapy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy) naming [Force of Will](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Force+of+Will) smacked home, giving him a chance again. [Circular Logic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Circular+Logic) off the top made it all irrelevant though, and with his next attack step Chang became the first dual title holder of both Legacy and Vintage World Championships.
