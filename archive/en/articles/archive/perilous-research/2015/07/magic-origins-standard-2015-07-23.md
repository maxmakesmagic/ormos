Magic Origins in Standard
=========================



 Posted in [Perilous Research](/en/articles/columns/perilous-research-archive)
 on July 23, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/080214_1291.jpg)
By Jacob Van Lunen




Jacob Van Lunen began playing Magic in 1995. He has participated in organized play at every level of competition and was a member of the winning team at Pro Tour San Diego in 2007, thanks to an innovative draft strategy. As a writer, Van Lunen has had more than three hundred Magic strategy pieces published 





Welcome to Jace week here at Perilous Research, DailyMTG's exclusive *Magic Online* column. *Magic Origins* Prerelease events are live right now on *Magic Online*, and it's only a matter of days before we're able to get our hands on some decklists that take advantage of the newest set. Today, in preparation for the upcoming Standard season, we'll be discussing some of the cards from *Magic Origins* that are already making a big impact on Standard.





---

[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy)[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jace%2C+Telepath+Unbound)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Telepath+Unbound)Transform

It's Jace week, so the first card we'll be talking about is [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy). The newest Jace is one of the most interesting and potentially powerful cards in *Magic Origins*. As a two-mana looter, [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) is already tremendous in a format where we have access to cards like [Treasure Cruise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise), [Dig Through Time](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dig+Through+Time), and [Murderous Cut](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murderous+Cut). The card makes less consistent strategies' draws cleaner while also providing a backbreaking end game if left unchecked. What kind of decks will [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) find a home in?

The first deck we've seen take full advantage of [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) is Jeskai Tokens. The deck hadn't been performing very well prior to the release of *Magic Origins*, but Jace does a very nice job of bringing this deck to the next level. By adding a two-mana looter to the deck, [Treasure Cruise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise) becomes a lot more powerful; not only will [Treasure Cruise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise) usually cost a lot less mana, but the lands we draw with [Treasure Cruise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise) may be traded in for more relevant cards because we're exiling our graveyard and resetting the timer on our Jace flip.



Jeskai Tokens was always very reliant on sticking a copy of [Jeskai Ascendancy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jeskai+Ascendancy), which it would use to trade in all lands in excess of three, four, five, or six. This interaction means that the deck will, when things are going according to plan, have a much higher density of spells than the opponent. [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) gives the deck redundancy in that capacity by giving it another avenue to trade lands for spells. Once [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) flips, the deck can trade in the -3 ability for another [Treasure Cruise](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treasure+Cruise) or more token production—and it usually forces the opponent to either spend a card or a lackluster attack to deal with the planeswalker before it represents another recast spell from the yard. In postboarded games, this deck can take advantage of Jace's ultimate alongside [Sphinx's Tutelage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%27s+Tutelage) to deck opponents who are planning to win the long game. It's clear that [Jace, Vryn's Prodigy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Vryn%27s+Prodigy) is very powerful, and the card will continue to do great things while it's alongside the delve mechanic.

Green Devotion decks splashing red for [Dragonlord Atarka](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dragonlord+Atarka) have been very popular and successful over the last few months, but the deck has always had some serious issues against decks with a lot of removal, board sweepers, and counter spells. Now, [Gaea's Revenge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Revenge) gives the Red-Green Devotion strategy the extra push it needed to compete with the control decks in postboarded games. Let's take a look at a deck that takes full advantage of [Gaea's Revenge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Revenge) in the new Standard!

Green Devotion's strength against the field has been augmented a great deal by the inclusion of [Gaea's Revenge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Revenge). The once-feared Esper Dragons matchup is now very beatable. Some players are also taking advantage of [Nissa, Vastwood Seer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nissa%2C+Vastwood+Seer) to further augment their ability in those matchups by not being as susceptible to board sweepers killing mana creatures. The one major issue for [Gaea's Revenge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaea%27s+Revenge) is that it's sometimes difficult to cast it when all the mana creatures are being dealt with via [Crux of Fate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crux+of+Fate).





---

[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Languish)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Languish)

[Languish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Languish) is a powerful board sweeping effect that conveniently leaves [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino) and [Tasigur, the Golden Fang](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tasigur%2C+the+Golden+Fang) untouched. The card allows Abzan players that have recently been forced to go smaller with cards like [Fleecemane Lion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fleecemane+Lion) to play a bigger game once more. Let's take a look at an Abzan Control deck that takes full advantage of [Languish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Languish).

Abzan Control decks were once a dominant force in Standard. As the format sped up over the last couple of months, these decks were forced to play cards like [Fleecemane Lion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fleecemane+Lion) to stay on pace with the rest of the format. [Languish](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Languish) gives the deck a new plan, allowing Abzan Control players to safely clear the board without killing [Siege Rhino](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Siege+Rhino) or [Tasigur, the Golden Fang](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tasigur%2C+the+Golden+Fang). Abzan players have a lot of wiggle room with their sideboards these days, and [Dromoka's Command](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dromoka%27s+Command) has never been better during its time in Standard. In postboarded games, this version of the deck can rebrand itself as a midrange strategy.

This is easily the most exciting deck we've seen with cards from *Magic Origins*. [Thopter Spy Network](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thopter+Spy+Network) allows the deck to create a steady stream of threats and maintain card advantage, while [End Hostilities](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=End+Hostilities) and counter spells help buy some time. [Hangarback Walker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hangarback+Walker) is essential to the deck's success, providing a big win condition and usually making a bump in the road for the opponent. When the pilot of this deck is able to untap with [Hangarback Walker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hangarback+Walker), they can assume a "draw, go" role wherein they'll simply play countermagic and card draw while repeatedly activating the Walker. This deck takes advantage of a lot of what *Magic Origins* has to offer.

There are a lot of reasons to play multicolored decks right now, but [Foundry of the Consuls](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Foundry+of+the+Consuls) gives us a pretty good reason to make our deck more consistent. This card does a lot, and it has a lot of fun interactions with cards like [Pia and Kiran Nalaar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pia+and+Kiran+Nalaar)—a card that seems particularly well positioned against the current Standard metagame—and others. White Devotion is better than ever, with [Archangel of Tithes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Tithes) providing one of the most aggressively costed effects/bodies we've seen in recent years. We can expect white strategies with [Knight of the White Orchid](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+White+Orchid) and [Archangel of Tithes](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Archangel+of+Tithes) to be tested by a lot of major teams leading up to the Pro Tour.
