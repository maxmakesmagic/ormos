This Article Doesn't Exist
==========================



 Posted in **Reconstructed**
 on March 19, 2013 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorPic_GavinVerhey.jpg)
By Gavin Verhey




 When Gavin Verhey was eleven, he dreamt of a job making Magic cards—and now as a Magic designer, he's living his dream! Gavin has been writing about Magic since 2005. 







If there were a Dimir, they might have a theme week. And, *if* they did, then I would select a deck and write about them. If that happened, I would probably choose to look at a deck that involved a mechanic theoretically called cipher, seeing how the Dimir would theoretically have a mechanic of their own.


That might be something I would do. You know, *if* there really was a Dimir.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc239_guildgate.jpg)
Is that... a [Dimir Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dimir+Charm) I see on the inside of your cloak? You really shouldn't make it so obvious. It stood out like a herd of indriks at a Rauck-Chauv festival.


I'll let it slide, Agent—this time. I have some new information to slip to you that needs immediate delivery.


This intelligence document has been procured by operative Ghostwave, real name “Diego Lacalamita.” Let me run you through it.

**The Battle Plan**


This deck is comprised of a two-pronged attack.


On one hand, this deck is set up to win by reducing your opponent's library to zero. (Or “milling,” as it is commonly called.) Cards like [Mind Sculpt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sculpt) and [Dream Twist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dream+Twist) lead right into that. Control decks are going to run into some serious trouble winning in time versus the deck-depleting clock of milling—especially with [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard) as one of the forefront cards in the plan. The Drownyard ensures you always have gas left in the milling tank.

However, like any good Dimir agent, Diego has a second plan as well.


The problem with milling is that a lot of the time you'll only get part of the way there and then all of that hard work will be for absolutely nothing. You will have made little progress on the game and, if you can't finish off your opponent, he or she will overwhelm you with creatures. The solution? Some powerful creatures of your own!


[Wight of Precinct Six](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wight+of+Precinct+Six), [Jace's Phantasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%27s+Phantasm), and [Consuming Aberration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consuming+Aberration) are all going to be inordinately sized relative to their mana cost in a deck like this. Even if your opponent isn't in striking distance of being milled out, that's no big deal: these creatures are happy to use all of those cards you milled away as a resource to mop up the game the old-fashioned way.


The key to improving this deck is going to be making sure that the pieces in both categories are optimized and at the right ratio. With that in mind, let's dive into the deck, shall we?


**Card Breakdown**


What works in this deck, and what doesn't? Well, let's go over the cards in the deck individually to find out!





![Jace's Phantasm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jace%27s+Phantasm)


 

[Jace's Phantasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%27s+Phantasm) is the typical card you might expect in this deck to bridge the gap. There's a huge differential between the power of this card when it's just a 1/1 and when it's kicked up. It can end the game extraordinarily quickly once it's online. Fortunately, in a milling deck, it's pretty easy to do just that!


[Jace's Phantasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%27s+Phantasm) is a fairly low investment card at only a single mana that works well with what the rest of the deck is doing, plus it has evasion so it makes a good cipher target early on if we end up adding cards with cipher. I definitely want to stick with four copies.





![Wight of Precinct Six](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Wight+of+Precinct+Six)


 

Like the [Phantasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phantasm), this is another great way this deck bridges the milling-to-creature gap. Now, it's worth noting that [Wight of Precinct Six](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wight+of+Precinct+Six) is fairly bad against control decks since they don't tend to play many creatures—but that's okay. Your matchup against control decks is pretty good to start off with since they can't kill you quickly and your milling strategy is particularly powerful against them. Where you really need the help is against beatdown decks, when your milling cards can be a little slow—and that's where Wight shines.


Against a deck chock-full of more creatures than Cinnamon Toast Crunch has swirls, the [Wight of Precinct Six](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wight+of+Precinct+Six) is a great offensive and defensive mechanism. It grows to huge sizes quickly and can either buy you the time you need or get in for some big hits once the coast is clear.


While you could consider something else, such as [Lazav, Dimir Mastermind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lazav%2C+Dimir+Mastermind), for example, in this spot, I'm pretty happy with [Wight of Precinct Six](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wight+of+Precinct+Six). It's half the price of [Lazav](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lazav) and doesn't need to be on the battlefield first. Although [Lazav](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lazav) is better against control and I would usually sideboard these Wights out against control decks, they are such crucial tools against beatdown decks at two mana that I want to leave all four copies in the main deck.





![Snapcaster Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Snapcaster+Mage)


 

After those two more unusual creatures, here's a face you are probably a bit more familiar with. [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) is fantastic for this deck.


There are a ton of cards you want to have redundancy on so you can adapt to whatever your plan is. Whether he serves as extra [Mind Sculpt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sculpt)s or buys you time against creatures by reusing your removal spells, [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) is doing a well-deserved job. He can even come down on turn two and carry a [Paranoid Delusions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paranoid+Delusions) (which is almost certainly being added in) if he needs to! He's so strong in this deck that I want to stick with the full four.





![Duskmantle Guildmage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Duskmantle+Guildmage)


 

[Duskmantle Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duskmantle+Guildmage) is a tricky card to evaluate in this deck. It does attack on both angles, and when it is good it is *quite* good. It can also provide you with some free wins. Imagine this sequence, for example:



> Turns 2–4: Cast a [Duskmantle Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duskmantle+Guildmage) on one of these turns.  
> 
> Turn 5: Cast [Jace, Memory Adept](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Memory+Adept).  
> 
> Turn 6: Double activate [Duskmantle Guildmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duskmantle+Guildmage)'s first ability. Mill your opponent ten cards with Jace. Your opponent's dead.


On the other hand, it doesn't help out a ton without support elements around it. Milling your opponent for two is nice, but for four mana it's just such a high investment—especially considering the deck already had [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard)s to use with four mana, which is just better.


The number of Guildmages I would like is two. It can be good in conjunction with other cards in the deck, and it's a cheap creature to potentially cipher onto, but it takes specific situations to be strong. If you draw one you can set up for those situations—but if you don't, your plans don't hinge on them.





![Consuming Aberration](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Consuming+Aberration)


 

[Consuming Aberration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consuming+Aberration) is going to be consistently gigantic in this deck. In many games it will hit the battlefield at 10/10—or larger! And yet, this is accompanied by a seemingly odd question: Is that good enough?


As a huge creature for five mana it looks impressive—but looks can be deceiving. A lot of the time, it's just going to be better for your core strategy to lay a [Jace, Memory Adept](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+Memory+Adept) and then mill your opponent for ten cards. You only have room for so many five-mana cards, and [Jace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace) surpasses [Consuming Aberration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consuming+Aberration) most of the time: [Jace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace) is harder to kill, you get an effect out of him right away, and that effect might improve some of your other cards on the battlefield. The grind trigger off of [Consuming Aberration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Consuming+Aberration) is much slower and pales in comparison. [Jace's Phantasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%27s+Phantasm) and [Wight of Precinct Six](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wight+of+Precinct+Six) will do much of the work of [Aberration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aberration) for you at a fraction of the cost.


While there are some situations where it might be nice to have a big blocker, there's only so much room at five mana and I would rather play a full set of Jaces than the [Aberration](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aberration).





![Undercity Informer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Undercity+Informer)


 

While the added milling provided by [Undercity Informer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Undercity+Informer) is nice, and he can help provide the finishing blow late in the game, as a 2/3 for three with a milling ability that won't be used until it needs to be he's just a little too weak to be what this deck is looking for. There are going to be better cards for this deck than this creature.





![Mind Sculpt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mind+Sculpt)


 

As one of the best mana-to-mill ratios in Standard, this is a key inclusion for this deck. It's two mana for seven cards, and then it sits in your graveyard to be [Snapcaster](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster)ed back later. This deck wants to draw as many as it can—I'll definitely stick with four.





![Mind Grind](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mind+Grind)


 

[Mind Grind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Grind) reads really exciting for a milling deck—but it can be a bit deceptive. While it does create occasional big swings, on average you're going to play it for two or three and it's not going to mill as many cards as you might expect. Especially with a full set of [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard)s already in this deck as a way to spend your extra mana on milling cards, playing four copies of an expensive milling card does not sound that exciting to me. I'd rather cut these and let my “big mill” card be Jace.





![Dimir Charm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dimir+Charm)


 

[Dimir Charm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dimir+Charm) is a pretty solid fit for this deck, giving it a nice range of versatility. Every mode is useful, with the last one being slightly better than usual in this deck since getting to mill your opponent for two cards is relevant. I'm going to slide down to three from four, just because I want to play some removal in this deck that goes larger than just killing 2-power creatures. However, this card is still plenty useful enough to warrant three spots.





![Dream Twist](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Dream+Twist)


 

While I'm willing to run [Mind Sculpt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sculpt) because it's such a crazy mana-to-mill ratio, [Dream Twist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dream+Twist) does not impress in the same way. I don't think this is going to be better than [Paranoid Delusions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paranoid+Delusions) most of the time, and I would rather run those in a deck with this many creatures before I went to [Dream Twist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dream+Twist).





![Jace, Memory Adept](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Jace%2C+Memory+Adept)


 

As I've mentioned a couple times already, this [Jace](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace) is something that really makes this deck tick. If your opponent can't deal with him, he or she is probably going to lose in just a couple turns—and even if your opponent immediately [Dreadbore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dreadbore)s him, you still ended up milling away ten crucial cards in the deal.


While he is a Planeswalker, I'm okay drawing multiples. If one dies it means you're more likely to replace it with another, and if one is stuck in your hand because you have a copy on the battlefield, you're probably winning anyway because you untapped with a Jace! I'd like to bump him all the way up to four copies.


 


### Looking in Dimir


With the core of the deck solidified and a few cards stripped out, that gives us some room to work with. What should go in those empty spots? Well, let's go through the list!





![Thought Scour](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Thought+Scour)


 

Traditionally, in Standard, you use [Thought Scour](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thought+Scour) to target yourself to set up your [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage)s or other graveyard shenanigans. In this deck, though, it's all about targeting your opponent! For a blue mana and no card disadvantage, you empty your opponent's library of two cards. Especially when you're trying to power up an early [Jace's Phantasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%27s+Phantasm) or [Wight of Precinct Six](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wight+of+Precinct+Six), this adds a nice little jump-start. There's little cost to playing it, and it helps draw us into the best cards in the deck. I'm happy with the full four.





![Paranoid Delusions](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Paranoid+Delusions)


 

A lot of the time, [Paranoid Delusions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paranoid+Delusions) is going to be two mana to mill six cards, since you cast it and then can immediately attack. While that's still worse than [Mind Sculpt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind+Sculpt), the ability of Paranoid Delusions to repeat over and over means it can quickly convert to nine, twelve, or more cards over time. Curving a turn-one [Jace's Phantasm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%27s+Phantasm) into a turn-two [Paranoid Delusions](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Paranoid+Delusions) is fantastic—and if you have another Delusions to follow it up the next turn, that's going to put a *very* quick clock on your opponent!


I'm interested in playing three. They can create blowout draws with early creatures and are good early on, but at the same time you want to cast them when you have a creature ready to attack, so they are a bit situational. I'd start with three, and then adjust from there depending on what decks you continue to face.


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/rc/rc239_removal.jpg)As mentioned earlier, a quick creature draw can be a problem for a deck like this. I wanted access to a smattering of removal to help curb those draws and ensure I could get this deck's creatures through. With the creatures in Standard being so varied and [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) in this deck, I wanted to spread out the kinds of removal spells.


While originally I considered [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) because of how strong this deck is at parity, thanks to the super-powerful [Nephalia Drownyard](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nephalia+Drownyard), she's best against control decks but a pretty weak removal spell against beatdown. If your local metagame is really heavily slanted toward control, I would consider main-decking two or three Lilianas.

Depending on your metagame, you can modify this shell. You can play more removal spells against beatdown decks and more cards like [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil); [Lazav, Dimir Mastermind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lazav%2C+Dimir+Mastermind); [Duress](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Duress); and [Psychic Strike](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychic+Strike) against control decks. Your control matchup is already pretty good to start with, though, so I caution you against moving this deck too much to focusing on fighting off those decks.

### ReConstructed Anniversary!


After this week, there's only one guild left to review: Orzhov. And that's coming up next week! Soon we'll be all the way through the *Gatecrash* guilds, and it'll be time to look at *Dragon's Maze*!


But wait! There's going to be one week between Orzhov Week and the beginning of *Dragon's Maze* previews. That week is not only ReConstructed's fifty-second article (making it its year anniversary), but it's also just a few days after my birthday. So, for a change of pace, let's do something a little different.


Some of you might be familiar with the idea of a Topical Blend article. It was an idea that first showed up on this website back in 2005, [pioneered by Mark Rosewater](/en/articles/archive/making-magic/topical-blend-1-%E2%80%93-err-human-2005-03-07-0). Since then, a few authors have done their own. (Including one of my most popular articles in my time before I worked at Wizards and was a DailyMTG.com writer, written back in 2010—[check it out](http://www.starcitygames.com/magic/misc/20307_Flow_of_Ideas_Topical_Blend_1_The_Day_Magic_Died.html).


If you're not familiar, it works like this: I give you a list of **Magic**-centric topics and a list of non-**Magic** topics. You all vote on which you like the most. I take the two highest vote-getters and write an article that somehow includes and interweaves both of them.


And, of course, since this is a deck-building column, naturally all of the **Magic** topics will relate to strategy and deck building.
