====================

 on March 8, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






For Fate Week, I decided to highlight some (more) of the various—and widely varied—ways players have earned themselves Blue Envelopes over this mighty Modern meta.

Andy Burden took down a Louisville, KY, PTQ last month with this super-efficient burn deck.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Lava Spike | Bump in the Night |

Reminiscent of the [Lava Spike](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Spike) deck, Burden did, in fact, play ye ole [Lava Spike](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Spike). He played [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) (the Cadillac of burn, of course), [Lava Spike](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lava+Spike), and (sort of [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), given the tenor of the deck) [Bump in the Night](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bump+in+the+Night). The baseline strategy of this deck is that there are tons and tons of cards that do 2–3 damage, typically for one mana, so it doesn't take a whole lot of them to count to 20.


Heck, he even had [Shard Volley](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shard+Volley) as a one-mana-for-3-damage spell!


![Shard Volley](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Shard+Volley)  
The Bump deck has avenues to card advantage in [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) (very low risk here, as the vast majority of the cards are so cheap), and a kind of red look at card advantage in [Searing Blaze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Searing+Blaze) (advances the goal of the deck while actually trading with a card). [Burst Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning) isn't much worse than [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) (which is, again, the Cadillac), and sometimes it hits 4 damage rather than just 2.




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Dark Confidant | Searing Blaze |

The picture of efficiency, the Bump deck can win quickly for a non-combo deck and is particularly punishing in a format full of both *Zendikar* Block and *Ravnica* Block dual lands so often working in the same decks.


That said, it is probably fair to say that Bump is not a powerhouse. [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) is powerful (in-context), but it's not like the deck can attack for infinite on turn four or annihilate all the opponent's permanents with a [Through the Breach](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Through+the+Breach)...


... but it has some tools to deal with decks throwing those kinds of haymakers.



> * **[Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek):** Trades with lots and lots of different kinds of cards, rips key cards out of the opponent's opening hand, and can break up fast combos in as many ways as there are combos ([Deceiver Exarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deceiver+Exarch), [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), [Pyromancer Ascension](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyromancer+Ascension)... told ya!).
> * **[Ensnaring Bridge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ensnaring+Bridge):** Because you will never have fifteen cards in hand on the opponent's attack phase.
> * **[Torpor Orb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Torpor+Orb):** Stops the [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) combo (and other incidental comes-into-play effects).

So... what's different?


We've seen [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) decks—and Ravager decks claiming invitations—all over this format. But most of those were either straight red or red-based with [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast) and [Galvanic Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast) as finishers, [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) in the sideboard as a spoiler.


The deck susbus played is blue-black. Black gives it [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant) (lots of 0–2 mana cards make the downside slim) and blue gives it [Master of Etherium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium) (top of the curve at a whopping three mana). [Master of Etherium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium) makes all the tiny 0/1 and 1/1 monsters into real threats, and they, in turn make the Master monstrous.


![Master of Etherium](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Master+of+Etherium)  
The blue-black deck Espers up a bit with [Ethersworn Canonist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ethersworn+Canonist) in the sideboard (a foil for decks like Storm), and Glimmervoid and [Welding Jar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Welding+Jar) help set up red (and green!) for [Whipflare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Whipflare) (essentially no downside) and [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) (so much upside).

What's the point of concentrating on tokens anyway?


[Raise the Alarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raise+the+Alarm), from some dimension, is 2 power (and 2 toughness) for two mana. When well wrought, [Spectral Procession](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spectral+Procession) is 3 and 3 for three. However, it is quite evident these cards far outstrip [Grizzly Bears](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grizzly+Bears) and [Trained Armodon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trained+Armodon) in terms of efficacy. We can call (such efficient) token production "card advantage" (in that power and toughness is spread across multiple bodies). So when Ajani buffs the squad, your jump isn't from 2-to-3 or 3-to-4 but 2-to-4 and 3-to-6.


![Ajani Goldmane](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ajani+Goldmane)  
So that's why a player like Romero might dip a little bit in terms of card quality for a [Raise the Alarm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raise+the+Alarm)... the payback across lots of different cards over-performs in aggregate.


The kinds of cards Romero went with allow him to slow down and interact with faster or more powerful decks ([Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek)), including putting the opponent on a clock at the same time ([Tidehollow Sculler](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tidehollow+Sculler)). He has a fair number of great anti-beatdown and anti-red capabilities in the side ([Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks), [Kor Firewalker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kor+Firewalker)), although the White-Black Tokens strategy as a whole has always been robust against conventional beatdown (you can leave some bodies back to block while attacking with others and constantly expanding your footprint on the battlefield).


Overall, an exciting list from my perspective, refreshingly different and reminiscent at the same time.

[Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) is a fine measure of additional functionality but it's not like that card—especially as a three-of only—breaks all matchups. If you really want to deal with more creatures in straight blue you can do stuff like play more [Vedalken Shackles](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vedalken+Shackles) or go crazy with faster response cards without dipping into more colors. Which is not to say that [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) isn't both super flexible and super good... more that cards like [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) (again, very little incremental cost on the green at this point) and—especially, [Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout)—take the Faeries strategy into wildly different directions.


![Firespout](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Firespout)  
Like... what if you are in a foot race, but all your guys fly (they mostly do)? Your opponent is laying guy after guy on the ground to bowl you over, you steal a turn with a Clique, hit with the other Clique, get in a good block. Your opponent is like, "No way am I losing a race to a bunch of blue guys! I don't care if they have flash..." and just farts the whole grip onto the table.


"[Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout); no green, BTW. thx!"


What is really insane is that depending on how the games play out, you can potentially make a play like that without ever revealing red beforehand. It's possible! And it provides value!


Other plays, like the big [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) (almost no skin off your back, but backbreaking to the other cat) make for great functionality on a deck like this, which has such a robust and not-too-frilly A-game.

Now this deck, on the other hand, is quite frilly.


It's straightforward and brutal, but unavoidably fancy.


RealDizzy's deck has great cards and recognizable strategies, but also defies expectations and demands us to "ooh" and "aah" with its successive plays.


Like, it is a Boros beatdown deck of the most obvious sort, sometimes.


"[Steppe Lynx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx), clear you with a [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) (mad value), in for a ton. Clear that with a [Burst Lightning](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning), in for a ton again..."




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Steppe Lynx | Lightning Helix |

That is, if not its game plan in total, certainly a very possible opening sequence.


There is quite a bit of crossover with the pure combat efficacy of [Delver of Secrets](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Delver+of+Secrets); you can sub Delver there and start getting in for 3 on turn two (again with the seemingly out-of-place red instants).


But the deck also has echoes of a Geist offense.


![Geist of Saint Traft](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft)  
And it has quite a Snapcaster suite (smashing the opponent with [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) + [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) is just a snowball of excitement, I think you will agree). There is so much great pure aggro here—3/2 for one, [Paul Rietzl's favorite one-drop](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx), a stack of finishing burn begging to be unleashed from the grip—but the deck can *also* (on that one strategic turn) play a little [CounterSliver](/en/articles/archive/secret-life-slivers-2004-03-10), a little Faeries, and break your back a different way.


"[Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) your [Day](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment)."


*thx.*


For goodness's sake... [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) + "[Remand](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Remand) your [Day](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment)" *while clocking* with the aforementioned one-, two-, and three-drops... *with a grip full of burn?*
