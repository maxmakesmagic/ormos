===============



 Posted in **Top Decks**

![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211024110319im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/themes/wiz_mtg/images/global/generic-avatar-150.png)
By Mike Flores











![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td24_threecards.jpg)  
It was close to ten years ago when Grand Prix kingpin Trey Van Cleave won the then-Extended Grand Prix–Philadelphia with his little beatdown deck called the Three-Deuce. This was the height of Trix—full-on Trix, mind you, with [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence), [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual), [Mana Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Vault), [Demonic Consultation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Consultation) .... Nearly every non-combination card in the main deck was eventually banned, but Trey took the title home with his little [River Boa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=River+Boa)s and full set of [Elvish Lyrist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Lyrist)s.

Any guesses why Trey's deck was called the Three-Deuce? Pretty odd, no?

Even if you had been playing back then, you probably wouldn't know why Van Cleave's methodical proto-Zoo had that particular nickname. The answer is actually in the numbers, three and two. You know what's three and two? Or in this case *specifically 3/2*?

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td24_rancoreddwarf.jpg)... A [Dwarven Miner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Miner) wearing a [Rancor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rancor).

Like Trey's deck, this article has got a three and a two as well. Because we have a brand spanking new set about to drop on us in the middle of a working Constructed format, we are going to look at *three* decks of three different styles that all benefitted from an injection of new cards in the middle of an established tournament format, already in progress.

And then there's the two: We are going to review two newish / not new Extended decks, updated takes on some performing favorites really, and discuss how they can make your life either easier (or more difficult) come the sweaty palms of a pre-PTQ Friday night.

But first up ....

The format was Standard; the era, Black Summer. The world was covered in [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence)s (and for the purpose of this article topic, you could probably make a case for [Contagion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Contagion), albeit with different criteria). The card that changed the rules was a new land from *Alliances* named [Kjeldoran Outpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Kjeldoran+Outpost)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost)So what was so significant about [Kjeldoran Outpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost)?

Bill was in a situation not unlike the modern White Weenie deck in Standard, actually! With his eight pack of protection from black, Bill was actually pretty good against the dominant [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) deck (just as today's efficient Kithkin can be built to be pretty good against the dominant Faeries); but while [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) was the most popular, most powerful, deck ... not everybody played it.

Some players were audacious enough to play white-blue-based control decks (still), which while at a disadvantage against [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence), were very powerful against a beatdown deck with no burn cards like White Weenie. White-blue control could lock down the game with [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) and [Swords to Plowshares](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swords+to+Plowshares) and poor White Weenie, no matter how good its opening salvo, could be locked out of the Red Zone and watch the game slip away in a cascade of creature removal and card advantage. It would be *really* insulting if the control player sat there on 1 ... for like forty minutes.

What Kjeldoran Outpost did was give White Weenie a way to finish these games.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td24_soldiers.jpg)  
Did you notice the two [Strip Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine)s in Bill's deck? At this point in history, players could play as many as *four*[Strip Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine)s ... But they didn't necessarily do so, especially not decks like White-Blue Control, which had ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif) requirements for [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) and ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif) requirements for [Wrath of God](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) in an era with neither [Mystic Gate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mystic+Gate) nor [Hallowed Fountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hallowed+Fountain) in Standard.

Woe to White-Blue! 

[Strip Mine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine) would have been one of the few reasonable ways to deal with [Kjeldoran Outpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost) for most White-Blue decks. Those that relied on the [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone) kill would be easily raced (so most white-blue decks after this would largely replace their [Millstone](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Millstone)s with Outposts of their own). 

The lesson: Bill was only able to solve the problem the way he did by opening his mind to a new world of possibilities. He didn't *just* add a new-set card to his White Weenie deck, he bravely showcased it to a world where many saw a card like the Outpost as "card disadvantage" ... especially in a room full of so many [*Strip Mine*](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Strip+Mine)s. If Bill had listened to every *That can't be good*, he would never have been able to go down so great.

With a whole new end game attached to his deck (and one where his Outposts would uniquely produce 2/2 creatures), Macey was able to qualify for his National Championships via the U.S. Open, the only non-[Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) deck to do so that *year*. Bill would accomplish the same feat three years later with a different uniquely positioned White Weenie deck ... But that is a story for another time.

So what about *Conflux*? Can you see a similar, maybe non-intuitive, way for an aggressive deck to improve its end game against control? I nominate [Banefire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Banefire)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire)  
[Banefire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire) is a little bit like [Kjeldoran Outpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost) in that you have to stretch your imagination a little bit before you can appreciate how we might want to position it. Yes it's kind of obviously good. Yes it's versatile. But we don't typically slot cards in creature decks just to end it all (who knows if it's even in-color?). [Kjeldoran Outpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost) in Bill's deck could theoretically have been played early (it was a land of course) just as [Banefire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire) can go for two to off a not-yet-exactly-dangerous [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch). But the true value of both cards is in winning games that the aggressive deck isn't supposed to win. [Kjeldoran Outpost](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kjeldoran+Outpost) could overwhelm a fist full of [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s and creature removal, and in the same way, [Banefire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Banefire) can potentially circumvent seven (or so) [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)s. If in modern Constructed it is relegated as a sideboard card, so be it ... but I wouldn't bet on that.

To understand the massive leap in technology that was going on as Truc won his "I'm already qualified" PTQ, you need to realize that not long before the release of *Tempest*, Constructed **Magic** was largely molasses. Slow four-mana spells, expensive finishers, all of it. It was not uncommon to see cards that would not make the cut in modern Block Constructed showcased in the Top 8 of an Extended premiere event ([Icy Manipulator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Icy+Manipulator), [Wildfire Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire+Emissary), [Erhnam Djinn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Erhnam+Djinn)). The Pro Tour itself was won by one of the finest decks every put together (LauerPotence with four [Demonic Consultation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Consultation)s) ... which was a deck that leaned heavily on [Lake of the Dead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lake+of+the+Dead).

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td24_tempest.jpg)*Tempest*, though, changed everything.

For the first time ever, players were paying attention to what they did on the first turn. To say the format "sped up" would not quite describe the degree to which the **Magic** landscape transformed. Never again would slow control decks that did nothing before the fourth (or the sixth!) turn be viable to play. They would have already been shredded by one-drops, and [Time Walk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Time+Walk)ed three times, and burned out for good measure.

And from the other side? [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) with [Lake of the Dead](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lake+of+the+Dead) powering [Drain Life](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Drain+Life)? Not the easiest when the beatdown decks could be playing [Dwarven Miner](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dwarven+Miner), four copies of [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland), [Ankh of Mishra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ankh+of+Mishra), and [Forsaken Wastes](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forsaken+Wastes). So many angles, none of them promising.

But maybe Truc's version didn't quite hammer this point home enough. 

The fundamental language of deck construction had changed. The rules were different. Some things had become possible and others that had been cemented as quite good had become asteroid-blasted dinosaur bones.

This was a time when [Suq'Ata Lancer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Suq%27Ata+Lancer) was commonly played. I remember the great Erik Lauer liking it ... "Until they started printing 2-power creatures for one mana instead of three."

The adoption of [Jackal Pup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup) in a format already so vulnerable to [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) was a dagger to the heart of the previous Pro Tour's established Extended deck hierarchy. 

The cards in *Tempest* were obviously good, but it still took a fair bit of initiative to fully embrace and realize just *how* good we are talking about. Truc, for instance, didn't *quite* get there. He did some things very well, but his red deck was frankly a bit loose with [Disenchant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disenchant) and [Diabolic Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Edict), one "Wild Thing" ([Wildfire Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wildfire+Emissary)) ... a whole lot of loose non-four-ofs that a modern red deck would never consider. Beltrando's deck is more seriously set up to start attacking from the first and second turns. He takes time away from the opponent, and exacerbates that with his uncounterable [Stone Rain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stone+Rain) land.

Both decks could slide down a [Cursed Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll) under the [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) wall (or in the face of a [Necropotence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) deck that had no [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell)s) as a flag planted in the soil, fluttering "No, you're not going to win this one."

Can you see an equivalent to [Jackal Pup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup), [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic), and [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland) in *Conflux*?

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td24_threecards2.jpg)  
It might not be as sweeping in effect, not quite undoing everything and all, but [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) seems like an equal and opposite equivalent for at least some strategies. Control instead of attack, maybe, but also quite cheap at one mana like [Jackal Pup](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jackal+Pup), [Mogg Fanatic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mogg+Fanatic), or [Cursed Scroll](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cursed+Scroll) (an "above-average card," Truc once commented). Like [Wasteland](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wasteland), [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) may single-handedly change how the game is played. How much incentive is there to playing a deck like All-In Red with this floating around? And playable for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)? What does this say about decks that try to play [Akroma, Angel of Wrath](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%2C+Angel+of+Wrath)? It's a kibosh on expensive and clunky cards (no matter how fast) all over again. 

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Path+to+Exile)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile)  
Can the return of cheap and targeted white removal signal the genesis of a new deck, thanks to breaking down some old barriers and removing the incentive to some popular, powerful, strategies?

The set was *Urza's Legacy*.

The card, [Memory Jar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memory+Jar) ... Briefly at least.

This deck—a combination deck that was so good in testing that Lauer bought a last-minute ticket to hit the Grand Prix on another *continent* and where both teammates finished Top 8 in a Grand Prix finale that included Jon Finkel and was won by Kai Budde himself—was the subject of the only emergency banning, ever.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td24_jar.jpg)The way this deck worked was to make lots of mana cheaply via the usual suspects ([Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual), [Mana Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Vault), [Lotus Petal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Petal)) so that it would have lots and lots of operating mana with essentially no cost, [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker+) and [Vampiric Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vampiric+Tutor+) up to [Memory Jar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memory+Jar), start drawing lots of cards to set up more [Memory Jar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memory+Jar)s with more [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker)s and [Yawgmoth's Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will)s (more mana there, too), get the solitary kill card in play, and watch the opponent lose when he had to discard for the Jar, due to the [Shock](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shock) value of that one lonely [Megrim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Megrim). Yes, [Megrim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Megrim).

Jar, printed. Too good, gone.

The deck was obviously jacked with a number of too-good cards that all got restricted or banned in various formats. [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker), [Lotus Petal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Petal), [Mana Vault](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Vault), [Yawgmoth's Will](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Yawgmoth%27s+Will) .... Any one of these cards could have produced a degenerate deck, but all together and with [Memory Jar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memory+Jar) as the centerpiece ... like I said, the only mid-season emergency banning, ever.

What was so good about [Memory Jar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memory+Jar) that it could do what it did with such impunity that it could produce a combo deck powerful enough to challenge [High Tide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=High+Tide+) for the throne at the height of [Hide Tide](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hide+Tide)'s power?

*It broke the rules.*

**Magic** starts to break down when players can either draw extra cards or circumvent the mana costs on powerful spells without paying (very much) mana. A card like [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker), for instance, makes a really expensive artifact (whatever Ramp;D wants it to cost) need only three mana. Suddenly you have the ability to draw seven cards for essentially the cost of a [Thirst for Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thirst+for+Knowledge)*without even drawing the card that lets you draw seven cards.* What cards in *Conflux* blatantly break the rules in this way? I know we don't have a supporting cast of [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual) and a bandwagon of eventual ban-ees, but could there be some anchor, some starting point? 

The new card that intrigues me most is [Maelstrom Archangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Archangel).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Maelstrom+Archangel)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Archangel)  
As I said before, **Magic** as a game starts getting fuzzy when you don't have to pay for your spells or you draw lots of cards for little or no mana. [Maelstrom Archangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Archangel) has one of those two lines at the top of her curriculum vitae.

I have tried desperately for years to make a viable Sunburst / Domain deck in Extended using the vast array of effective mana-fixing tools such as [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder), experimenting with [Bringer of the Blue Dawn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bringer+of+the+Blue+Dawn), and so on. [Maelstrom Archangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Archangel) finally gives that kind of a strategy a real target. If you can make it to her, maybe you can just *make it* ([Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) notwithstanding).

Yes, you have to jank your deck up somewhat with expensive, otherwise-uncastable spells in order to break [Maelstrom Archangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Maelstrom+Archangel), but for our purposes, those are rich people problems. I can't imagine Erik and Randy looked at the *Urza's Legacy* spoiler and said "Man, there is going to be such a [Megrim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Megrim) deck!"

Will any of these three cards produce in the ways that I have suggested? Probably at least two of them. Guess which.

Speaking of the Fae, one of the big reasons for Storm's breakout was the popularity of Faerie Wizards, most assuredly *prey* for the Storm. Why is Storm so effective against the Fae?

Storm decks are notoriously difficult to beat with permission once they start going, and Faerie Wizards is a deck full of largely irrelevant permission. The most important defensive cards are [Trickbind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trickbind) or [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle), and especially sideboarded, Storm can set up the Wizards with [Gigadrowse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gigadrowse) to remove the permission option entirely.

LSV's deck is near perfect for another reason .... Check out those [Shattering Spree](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shattering+Spree)s! This is a deck that will not be locked down by [Chalice of the Void](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chalice+of+the+Void), even set to one. You can counter my spell, but *not* all my replicate copies!

The player, this time, was reigning U.S. National Champion Michael Jacob ... and he was positively dominant, first seed at the end of the Swiss.

For many intents and purposes, this deck is a [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) card advantage deck operating largely from the graveyard, similar to the one we discussed with Gavin Verhey some weeks ago. 

Michael's specific deck eschewed the [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud) control plan for a streamlined attack, incorporating the best threats: [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) and [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom). While the deck played many of the same spells—with the same numbers—as [Death Cloud](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Death+Cloud), this Rock had its own agenda, set apart by a full set of [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast)s main.

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/td/td24_darkblasts.jpg)  
These [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast)s do a number of things well. They suppress the fast [Spark Elemental](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spark+Elemental) draws out of the Burn deck. They demolish Elves one-for-one, forever. They help dig up [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam), operating as a kind of tutor crew (your graveyard is just more cards in hand in this deck), and set up [Raven's Crime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Raven%27s+Crime) and [Worm Harvest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Worm+Harvest). [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast) was the card Jacob wanted to build around, and he was extraordinarily successful with this execution.

If I wanted to play some kind of The Rock at this point, Jacob's deck is where I would start.

Want more? Here is a video on Jacob's Rock deck:

Successful as it was, the Jacob deck is subtly a great lens by which we can see the unchecked [Opportunity](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opportunity) that Storm had at Grand Prix–Los Angeles. Here is a deck that did very well, where Michael Jacob played a specific card specifically to kill a lot of Elves and keep them out of combo position. Is it "good against combo" then? Well, if the deck draws [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast) in time, it can keep the Elves. However, a deck like Storm really benefitted from the fact that cards were chosen to stop Elves rather than stopping combo in general.

Well, there you have them all: three from yesteryear feeding us ideas to help hone our deuce in the PTQs moving forward. I hope you enjoyed this!
