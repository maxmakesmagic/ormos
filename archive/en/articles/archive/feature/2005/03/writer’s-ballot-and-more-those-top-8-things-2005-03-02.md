### Red Deck Wins... in the Swiss, Anyway


Overall, Red Deck Wins remains the most represented deck across [the Top 8s](/en/events/coverage/pro-tour%E2%80%93philadelphia-qualifying-season-extended-top-8-decklists) I studied, including both domestic and international PTQs and [Grand Prix Eindhoven](/en/events/coverage/roux-rules-over-eindhoven). That said, Dan Paskins' favorite deck has zero first place finishes this week... While Red Deck Wins seems over the past several weeks to be your best bet to make Top 8, this week's runner up -- edging out Goblins -- seems like your best bet to actually win the PTQ.


Over the eight Top 8s I polled for this week, Mind's Desire fell shy of Red Deck Wins by a single Top 8 appearance. That said, of its seven spots, Mind's Desire took four blue envelopes -- more than half the Mind's Desire players got their hearts' desires!


Personally, I find Mind's Desire to be a very difficult deck to play. It is complicated and many times non-intuitive. Take a look at Nathan Zimmerman's deck from this week's High Point PTQ:

Mind's Desire has only 17 lands. Even with four copies of [Chrome Mox](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox), it needs a little more oomph to get going than your typical deck with 24 or so lands. Luckily, it plays cards like these:




|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Accumulated Knowledge | Brainstorm |

Since about 1997, players like Alan Comer have pioneered a less than obvious method of deck design that says that for every four cheap cantrips in your deck (cards that allow you to draw or dig for low mana, such as [Portent](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Portent), [Foreshadow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Foreshadow), [Impulse](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Impulse), or the [Brainstorm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brainstorm) and [Accumulated Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Accumulated+Knowledge) that we see here), your deck can be said to play like it has (virtually) two additional lands. The reason is that these cantrips essentially reduce the size of your deck so that you are basically counting 17 land in a 50 card deck rather than a 60 card deck. In the early game, you have to use your cantrips to make sure your mana is flowing, but once your deck gets going, it will draw a lower percentage of lands, and therefore, more good stuff.


![Mind's Desire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Mind%27s+Desire)Mind's Desire the deck takes its name from [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) the Storm bomb. The deck is built to generate a mana advantage using the Urza's Block untap mechanic in concert with enablers like [Sapphire Medallion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sapphire+Medallion), [Sunscape Familiar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sunscape+Familiar), or (in the black version) [Nightscape Familiar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nightscape+Familiar). Note that with a single Medallion out, the Mind's Desire player can play a [Cloud of Faeries](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloud+of+Faeries) or [Snap](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snap) for one mana, but untap two lands. Therefore he could tap two lands for his creature, floating ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif), spend ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif), and have ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif) left over. Done a couple of times, especially in concert with one another, hopefully finishing with a big [Turnabout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Turnabout), the Mind's Desire player can simultaneously ramp up his single turn mana and set up many spells for his Desire Storm count.
 Once the Mind's Desire player plays his namesake sorcery, the fun starts (or ends, depending on which side of the table you are on). The deck seeks to generate a critical mass of cards culminating in multiple [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire)s and/or [Brain Freeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze)s (some versions don't run [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) at all, focusing only on the latter Storm killer). Remember, the Storm counts of [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) into [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire) into [Brain Freeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze) are going to be cumulative, so [Brain Freeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze) can go lethal from even a small number of "actually" played spells.


Mind's Desire can win even if its [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire)s themselves fall victim to [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction) (remember, some versions don't run Desire at all). The deck merely needs to play two total Medallions or Familiars. At this point, [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) turns into a one mana spell. The Desire player will typically [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) for something to put [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) in his sideboard (probably something along the lines of a [Turnabout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Turnabout)), and then chain a second [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) for [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) between his hand and his sideboard for no reason other than to up the Storm count for one mana each; at the end of his mana, the Desire player will choose a sideboard [Brain Freeze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Brain+Freeze) and win with that powered up Storm spell.


### Blue Envelopes with Blue/Black


While Mind's Desire had a great week, the real story this time around is the return of Doctor Teeth. With six Top 8 finishes, Psychatog finished only slightly behind mighty Mind's Desire. The difference is that Psychatog took not only three first place finishes, but GP Eindhoven itself. In fact, these two decks made up 7/8 of the first place finishes for Top 8s I studied for this week.

All three of these Psychatog decks run 23 lands and 3 [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)s. Roux and Simoneaux run two creatures in addition, a [Wonder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wonder) and a more traditional control creature. Roux's [Masticore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Masticore) makes a lot of sense because [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) is the natural inheritor to Draw-Go, but Simoneaux's choice of [Meloku the Clouded Mirror](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meloku+the+Clouded+Mirror) of Victory, is a testament to that legendary creature's raw power (at PT Columbus, Team TOGIT ran [Morphling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Morphling) in the secondary finisher slot against [Cranial Extraction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction)). Good enough to finish out of [Aluren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aluren)'s sideboard, Simoneaux's Meloku can also set up additional damage for his [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) by picking up lands for Dr. Teeth to munch on.


In previous years, one of Psychatog's strengths was its combination of creature kill and true control elements. However, this week's three top finishers ran a total of 4 [Engineered Explosives](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Explosives), 2 [Engineered Plague](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Engineered+Plague)s, 4 [Smother](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother)s, and 2, um, [Boomerang](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boomerang)s in their main decks. To be fair, all three decks could use [Cunning Wish](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Wish) to grab [Diabolic Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Diabolic+Edict), [Ghastly Demise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghastly+Demise), [Smother](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smother), or [Rebuild](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rebuild) out of their sideboards if under creature pressure.


This selfsame drop in creature kill also affects how the Psychatog decks themselves are set up. Previously a card like [Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval) would be automatic (to defend against [Chainer's Edict](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chainer%27s+Edict), a card in the same block as [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) itself). At eight mana, [Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval) + [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) was almost always a kill (the Tog player would re-play one of his lifted lands post-[Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval) to cast the Tog); only decks with one drops would even be able to chump. At nine mana, a Psychatog deck could even represent a response card such as a one mana counter. This week (again among the winners) only Simoneaux played [Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval) main. The other two decks relied on attrition to power up their [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)s to lethal damage.


The creature [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) has been called the finest control creature of all time, better even than [Morphling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Morphling). At ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227195556im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/black.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif), [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) costs half as much (who really plays [Morphling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Morphling) without a mana open?), and unlike [Morphling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Morphling), [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) can defend itself without additional mana requirements. [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) has essentially the same ability as [Wild Mongrel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel)... but goes one better. While a [Wild Mongrel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Mongrel) can turn two cards into two additional power and toughness, a [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) will turn two cards into three! You can see how a card like [Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval), that not only annihilates the opponent's defensive position but also fills the Tog player's hand with ready-to-pitch ammunition, is a synergistic weapon that can give this archetype an almost-combo feel.


Similarly, the [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) can go one-for-one the entire game, relying on [Accumulated Knowledge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Accumulated+Knowledge), [Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction), and its low mana count to stay one step ahead of the opponent's threats. Remember, every time the ‘Tog player trades a [Counterspell](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterspell) for a threat, that card in his graveyard is going to contribute to half a point of power down the line. At some point, you get into too many trades and the [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog) goes lethal, [Upheaval](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Upheaval) or no.

### The Writer's Ballot


Here's how I cast my five votes:


*Osyp Lebedowicz*  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/ptven03/a757.jpg)Our criteria for the Writer's Ballot was not just on play skill and resume, but rather on their *combination* of play skill and personality. My top vote went to Osyp Lebedowicz, my commentary colleague, a PT and GP champion that has just come off yet another great GP Top 8. Magic has a great many great storytellers, but Osyp is the game's unquestioned number one fib machine. A blatant liar who has gotten in trouble on airplanes for his imaginary poker skills, a longtime (alleged!) latin dance chumpion who was finally called on it by the appearance of actual salsa instructor Pierre Canali on the PT scene, you never know when Osyp is helping you out or reeling you in, in person or in print. The only thing not in dispute regarding **Magic's** popular Joe Black is his professed love of -- believe it or not -- testing constructed. 


*Brian Kibler*  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/gpnj04/kibler.jpg)The recently absent tie-dyed favorite nevertheless has a GP Top 2 in the last several months. One of the game's most relentless deck designers, the fang faced former rassler is beloved of all seven girls who play **Magic** as well as any small child who has ever summoned a 5/5 or 6/6 flying beater, regardless of creature type. Let's hope the "Dragonmaster" returns to his rightful place in the Top 8 of premiere events, armed as usual with a mana base only a GAT advocate could love.


*Jeff Cunningham*  
 LOL


*Tim Aten*  
 I've known Tim Aten since he was knee high to a grasshopper. In fact, at a local tournament back in the summer of 1998, Tim defeated the mono-blue deck I had copied from Andrew Cuneo by summoning a Scragnoth. Sure, I had such cards as the equally large [Steel Golem](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+Golem) and the [Scragnoth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scragnoth) removal capable [Nevinyrral's Disk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nevinyrral%27s+Disk) in my stack, but Tim dispatched me with green's most expensive hate card anyway. In the subsequent seven years, Tim has become no less hateful, though these days, most of his ire seems depressingly, humorously, self-directed. Quite possibly today's top Limited commentator, Tim has an active following. 


*Antonino De Rosa*  
![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/gporl04/fm1_2.jpg)Ask any ten kids (who look like they study Internet **Magic** sites or perhaps deck history archives anyway) who invented Deep Dog, and eight out of ten will tell you "Jeff Cunningham." But the other two will definitely say "Antonio." That point is arguable, especially if you are from Britain, but what isn't is that this guy's name isn't Antonio. Antonino has had regular success, especially on the GP circuit, but unlike most pros at his level shares his successes and experiments with his readership on a regular basis. He has long been considered one of U/G Madness' most successful advocates, and is one of the minds behind the currently successful Vial Goblins deck.
