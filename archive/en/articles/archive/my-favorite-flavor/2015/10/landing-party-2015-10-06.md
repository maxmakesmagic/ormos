Landing Party
=============



 Posted in [My Favorite Flavor](/en/content/my-favorite-flavor-archive)
 on October 6, 2015 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/Chas_Andres.jpg)
By Chas Andres




 Chas Andres is a freelance writer and MFA student living in Wilmington, North Carolina. When he's not at his keyboard dreaming up stories, you can find him playing with his cats, listening to records, or building yet another Magic deck. 





The battle between the Zendikari and the Eldrazi might grab all the headlines these days, but I don't want us to forget what made Zendikar exciting in the first place.


Before the titans woke up, Zendikar was an adventure world. Daredevils sought their fortunes atop harrowing peaks and hacked their way through thick jungles in search of hidden treasures. It was a wild place where mana worked in unpredictable ways and the land itself fought to keep its secrets hidden.


One of my favorite parts of playing *Magic* during the first *Zendikar* block was adjusting my strategy depending on what lands I had to traverse that round. Would I need my [Trailblazer's Boots](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trailblazer%27s+Boots), or should I bring a [Grappling Hook](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grappling+Hook) and some [Adventuring Gear](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Adventuring+Gear)? Should I hire a [Frontier Guide](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Frontier+Guide), or could I get away with just a [Nimana Sell-Sword](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nimana+Sell-Sword)? Would I need to hole up in the [Sejiri Refuge](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sejiri+Refuge)? Would our party have to traverse the [Teetering Peaks](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Teetering+Peaks) on our way to Valakut?


The Eldrazi have tried their best to suck the life out of Zendikar, but the land never stopped fighting back. And since it's Land Week here on DailyMTG.com, I wanted to highlight a couple of different ways to celebrate Zendikar's resilient and beautiful terrain when deck building.


The Fires of Akoum
==================


Zendikar has five continents, each of which has a distinctive look and feel. Ondu, for example, is filled with vertical cliffs, deep trenches, and raging rivers. [Murasa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murasa) has a vast forest that's walled by sharp mountains and pillar plains. [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) is actively volcanic with shifting patches of lush vegetation.


If you're up for a real challenge, try to build a deck designed to highlight each of Zendikar's continents. It's harder than it looks, I warn you. One of the things that makes Zendikar so special is the diversity of each region. Unlike some of *Magic*'s other planes, where the majority of the forests are on one continent and another is all swampland, each of Zendikar's continents is packed with exciting terrain. [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) may be volcanic, but the most iconic volcano on Zendikar—[Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle)—is actually on the island of Beyeen in Ondu. [Murasa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murasa) also has a lot of mountains, including the Skyfang range that's home to Shatterskull Pass and its powerful minotaurs. It can be hard to figure out exactly where each basic land is supposed to depict and where each creature makes its home, but I can't think of a better way to completely immerse yourself in the flavor of Zendikar.


To show you what I'm talking about, let's take a quick walk through the [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) deck I made. The continent of [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) is mostly aligned with red mana, but all sorts of creatures call it home. [Anowon, the Ruin Sage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Anowon%2C+the+Ruin+Sage)'s encampment was just below the Teeth of [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum), and Bruse Tarl's traveling caravan "city" of Goma Fada is in the region as well. The [Ior Ruin Expedition](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ior+Ruin+Expedition) took place on [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum), as did the [Khalni Heart Expedition](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Khalni+Heart+Expedition). I want to make sure that the deck is playable, though, and the best way to do that is by focusing on red. We may not have Valakut at our disposal, but we've got the next best thing:


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Akoum+Hellkite)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum+Hellkite)
What is the flavor rationale behind [Akoum Hellkite](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum+Hellkite)'s rules text? Well, I picture the dragon using the additional mountains as a way to extend its hunting grounds, picking off small creatures as distracting fissures open up and new mountains burst into being.


Keeping the deck mono-red also allows us to maximize [Molten Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molten+Ravager), another denizen of [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum), whose power level is directly tied to the land it inhabits. Ravager gets bonus points for having perfectly on-theme flavor text, too.


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Molten+Ravager)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Molten+Ravager)
[Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum)'s landscape used to be dominated by the tower of Tal Terig, which is where the Eldrazi titans were imprisoned before the events of *Rise of the Eldrazi*. I wanted to make sure that my [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) deck could show off what Tal Terig used to look like, and it's conveniently front-and-center on one of my favorite cards, [Burst Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning).


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Burst+Lightning)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Burst+Lightning)
[Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) is the most inhospitable of Zendikar's continents, so many of the best [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum)-related creatures and spells evoke the idea of the land itself crushing its enemies—through hidden pockets of magma, rocks hurled through the sky, or stones woken from their slumber and animated for battle:


![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/images/daily/MFF20151006_ZEN_Red-Cards.png)


The creatures of [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) tend to be hearty and lava-born, either able to thrive in the fiery landscape or small enough to dodge the continuous barrage of rocks and pillars of molten lava. There are non-red creatures on [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum), but it's clear who has the advantage:


![](https://media.wizards.com/2015/images/daily/MFF20151006_ZEN_Other-Red-Cards.png)


I don't want to run a bunch of nonbasic lands in our [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) deck since it's mono-red, but I'd still like to highlight some of the continent's more interesting terrain:


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Akoum+Refuge)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum+Refuge) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Cinder+Glade)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cinder+Glade) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Smoldering+Spires)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Smoldering+Spires)
As for basic lands, I really love Noah Bradley's lava-tinged Mountain from *Battle for Zendikar*—that's [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) all over. Véronique Meignaud's whirlpool Island depicts the coast of [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) as well, but I'm only going to run one copy of that since we don't have any blue in our deck.


It's unclear to me whether Meignaud's portal Mountain is supposed to be [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum) or the Skyfang Mountains of [Murasa](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Murasa). I suspect the latter, so I'm going to leave it on the sidelines. If you want to call it part of [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum), though, I wouldn't blame you.

Awakening Zone
==============


Awaken is my favorite new mechanic in *Battle for Zendikar*. I love the idea of casting spells that will turn my most loyal lands into lumbering Elementals. My first thought upon seeing the spoiler was to see if there was a way to fit all the Awaken cards into a single deck . . . but is that actually possible?


Well, *Battle for Zendikar* has fourteen cards with Awaken—four in white, four in blue, three in black, one in red, one in green, and one in blue/white. Based on the artwork and colors involved, it appears that Ondu and the under-inhabited polar continent of Sejiri are experiencing most of the awakening. Instead of spreading things too thin, let's focus our deck on blue and white with a secondary focus on black.


The card that immediately caught my eye was [Part the Waterveil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Part+the+Waterveil). If you played back in the first *Zendikar* block, you might have caught the reference:


[![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Part+the+Waterveil)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Part+the+Waterveil) [![](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Magosi%2C+the+Waterveil)](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Magosi%2C+the+Waterveil)
I've always loved Magosi because it leads to so many fun storytelling opportunities. Usually, I'll play Magosi as early in a game as possible so I can take a turn off and go check out the falls. This involves me turning on the tap in the kitchen, staring at in awe for a few seconds, and returning with a giant glass of water and a couple of snobby vacation anecdotes about how water tastes SOOO much better at Magosi than it does back here in the US. Later in the game, I like to take out my phone, start playing TLC's classic pop song "Waterfalls," and use my extra turn to try and kill everyone.


[Part the Waterveil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Part+the+Waterveil) is even better. It allows us to visit Magosi without having to skip a turn, and if you happen to have Magosi in play, you can awaken it and kill your opponent with your animated waterfall over the next couple of turns. Time to dust off the TLC—a 6/6 Magosi is a compelling argument to stick to the rivers and the lakes that you're used to.


The biggest problem with the awaken cards, of course, is that most of them cost a lot of mana to play. [Part the Waterveil](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Part+the+Waterveil) costs nine mana if you want to wake up a land, and [Planar Outburst](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Planar+Outburst) at eight isn't much better. How are you going to survive that long without any creatures?


The answer, I think, lies in *Worldwake*'s Zendikons. [Wind Zendikon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wind+Zendikon) and [Guardian Zendikon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Guardian+Zendikon) are both welcome additions to the deck, and I'm going to play with four of each. We'll also want to take advantage of the cycle of lands that can come to life and help themselves even without an awaken spell. [Celestial Colonnade](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade), [Creeping Tar Pit](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Creeping+Tar+Pit), and *Battle for Zendikar*'s awesome new [Shambling Vent](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shambling+Vent) are auto-includes for me.


Here's my first stab at a list:

You may want to consider adding a few more lands to this deck—22 is land-light for a deck that wants to attack and block with its mana producers, and it's possible that the correct number is closer to 27 or 28. It might also be worth adding green-mana ramp spells to the deck in order to increase its velocity. I like the purity of sticking to the primary awaken colors here, though, so that's why I chose this path.


Which basic lands does this deck want? That depends on what landscapes you want to bring to life! I've always been a fan of Vincent Proce's bowl Island, and I love the idea of turning Lorthos's fishbowl into a giant Elemental that can come to life and smash face. Adam Paquette's *BFZ* Swamp #262 looks menacing as well—lots of sharp, floating rocks and gnarled trees that can be re-formed into muscles and teeth there. I like Noah Bradley's Plains for almost the opposite reason—there's no telling what lies just beyond that outcropping on the hazy horizon, and I can imagine all manner of Elementals coming to life just beyond the edge of the frame.


Whether you're exploring the fires of [Akoum](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akoum), bringing Ondu to life, or just gawping at Noah Bradley's latest masterpiece, it's difficult to play a game of *Battle for Zendikar* where the lands aren't front and center—Zendikar's terrain is just that rich and varied. I'm so glad that we've been given the chance to grab our [Expedition Map](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Expedition+Map)s and go thrashing across the dizzying landscape yet again.


Until next time,
