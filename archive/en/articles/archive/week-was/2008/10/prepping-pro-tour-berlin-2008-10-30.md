Prepping for Pro Tour-Berlin
============================



 Posted in **The Week That Was**
 on October 31, 2008 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_BillStark.jpg)
By Bill Stark











Brian David-Marshall was en route to Pro Tour–Berlin over his usual deadline, so coverage reporter Bill Stark stepped in to give you an overview of some of the sights—and decks—you'll see this weekend. Enjoy, and be sure to check out the event coverage throughout the weekend here on **magicthegathering.com**  
 –Kelly Digges,**magicthegathering.com** editor

![](https://media.wizards.com/legacy//mtg/images/daily/twtw/twtw10_icon.jpg)Berlin. For a continent as old as Europe, it is rare for a city to have so much history above and beyond many of those around it. And yet, Berlin. What more can be said? The city virtually teems with historical significance, filled with museums, landmarks, and right down the middle of it all a memorial line that serves as a silent reminder of the wall that once defined the place. It makes sense, then, that the Pro Tour held within its confines would feature the Extended format.  
 After all, no other premier level format offers the same peek back through **Magic**'s history. With the Pro Tour just hours away from starting, players are busy shuffling their *Onslaught* sac lands, their duals from the *Ravnica* block. But, like Berlin, today's Extended is impacted by modern history as much as the true past. A host of cards from the past few sets, most notably *Shards of Alara*, are promising to re-shape even in this late hour how Extended will play out. Before we look at decks, however, it's important to examine two major changes that have happened to Extended since the last time we saw it.

The Changes
First and foremost, the biggest change since the last time we saw the Extended format is the rotation. As a result of DCI policy changes, Extended now rotates yearly just like Standard. It includes the seven most recent blocks of cards, signified by a large-set "standalone" like *Shards of Alara* and two smaller mini-expansions, like *Fifth Dawn* and *Darksteel*, and the three most recent core sets. That means the format for Berlin includes everything from *Onslaught* forward as well as *Eighth*, *Ninth*, and *Tenth**Editions*. 

It also means some previous favorites have rotated, shaking up the top decks of the format. Blue-green Madness? Gone. [Cabal Coffers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Coffers) and [Psychatog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Psychatog)? Gone. [Fact or Fiction](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fact+or+Fiction)? Gone. Gaea's Might, [Orim's Chant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Orim%27s+Chant), [Cabal Therapy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cabal+Therapy), [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed), [Meddling Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Meddling+Mage)? Gone, gone, gone, all gone. A multitude of cards that were critical foundations of last season's top decks will ride the pine in Berlin, paving the way for a whole host of new cards, decks, and archetypes to shine.

Of course, the second major shift involved the biggest breakout card of last Extended season: [Sensei's Divining Top](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top). 

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sensei%27s+Divining+Top)Earlier this year the DCI made the decision to ban the card from the format, effectively neutering the [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance) decks (known as some form of "Next Level Blue") that were all the rage during the last Extended season. Does that mean some brave souls won't try to combine the power of [Counterbalance](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Counterbalance), [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf), sac lands, and [Vedalken Shackles](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vedalken+Shackles) in order to re-live the glory days of the deck? We'll know in a few hours, but suffice to say the lack of the Divining Top will significantly alter the landscape of the format.

The Decks
Rumors abound that the new Extended is aggressive but fair. Decks can kill as early as the third turn, but the disruption available to players to combat such shenanigans is numerous and varied. On top of it all, *Shards of Alara* waits ready in the wings, adding a few hundred brand new cards to the format that haven't been tested and whose merits are truly unknown.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Master+of+Etherium)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium)One of the decks that benefits most from the new gold set is a tried and true favorite: Affinity. The newly minted [Master of Etherium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium) is an absolute monster in the deck, [Crusading](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Crusading) the team while serving as a hefty beater itself. Thanks to *Mirrodin*'s artifact lands and the fast mana starts revolving around [Ornithopter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter) and [Springleaf Drum](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Springleaf+Drum), the Master hits the table quickly and must be dealt with as soon as it does so. Also adding to the Affinity deck's status are [Salvage Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Salvage+Titan) and [Soul's Fire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soul%27s+Fire). The Fire adds a [Fling](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fling)-like affect to the deck allowing players to "go large" with an [Arcbound Ravager](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager), [Atog](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog), [Master of Etherium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium), or [Cranial Plated](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plated) anything and punch through for the final points of damage outside the combat step. You know things are going well for Affinity when their preferred burn spell isn't even [Shrapnel Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast). The [Salvage Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Salvage+Titan), which may or may not see as much play as [Master of Etherium](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Master+of+Etherium), is very easy to play, is a very large monster that can hit as early as the first turn, and provides a repetitive threat in the late game. All in all, Affinity's stock is definitely up. Gerry Thompson, Grand Prix-Denver champion and Starcitygames.com columnist, offered up this version of the deck just a few weeks ago.

As much as the robots have gained, however, there are still some powerful hate spells keeping it in check. [Kataki, War's Wage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kataki%2C+War%27s+Wage), and [Ancient Grudge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancient+Grudge) are both waiting in the wings, as they have in many seasons past, to keep the artifact monsters contained. One deck set up particularly well to utilize such threats out of the sideboard? Zoo, which itself managed to pick up some big hits since the last time we saw it. *Shards of Alara* gives the deck the "best [Rogue Elephant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rogue+Elephant) ever" in the form of [Wild Nacatl](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wild+Nacatl), and a scare-free [Phyrexian Negator](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Phyrexian+Negator) by way of [Woolly Thoctar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Woolly+Thoctar). With a combination of *Ravnica* dual lands and *Onslaught* sac lands the Zoo deck's manabase is very solid, letting it play a 3/3 Nacatl early and a 5/4 Thoctar not long behind. Getting [Figure of Destiny](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Figure+of+Destiny) as an early game threat and a late game bomb doesn't hurt either, and rumors abound of Antoine Ruel's Invitational card, [Ranger of Eos](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ranger+of+Eos), hitting Zoo sideboards across the land as mirror tech (imagine paying four mana for a 3/2 that tutors up *two* 3/3s, or a 3/3 and a 1/1 that becomes an 8/8 super monster!). Pro Tour-LA finalist Billy Moreno came up with this list for an article on Starcitygames.com.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ad+Nauseam)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam)Also gaining a lot of steam from the last season are variations on Storm. Proving itself as a solid combo mechanic, many players thought the Storm decks of years past would surely be gone from the format with the passing of the *Invasion* era sac lands, whose comes into play downside quickly turned into upside as they could be sacrificed for a quick boost of two mana instead of one. That's not the case, and again is in part because of new cards from *Shards of Alara*. In particular, [Ad Nauseam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ad+Nauseam) has created a lot of buzz providing a deck already packed full of cheap rituals, [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom)s, and other spells a hefty amount of card drawing to fuel the critical turn (just make sure you don't flip too many [Mind's Desire](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mind%27s+Desire)s!). [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) and [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose) combo alongside [Rite of Flame](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rite+of+Flame), [Seething Song](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Seething+Song), and [Desperate Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Desperate+Ritual) to make sure critical mass can be achieved fast enough, and a large [Tendrils of Agony](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tendrils+of+Agony) ends the game when sent to the dome. Still, [Gaddock Teeg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gaddock+Teeg), [Thoughtseize](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtseize), and [Stifle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stifle) force Storm players to work as hard as ever to get the job done. The big question is what the rest of the format will look like. Will decks from the game's history find a way to resurrect themselves? Can [Chameleon Colossus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chameleon+Colossus) provide a big enough boost to Rock decks to make up for the loss of [Pernicious Deed](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pernicious+Deed)? Will something we've seen before reappear in a new form? Perhaps [Tooth and Nail](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) will be re-imagined by a Japanese contingent led by Shuhei Nakamura. Or perhaps the French Ruel brothers have managed to find a way to make the Urzatron viable once more, with new hits like [Scourglass](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scourglass). We will know very soon, but one thing is certain: this Extended is shaping up to be one of the most exciting, highly anticipated formats of all time.
