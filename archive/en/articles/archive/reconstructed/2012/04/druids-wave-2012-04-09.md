The Druid's Wave
================



 Posted in **Reconstructed**
 on April 10, 2012 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorPic_GavinVerhey.jpg)
By Gavin Verhey




 When Gavin Verhey was eleven, he dreamt of a job making Magic cards—and now as a Magic designer, he's living his dream! Gavin has been writing about Magic since 2005. 






How much mana does a card that costs three have to generate to be playable?

  
One mana? Two mana? *Four mana?!?*

Today's preview card answers that question by offering an incredible surge in mana production for only ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20211201201921im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/colorless1.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20220123123600im_/https://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/green.gif)—provided you're willing to jump through some hoops to get there.

Let's see what spices this particular chef is cooking with today.

Introducing Druid's Repository:

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/tcg/products/avr/m5qui7g053_en.jpg)  
![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/rc/rc190_dru.jpg)Druid's Repository | Art by [Daarken](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;artist=+%5B%22Daarken%22%5D)

Let's establish what this card is good for and where this card fits. Druid's Repository is good in decks where you can meet the intersection of wanting to attack a lot *and* having a use for a lot of mana. Druid's Repository is *not* good in some of these decks:


> * Ramp decks that don't play many creatures. Although ramp decks do want a lot of mana, without the creatures to put counters on the Repository it's not going to be as efficient as other three-mana cards.
> * Heavy creature decks that don't play many mana sinks. If you're going to be attacking a lot but don't have any use for six extra mana at some point in the game, Druid's Repository is just wasting a card.
> * Decks where the Repository is going to be "win more." If Repository is only good when you're already winning—such as when you're able to attack with several creatures—then it's not going to be that effective.
> 

Fortunately, Standard is full of great interactions with the Repository. Lands like [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) and [Gavony Township](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township) can be activated after attacking, but before damage, and the Repository lets the attack pay for the activation. There are expensive flashback costs to be paid, such as the one on [Increasing Devotion](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Increasing+Devotion)—which I might add works mighty well with the Repository.

To top it all off, *Avacyn Restored* brings even more to the table. There are a ton of green goodies in this set, including one jaw-dropping ten mana mythic rare that you're *really* going to want to accelerate into... 

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/rc/rc190_gav.jpg)[Gavony Township](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township) | Art by [Peter Mohrbacher](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;artist=+%5B%22Peter+Mohrbacher%22%5D)

But for now, let's focus on what I won't be fired for talking about. Last week, I asked you to submit a green deck with a mix of cheap and expensive cards. I looked through all of them and found the list that could support the Repository best. Remember, I was looking for decks that best met the intersection of wanting to attack and using a lot of mana.

After carefully looking over each of them and making agonizing decisions, I came to one final decklist. Congratulations to Alex Aust!

Let's break down what this deck does, shall we?

Strengths
This deck has two strong main plans:

**1. Quickly amass an army of Elves, and then attack with them.** 

If your opponent isn't dead, repeat next turn as necessary.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/rc/rc190_play1.png)  
**2. [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) for (roughly) 42.**

Putting nearly your entire deck onto the battlefield is a completely unreasonable thing that this deck makes completely reasonable to do.

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/rc/rc190_play2.png)  
What we have here is definitely an aggressive deck with a combo plan built in. If this deck is undisturbed, it should fairly easily (and with Ezuri, quite literally) overrun the opponent.

Unfortunately, save for the infamously kind Mr. Bye, most opponents seem to enjoy disturbing your chances of winning. It's important we also identify weaknesses so the opposition has a harder time stopping us.

Weaknesses
Some of the elements that might hinder this deck are:

**1. Removal**

If the opponent has a collection of removal spells, especially mass removal spells like [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment), this deck's engine can fall flat. We want to ensure the deck is as resilient as we can make it to removal.

**2. Inconsistency**

Sometimes hands from decks like this make winning as easy as delicious chocolate cake. Other times, your hands are so clunky and disgusting that it's a little more like fish fingers and custard. It's great when you open on the [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) into [Elvish Archdruid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid) draw, but what about all of the times your hands are full of four three-drops and no accelerators? We're going to want to keep our cards cheap and powerful.

Card Breakdown
Unlike last week, where I featured more of a midrange control deck that relied on forced interactions and individual card power, this week we're looking at a much more linear-based strategy. (Linear meaning a deck build behind one easily identifiable theme, such as a creature type.) Some of these cards simply cannot afford to be taken out of the deck. I'll point those ones out as we go along, as well as explaining why they are so crucial.

**[Bellowing Tanglewurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bellowing+Tanglewurm)**

This Wurm can help push through all of your Elves. If your opponent is playing a bunch of small blockers—like, say, tokens—[Bellowing Tanglewurm](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bellowing+Tanglewurm) gets all of your Elves out of the way. Ezuri already accomplishes a similar goal, though—and at five mana, it's not clear this Tanglewurm is really needed.

**[Copperhorn Scout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Copperhorn+Scout)**

While this Scout doesn't look like much, it does a lot of subtle work in this deck. It untaps your [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) and [Elvish Archdruid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid)s, allowing you to attack, put counters on Repository, and still get the mana boost post-combat—or even inside of combat to activate Ezuri while you're attacking! As an Elf that fuels your strategy, it's crucial to keep around.

**[Elvish Archdruid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid)**

Pop quiz! How many Elves are in Standard right now?

The answer is probably less than you think. With *Innistrad* block trading in pointy ears for pointy hats, there are only [fifteen Elves in Standard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;format=+%5Bamp;quot;Standardamp;quot;%5Damp;subtype=+%5Bamp;quot;Elfamp;quot;%5D). To make matters more complex, only a handful of them are playable.

However, even with that restriction, the Archdruid is too good for this deck to not play. It helps your beatdown plan and can accelerate your mana in absurd ways. This is a cornerstone of the deck that will make it from start to finish.

**[Ezuri, Renegade Leader](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ezuri%2C+Renegade+Leader)**

Although the dearth of Elves once again hurts Ezuri, like the Archdruid he is far too strong to not play. Making sure your creatures stay alive *and* serving as a perpetual [Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun) engine is exactly what this deck is looking for.

In fact, even though he's a legend, I'm willing to play four copies. You're probably winning if he's in play, and he is so integral to your game plan that you always want to draw him. You will occasionally get hands with three Ezuri and be sad about it, but the games he wins for you should make up for that quite handsomely.

**[Glistener Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glistener+Elf)**

On one hand, this deck really needs Elves. On the other, the poison subtheme is a bit problematic. It's strong when you're activating Ezuri's overrun, but otherwise it doesn't add a lot to the deck. Too often, [Glistener Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glistener+Elf) is going to be a one-mana Elf with no abilities. There are enough options in Standard that I would like to remove the poison angle from this deck, even though that does mean cutting a set of Elves.

**[Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves)**

Mana production? Check. One-drop? Check. Elf? Check. It might not be [Storm Crow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Storm+Crow), but [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) is completely perfect for this deck.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Viridian+Corrupter)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Corrupter)  
**[Viridian Corrupter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Corrupter)**

I'd like to remove the poison theme from this deck, but that doesn't inherently mean the Corrupters can't stay. Removing artifacts can be important in this metagame. However, it seems most likely I will end up sideboarding these guys and bringing them in when appropriate. It's going to be [Gray Ogre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gray+Ogre) with infect too often.

**[Viridian Emissary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Emissary)**

Ideally, I would love to have another one-drop mana-accelerating Elf here, or a strong aggressive two-drop Elf, but with the lack of Elves in Standard I am fine settling for this guy if we have to. It's a 2/1 for two that promotes attacking for your Repository and enhances the Elf theme, so it does work alright.

The Spells
I'm lumping the spells all together here for one very important reason: they need to be minimized as much as possible. If we're going down the [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) path—which is something this deck wants to do—then ensuring we hit as many permanents as possible is crucial. I want to cut every other instant or sorcery from the deck that isn't [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave).

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/rc/rc190_gen.jpg)[Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) | Art by [James Paick](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Search/Default.aspx?output=spoileramp;method=visualamp;action=advancedamp;artist=+%5B%22James+Paick%22%5D)

If I was going to play spells I would look into [Lead the Stampede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lead+the+Stampede) or [Green Sun's Zenith](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Green+Sun%27s+Zenith), but we're riding the Wave instead. It's time to cut your spells and thrust your hands into the air every time the Wave comes around.

New Prospects
Even with the shortage of Elves, there are still several cards worth considering for this deck. Let's take a look over our options, keeping our weaknesses in mind.

**[Avacyn's Pilgrim](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?multiverseid=243212) and [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise)**

Elf? No. One-mana accelerator? Yes. This deck is in desperate need of more one-mana accelerants, and, despite not being Elves, these two cards fit perfectly. This deck definitely wants four copies of one of these cards, and potentially a couple of the other.

Which one is better? It depends where we end up. If I'm playing Equipment like the Swords, I almost certainly want the evasion of Birds. Birds also is better at casting [Predator Ooze](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Predator+Ooze) on turn two, and good with Repository since it can squawk in for 0 and add a counter each turn. Pilgrim, on the other hand, has a point of power and can also fix your mana if the deck splashes white. Birds is likely the better candidate, but if we look into a white splash Pilgrim will be the way to go.

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=243212)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=243212)  
**[Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod)**

There is certainly a [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) bent that this deck can take. You can play some good low-curve targets, use it mostly to find [Elvish Archdruid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid), and then slowly assemble the best creatures in your deck via [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod).

Unfortunately, in a deck that wants to be as aggressive as this one, I don't want to be taking time simply trading out creatures via [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod), nor do I want to add a bunch of one-of targets for [Birthing Pod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birthing+Pod) that will make my deck have less consistent draws.

**[Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)**

Hexproof, cheap, and monumentally large after a [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave), [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder) is a threat many decks will have trouble dealing with. In a deck like this, which uses so much creature acceleration instead of actual land ramp, its power does decrease—but then again, even a 3/3 or 4/4 hexproof creature is still plenty powerful.

It is worth noting that [Dungrove Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dungrove+Elder)*does* have the hidden drawback of preventing you from playing other colors, so it's not a good choice if we identify other color options to try.

**[Garruk, Primal Hunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk%2C+Primal+Hunter)**

This is certainly a five-mana card I can get behind! A permanent that [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) can put into play, a constant stream of creatures, and card advantage in a pinch, this Garruk is brutally powerful. I want to make sure I have plenty of low-mana-cost cards, but I will want a couple expensive cards to put at the top of my curve and I would prefer them to be card advantage like [Garruk, Primal Hunter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Garruk%2C+Primal+Hunter) instead of random creatures like [Vorapede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vorapede).

Other Colors
It's definitely worth looking into other colors with this deck. Birds and Pilgrim both help fix your mana early, and Repository provides you with mana of any color. If you want to play another color, it's not difficult to make that happen.

The problem is that there just aren't that many creatures or spells that are good to splash here. I don't want to splash for any removal spells because [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) asks for all permanents. [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) was the one good permanent-based removal option, but I didn't want to be clogged up on reactive cards. I hit thirty-five nonland cards I wanted to play pretty easily, which made another color look worse and worse.

The most enticing cards, in fact, aren't even creatures or spells at all! [Gavony Township](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gavony+Township) and [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run) both play well with this deck and don't eat up spell slots. With Birds, Repository, and Emissary around, it's not too hard to play one or two copies of these lands.

The problematic theme with all of these cards? They're all spells!

If you're going to sideboard out [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) in a matchup, boarding in spells is a fine idea. However, if that's not the case, you can't afford to put any more spells in.

[Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) wants to go out against the blue Delver decks and control decks because they might keep in some countermagic. But against beatdown, midrange, and ramp, you definitely want to keep in [Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) to overwhelm them and provide card advantage.

Against this deck, you want to attack fast and furiously. [Corrosive Gale](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corrosive+Gale) makes it difficult for them to tempo you out with fliers, and you remove some of your clunky top end.

[Genesis Wave](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Genesis+Wave) is pretty poor in the first game because of their [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s, but they will likely sideboard out their [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak)s for Game 2. You don't want a hand full of them, but a properly placed Wave in the midgame should dominate the Delver opponent.

Between [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment), [Curse of Death's Hold](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Curse+of+Death%27s+Hold), and a variety of pinpoint removal, this matchup is tough. They also have [Ratchet Bomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ratchet+Bomb) after sideboarding, although hopefully [Corrupt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Corrupt)er helps some there.

While you *could* put [Acidic Slime](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Acidic+Slime) into your sideboard to try and fight the Curse, or bring in [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring), it's very difficult to beat the Curse if it sweeps you in the first place. Your best route is just to overwhelm them quickly with a bunch of creatures and Archdruid to survive the Curse and Ezuri to survive [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment). You're going to need [Lead the Stampede](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lead+the+Stampede) for card advantage as they pick apart your team. It's a difficult matchup, but winnable.
