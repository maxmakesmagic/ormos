2004 World Championships Feature Match
======================================



 Posted in **Event Coverage**
 on September 4, 2004 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_aaronforsythe.jpg)
By Aaron Forsythe












![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/worlds04/fm21_1.jpg)


After 20 rounds of battling, Japan and the Netherlands found themselves in a tie for third place with two rounds to go. A long look at the standings and some simple math showed that each team could make the finals by winning both remaining rounds. To do so, they'd have to get by each other.


### The Draft


The Netherlands, made up of Pro Tour fixture Jeroen Remie (sporting a mesh baseball hat with "Heroin" written on it graffiti-style -- a play on the pronunciation of his own name), young Worlds Top 8'er Julien Nuijten, and Rogier Maaten went into the draft with a distinct plan. Maaten, in the A seat, would be black-blue, Remie in the middle would be white-red equipment, and Nuijten, in seat C, would be green with an eye toward sunburst.


The Japanese had a plan as well -- Shuuhei Nakamura would be the potential five-color-green drafter in seat A, National Champion Tsuyoshi Fujita would be red-black in the middle, and Kenji Tsumura would be white in seat C. Note that each team has a very different strategy, which is abnormal for this format. Usually by the time Worlds rolls around, team draft is mostly "solved," and the A, B, and C seat strategies are generally the same from team to team.


### *Mirrodin*: Japan Out-Opens


The second pack of the draft gave the Dutch a sweet, sweet [Loxodon Warhammer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Warhammer), but it gave Japan a [Betrayal of Flesh](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Betrayal+of+Flesh), a [Spikeshot Goblin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spikeshot+Goblin), and a [Skyhunter Cub](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skyhunter+Cub). The next pack was even worse for the Dutch, as Japan got identical picks: Nakamura took a second Betrayal, Fujita a second Spikeshot, and Tsumura a [Viridian Longbow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Longbow) to give to his Cub. Two packs later, Tsumura got a second Longbow as well.


After *Mirrodin*, Japan was way up on card quality.


### *Darksteel*: One Flub and Lots of Equipment


In the first pack of *Darksteel*, Japan made their first mistake. Fujita took an [Essence Drain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Essence+Drain) for his red-black deck over an [Echoing Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Ruin), leaving the Ruin for Nakamura. Nakamura had no red cards to this point but a couple potent black ones (the Betrayals), and the Ruin would have put him into color number four. Had they swapped picks, both cards would have been played, but the Ruin ended up in the sideboard.



![Echoing Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Echoing+Ruin)

Fujita claimed that he knew Remie's white-red deck would have lots of 3/3's he'd need to kill, citing [Loxodon Mystic](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Mystic) specifically, and that he had no equipment to worry about yet, so he wanted the creature-removal spell. He also said he anticipated Nakamura having no trouble playing [Echoing Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Ruin), but that turned out to not be the case.


It hardly mattered for Japan, however as they continued opening the hits. Fujita, looking for ways to pump his Spikeshots, took [Vulshok Morningstar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vulshok+Morningstar) over [Barbed Lightning](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Barbed+Lightning), which ended up going to Remie. That Morningstar paled to the equipment that Nakamura was accumulating, however: he picked up a [Leonin Bola](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Bola), a [Sword of Fire and Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice), and a [Skullclamp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp). All he needed know were some creatures.


The Dutch picked up a few decent cards -- [Razor Golem](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Razor+Golem), [Hoverguard Observer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Hoverguard+Observer), a Bola of their own -- but were still way behind in quality.


### *Fifth Dawn*: Fujita's Big Blunder


In pack three of *Fifth Dawn*, Fujita was presented with the choice of [Devour in Shadow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devour+in+Shadow) and [Beacon of Destruction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beacon+of+Destruction). If he chose the black card, the red one would go to his opponent, Remie. If he chose the red one, the black one would go to Maaten. All of this, of course, wouldn't matter if Tsumura counterdrafted the other with his pick.


I thought the Beacon was a no-brainer. It is a lot of damage, can hit players, and gives you the slim hope of being used more than once per game. [Devour in Shadow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Devour+in+Shadow) can kill anything, but it's painful. He chose the black spell, and Tsumura took an Auriok Squire. Remie looked around in disbelief as he drafted the Beacon. His deck now had two ways to kill [Spikeshot Goblin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spikeshot+Goblin), and both of them were opened by the Japanese team.


Both teams fared well in the other packs. There were plenty of [Trinket Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage)s to go around, and the Dutch got both of the [Vulshok Sorcerer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vulshok+Sorcerer)s that were opened.

### Days Eaten, Games Lost: Maaten vs. Nakamura


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/worlds04/fm21_2.jpg)Maaten needed a miracle to win, and the beginnings of that miracle showed up in his opening hand -- he came out quick with [Grimclaw Bats](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grimclaw+Bats), [Neurok Spy](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Neurok+Spy), and [Leonin Bola](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Bola). Nakamura was calm and kept playing out his mediocre creatures and attacking back. The Dutchman was activating his Bats generously on offense, dropping both players' life totals quickly. But, as anticipated, Nakamura had the devastating [Betrayal of Flesh](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Betrayal+of+Flesh) for Maaten's key blocker, and pulled the game out.


Maaten dug in the sideboard for the [Eater of Days](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eater+of+Days) and a Dream Plan -- a decision he'd regret. Again he started out great -- [Grimclaw Bats](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grimclaw+Bats), [Talon of Pain](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Talon+of+Pain), and [Trinket Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) for his Bola. The Talon took down Nakamura's [Thought Courier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thought+Courier) and Maaten was looking good.


But Nakamura had an ace up his sleeve, and waited for an opening. When Maaten used his Bola on his own turn to force through damage, Nakamura played and equipped [Sword of Fire and Ice](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Fire+and+Ice) on his [Myr Quadropod](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Myr+Quadropod), swung in for 3 and killed the [Grimclaw Bats](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grimclaw+Bats). Maaten sighed.


[Eater of Days](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eater+of+Days) came down -- the only creature the Dutchman could play that would be big enough to stop an equipped 'Pod. During Nakamura's extra turns, the floodgates opened: [Skullclamp](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skullclamp), clamp [Myr Moonvessel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Myr+Moonvessel), Bola, tap the Eater, attack, play more guys, clamp them, attack, Betrayal the Eater, win the game. Japan was up one match.


**Score Update**  

Japan 1, Netherlands 0


### Regrettable Burn: Remie vs. Fujita


In the matchup between the two national champions, Fujita started off backpedaling by mulliganing twice in game 1. He did manage to play both of his [Spikeshot Goblin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spikeshot+Goblin)s in the early turns, but his mana was so tight that he couldn't afford to activate them and play other cards. Remie tried hard to keep up the beats with an [Oxidda Golem](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidda+Golem), a [Vulshok Berserker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vulshok+Berserker), and a [Darksteel Brute](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Brute). A timely [Razor Barrier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Razor+Barrier) nullified both of Fujita's Spikeshot pings one turn, and the damage piled up high enough for [Beacon of Destruction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beacon+of+Destruction) to end the game. Poetic in light of Fujita's refusal to draft the red rare.


Game 2 was over quickly, as Fujita evened the match with aggressive use of his [Grimclaw Bats](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Grimclaw+Bats) and [Fleshgrafter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fleshgrafter) -- he discarded two artifacts to the black Warrior on the last turn to surprise Remie with lethal damage.


In game 3, Remie played [Stasis Cocoon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stasis+Cocoon)s on Fujita's first two creatures and began bashing away with [Krark-Clan Grunt](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Krark-Clan+Grunt), [Darksteel Brute](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Brute), and [Soldier Replica](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soldier+Replica). Fujita managed to stabilize with a Bats wearing a 3-power [Opaline Bracers](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Opaline+Bracers) (thanks to his [Darksteel Ingot](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Ingot)). He swung in with the Bats, trying to retake the lead, then dropped a [Slith Firewalker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Slith+Firewalker).


Remie attacked with his team, and Fujita blocked the Grunt with his 1/1 and played [Wail of the Nim](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wail+of+the+Nim) with entwine, killing the 2/2 and saving his Slith . . . or so he thought. Kai Budde, watching from the sidelines, pointed out that the Slith took lethal damage twice -- once in combat, and once from the Wail -- and was not saved by the regeneration shield. The table judge explained it, and Fujita slumped. At the end of the turn, Remie aimed Barbed Lighting at Fujita's head, then laid the Beacon on the table from his hand, showing that if he drew land number five the match was over. He did in fact draw it, clapped his hands as he stoop up, and did a lap around the table.


**Score Update**  

Japan 1, Netherlands 1


### The Bow Knows: Tsumura vs. Nuijten


Game 1 in the pivotal A seat was a foregone conclusion. Nuijten kept a hand with only two lands, but it was better than the one his opponent kept: six land and a [Leonin Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Elder)! Who keeps that?! The Japanese had a chance if he drew several spells before Nuijten drew a single land, but that wasn't the case. To make matters worse, when Tsumura cast [Acquire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Acquire) on turn five, Nuijten had his three best artifacts in his hand. "Looking for this?" teased Nuijten, flashing the [Loxodon Warhammer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Warhammer).


![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/sideboard/images/worlds04/fm21_3.jpg)In game 2, Nuijten was deploying fatty after fatty, but all they could do was stare across the table at Tsumura's [Vulshok Gauntlets](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vulshok+Gauntlets)-wielding [Auriok Glaivemaster](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Auriok+Glaivemaster). A 6-power first-striker was more than any of Nuijten's creatures could handle. [Viridian Longbow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Viridian+Longbow) eventually hit the table, and the Japanese player was content to win a point at a time. It worked, too -- eventually Nuijten drew [Echoing Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Ruin) and was forced to kill the Bow as opposed to the Moat-like Gauntlets to prevent himself from dying. The cagy Tsumura was holding his second Bow in his hand anticipating the Ruin, however, and dropped it, ending the game in two turns.


In the deciding game 3, Nuijten missed his third land drop for three consecutive turns, but was making progress in spite of himself by playing out [Sylvok Explorer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvok+Explorer), a Talisman, and [Dawn's Reflection](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dawn%27s+Reflection). Tsumura started in on the offensive with a [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) on a [Soldier Replica](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Soldier+Replica). Nuijten played a second Reflection, then a [Tel-Jilad Chosen](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tel-Jilad+Chosen) and an [Echoing Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Ruin) on the Plating.


Tsumura played his [Trinket Mage](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage) on the next turn, getting a Longbow, and then played [Leonin Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Elder) and the Bow with his last two mana. He could have played [Echoing Truth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Truth) on the two [Dawn's Reflection](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dawn%27s+Reflection)s, setting Nuijten back into the Stone Age, but he did not. It ended up not mattering much, however, as he played a [Quicksilver Behemoth](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Quicksilver+Behemoth) to hold the ground (with [Awe Strike](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Awe+Strike) backup if necessary), and pinged the young Dutchman to death.


**Final Score**  

Japan 2, Netherlands 1


Japan moved on to the final round in good shape to make Sunday's finals. Fujita was quick to praise his teammates, saying that he hadn't won a draft match yet Saturday, and they were still near the top of the standings.
