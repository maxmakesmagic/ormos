How Many Wizened Cenns?
=======================



 Posted in **Top Decks**
 on March 24, 2011 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






Last week's PTQ Top 8 results were pretty unbelievable if you ask me. More or less since the release of *Ravnica: City of Guilds* this column (and its predecessor, Swimming With Sharks) has tried to promote the idea of diversity in competitive metagames.

... But I don't know if I ever anticipated a set of performances like last week's!

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td135_chart.jpg)  
Last week we saw five different decks winning the five PTQs [reported](/en/events/coverage/pro-tour-nagoya-qualifier-season-top-8-extended-deck-lists) here at **DailyMTG.com**. And what's more, not a one of those decks was Faeries or even Naya! Instead we saw everything from [Steppe Lynx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx) to [Arbor Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arbor+Elf) at the one, [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) cleaning up with its clomping feet and heavy hands, and, believe it or not, [Flickerwisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flickerwisp) and [Lone Missionary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lone+Missionary) paving the way—or should I say *clearing the path*—with [Mortarpod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortarpod) and [Day of Judgment](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Day+of+Judgment).

And on top of those, the week saw some familiar names with some (sometimes) unfamiliar decks, updates to existing winners, and two or more combo decks stapled together by [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose).

Let's begin.

There were five different Bant aggro decks in last week's Top 8s; buuchan's was the one that won the 3/13 online PTQ.

One of the things that I like about Extended Bant decks is that they can be so good at so many different things. Traditionally we have seen decks with [Rhox War Monk](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Rhox+War+Monk) and [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak); great offensive (even offensive / defensive) options, plus the ability to say "no" if necessary.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td135_bantCharm.jpg)  
The version that buuchan played ran a pretty decent amount of land given its inclusion of eight one-drop accelerators... but [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) and [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch) can also be useful for powering up [Celestial Colonnade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Celestial+Colonnade) and [Stirring Wildwood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stirring+Wildwood).

[Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) makes the color combination's already awesome creatures into even more significant threats; [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine) and [Sword of Body and Mind](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Body+and+Mind) make even [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise) into a viable threat creature.

But above and beyond everything else, this deck plays arguably the best card in the current Extended, [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command).

As far as two-color aggro decks go, Boros is proving to be pretty flexible and customizable.

Hawley's version focuses on a couple of different creature-based two-card combinations:

**[Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage) + [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar):** Bryan played four big copies of [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage). Sparkmages are awesome in a lot of matchups even when going around in the Emperor's New Clothes, with no [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) in sight. Examples of things that have big problems with [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage): [Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise), [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch), [Steppe Lynx](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steppe+Lynx), [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) tokens, other [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage)s... But when you add [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) for [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar), [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage) can also easily kill: anything.

While the strategy has roughly a bazillion one-mana accelerators ([Arbor Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arbor+Elf) is essentially a [Llanowar Elves](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Llanowar+Elves) in this deck) to put you into the position for a second-turn [Elvish Archdruid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid) or [Imperious Perfect](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperious+Perfect), the powerhouse mana engine—same as ever—is [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid) + [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel).



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Heritage Druid | Nettle Sentinel |

With multiple [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel)s out, you can potentially net mana by casting a cheap green spell (say a one-mana Elf), then untapping your [Nettle Sentinel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nettle+Sentinel)s, re-tapping them for more mana via [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid), and so on. No one ever *really* gets out of being hit by a [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command), and in this deck, [Primal Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primal+Command) can go and get either a small card to keep the mana flowing, or the one [Regal Force](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Regal+Force) to almost guarantee ongoing velocity once you already have the other components on the battlefield.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Primal Command | Regal Force |

The potentially not-obvious one-turn kill works via LaVassaur's one copy of [Ezuri, Renegade Leader](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ezuri%2C+Renegade+Leader).

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td135_ezuri.jpg)  
Basically you want to have at least one Elf o that can actually attack this turn, make a ton of mana using [Heritage Druid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Heritage+Druid)s (which don't actually require that your Elves have been hanging out for the full "I'm no longer summoning sick" turn), which you use to go and get Ezuri, either by natural means or some tonnage or manipulation. With enough mana, you can easily get your potentially attacking Elves big enough to trample the opponent to death using Ezuri's "[Overrun](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Overrun)" ability again and again.

![Ezuri, Renegade Leader](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ezuri%2C+Renegade+Leader)  
Which is not to say you can't nickel-and-dime your opponents, just develop on the battlefield with more power ([Elvish Archdruid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Archdruid) and [Imperious Perfect](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Imperious+Perfect)), and play a traditionally progressive offense (say [Bramblewood Paragon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bramblewood+Paragon) into [Joraga Warcaller](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Joraga+Warcaller)), because you certainly can. Just be aware of Elves as a dual-nature creature deck and combo deck.

Jeremiah certainly wins the creativity award for viable deck choices!

This is a deck with quite a few things going on, and one that probably most of you have never seen in action (I know that I fall into this camp). I will try to do the build justice.

All the creatures have some kind of "enters the battlefield" ability. Of these, the most unusual (of, frankly, an unusual bunch) are [Flickerwisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flickerwisp) and [Glimmerpoint Stag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpoint+Stag). Even on their own, these creatures can remove [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom) tokens permanently, but when combined with other cards, they can help the squad overall build value. For example, [Flickerwisp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flickerwisp) and [Glimmerpoint Stag](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmerpoint+Stag) can reload a [Mortarpod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortarpod) or reset an [Oblivion Ring](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oblivion+Ring) or [Runed Halo](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Runed+Halo)... or get it going with any of the other sixteen "enters the battlefield" creatures in the deck.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Flickerwisp | Glimmerpoint Stag |

A mono-white deck might theoretically have issues with card advantage relative to a blue-based control deck, but Haney's weapon of choice played four copies of [Pilgrim's Eye](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pilgrim%27s+Eye) and four copies of [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens). Together, all of these help draw more lands and help the deck play lots of lands, such that it can eventually get sufficient [Plains](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plains) in play to make [Emeria, the Sky Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria%2C+the+Sky+Ruin) interesting.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Pilgrim's Eye | Wall of Omens |

[Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan) remains [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan), one of the most indomitable finishers in the format. Though here if you get rid of the first one you will very often have to deal with it again, eventually, due to [Emeria, the Sky Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Emeria%252C%2Bthe%2BSky%2BRuin) shenanigans. And while there are no [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s or [Swamp](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Swamp)s in sight, [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa) + [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan) can play a very [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan)-like combination of cards that, again, works more land onto the board for those inevitable [Emeria, the Sky Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Emeria%252C%2Bthe%2BSky%2BRuin)s.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td135_sun.jpg)  
Even [Lone Missionary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lone+Missionary) can become an annoying source of repeatable life gain, between [Emeria, the Sky Ruin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Emeria%2C+the+Sky+Ruin) and repeated work with the [Mortarpod](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mortarpod).

![Lone Missionary](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Lone+Missionary)  
Overall, the deck has a ton to frustrate attackers and voluminous creature removal, much of it quite efficient.

It will be interesting to see if this deck, so unusual next to what we "usually" see in Top 8s (let alone amongst the actual weekly winners) picks up in popularity and continues to rack up Blue Envelopes.

There are two main lines going on here, and they are of course deeply entwined with one another.

The first is that of creature density. Billy played more than 50% creatures. Having a lot of creatures means revealing a lot of creatures as the top card of his deck, which makes [Mul Daya Channelers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mul+Daya+Channelers) big. Of course he can manipulate what is on the top of his deck with [Elvish Harbinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Harbinger); [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor); and to a lesser degree [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman). Having a lot of creatures lets Billy re-buy his [Vengevine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vengevine)s pretty consistently (especially when he is drawing more than one card in a turn, however he is doing that), though it bears mentioning that his [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) re-buy percentage is significantly lower than a stock Naya deck, simply because of the presence of three different pieces of [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf)–eligible Equipment.

Speaking of Equipment, in addition to [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine), Billy played a lone [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar), which he could find with his lone [Trinket Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Trinket+Mage), which he could find with one of four [Fauna Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Fauna+Shaman)s (three main). [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) is potentially a beating with, well, *anything*, but in particular it is a much-discussed combination with both [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage) and [Inferno Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inferno+Titan).

The second theme to Billy's deck, already hinted at, is the heretofore unexplored deck design space around the top of one's deck. Is this the first viable deck that plays [Elvish Harbinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Harbinger)? I don't recall that Elf being played even in other Elves decks in the past. Well, in this deck the Harbingers allow Moreno to set up [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf), or jump all the way to [Sages of the Anima](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sages+of+the+Anima)... and then let's see what happens! When [Jace, the Mind Sculptor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace%2C+the+Mind+Sculptor) gets involved we can see as many as three cards being drawn in a turn, and Billy will draw more than one card per turn, on average, constantly churning through his deck for fresh looks. Sages + Jace, in particular, seems almost overwhelming... and it is a good thing that Billy has so many creatures that can potentially tap for mana ([Birds of Paradise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Birds+of+Paradise), [Elvish Harbinger](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elvish+Harbinger), [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch), and often [Mul Daya Channelers](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mul+Daya+Channelers)), because he is going to have quite a few cards in hand, and you need a lot of mana to handle all those extra things to do.

Here we have essentially two different combo decks, laced together in a "good stuff" framework.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td135_splinter.jpg)  
First off, you can kind of just play as a passable blue-red control deck. [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle) is a fine card, and we have seen it perform in Standard blue-black decks. In this deck it just kind of keeps things moving so that you can draw into your [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) or your [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak) or whatever you need to make a one-for-one or possibly profitable trade. In addition, you have the weak combo of [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle) + [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), which is not what you want at all, but especially when your opponent can't kill a [Sea Gate Oracle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sea+Gate+Oracle) (say the opposing deck is Green-White Trap), this combo is going to be dominant, and embarrassingly so.

![Splinter Twin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Splinter+Twin)  
What *do* you want with your [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin)?

Obviously you want [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) + [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin), which is an infinite damage combo. Cast [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), say at the end of the opponent's turn. Cast [Splinter Twin](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Splinter+Twin) on [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite). Tap [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) to make an extra, hasty, [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite); the new [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) untaps the first [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite), which taps again to make another one... forever until you decide to attack with, say, a hundred temporary hasty [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite)s.

Ho hum, turn four kill.

![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/images/magic/daily/td/td135_pestermiteKill.jpg)  
We have seen [Pestermite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pestermite) kills before, but this deck *also* has a [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) one, though it might be hard to see, initially.

In a good number of cases you will use [Manamorphose](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Manamorphose) as a red spell to get the green mana to cast [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift), but there are also [Flooded Grove](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flooded+Grove)s in the deck (which help you play [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) out of the sideboard, as well). [Flooded Grove](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Flooded+Grove) + [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) is almost a two-card combination itself.

SpeCialK's deck has enough [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s that it can actually just go for a [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle) kill off of [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) without [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen), but unlike the green-red or [Wargate](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wargate)-type [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?&name=Valakut%252C%2Bthe%2BMolten%2BPinnacle) decks, you have to be much more aware of which lands you play, which lands you have on the battlefield, and how many lands you have access to in your deck, rather than just assuming that your [Primeval Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Primeval+Titan) or [Prismatic Omen](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Prismatic+Omen) and a bunch of fetch lands are going to get you there.

As with so many decks since the printing of [Sword of Feast and Famine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sword+of+Feast+and+Famine), this one is built on the power of [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) as its primary consumer of mana in the early turns, and one of its main sources of card economy. The addition of red—and along with that the addition of [Cunning Sparkmage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cunning+Sparkmage)—makes [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) good, and [Basilisk Collar](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Basilisk+Collar) is added to the [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) package here.

Much of the rest of the deck is straightforward good stuff—four copies of [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command), four copies of [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt)—but the big finisher is the solo [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan). [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan) has been an over-performer all season, first as the full four-of in White-Blue early on (re-buying [Jace Beleren](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Jace+Beleren) and [Wall of Omens](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wall+of+Omens), if you recall), and now in decks like the PTQ-winning Mono-White Control from Seattle. In the Red-White-Blue Mystic deck, [Sun Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sun+Titan) protects your Equipment, keeps [Kitchen Finks](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kitchen+Finks) annoying for beatdown decks, and of course advances your mana via [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa) and [Scalding Tarn](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scalding+Tarn).
