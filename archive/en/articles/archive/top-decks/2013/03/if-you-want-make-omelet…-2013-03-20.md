Nathan Holiday took down Grand Prix San Diego with an updated Eggs list, because “...two-mana [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker) for [Black Lotus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Lotus) is a legal play.”

Holiday, of course, was referencing a [Reshape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) for ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif) ([Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) being effectively zero here) to put [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom) directly in play. When you consider that [Tinker](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tinker) and [Black Lotus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Black+Lotus) are restricted in Vintage (**Magic**‘s largest and most busted combo format), it is probably saying something about the strategy’s relative power level that Holiday could play four of both cards! (While other mages were “attacking for 2.”)

**So How Does Eggs Work?**

The original Eggs deck used *actual* “Egg” cards like [Darkwater Egg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkwater+Egg) and [Skycloud Egg](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Skycloud+Egg) to draw cards and make/filter mana. Today, the deck replaces the Egg cycle with a variety of stand-ins like [Chromatic Star](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Star), [Chromatic Sphere](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chromatic+Sphere), and (especially) [Conjurer’s Bauble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Conjurer%E2%80%99s+Bauble).

Essentially, Eggs plays (and sacrifices) many of these Egg-like artifacts to draw cards while staying more-or-less even on mana. Then [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) or [Faith’s Reward](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%E2%80%99s+Reward) can return many permanents to the battlefield to do it all over again!

Over the course of going off with the Eggs deck, a player might destroy some of his or her own lands with [Ghost Quarter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ghost+Quarter) (putting both into the graveyard); get another land (of course), only to get both originals back the same turn; and, like Holiday said, start off with a mana explosion from a [Lotus Bloom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Bloom)... which would (of course) return to play ready to produce another ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227125855im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/white.gif), ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161115194122im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/blue.gif), or even ![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif)![](https://web.archive.org/web/20161227130159im_/http://magic.wizards.com/sites/all/modules/custom/wiz_autocard/wiz_manacost/manasymbols/red.gif).

Eventually, an Eggs player can rip through his or her deck, to the point where he or she has no library. Then the Eggs player can just put back a [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) with [Conjurer’s Bauble](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Conjurer%E2%80%99s+Bauble) and just cast the same [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) over and over again. Once you have jumped through sufficient hoops to get to this point, you can do unlimited damage with a [Pyrite Spellbomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyrite+Spellbomb). Easy game, right?

**How is *This* Eggs Different?**

One important innovation in Holiday’s deck is [Twincast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twincast). He mentioned in his [Top 8 profile](http://archive.wizards.com/magic/magazine/article.aspx?x=mtg/daily/eventcoverage/gpsd13/welcome#10) that [Twincast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twincast) allowed him to go off through multiple copies of [Faerie Macabre](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faerie+Macabre). Holiday could respond to a hate card with [Twincast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Twincast) (likely on his [Second Sunrise](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Second+Sunrise) or [Faith’s Reward](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Faith%E2%80%99s+Reward)) giving him a two-mana workaround and general piece of redundancy.

Bryan De La Torre played Robots to his San Diego Top 8.

Affinity is an offense machine that can drop its hand in the first few turns and transform a lowly [Memnite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Memnite) into a two-shot robot via [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating)... *or* can build an inevitable wave of damage over time via [Steel Overseer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Steel+Overseer). In this deck, [Galvanic Blast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Galvanic+Blast) basically always does 4 damage for one mana; and [Thoughtcast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thoughtcast) does—for generally one mana—what other decks are willing to spend three or more mana to accomplish. And what if an opponent does something super annoying with [Martyr of Sands](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Martyr+of+Sands) or otherwise gains infinite life? No problem! You might just kill him or her in one shot *anyway* by attaching [Cranial Plating](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) to [Inkmoth Nexus](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inkmoth+Nexus)!



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

  
As offensively capable as Robots might be, it is also one of the most flexible strategies in Modern. [Glimmervoid](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Glimmervoid) and [Mox Opal](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mox+Opal) allow the deck to produce any color of mana, and because of that, it can play a wide variety of sideboard cards. Interestingly, Robots is equally at home with a [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine)-trumping [Deglamer](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deglamer) or (despite being a deck with only one basic land—and an [Island](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Island) at that) the deck is capable of stealing free games with a [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon).

It probably surprises long time fans not at all to see Pro Tour Hall of Famer Brian Kibler battling at the top table with his favorite card, [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary), in front of him. With only one [Treetop Village](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Treetop+Village), one [Kessig Wolf Run](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Kessig+Wolf+Run), and one [Horizon Canopy](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Horizon+Canopy) (okay, and I suppose one [Blood Crypt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Crypt) for the [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman)s) as flashy targets, [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary) here is mostly a “[Terravore](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terravore)” rather than limited [Demonic Tutor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Demonic+Tutor) with legs. Mostly, it’s just big and powerful, bolstered by an excess of [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa)s, [Misty Rainforest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Misty+Rainforest)s, and [Verdant Catacomb](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Verdant+Catacomb)s.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

  
This particular deck is a Naya good-stuff deck focused on tough creatures. Most of Kibler’s big threats—from two-drop [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) to three-drop [Loxodon Smiter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Smiter) and beyond—are too big to swat with a [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) or [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix); it should go without saying that they also fight small creatures well when combined with [Domri Rade](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Domri+Rade)’s -2 ability.

Clearly set up to fight smaller creatures in fair decks, Kibler’s Domri Naya does not exactly lie down for “broken” angles of attack. His [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch)s and [Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman)s can potentially set up a second-turn [Blood Moon](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Blood+Moon) to cripple opposing mana bases while leaving him just enough mana flexibility to get his beat going thanks to a couple of basic [Forest](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Forest)s and one [Plain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plain)s. And hey, [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite) is big, fast, and doesn’t care where you get its red from.

Matt Ferrando went with a similar [Knight of the Reliquary](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knight+of+the+Reliquary)–centric midrange attack deck as Kibler, but his [Knights](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Knights) did a lot more sensational work as well as being big. In addition to [Stirring Wildwood](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stirring+Wildwood) and [Vault of the Archangel](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Vault+of+the+Archangel), Ferrando’s deck packed multiple copies of [Tectonic Edge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge)—very suitable for banging up [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) or other specialty lands.



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
|  |  |

  
Here we have a midrange deck that essentially focuses on high-quality cards in its three colors. Where it differs from Kibler (and I suppose later Jund variants) is that Ferrando eschewed red completely for black removal ([Abrupt Decay](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Abrupt+Decay)), disruption ([Inquisition of Kozilek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inquisition+of+Kozilek)), or both ([Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil)).

This deck has some interesting graveyard and recurring advantage combinations. Of course [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) and [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) get along well. We have seen this in numerous places, including Modern Jund decks that simply splashed for the hyper-efficient [Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Souls). Here, [Darkblast](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darkblast) and [Life from the Loam](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Life+from+the+Loam) do much the same thing: allowing Ferrando to continually get creature removal (or a powerful card-advantage engine that can especially recur his [Tectonic Edge](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tectonic+Edge)s) while setting up [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) over and over again to great advantage.

From its inception, Modern has been a curious animal and work in progress as a format. Start off with no [Stoneforge Mystic](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stoneforge+Mystic) or [Bitterblossom](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bitterblossom), counter the initial success of combo by banning [Ponder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ponder) and [Preordain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Preordain). Despite the prior success of combo decks like Eggs, I think it would be fair to say that the most prevalent strategy for many months was Jund. The format is expansive and many of the overpowered cards started off unavailable... so why not play for maximum per-card efficacy? Jund saw unparalleled efficiency at each mana point: [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt), [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) and [Dark Confidant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Confidant), [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil). All played bigger than their top-rights but none so much as [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf).

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Bloodbraid+Elf)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf)Part [Ancestral Recall](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ancestral+Recall), part [Dark Ritual](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Dark+Ritual), and even a little bit [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) striking for 3 the turn it appeared, [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) was the Jund flag bearer, the card it really wanted to draw into, the ace justifying a four-mana price tag. Card advantage and initiative in one, [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) would sometimes cascade out [Liliana of the Dark Realms](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Dark+Realms) and often flip a [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) or the equivalent to clear the way.

And then it went away.

It is interesting to see how various capable players have reacted to the loss of the Jund ace. Matt Ferrando played highly efficient individual cards sans red, while Brian Kibler kept some of the powerful—if fringe—explosive Jund elements like [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) and [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite) (even [Lotus Cobra](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lotus+Cobra) INTO [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite))... if not in “Jund” colors exactly.

Eric Froehlich continued a dip into white that started with [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) (onetime minority splash and paragon of single-card efficiency at three, great solution to [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil), and card capable of trading with [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) with no loss of card advantage), replacing [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) at four with [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant)!

[![](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Ajani+Vengeant)](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant)  
EFro’s post–[Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf) Jund dips deeply into white for [Path to Exile](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Path+to+Exile) and a variety of powerhouse sideboard spells. [Stony Silence](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Stony+Silence) helps justify the mana stretch against multiple Jund foils, and [Timely Reinforcements](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Timely+Reinforcements) plays like a [Lingering Souls](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lingering+Souls) custom-built to dig you out of a beatdown hole. [Aven Mindcensor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aven+Mindcensor) does good work against all manner of [Arid Mesa](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arid+Mesa)s and [Marsh Flat](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Marsh+Flat)s but in particular buys time—hopefully enough time—against a fast combo deck relying on [Reshape](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reshape) or [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift).

Ken Yukuhiro claimed to be playing “Multicolor Jund” but this is a Domain Zoo deck to anyone who has ever marveled at Raphael Levy’s [back](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Events.aspx?x=mtgevent/gpdal07/welcome)-to-[back](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Events.aspx?x=mtgevent/gpsin07/welcome) or, you know, just seen a Domain Zoo deck.

While from some perspectives this deck furthers the Jund philosophy of maximum efficacy at each drop ([Deathrite Shaman](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Deathrite+Shaman), [Noble Hierarch](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Noble+Hierarch), and [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) at one; [Tarmogoyf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tarmogoyf) and [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) at two), once you get to [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) on three I think you are far enough afield of BRG to call this offense something else (especially when the [Geist](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist) appears on the second turn).



|  |  |

|  |  |

  
The big game in this deck is jumping [Geist of Saint Traft](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Geist+of+Saint+Traft) into the red zone with [Elspeth, Knight-Errant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Elspeth%2C+Knight-Errant) on turn three (who can even count that high?) but the really big game is any time Ken could lean on his domain spells. [Might of Alara](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Might+of+Alara) makes anything in this deck bigger than a [Thundermaw Hellkite](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Thundermaw+Hellkite) and [Tribal Flames](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tribal+Flames) is a massive 5-damage for two mana... in a [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage) deck.

Think about it.

You probably already know how [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) works: The “one-card combo” of [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) with a ton of lands in play—those lands dug up by [Search for Tomorrow](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Search+for+Tomorrow), [Farseek](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Farseek), and the defensive wonder-Snake [Sakura-Tribe Elder](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder) (possibly the format’s foremost chump blocker). Then [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s upon [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s upon [Mountain](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mountain)s enter play with one or two copies of [Valakut, the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Valakut%2C+the+Molten+Pinnacle) (depending); hilarity ensues and opponents die. [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) buys time with card advantage and/or control spells *a la*[Firespout](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Firespout) and [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command). And hey, one-card combo.

What I would rather talk about is David’s sideboard!

When opponents try to stop your shuffling with [Aven Mindcensor](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aven+Mindcensor), force you to discard your powerful combo pieces, or attack your very land base (including, of course, [the Molten Pinnacle](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=the+Molten+Pinnacle)) what’s a mage to do?

Transform!

![](https://media.wizards.com/images/magic/daily/td/td239_wurmcoilcon.jpg)  
Sharfman not only packs a ton of creature power in his sideboard, they are creatures with purpose. He has tons of mana acceleration, so six-drops are no problem; [Obstinate Baloth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obstinate+Baloth) and [Wurmcoil Engine](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wurmcoil+Engine) buy time against beatdown (while providing suitable size-to-cost ratios); and [Inferno Titan](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Inferno+Titan) is like a [Bonfire of the Damned](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bonfire+of+the+Damned) with feet. Like Brian Kibler’s [Loxodon Smiter](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Loxodon+Smiter)s, [Obstinate Baloth](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Obstinate+Baloth) in [Scapeshift](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Scapeshift) also provides tremendous [Liliana of the Veil](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Liliana+of+the+Veil) defense: if the opponent forces you to discard... he or she might not like what you discard.

Rounding out the San Diego Top 8 is... a legitimate control deck!

Control has certainly had its challenges in a world of such speed and card-advantageous threat cards. Sammy Tukeman made it work by focusing on per-card value himself. [Lightning Bolt](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Bolt) and [Lightning Helix](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Helix) are great creature killers, especially when combined with [Snapcaster Mage](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Snapcaster+Mage). A thin array of permission, starting on [Spell Snare](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Spell+Snare) and [Mana Leak](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mana+Leak), can help keep an opponent honest, although [Cryptic Command](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cryptic+Command)—itself quite ace-like—is the only hard counter.

Tukeman’s big games are [Ajani Vengeant](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ajani+Vengeant) (where his bountiful removal plus permission can help buy time for the ultimate) and Standard nail-in-the-coffin [Sphinx’s Revelation](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sphinx%E2%80%99s+Revelation). Because what goes better with card advantage, great instants, and value lifegain... but an instant providing more card advantage and more lifegain?

San Diego provided Modern—and **Magic**—fans a truly diverse format. Tons of different strategies, ways to win, and the rebirth of an old favorite finisher or two. So... how are you liking life after [Bloodbraid Elf](https://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Bloodbraid+Elf)?
