Preparing for Champs
====================



 Posted in **Feature**
 on October 20, 2004 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/styles/auth_small/public/images/person/authorpic_mikeflores.jpg)
By Mike Flores




Michael Flores is the author of Deckade and The Official Miser's Guide; the designer of numerous State, Regional, Grand Prix, National, and Pro Tour–winning decks; and the onetime editor-in-chief of The Magic Dojo. He'd claim allegiance to Dimir (if such a Guild existed)… but instead will just shrug "Simic." 






![](https://media.magic.wizards.com/image_legacy_migration/global/images/events_magic_champs_picMain_en.jpg)[Champs](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=events/magic/champs) is the first major tournament -- series of tournaments around the world, really -- showcasing a Standard that includes *Champions of Kamigawa*. That means that Champs is your first shot at dueling with the new Legendary Creatures, bashing from the first turn with the Hound of Konda, and summoning the most dangerous [Dragons](http://gatherer.wizards.com/default.asp?term=dragon&fields=%7Ctype&format=Allsets&color=All&output=summary&sort=name&first=1) since Rith ruled the sky. You can try out new ideas and add powerful new tools or perfectly fitting new puzzle pieces to your favorite decks. But to be successful, you will have to know what you are up against.

Standard for Champs this year is essentially *Mirrodin* Block Constructed with two additional inclusions: *Eighth Edition* and *Champions of Kamigawa*. Especially given the caliber of decks that came out of *Mirrodin* Block Constructed, that means that you should prepare for the best decks to come out of the last block first. 

### Public Enemy Number One

For those of you who will make Champs your first sanctioned tournament experience, let me introduce you to the bad guys:



|  |  |
| --- | --- |
| Arcbound Ravager | Disciple of the Vault |

These two notorious villains ruled *Mirrodin* Block Constructed and have been a force in Standard since the release of *Darksteel*. For his part, [Disciple of the Vault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault) has been played since Champs *last* year, albeit back then, he was mostly a foil for Affinity-killer [Akroma's Vengeance](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Akroma%27s+Vengeance). Today, [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) and [Disciple of the Vault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault) make up the finest two-card combination since long unplayable [Illusions of Grandeur](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Illusions+of+Grandeur) met the seemingly innocuous [Donate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Donate) in 1999. Both cards are solid alone, but together, they push the Affinity deck over the top. 

Affinity is the fastest beatdown deck currently available in Standard. It comes out with efficient first turn plays followed by free 2/2 creatures and near-free 4/4s and 3/2 flyers. Affinity can take your first 10 life points in the blink of an eye with relentless waves of offense or a single successful hit from [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating). Once you are at a manageable life total, Standard's consensus best deck can finish you in many different ways. With two sources of black mana in play, Affinity can pass a [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) onto the only creature you didn't block. With [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) in play, it can move a pile of modular counters onto the same. With both [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) and [Disciple of the Vault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault) in play, Affinity can “Fireball” you for however many artifacts it has in play without giving the average blue mage an opportunity to counter.

Affinity originally hit the scene with giant [Broodstar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Broodstar)s pumped forward with [Lightning Greaves](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Lightning+Greaves). Today, it is a much more streamlined offensive deck that comes, typically, in one of two flavors. Though they have many cards and proactive tactics in common, playing against the two builds is quite different.

The above are two versions of Affinity that made the Top 8 of the recent World Championships. Though there are other variations that use cards like [Moriok Rigger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moriok+Rigger) and [Terror](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Terror), I believe that these decks are fairly representative of what you will see, even with the inclusion of *Champions of Kamigawa*, as neither deck plays cards from *Onslaught* block.

Likely the more common version of Affinity you will face is based on [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial). This deck is remarkably steady, robust, and resilient. Though it lacks the pure explosiveness of “speed” Affinity, this group of Affinity decks has a tremendous advantage generated by the card [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial). [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial) is a perfect foil to [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) (the card with the most notable impact out of *Eighth Edition*). Where most offensive decks will wither against that powerful defensive sorcery, [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial) can pump out a creature at the end of turn, after [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) has resolved. With a [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) in play, this allows Aether Vial Affinity to set up a lethal turn out of nowhere. 

While all Affinity decks take advantage of the mana benefits afforded by the Affinity for Artifacts mechanic itself, Aether Vial Affinity also plays an abnormally high concentration of 1- and 2-drops. This lets the deck pump out creatures with [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial) with greater consistency (consider the difficulty of using the card to deploy [Somber Hoverguard](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Somber+Hoverguard) or even [Moriok Rigger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Moriok+Rigger)). Among its 2-drops is [Atog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog). While [Atog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog) seems like a much worse [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager) that is not an artifact (and therefore does not contribute to Affinity for Artifacts), has a non-permanent boost effect, and requires colored mana, [Atog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog) is also a tremendous threat against decks with red and green removal. While many opponents seek to overwhelm Affinity with cards like [Shatter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shatter) and [Oxidize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize), those cards have no effect against [Atog](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Atog)… except to make it a little bigger.

Speed Affinity is capable of incredibly explosive starts. Its best first turns will see multiple threats in play, which will be complimented by [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) and [Myr Enforcer](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Myr+Enforcer) in short order. Though this school of Affinity lacks the consistency and lasting power of [Aether Vial](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Aether+Vial), the speed decks play a cheater's game. Speed Affinity generates mana from [Chrome Mox](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Chrome+Mox) on the first turn whether or not it has been imprinted, and hits with [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating) with great frequency because of its diverse flyers. While most decks need to reduce the opponent to 0, the speed decks only really need to do 15 damage – and in some games only 10 – because of the presence of [Shrapnel Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast). No deck in the Standard format has a better best draw than this one.

Fighting the speed Affinity decks can be very annoying because of [Welding Jar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Welding+Jar). The deck wins a ridiculous amount of games by sacrificing its board to [Arcbound Ravager](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arcbound+Ravager), dumping all the counters on an [Ornithopter](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Ornithopter), and swinging a couple of times while hiding behind the Jar. You may have noticed that some successful decks – from Brian Kibler's otherwise mono-white deck at US Nationals to Brock Parker's otherwise mono-red deck from the World Championships – play [Oxidize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize). Many of you might think this is a strange choice. Wouldn't [Naturalize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize) at just one more mana be more flexible? In a sense, [Naturalize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize) is a stonger card for its ability to fight enchantments, but conversely, [Oxidize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize) can rip through a [Welding Jar](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Welding+Jar) defense while [Naturalize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Naturalize) barely slows Affinity down.

### The Limiting Factor

The next biggest deck to come out of the *Mirrodin* Block is Tooth and Nail. Tooth and Nail (called “[TwelvePost](/en/articles/archive/event-coverage/deck-tech-twelvepost-2004-02-27)” at that event) came on the scene at the [Block Pro Tour early this year](/en/events/coverage/live-coverage-2004-pro-tour-kobe), and nearly took home the top prize. While the game's finest designer had to settle for second place in Kobe, Gabriel Nassif's performance at the Pro Tour helped propel him to Player of the Year. 

The core of Tooth and Nail is [Sylvan Scrying](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Scrying) and a set of entwine sorceries – [Reap and Sow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reap+and+Sow) and [Tooth and Nail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) itself. The goal of the deck is to generate a ton of mana and then entwine the latter sorcery for a ridiculous effect. What actually comes down is to some extent irrelevant… most of the time a resolved [Tooth and Nail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) is the death knell. The end can take the form of two copies of [Darksteel Colossus](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Darksteel+Colossus), ready to trample over for 22, invulnerable to [Wrath of God](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Wrath+of+God) or other sweep. It can be the team of [Platinum Angel](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Platinum+Angel) and [Leonin Abunas](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Leonin+Abunas); one ensures victory while the other prevents its partner from being removed. Victory can take the form of [Triskelion](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Triskelion) and [Mephidross Vampire](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Mephidross+Vampire)… a couple that sweeps the board of offending creatures on the other side of the table. Colored mana requirements are irrelevant once [Tooth and Nail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Tooth+and+Nail) is within range.

The original Tooth and Nail decks, being limited to *Mirrodin* Block, relied on [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) for accelerated mana. Later variations in Standard moved to the Urzatron – [Urza's Tower](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Tower), [Urza's Mine](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Mine), and [Urza's Power Plant](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Urza%27s+Power+Plant) – strung together via the aforementioned [Sylvan Scrying](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sylvan+Scrying) and [Reap and Sow](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Reap+and+Sow). While technically more powerful than their [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) predecessors, the Urzatron decks typically had to rely on [Talisman of Unity](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Talisman+of+Unity) for early mana because, with at least twelve colorless lands, they could not reliably hit their green mana on time. This has become a generally inferior design paradigm for two reasons: First of all, people run a lot of [Oxidize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize)s for Affinity opponents, and including cards like [Talisman of Unity](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Talisman+of+Unity) just lets them play target practice. Moreover, *Champions of Kamigawa* and *Eighth Edition* provide some fantastic cards that do the same job as [Talisman of Unity](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Talisman+of+Unity), but block Affinity creatures at the same time.

Because of that, I recommend going back to the [Cloudpost](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cloudpost) versions. Tooth and Nail will win most long games against most decks; the trick is to weather early beatdown, and the ability to block while accelerating mana can go a long way.

In addition, *Champions of Kamigawa* also gives us a powerful new Legendary creature that fits right into Tooth and Nail:

![Kiki-Jiki, Mirror Breaker](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Handlers/Image.ashx?type=card&name=Kiki-Jiki%2C+Mirror+Breaker)Kiki-Jiki is a perfect tag team partner to [Sundering Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sundering+Titan), a creature many Tooth and Nail players were already using. Against a two color deck, Kiki-Jiki and [Sundering Titan](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sundering+Titan) will typically take out *six* opposing lands on the first turn, and go lethal on the next.

Tooth and Nail is the Limiting Factor because even though testing against Affinity is the most important, few decks can beat both Affinity *and* Tooth and Nail. Practicing against Tooth and Nail after becoming comfortable against Affinity is an important key to tuning a deck that you believe can be successful at Champs. Control decks especially find Tooth and Nail a difficult opponent. Adding [Boseiju, Who Shelters All](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Boseiju%2C+Who+Shelters+All) to the mix only makes matters worse for blue mages.

The preceding deck is an updated Big Red listing by former Block PT Champ Osyp Lebedowicz. While Kuroda's deck ran [Detonate](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Detonate) main *and* brought in [Echoing Ruin](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Echoing+Ruin) *and* [Furnace Dragon](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Furnace+Dragon) out of the board, Osyp's deck concentrates more on being able to beat more, different, decks. While Kuroda, playing a *Mirrodin* Block deck, knew that he would have to beat Affinity time and again, Osyp's listing has main deck land destruction for Tooth and Nail, and comes prepared for a variety of responses, using far flung answers like [Pyroclasm](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Pyroclasm) for small creatures and [Cranial Extraction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Extraction) for big cards or combinations.

Either strategy is entirely defensible. [Arc-Slogger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger) goes a long way. Few decks can reliably deal with [Arc-Slogger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger) main deck, and even the anti-artifact Block versions can mow over green decks with a single 4/5 Beast and very little backup. 

I find Big Red to be a tough opponent to test against. While Affinity is known for the strength of its aggression and Tooth and Nail has a big sorcery to squash almost any opponent in almost any situation, Big Red has *burn*. Burn hasn't gotten any friendlier in the last ten years: in a deck like this one, it plays equal parts creature defense and straight-to-the-head endgame kill. Burn can help Big Red come out of even a losing situation with a flurry of [Shrapnel Blast](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Shrapnel+Blast)s, Pulses, and [Arc-Slogger](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Arc-Slogger) activations to abruptly reduce an ostensibly healthy opponent to ash.

There are some clunky choices, notably [Relic Barrier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+Barrier)… but I figure if a Goblin deck can afford to play [Oxidize](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Oxidize), then a Rats deck can afford to play [Relic Barrier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+Barrier). [Relic Barrier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+Barrier) is in fact amazing in combination with [Horobi, Death's Wail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Horobi%2C+Death%27s+Wail). Horobi shuts down Affinity's [Cranial Plating](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Cranial+Plating)s and Modular counters all by itself; in concert with [Relic Barrier](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Relic+Barrier), this Legendary Spirit can set up an artifact bloodbath. 

The rat deck can get a quick advantage in cards, then mop up the opponent's poor luck with [Nezumi Graverobber](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nezumi+Graverobber). It can lock down a control deck with [Nezumi Shortfang](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Nezumi+Shortfang), then go immediately lethal with its flipping Rack effect. It can block early with the many Rats, then go long with [Horobi, Death's Wail](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Horobi%2C+Death%27s+Wail) and [Night's Whisper](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Night%27s+Whisper). 

That said, the sideboard will be very important. I am guessing [Persecute](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Persecute) will remain strong against dedicated Red and Green decks, while additional disruption and removal waits in escrow for control and creatures, respectively.

My favorite deck right now, though, is U/G Control. Another holdover from *Mirrodin* Block, U/G Control makes arguably the best use of new cards [Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) and [Sakura-Tribe Elder](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Sakura-Tribe+Elder).

[Gifts Ungiven](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Gifts+Ungiven) is a tricky spell to play. In concert with U/G's [Eternal Witness](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Eternal+Witness), it hits much more reliably, and can set up the cards it needs to win the game with great frequency. This version has strong game against Affinity with its multiple fronts of anti-artifact cards, and can break up Tooth and Nail's mana base with [Plow Under](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Plow+Under). It is not particularly strong against Big Red.

### If You Can't Beat ‘em…

The Champs format is extremely diverse. Even though there is a known “best deck” in Affinity, players will show up with threats and answers of all five colors, and no overview will be able to adequately represent every possible opponent. Your foes will attack with white creatures and green, counter spells, shuffle their decks with [Beacon of Destruction](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Beacon+of+Destruction), and finish you off with [Disciple of the Vault](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Disciple+of+the+Vault). Success at Champs will require a little ingenuity on your part, but more than that, a strong working knowledge of what you are up against, and how to fight each of the different potential opponents. 

This article is intended to be a primer for what kinds of decks you might face across the table, something to help you prepare yourself and your own creations… but as my old friend Worth Wollpert taught me many years ago, *“If you can't beat ‘em, join ‘em.”* Worth and I both adopted popular [Necropotence](http://gatherer.wizards.com/Pages/Card/Details.aspx?name=Necropotence) strategies in our early Pro Tour careers, and today, he has gone all the way to joining Wizards of the Coast's R&D team!

Good luck [this weekend](http://archive.wizards.com/Magic/Magazine/Article.aspx?x=events/magic/champs), and don't forget: If you can't beat Affinity… You know the rest.
